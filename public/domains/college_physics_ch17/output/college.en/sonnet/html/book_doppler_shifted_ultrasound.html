🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the magnitude of the normal force $F_\perp$ exerted on a surface divided by the area $A$ over which that force is distributed:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; at any point within a static fluid, it acts equally in all directions (Pascal's principle). In a fluid column of uniform density $\rho$ under gravitational acceleration $g$, the gauge pressure at depth $h$ below the free surface is:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface. This linear dependence on depth arises because each additional fluid layer contributes its weight per unit area to the layers beneath it.

Worked Example. A rectangular tank is filled with water ($\rho = 1000\,\text{kg/m}^3$) to a depth of $h = 3.0\,\text{m}$. The atmospheric pressure at the surface is $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Find the absolute pressure at the bottom of the tank.

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1000)(9.81)(3.0) $$ $$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 2.943 \times 10^4 = 1.307 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa} $$

The gauge pressure (excess above atmospheric) is simply $\rho g h = 2.94 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa} \approx 0.29\,\text{atm}$.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. Any change in pressure applied to an enclosed incompressible fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if the applied surface pressure changes by $\Delta P$, then at every interior point the pressure also changes by $\Delta P$. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small piston area $A_1$ produces an equal pressure increase throughout the fluid, generating a large force $F_2 = P \cdot A_2$ over a larger piston area $A_2$.

Laboratory Exercise. Using a U-tube manometer partially filled with water, apply a known gauge pressure to one arm by connecting it to a sealed reservoir at a measured height. Record the height difference $\Delta h$ between the two arms and verify that $\Delta P = \rho g \Delta h$. Repeat with a denser fluid (e.g., glycerin, $\rho \approx 1260\,\text{kg/m}^3$) and compare the height differences for the same applied pressure, confirming the inverse dependence of $\Delta h$ on $\rho$.

🌱 Simple Harmonic Motion

🌱 Simple Harmonic Motion

Definition. Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a special class of periodic motion in which the restoring force on an object is directly proportional to its displacement from equilibrium and directed opposite to that displacement. Mathematically, this condition is expressed as

$$ F = -kx, $$

where $k > 0$ is the spring constant (or, more generally, the stiffness coefficient) and $x$ is the displacement from the equilibrium position. Applying Newton's second law, $F = ma$, yields the equation of motion

$$ m\ddot{x} + kx = 0, $$

whose general solution is

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), $$

with angular frequency $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$, amplitude $A$, and phase constant $\phi$ determined by initial conditions. The period and frequency follow immediately:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T}. $$

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 0.50\ \text{kg}$ is attached to a horizontal spring with $k = 200\ \text{N/m}$. At $t = 0$ the block is displaced $x_0 = 0.10\ \text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest.

Find the angular frequency, period, and position at $t = 0.30\ \text{s}$.

Step 1: $\omega = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{200/0.50} = 20\ \text{rad/s}$.

Step 2: $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/20 \approx 0.314\ \text{s}$.

Step 3: Since $v(0) = 0$ and $x(0) = x_0$, we identify $A = 0.10\ \text{m}$ and $\phi = 0$. Therefore

$$ x(0.30) = 0.10\cos(20 \times 0.30) = 0.10\cos(6.0) \approx 0.10 \times 0.960 = 0.096\ \text{m}. $$

Key Theorem. Because the spring force is conservative, the mechanical energy conservation principle established in classical mechanics applies directly to SHM:

$$ E = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2 = \text{const.} $$

This result, rather than introducing new physics, is the familiar kinetic-plus-potential energy balance evaluated for a linear restoring force. It immediately yields the maximum speed $v_{\max} = A\omega$ at $x = 0$, and confirms that the object momentarily stops ($v = 0$) at the turning points $x = \pm A$.

🌱 Wave

🌱 Wave

Definition. A wave is a disturbance that propagates through a medium (or through free space, in the case of electromagnetic waves), transferring energy and momentum without the net transport of matter. Mathematically, any function of the form

$$ y(x, t) = A\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

describes a sinusoidal traveling wave moving in the positive $x$-direction, where $A$ is the amplitude, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, and $\phi$ is the initial phase. The wave speed is related to these quantities by

$$ v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \lambda f. $$

Waves are classified as transverse (displacement perpendicular to propagation, e.g., light on a string) or longitudinal (displacement parallel to propagation, e.g., sound in air).

Worked Example. A sinusoidal wave on a stretched string is described by $y(x,t) = 0.020\sin(6.28x - 31.4t)$ meters, where $x$ is in meters and $t$ in seconds. Identify the amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave speed.

  • Amplitude: $A = 0.020\ \text{m}$
  • Wave number: $k = 6.28\ \text{rad/m}$, so $\lambda = 2\pi/k = 1.00\ \text{m}$
  • Angular frequency: $\omega = 31.4\ \text{rad/s}$, so $f = \omega/2\pi = 5.00\ \text{Hz}$
  • Wave speed: $v = \omega/k = 31.4/6.28 = 5.00\ \text{m/s}$

Key Theorem — Wave Equation. Any wave satisfying the linear wave equation

$$ \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} $$

obeys the superposition principle: if $y_1$ and $y_2$ are both solutions, then $y_1 + y_2$ is also a solution. This underpins phenomena such as interference and standing waves. For a transverse wave on a string under tension $T$ with linear mass density $\mu$, the wave speed is $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$, confirming that $v$ depends only on the medium's physical properties.

🍃 Sound

🍃 Sound

Definition. Sound is a mechanical, longitudinal wave that propagates through a compressible medium by alternating compressions and rarefactions of the medium's constituent particles. Unlike electromagnetic waves, sound requires a material medium and cannot travel through a vacuum. A sound wave is fully characterized by a single primitive quantity—frequency $f$ (Hz), the number of complete oscillation cycles per second—together with derived quantities: wavelength $\lambda$ (m), amplitude $A$ (Pa for pressure waves), and propagation speed $v$ (m/s). These are related by the fundamental wave equation:

$$ v = f\lambda $$

The speed of sound in an ideal gas is given by:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_0}{\rho}} $$

where $\gamma$ is the adiabatic index (approximately 1.40 for diatomic gases such as air), $P_0$ is the equilibrium pressure, and $\rho$ is the mass density. For dry air at $T = 20\,^\circ\text{C}$, this yields $v \approx 343\,\text{m/s}$.

Worked Example. A tuning fork vibrating at $f = 440\,\text{Hz}$ (concert A) is struck in air at $20\,^\circ\text{C}$. Determine the wavelength of the sound wave produced.

Using $v = f\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343\,\text{m/s}}{440\,\text{Hz}} \approx 0.780\,\text{m} $$

This wavelength falls within the range of typical room dimensions, which is why standing-wave resonances are audible in enclosed spaces.

Key Theorem: Newton–Laplace Correction. Newton originally computed the speed of sound assuming an isothermal process, obtaining $v = \sqrt{P_0/\rho}$, which underestimates the measured value by roughly 15%. Laplace corrected this by recognizing that compressions and rarefactions in a sound wave occur too rapidly for heat exchange with the surroundings, making the process adiabatic rather than isothermal. Replacing $P_0$ with $\gamma P_0$ yields the accurate expression above. This correction reflects a fundamental principle: the thermodynamic process governing wave propagation determines the appropriate elastic modulus.

Repeat at three different temperatures to verify the temperature dependence.

🌱 Frequency

🌱 Frequency

Definition. The frequency of a periodic phenomenon is the number of complete cycles occurring per unit time. For a wave or oscillator with period $T$ (the duration of one full cycle), frequency is defined as

$$ f = \frac{1}{T}. $$

The SI unit of frequency is the hertz (Hz), where $1\ \text{Hz} = 1\ \text{s}^{-1}$. For mechanical and sound waves propagating at speed $v$ with wavelength $\lambda$, frequency is related to these quantities by the fundamental wave relation

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Frequency characterizes how rapidly oscillation repeats and is entirely independent of amplitude — doubling the amplitude of a tuning fork does not alter its pitch, which is determined solely by $f$.

Worked Example. A sound wave in air travels at $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$ and has a measured wavelength of $\lambda = 0.686\ \text{m}$. Find its frequency and period.

Applying the wave relation:

$$ f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{343\ \text{m/s}}{0.686\ \text{m}} = 500\ \text{Hz}. $$

The period follows directly:

$$ T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{500\ \text{Hz}} = 2.00 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{s} = 2.00\ \text{ms}. $$

This frequency lies comfortably within the human audible range of approximately $20\ \text{Hz}$ to $20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$, corresponding to a musical pitch near $B_4$.

Key Theorem (Fourier's Theorem). Any periodic waveform of frequency $f_0$ can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoidal components at integer multiples $f_0, 2f_0, 3f_0, \ldots$ called harmonics:

$$ y(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \sin(2\pi n f_0 t + \phi_n). $$

This result underpins the analysis of musical timbre, electrical signals, and acoustic resonance: two instruments playing the same fundamental frequency $f_0$ differ in sound because their harmonic amplitudes $A_n$ differ.

🌱 Density

🌱 Density

Definition. The density of a substance is defined as its mass per unit volume:

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} $$

where $\rho$ (rho) is the density in kilograms per cubic meter ($\text{kg/m}^3$), $m$ is the mass in kilograms, and $V$ is the volume in cubic meters. Density is an intensive property — it characterizes the material itself, independent of the sample size. For a non-uniform object, the local density at a point is defined as the limiting ratio $\rho = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\Delta m}{\Delta V}$, which reduces to the uniform definition when the material is homogeneous.

Common reference values: water at $4^\circ\text{C}$ has $\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3$, air at standard conditions has $\rho \approx 1.29\ \text{kg/m}^3$, and lead has $\rho \approx 11{,}300\ \text{kg/m}^3$.

Worked Example. A solid aluminum cylinder has a measured mass of $m = 0.540\ \text{kg}$ and dimensions: radius $r = 2.00\ \text{cm} = 0.0200\ \text{m}$, height $h = 15.0\ \text{cm} = 0.150\ \text{m}$. Find the density.

First, compute the volume:

$$ V = \pi r^2 h = \pi (0.0200)^2 (0.150) = \pi (4.00 \times 10^{-4})(0.150) \approx 1.885 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^3 $$

Then:

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{0.540}{1.885 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 2{,}870\ \text{kg/m}^3 $$

The accepted value for aluminum is $2{,}700\ \text{kg/m}^3$, placing this result within reasonable experimental agreement.

Key Theorem (Archimedes' Principle). When an object is submerged in a fluid of density $\rho_f$, the buoyant force equals the weight of displaced fluid:

$$ F_b = \rho_f V_{\text{submerged}}\, g $$

An object floats when $\rho_{\text{object}} \leq \rho_{\text{fluid}}$, and sinks when $\rho_{\text{object}} > \rho_{\text{fluid}}$. This follows directly from the definition of density combined with hydrostatic pressure analysis.

Calculate $\rho = m/V$ and compare with published values for granite ($\approx 2{,}700\ \text{kg/m}^3$). Estimate the percent uncertainty by propagating errors in $m$ and $V$.

🌱 Temperature

🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar thermodynamic state variable that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. For an ideal monatomic gas, the relationship between absolute temperature $T$ and mean translational kinetic energy $\langle KE \rangle$ per molecule is given by the equipartition theorem:

$$ \langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2} k_B T $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{J K}^{-1}$ is the Boltzmann constant. Temperature is measured on the absolute (Kelvin) scale, defined so that $T = 0\ \text{K}$ corresponds to the theoretical minimum energy state (absolute zero). Conversion from Celsius is given by $T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273.15$.

Worked Example. A sample of nitrogen gas ($\text{N}_2$) is held at room temperature, $T = 25\ °\text{C} = 298.15\ \text{K}$. Determine the root-mean-square (rms) speed of a nitrogen molecule, given its molar mass $M = 28.0\ \text{g mol}^{-1} = 0.0280\ \text{kg mol}^{-1}$.

The rms speed is derived from the equipartition result applied to translational kinetic energy:

$$ \frac{1}{2} m v_{\text{rms}}^2 = \frac{3}{2} k_B T \implies v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_B T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}} $$

where $R = N_A k_B = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant. Substituting:

$$ v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3(8.314)(298.15)}{0.0280}} = \sqrt{265{,}450}\ \approx\ 515\ \text{m s}^{-1} $$

This result illustrates that molecular speeds at ambient temperature are on the order of hundreds of meters per second.

Key Theorem (Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics). If system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $B$, and system $B$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then systems $A$ and $C$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This transitivity property establishes temperature as a well-defined, measurable quantity and underpins the logical consistency of thermometry.

🍃 Hearing

🍃 Hearing

Definition. Hearing is the physiological process by which the auditory system converts mechanical pressure waves in air into neural signals interpreted by the brain as sound. A sound wave is a longitudinal pressure disturbance characterized by its frequency $f$ (measured in hertz, Hz). The human ear responds to a range of intensities, quantified by the sound level

$$ \beta = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{I}{I_0}\right) \text{ dB}, $$

where $I$ is the wave intensity (power per unit area, $\text{W/m}^2$) and $I_0 = 10^{-12}\ \text{W/m}^2$ is the reference intensity corresponding to the threshold of human hearing. The audible frequency range for a healthy young adult spans approximately $20\ \text{Hz}$ to $20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$, while the threshold of pain occurs near $130\ \text{dB}$.

Worked Example. A concert speaker produces a sound intensity of $I = 1.0 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{W/m}^2$ at a listener 3.0 m away. Determine the sound level in decibels.

$$ \beta = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{1.0 \times 10^{-2}}{1.0 \times 10^{-12}}\right) = 10 \log_{10}(10^{10}) = 10 \times 10 = 100\ \text{dB}. $$

This level is well above the threshold of hearing ($0\ \text{dB}$) and approaches the range where prolonged exposure causes cochlear damage (typically above $85\ \text{dB}$).

Key Theorem — Inverse-Square Law for Sound Intensity. For a point source radiating uniformly into free space, intensity falls off as

$$ I \propto \frac{1}{r^2}, $$

so that $I_1 r_1^2 = I_2 r_2^2$. Consequently, doubling the distance from a source reduces intensity by a factor of four, decreasing the sound level by approximately $6\ \text{dB}$.

Record the measured levels and compare the observed decrease per doubling of distance to the theoretical $6\ \text{dB}$ prediction. 🍃 Speed Of Sound

🍃 Speed Of Sound

Definition. The speed of sound is the rate at which a mechanical pressure wave propagates through a medium. For an ideal fluid or gas, it is determined by the medium's bulk modulus $B$ and equilibrium density $\rho_0$:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{B}{\rho_0}} $$

For an ideal gas undergoing adiabatic compression—the physically correct model for audible sound—this reduces to

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_0}{\rho_0}} = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma R T}{M}} $$

where $\gamma = C_P/C_V$ is the adiabatic index, $R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant, $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvin, and $M$ is the molar mass of the gas in $\text{kg mol}^{-1}$. For dry air, $\gamma \approx 1.40$ and $M \approx 0.02896\ \text{kg mol}^{-1}$, yielding the familiar approximation $v \approx 331\sqrt{T/273}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$.

Worked Example. Find the speed of sound in dry air at $T = 20\ ^\circ\text{C} = 293\ \text{K}$.

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{(1.40)(8.314)(293)}{0.02896}} = \sqrt{\frac{3413.5}{0.02896}} \approx \sqrt{117{,}869} \approx 343\ \text{m s}^{-1} $$

This agrees with the commonly cited value and confirms that sound travels approximately $1\ \text{km}$ every $2.9\ \text{s}$ at room temperature.

Key Theorem. Because $v \propto \sqrt{T}$, the speed of sound depends on temperature but is independent of static pressure at constant temperature. This follows directly from the ideal-gas relation $P_0/\rho_0 = RT/M$: when pressure rises, density rises proportionally, so the ratio $P_0/\rho_0$ remains fixed at constant $T$. Consequently, sound travels faster in warmer air and slower in cooler air, a dependence that governs how sound beams curve as they pass through air masses of varying temperature.

🍃 Doppler Effect

🍃 Doppler Effect

Definition. The Doppler effect describes the perceived change in frequency (and wavelength) of a wave when there is relative motion between the source of the wave and the observer. For sound waves in a medium, if the source moves with speed $v_s$ and the observer moves with speed $v_o$ relative to the medium, and the speed of sound in the medium is $v$, the observed frequency $f_o$ is related to the source frequency $f_s$ by

$$ f_o = f_s \frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s} $$

The upper signs apply when the source and observer move toward each other (increasing observed frequency), and the lower signs apply when they move apart (decreasing observed frequency). When both source and observer are stationary, $f_o = f_s$ trivially recovers.

Worked Example. A train emitting a whistle at $f_s = 440\ \text{Hz}$ approaches a stationary observer at $v_s = 30\ \text{m/s}$. The speed of sound in air is $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$. The observer is at rest, so $v_o = 0$.

As the train approaches, we use the upper sign in the denominator:

$$ f_o = 440 \cdot \frac{343 + 0}{343 - 30} = 440 \cdot \frac{343}{313} \approx 440 \cdot 1.096 \approx 482\ \text{Hz} $$

After the train passes and recedes, the lower sign applies:

$$ f_o = 440 \cdot \frac{343}{343 + 30} = 440 \cdot \frac{343}{373} \approx 440 \cdot 0.919 \approx 404\ \text{Hz} $$

The observer hears a drop from approximately 482 Hz to 404 Hz as the train passes — a perceptually dramatic shift of roughly a minor third.

Key Theorem. For a stationary observer and a moving source, the Doppler formula is equivalent to a statement about wavelength. The wavefronts ahead of the source are compressed to $\lambda_{\text{ahead}} = \frac{v - v_s}{f_s}$ and stretched behind to $\lambda_{\text{behind}} = \frac{v + v_s}{f_s}$. Since $f_o = v / \lambda$, the observed frequency increases in front and decreases behind, consistent with the sign convention in the general formula. This wavelength interpretation makes clear that the effect is a geometric consequence of the source's motion through the medium, not a change in the wave's propagation speed.

🍃 Ultrasound

🍃 Ultrasound

Definition. Ultrasound refers to acoustic waves with frequencies above the upper threshold of human hearing, conventionally taken as $f > 20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$ (20 kHz). Unlike audible sound, ultrasonic waves occupy the frequency range from approximately $20\ \text{kHz}$ to several gigahertz ($10^9\ \text{Hz}$), depending on the application. As mechanical pressure waves, they obey the same wave equation as audible sound:

$$ v = f\lambda $$

where $v$ is the wave speed in the medium, $f$ is frequency, and $\lambda$ is wavelength. In soft biological tissue, $v \approx 1540\ \text{m/s}$; thus a $5\ \text{MHz}$ diagnostic ultrasound pulse has a wavelength of $\lambda = v/f = 1540/5{\times}10^6 \approx 0.31\ \text{mm}$, which sets the spatial resolution achievable in medical imaging.

Worked Example. A diagnostic ultrasound transducer operating at $f = 3.5\ \text{MHz}$ emits a pulse into abdominal tissue. An echo returns from an organ boundary after a round-trip time of $\Delta t = 1.4 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{s}$. Find the depth $d$ of the boundary.

The total path length traveled is $v \cdot \Delta t$, and since the pulse travels to the boundary and back, the depth is

$$ d = \frac{v \cdot \Delta t}{2} = \frac{1540 \times 1.4 \times 10^{-4}}{2} \approx 0.108\ \text{m} = 10.8\ \text{cm}. $$

This pulse-echo timing principle underlies all ultrasound imaging (sonography).

Key Theorem — Acoustic Impedance and Reflection. At the interface between two media, the fraction of ultrasound intensity reflected is governed by the acoustic impedance $Z = \rho v$, where $\rho$ is the medium density and $v$ is the wave speed already introduced above. The intensity reflection coefficient is

$$ R = \left(\frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}\right)^2. $$

When $Z_1 \approx Z_2$ (e.g., tissue–tissue interfaces), $R \approx 0$ and most energy is transmitted; when the mismatch is large (e.g., tissue–bone or tissue–air), $R \to 1$ and reflection dominates. This is why coupling gel is applied during sonography: it eliminates the air gap ($Z_{\text{air}} \operatorname{ll} Z_{\text{tissue}}$) between the transducer and skin, ensuring efficient energy transfer into the body.

🌸 Doppler Shifted Ultrasound

🌸 Doppler Shifted Ultrasound

Definition. The Doppler effect describes the change in observed frequency of a wave when the source and observer are in relative motion. For ultrasound — high-frequency sound waves typically in the range of 1–20 MHz — this principle underpins a critical medical imaging technique. When an ultrasound transducer emits a wave of frequency $f_s$ toward a moving target (such as flowing blood or a beating heart wall), the reflected wave returns at a shifted frequency $f_o$. The general Doppler formula for a moving observer and moving source in a medium where sound travels at speed $v$ is:

$$ f_o = f_s \left(\frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s}\right) $$

where $v_o$ is the observer's speed (positive when moving toward the source) and $v_s$ is the source's speed (positive when moving toward the observer). In medical ultrasonography, the transducer acts simultaneously as source and detector, so the relevant quantity is the Doppler shift $\Delta f = f_o - f_s$, which for a reflector moving at speed $v_r$ directly toward the transducer simplifies to:

$$ \Delta f = \frac{2 f_s v_r}{v} $$

The factor of 2 arises because the wave undergoes two Doppler shifts — once on the way to the reflector and once on the return path.

Worked Example. A Doppler ultrasound device operates at $f_s = 5.00\,\text{MHz}$. The speed of sound in tissue is approximately $v = 1540\,\text{m/s}$. Blood flows directly toward the transducer at $v_r = 0.250\,\text{m/s}$. The Doppler shift is:

$$ \Delta f = \frac{2 \times (5.00 \times 10^6\,\text{Hz}) \times (0.250\,\text{m/s})}{1540\,\text{m/s}} \approx 1623\,\text{Hz} \approx 1.62\,\text{kHz} $$

This shift falls within the audible range, which is why early Doppler instruments could be monitored by ear.

Key Theorem. For a reflector moving at angle $\theta$ relative to the beam axis, the effective component of velocity is $v_r \cos\theta$, giving:

$$ \Delta f = \frac{2 f_s v_r \cos\theta}{v} $$

At $\theta = 90°$, no frequency shift is detected, illustrating that only the component of motion along the beam axis contributes to the Doppler signal.

Payoff 🎯

Doppler shifted ultrasound represents the convergence of wave mechanics, fluid dynamics, and medical instrumentation into a single diagnostic technique of remarkable precision. Throughout this chapter, the Doppler effect has been developed from first principles—the apparent frequency shift arising whenever a source and observer are in relative motion—and ultrasound has been established as acoustic radiation above the threshold of human hearing, typically between 1 and 20 MHz in clinical settings. Doppler shifted ultrasound unites these threads: by directing a high-frequency acoustic beam at moving structures within the body and analyzing the returned frequency, clinicians extract quantitative velocity information from tissue and blood that no static imaging modality can provide.

The governing relation is the Doppler equation adapted for a medium in which both source and reflector may move. For a transducer stationary relative to the patient and a reflector moving at speed $v$ along the beam axis, the observed frequency $f_o$ satisfies

$$ f_o = f_s \frac{c + v}{c - v}, $$

where $f_s$ is the transmitted frequency and $c$ is the speed of sound in tissue (approximately $1540\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$). The frequency shift is therefore $\Delta f = f_o - f_s$, and solving for the reflector speed gives

$$ v = \frac{c\,\Delta f}{2f_s \cos\theta}, $$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the beam and the direction of motion. A clinical example: a $5\ \mathrm{MHz}$ probe detects a shift of $\Delta f = 1\ \mathrm{kHz}$ from a blood cell column oriented at $\theta = 60°$. Substituting yields $v = (1540 \times 1000)/(2 \times 5\times10^6 \times 0.5) \approx 0.31\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, consistent with normal aortic flow—a result obtained non-invasively and in real time.

The technique underpins several consequential application domains. In cardiovascular medicine, color-flow Doppler maps ventricular output and valve competency without catheterization. In obstetrics, fetal umbilical artery waveforms reveal placental resistance and gestational risk. In neurology, transcranial Doppler monitors cerebral autoregulation during surgery. Each domain inherits the same physics, differing only in transducer geometry, frequency selection, and signal-processing depth.

The foundational laboratory exercise asks you to measure the Doppler shift produced by a moving reflector on a bench-top ultrasound system, vary $\theta$ systematically, and compare extracted velocities against a tachometer standard—quantifying both the power and the angular sensitivity of the technique.
