🍃 Intensity Reflection Coefficient

🍃 Intensity Reflection Coefficient

Definition. When a sound wave traveling through one medium encounters a planar boundary with a second medium, part of the wave energy is reflected and part is transmitted. The intensity reflection coefficient, denoted $R$, quantifies the fraction of incident intensity that is reflected:

$$ R = \frac{I_r}{I_i} $$

where $I_i$ is the incident intensity and $I_r$ is the reflected intensity. Because energy must be conserved at the boundary, the complementary fraction is transmitted, so $T = 1 - R$.

The value of $R$ is governed by the acoustic impedance $Z = \rho v$ of each medium, where $\rho$ is the equilibrium mass density and $v$ is the speed of sound. At normal incidence:

$$ R = \left(\frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}\right)^2 $$

This expression follows from matching acoustic pressure and particle velocity at the boundary — two continuity conditions analogous to those imposed on electromagnetic fields at a dielectric interface. Note that $R$ depends only on the ratio of impedances, so the formula is symmetric: a wave traveling from medium 1 into medium 2 reflects with the same coefficient as one traveling in the reverse direction.

Worked Example. A sound wave in air ($\rho_1 = 1.21\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}$, $v_1 = 343\ \mathrm{m/s}$) strikes a water surface ($\rho_2 = 1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}$, $v_2 = 1480\ \mathrm{m/s}$) at normal incidence. Compute $R$.

$$ Z_1 = (1.21)(343) \approx 415\ \mathrm{Pa\cdot s/m}, \qquad Z_2 = (1000)(1480) = 1.48\times10^6\ \mathrm{Pa\cdot s/m} $$ $$ R = \left(\frac{1.48\times10^6 - 415}{1.48\times10^6 + 415}\right)^2 \approx \left(\frac{1.4796\times10^6}{1.4804\times10^6}\right)^2 \approx 0.999 $$

Nearly all incident intensity is reflected — consistent with the everyday observation that sound barely penetrates a water surface from air.

Key Theorem. $R = 0$ if and only if $Z_1 = Z_2$ (impedance matching). When the media are impedance-matched, the boundary is acoustically invisible: no reflected wave exists and all energy is transmitted. This principle underlies the design of ultrasound coupling gels and acoustic transducer coatings, where a thin intermediate layer is engineered to match the impedances of two otherwise mismatched materials.

Lab Exercise. Using a signal generator, speaker, and calibrated microphone, measure the sound intensity reflected from flat panels of cork, wood, and aluminum. For each material, look up or estimate $\rho$ and $v$ to calculate the predicted $R$, then compare with your measured $I_r/I_i$. Discuss sources of discrepancy, particularly the role of material thickness and surface roughness relative to the acoustic wavelength.
