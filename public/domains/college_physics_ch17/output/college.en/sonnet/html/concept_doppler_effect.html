🍃 Doppler Effect

🍃 Doppler Effect

Definition. The Doppler effect describes the perceived change in frequency (and wavelength) of a wave when there is relative motion between the source of the wave and the observer. For sound waves in a medium, if the source moves with speed $v_s$ and the observer moves with speed $v_o$ relative to the medium, and the speed of sound in the medium is $v$, the observed frequency $f_o$ is related to the source frequency $f_s$ by

$$ f_o = f_s \frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s} $$

The upper signs apply when the source and observer move toward each other (increasing observed frequency), and the lower signs apply when they move apart (decreasing observed frequency). When both source and observer are stationary, $f_o = f_s$ trivially recovers.

Worked Example. A train emitting a whistle at $f_s = 440\ \text{Hz}$ approaches a stationary observer at $v_s = 30\ \text{m/s}$. The speed of sound in air is $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$. The observer is at rest, so $v_o = 0$.

As the train approaches, we use the upper sign in the denominator:

$$ f_o = 440 \cdot \frac{343 + 0}{343 - 30} = 440 \cdot \frac{343}{313} \approx 440 \cdot 1.096 \approx 482\ \text{Hz} $$

After the train passes and recedes, the lower sign applies:

$$ f_o = 440 \cdot \frac{343}{343 + 30} = 440 \cdot \frac{343}{373} \approx 440 \cdot 0.919 \approx 404\ \text{Hz} $$

The observer hears a drop from approximately 482 Hz to 404 Hz as the train passes — a perceptually dramatic shift of roughly a minor third.

Key Theorem. For a stationary observer and a moving source, the Doppler formula is equivalent to a statement about wavelength. The wavefronts ahead of the source are compressed to $\lambda_{\text{ahead}} = \frac{v - v_s}{f_s}$ and stretched behind to $\lambda_{\text{behind}} = \frac{v + v_s}{f_s}$. Since $f_o = v / \lambda$, the observed frequency increases in front and decreases behind, consistent with the sign convention in the general formula. This wavelength interpretation makes clear that the effect is a geometric consequence of the source's motion through the medium, not a change in the wave's propagation speed.

Lab Exercise. Using a smartphone frequency-analysis app, record the pitch of a buzzing device attached to a string swung in a horizontal circle. Plot the time-varying observed frequency and, using the Doppler formula with $v_o = 0$, extract the tangential speed of the source. Compare against the speed computed from the string's length and the angular period measured by video analysis.
