🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the magnitude of the normal force $F_\perp$ exerted on a surface divided by the area $A$ over which that force is distributed:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; at any point within a static fluid, it acts equally in all directions (Pascal's principle). In a fluid column of uniform density $\rho$ under gravitational acceleration $g$, the gauge pressure at depth $h$ below the free surface is:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface. This linear dependence on depth arises because each additional fluid layer contributes its weight per unit area to the layers beneath it.

Worked Example. A rectangular tank is filled with water ($\rho = 1000\,\text{kg/m}^3$) to a depth of $h = 3.0\,\text{m}$. The atmospheric pressure at the surface is $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Find the absolute pressure at the bottom of the tank.

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1000)(9.81)(3.0) $$ $$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 2.943 \times 10^4 = 1.307 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa} $$

The gauge pressure (excess above atmospheric) is simply $\rho g h = 2.94 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa} \approx 0.29\,\text{atm}$.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. Any change in pressure applied to an enclosed incompressible fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if the applied surface pressure changes by $\Delta P$, then at every interior point the pressure also changes by $\Delta P$. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small piston area $A_1$ produces an equal pressure increase throughout the fluid, generating a large force $F_2 = P \cdot A_2$ over a larger piston area $A_2$.

Laboratory Exercise. Using a U-tube manometer partially filled with water, apply a known gauge pressure to one arm by connecting it to a sealed reservoir at a measured height. Record the height difference $\Delta h$ between the two arms and verify that $\Delta P = \rho g \Delta h$. Repeat with a denser fluid (e.g., glycerin, $\rho \approx 1260\,\text{kg/m}^3$) and compare the height differences for the same applied pressure, confirming the inverse dependence of $\Delta h$ on $\rho$.

🌱 Simple Harmonic Motion

🌱 Simple Harmonic Motion

Definition. Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a special class of periodic motion in which the restoring force on an object is directly proportional to its displacement from equilibrium and directed opposite to that displacement. Mathematically, this condition is expressed as

$$ F = -kx, $$

where $k > 0$ is the spring constant (or, more generally, the stiffness coefficient) and $x$ is the displacement from the equilibrium position. Applying Newton's second law, $F = ma$, yields the equation of motion

$$ m\ddot{x} + kx = 0, $$

whose general solution is

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), $$

with angular frequency $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$, amplitude $A$, and phase constant $\phi$ determined by initial conditions. The period and frequency follow immediately:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T}. $$

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 0.50\ \text{kg}$ is attached to a horizontal spring with $k = 200\ \text{N/m}$. At $t = 0$ the block is displaced $x_0 = 0.10\ \text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest.

Find the angular frequency, period, and position at $t = 0.30\ \text{s}$.

Step 1: $\omega = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{200/0.50} = 20\ \text{rad/s}$.

Step 2: $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/20 \approx 0.314\ \text{s}$.

Step 3: Since $v(0) = 0$ and $x(0) = x_0$, we identify $A = 0.10\ \text{m}$ and $\phi = 0$. Therefore

$$ x(0.30) = 0.10\cos(20 \times 0.30) = 0.10\cos(6.0) \approx 0.10 \times 0.960 = 0.096\ \text{m}. $$

Key Theorem. Because the spring force is conservative, the mechanical energy conservation principle established in classical mechanics applies directly to SHM:

$$ E = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2 = \text{const.} $$

This result, rather than introducing new physics, is the familiar kinetic-plus-potential energy balance evaluated for a linear restoring force. It immediately yields the maximum speed $v_{\max} = A\omega$ at $x = 0$, and confirms that the object momentarily stops ($v = 0$) at the turning points $x = \pm A$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a mass on a vertical spring and measure the period for three different masses. Plot $T^2$ versus $m$ and extract $k$ from the slope. Compare with the value obtained from a static force–extension measurement. Identify two potential sources of systematic error in each method.

🌱 Wave

🌱 Wave

Definition. A wave is a disturbance that propagates through a medium (or through free space, in the case of electromagnetic waves), transferring energy and momentum without the net transport of matter. Mathematically, any function of the form

$$ y(x, t) = A\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

describes a sinusoidal traveling wave moving in the positive $x$-direction, where $A$ is the amplitude, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, and $\phi$ is the initial phase. The wave speed is related to these quantities by

$$ v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \lambda f. $$

Waves are classified as transverse (displacement perpendicular to propagation, e.g., light on a string) or longitudinal (displacement parallel to propagation, e.g., sound in air).

Worked Example. A sinusoidal wave on a stretched string is described by $y(x,t) = 0.020\sin(6.28x - 31.4t)$ meters, where $x$ is in meters and $t$ in seconds. Identify the amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave speed.

  • Amplitude: $A = 0.020\ \text{m}$
  • Wave number: $k = 6.28\ \text{rad/m}$, so $\lambda = 2\pi/k = 1.00\ \text{m}$
  • Angular frequency: $\omega = 31.4\ \text{rad/s}$, so $f = \omega/2\pi = 5.00\ \text{Hz}$
  • Wave speed: $v = \omega/k = 31.4/6.28 = 5.00\ \text{m/s}$

Key Theorem — Wave Equation. Any wave satisfying the linear wave equation

$$ \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} $$

obeys the superposition principle: if $y_1$ and $y_2$ are both solutions, then $y_1 + y_2$ is also a solution. This underpins phenomena such as interference and standing waves. For a transverse wave on a string under tension $T$ with linear mass density $\mu$, the wave speed is $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$, confirming that $v$ depends only on the medium's physical properties.

Using a function generator and a stretched string of known linear mass density $\mu = 5.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{kg/m}$, set the tension to $T = 12\ \text{N}$ and drive the string at $f = 15\ \text{Hz}$. Predict the wavelength using $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$ and $\lambda = v/f$, then measure the node spacing in the resulting standing wave pattern to verify your prediction experimentally. Quantify any discrepancy and identify its likely sources.

🍃 Sound

🍃 Sound

Definition. Sound is a mechanical, longitudinal wave that propagates through a compressible medium by alternating compressions and rarefactions of the medium's constituent particles. Unlike electromagnetic waves, sound requires a material medium and cannot travel through a vacuum. A sound wave is fully characterized by a single primitive quantity—frequency $f$ (Hz), the number of complete oscillation cycles per second—together with derived quantities: wavelength $\lambda$ (m), amplitude $A$ (Pa for pressure waves), and propagation speed $v$ (m/s). These are related by the fundamental wave equation:

$$ v = f\lambda $$

The speed of sound in an ideal gas is given by:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_0}{\rho}} $$

where $\gamma$ is the adiabatic index (approximately 1.40 for diatomic gases such as air), $P_0$ is the equilibrium pressure, and $\rho$ is the mass density. For dry air at $T = 20\,^\circ\text{C}$, this yields $v \approx 343\,\text{m/s}$.

Worked Example. A tuning fork vibrating at $f = 440\,\text{Hz}$ (concert A) is struck in air at $20\,^\circ\text{C}$. Determine the wavelength of the sound wave produced.

Using $v = f\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343\,\text{m/s}}{440\,\text{Hz}} \approx 0.780\,\text{m} $$

This wavelength falls within the range of typical room dimensions, which is why standing-wave resonances are audible in enclosed spaces.

Key Theorem: Newton–Laplace Correction. Newton originally computed the speed of sound assuming an isothermal process, obtaining $v = \sqrt{P_0/\rho}$, which underestimates the measured value by roughly 15%. Laplace corrected this by recognizing that compressions and rarefactions in a sound wave occur too rapidly for heat exchange with the surroundings, making the process adiabatic rather than isothermal. Replacing $P_0$ with $\gamma P_0$ yields the accurate expression above. This correction reflects a fundamental principle: the thermodynamic process governing wave propagation determines the appropriate elastic modulus.

Using a resonance tube apparatus open at one end and closed at the other, strike a tuning fork of known frequency $f$ and slide the tube until the first resonance is detected (tube length $L = \lambda/4$). Measure $L$ with a ruler and compute $v = 4Lf$. Compare your result to the theoretical value $v = 331\sqrt{1 + T/273}\,\text{m/s}$ (with $T$ in degrees Celsius) and calculate the percent error. Repeat at three different temperatures to verify the temperature dependence.

🌱 Amplitude

🌱 Amplitude

Definition. The amplitude of a periodic oscillation is the maximum magnitude of displacement from the equilibrium position. For a particle undergoing simple harmonic motion (SHM), the displacement as a function of time is

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi_0), $$

where $A > 0$ is the amplitude, $\omega$ is the angular frequency, and $\phi_0$ is the initial phase. The amplitude is not the range of the oscillation but rather the half-range: the motion spans the interval $[-A, A]$, giving a total excursion of $2A$. Dimensionally, amplitude carries the same units as the displacement it measures — meters for translational oscillations, radians for angular ones.

The mechanical energy of an ideal SHM system (spring constant $k$, mass $m$) is entirely determined by $A$:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2. $$

This relation makes amplitude the single most important parameter characterizing the intensity of an oscillation.

Worked Example. A $0.50\,\text{kg}$ block attached to a spring with $k = 200\,\text{N/m}$ is displaced $8.0\,\text{cm}$ from equilibrium and released from rest. Find the amplitude and the total mechanical energy.

Because the block is released from rest at maximum displacement, the release point coincides with the turning point, so

$$ A = 8.0\,\text{cm} = 0.080\,\text{m}. $$

The total energy is

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.080)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.0064) = 0.64\,\text{J}. $$

At equilibrium ($x = 0$) this energy is entirely kinetic. The maximum speed follows from energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}mv_{\max}^2 = E$, giving $v_{\max} = \sqrt{2E/m} = \sqrt{2(0.64)/0.50} = 1.6\,\text{m/s}$.

Key Theorem. For undamped SHM, the amplitude is a conserved quantity: it remains constant for all time because no energy leaves the system. Formally, since $E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \text{const}$, any combination of potential and kinetic energy at any phase yields the same $A$. This contrasts sharply with damped oscillations, where $A(t) = A_0 e^{-\gamma t}$ decays exponentially.

Lab Exercise. Mount a spring vertically, hang a known mass, and displace it by measured amounts $A = 2, 4, 6, 8\,\text{cm}$. Record the period $T$ and the maximum speed using a motion sensor. Verify that $T$ is independent of $A$ and that $v_{\max}$ scales linearly with $A$, confirming $E \propto A^2$.

🍃 Interference

🍃 Interference

Definition. Interference is the phenomenon arising when two or more coherent waves superpose in the same region of space, producing a resultant amplitude determined by the principle of superposition. For two sinusoidal waves of equal amplitude $A$ and angular frequency $\omega$, differing only by a phase difference $\delta$, the superposition yields:

$$ y = 2A\cos\!\left(\frac{\delta}{2}\right)\sin\!\left(\omega t - kx + \frac{\delta}{2}\right) $$

The resultant amplitude is $2A\cos(\delta/2)$. When $\delta = 2m\pi$ for integer $m$, the amplitude reaches its maximum $2A$ — constructive interference. When $\delta = (2m+1)\pi$, the amplitude vanishes — destructive interference. The path length difference $\Delta L$ relates to phase by $\delta = 2\pi\Delta L/\lambda$, so constructive interference requires $\Delta L = m\lambda$ and destructive interference requires $\Delta L = (m + \tfrac{1}{2})\lambda$.

Worked Example. Two coherent speakers separated by $d = 4.00\ \text{m}$ emit sound at $f = 686\ \text{Hz}$ in air ($v = 343\ \text{m/s}$, so $\lambda = 0.500\ \text{m}$). A listener stands on the perpendicular bisector at distance $L = 3.00\ \text{m}$ from the midpoint, then moves laterally to position $y$ along a line parallel to the speaker axis. In the small-angle approximation ($y \operatorname{ll} L$), the path difference is:

$$ \Delta L \approx \frac{yd}{L} $$

For the first destructive minimum, set $\Delta L = \lambda/2 = 0.250\ \text{m}$:

$$ y = \frac{\Delta L \cdot L}{d} = \frac{0.250 \times 3.00}{4.00} = 0.188\ \text{m} $$

The listener first hears silence at approximately $18.8\ \text{cm}$ off-center.

Key Theorem (Young's Double-Slit). For a double-slit geometry with slit separation $d$ and screen distance $L \operatorname{gg} d$, bright fringes (constructive maxima) occur at lateral positions:

$$ y_m = \frac{m\lambda L}{d}, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

Dark fringes satisfy $y_m = \left(m + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\lambda L/d$. The fringe spacing $\Delta y = \lambda L/d$ provides a direct experimental measure of wavelength. This result follows directly from applying the constructive-interference condition $\Delta L = m\lambda$ to the small-angle approximation introduced above.

Lab Exercise. Set up a laser ($\lambda \approx 633\ \text{nm}$) incident on a double slit with $d = 0.250\ \text{mm}$ and project the pattern on a screen $1.20\ \text{m}$ away. Measure five consecutive bright-fringe positions, compute the mean fringe spacing $\Delta y$, and solve for $\lambda$. Compare your result with the labeled wavelength and compute the percent error. Identify one systematic error source and propose a correction.

🌱 Wavelength

🌱 Wavelength

Definition. The wavelength of a periodic wave is the spatial distance over which the wave's shape repeats exactly once. For a sinusoidal wave propagating in one dimension, the displacement field takes the form

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{\lambda} x - \omega t + \phi_0\right), $$

where $A$ is the amplitude, $\omega$ is the angular frequency, $\phi_0$ is the initial phase, and $\lambda$ (the Greek letter lambda) denotes the wavelength. Equivalently, $\lambda$ is the distance between any two successive points that are in phase — for instance, crest to crest or trough to trough. The SI unit of wavelength is the metre (m).

The wavelength is related to wave speed $v$ and frequency $f$ by the fundamental wave relation:

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Since $f = 1/T$ where $T$ is the period, this can also be written $\lambda = vT$.

Worked Example. A sound wave in air at $20\,^\circ\text{C}$ has a frequency of $440\,\text{Hz}$ (concert A). The speed of sound in air at this temperature is approximately $v = 343\,\text{m/s}$. Find the wavelength.

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343\,\text{m/s}}{440\,\text{Hz}} = \frac{343}{440}\,\text{m} \approx 0.780\,\text{m}. $$

This result places the wavelength on the order of a typical human arm-span, which helps explain why sound diffracts readily around ordinary objects.

Key Theorem (Non-Dispersive Wave Relation). In a non-dispersive medium, the wave speed $v$ is independent of frequency. Consequently, $\lambda \propto 1/f$: doubling the frequency halves the wavelength, and halving the frequency doubles it. This inverse proportionality is a direct consequence of $v = f\lambda$ with $v$ held constant. It means that high-pitched sounds (large $f$) have short wavelengths, while low-pitched sounds (small $f$) have long wavelengths — a fact with direct implications for how sound interacts with objects and apertures of different physical sizes.

Lab Exercise. Using a ripple tank or simulation, generate surface waves at two frequencies $f_1 = 5\,\text{Hz}$ and $f_2 = 10\,\text{Hz}$. Measure the corresponding wavelengths $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ directly from the wave pattern. Compute the ratio $\lambda_1/\lambda_2$ and compare it to the theoretical prediction from $v = f\lambda$, assuming the wave speed remains constant. Discuss any deviation and propose a physical explanation.

🌱 Frequency

🌱 Frequency

Definition. The frequency of a periodic phenomenon is the number of complete cycles occurring per unit time. For a wave or oscillator with period $T$ (the duration of one full cycle), frequency is defined as

$$ f = \frac{1}{T}. $$

The SI unit of frequency is the hertz (Hz), where $1\ \text{Hz} = 1\ \text{s}^{-1}$. For mechanical and sound waves propagating at speed $v$ with wavelength $\lambda$, frequency is related to these quantities by the fundamental wave relation

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Frequency characterizes how rapidly oscillation repeats and is entirely independent of amplitude — doubling the amplitude of a tuning fork does not alter its pitch, which is determined solely by $f$.

Worked Example. A sound wave in air travels at $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$ and has a measured wavelength of $\lambda = 0.686\ \text{m}$. Find its frequency and period.

Applying the wave relation:

$$ f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{343\ \text{m/s}}{0.686\ \text{m}} = 500\ \text{Hz}. $$

The period follows directly:

$$ T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{500\ \text{Hz}} = 2.00 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{s} = 2.00\ \text{ms}. $$

This frequency lies comfortably within the human audible range of approximately $20\ \text{Hz}$ to $20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$, corresponding to a musical pitch near $B_4$.

Key Theorem (Fourier's Theorem). Any periodic waveform of frequency $f_0$ can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoidal components at integer multiples $f_0, 2f_0, 3f_0, \ldots$ called harmonics:

$$ y(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \sin(2\pi n f_0 t + \phi_n). $$

This result underpins the analysis of musical timbre, electrical signals, and acoustic resonance: two instruments playing the same fundamental frequency $f_0$ differ in sound because their harmonic amplitudes $A_n$ differ.

🍃 Standing Wave

🍃 Standing Wave

Definition. A standing wave is a vibrational pattern produced by the superposition of two traveling waves of equal amplitude and frequency propagating in opposite directions along the same medium. Unlike a traveling wave, a standing wave does not transport energy along the medium; instead, energy oscillates locally between kinetic and potential forms. The displacement of a standing wave on a string is given by

$$ y(x, t) = 2A\sin(kx)\cos(\omega t), $$

where $A$ is the amplitude of each component wave, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, and $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency. Points where $\sin(kx) = 0$ are called nodes — positions of permanent zero displacement. Points where $|\sin(kx)| = 1$ are called antinodes — positions of maximum displacement amplitude $2A$. Adjacent nodes are separated by $\lambda/2$, as are adjacent antinodes.

Worked Example. A string of length $L = 0.80\text{ m}$ is fixed at both ends and supports a standing wave with three antinodes. Determine the wavelength and, given a wave speed $v = 240\text{ m/s}$, find the frequency.

Three antinodes correspond to the third harmonic ($n = 3$). For a string fixed at both ends, the condition for standing waves is

$$ L = n\frac{\lambda}{2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

Solving for $\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{2L}{n} = \frac{2(0.80)}{3} \approx 0.533\text{ m}. $$

The frequency follows from $v = f\lambda$:

$$ f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{240}{0.533} \approx 450\text{ Hz}. $$

Key Theorem (Harmonic Series). For a string of length $L$ fixed at both ends with wave speed $v$, the allowed frequencies form a harmonic series — a discrete set of normal modes in which each frequency is an integer multiple of the lowest allowed frequency:

$$ f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

The lowest mode, $f_1 = v/(2L)$, is the fundamental frequency; every higher harmonic satisfies $f_n = n f_1$. The discretization of allowed frequencies is the physical basis for musical pitch in stringed instruments: only wavelengths that fit an integer number of half-wavelengths within the string length can sustain a standing wave.

Lab Exercise. Attach one end of a 1.2 m string to a mechanical oscillator and the other end over a pulley to a hanging mass. Repeat for $n = 3$ and confirm that the frequency ratio $f_3/f_2 = 3/2$.

🌱 Density

🌱 Density

Definition. The density of a substance is defined as its mass per unit volume:

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} $$

where $\rho$ (rho) is the density in kilograms per cubic meter ($\text{kg/m}^3$), $m$ is the mass in kilograms, and $V$ is the volume in cubic meters. Density is an intensive property — it characterizes the material itself, independent of the sample size. For a non-uniform object, the local density at a point is defined as the limiting ratio $\rho = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\Delta m}{\Delta V}$, which reduces to the uniform definition when the material is homogeneous.

Common reference values: water at $4^\circ\text{C}$ has $\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3$, air at standard conditions has $\rho \approx 1.29\ \text{kg/m}^3$, and lead has $\rho \approx 11{,}300\ \text{kg/m}^3$.

Worked Example. A solid aluminum cylinder has a measured mass of $m = 0.540\ \text{kg}$ and dimensions: radius $r = 2.00\ \text{cm} = 0.0200\ \text{m}$, height $h = 15.0\ \text{cm} = 0.150\ \text{m}$. Find the density.

First, compute the volume:

$$ V = \pi r^2 h = \pi (0.0200)^2 (0.150) = \pi (4.00 \times 10^{-4})(0.150) \approx 1.885 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^3 $$

Then:

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{0.540}{1.885 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 2{,}870\ \text{kg/m}^3 $$

The accepted value for aluminum is $2{,}700\ \text{kg/m}^3$, placing this result within reasonable experimental agreement.

Key Theorem (Archimedes' Principle). When an object is submerged in a fluid of density $\rho_f$, the buoyant force equals the weight of displaced fluid:

$$ F_b = \rho_f V_{\text{submerged}}\, g $$

An object floats when $\rho_{\text{object}} \leq \rho_{\text{fluid}}$, and sinks when $\rho_{\text{object}} > \rho_{\text{fluid}}$. This follows directly from the definition of density combined with hydrostatic pressure analysis.

Lab Exercise. Using a digital balance and a graduated cylinder partially filled with water, determine the density of an irregular stone by the water-displacement method. Record the initial water volume $V_1$, submerge the stone, and record $V_2$. Compute $V = V_2 - V_1$ and measure the stone's mass $m$ directly. Calculate $\rho = m/V$ and compare with published values for granite ($\approx 2{,}700\ \text{kg/m}^3$). Estimate the percent uncertainty by propagating errors in $m$ and $V$.

🍃 Fundamental Frequency

🍃 Fundamental Frequency

Definition. The fundamental frequency (also called the first harmonic) is the lowest resonant frequency at which a system vibrates when disturbed. For a standing wave on a string of length $L$ fixed at both ends, the fundamental arises when exactly one half-wavelength fits within the string:

$$ \lambda_1 = 2L $$

Combined with the wave speed relation $v = f\lambda$, the fundamental frequency is

$$ f_1 = \frac{v}{2L} $$

where $v$ is the transverse wave speed. Every higher resonant frequency (overtone) is an integer multiple of this value: $f_n = n f_1$, $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$

Worked Example. A guitar string has length $L = 0.650\,\text{m}$ and is held under tension $T = 120\,\text{N}$. Its linear mass density (mass per unit length) is $\mu = 5.38\times10^{-3}\,\text{kg/m}$.

  1. Find the wave speed using $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$: $v = \sqrt{120/(5.38\times10^{-3})} = \sqrt{2.23\times10^{4}} \approx 149\,\text{m/s}$.
  2. Apply the fundamental formula: $f_1 = v/(2L) = 149/(2\times0.650) \approx 115\,\text{Hz}$.

This falls within the range of the B$_2$ note, consistent with bass guitar registers.

Key Theorem (Harmonic Series). For any one-dimensional resonator with boundary conditions that support standing waves, the allowed resonant frequencies form a discrete harmonic series $\{nf_1 : n \in \mathbb{Z}^+\}$. The fundamental $f_1$ is uniquely determined by the geometry (length $L$) and the medium (wave speed $v$). Altering $T$ or $\mu$ shifts the entire series proportionally, since $f_1 \propto \sqrt{T/\mu}$.

🌱 Temperature

🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar thermodynamic state variable that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. For an ideal monatomic gas, the relationship between absolute temperature $T$ and mean translational kinetic energy $\langle KE \rangle$ per molecule is given by the equipartition theorem:

$$ \langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2} k_B T $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{J K}^{-1}$ is the Boltzmann constant. Temperature is measured on the absolute (Kelvin) scale, defined so that $T = 0\ \text{K}$ corresponds to the theoretical minimum energy state (absolute zero). Conversion from Celsius is given by $T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273.15$.

Worked Example. A sample of nitrogen gas ($\text{N}_2$) is held at room temperature, $T = 25\ °\text{C} = 298.15\ \text{K}$. Determine the root-mean-square (rms) speed of a nitrogen molecule, given its molar mass $M = 28.0\ \text{g mol}^{-1} = 0.0280\ \text{kg mol}^{-1}$.

The rms speed is derived from the equipartition result applied to translational kinetic energy:

$$ \frac{1}{2} m v_{\text{rms}}^2 = \frac{3}{2} k_B T \implies v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_B T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}} $$

where $R = N_A k_B = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant. Substituting:

$$ v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3(8.314)(298.15)}{0.0280}} = \sqrt{265{,}450}\ \approx\ 515\ \text{m s}^{-1} $$

This result illustrates that molecular speeds at ambient temperature are on the order of hundreds of meters per second.

Key Theorem (Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics). If system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $B$, and system $B$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then systems $A$ and $C$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This transitivity property establishes temperature as a well-defined, measurable quantity and underpins the logical consistency of thermometry.

🍃 Overtones And Harmonics

🍃 Overtones And Harmonics

Definition. When a mechanical system—such as a vibrating string or an air column—supports standing waves, it does so at a discrete set of frequencies. The lowest such frequency is the fundamental (first harmonic), $f_1$. The harmonics are all integer multiples of the fundamental:

$$ f_n = n f_1, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

The overtones are the harmonics above the fundamental, indexed by their order above it: the first overtone is $f_2$, the second overtone is $f_3$, and so on. For an ideal string fixed at both ends or an open pipe, every harmonic is present. For a closed pipe (open at one end, closed at the other), only odd harmonics are supported: $f_n = n f_1$ with $n = 1, 3, 5, \ldots$

Worked Example. A guitar string of length $L = 0.65\ \text{m}$ is fixed at both ends. The wave speed on the string is $v = 520\ \text{m/s}$. The condition for standing waves requires $L = n\lambda/2$, giving $\lambda_n = 2L/n$. The harmonic frequencies are therefore:

$$ f_n = \frac{v}{\lambda_n} = \frac{nv}{2L} $$

For $n = 1$: $\displaystyle f_1 = \frac{(1)(520)}{2(0.65)} = 400\ \text{Hz}$ (fundamental).

For $n = 2$: $f_2 = 800\ \text{Hz}$ (first overtone, second harmonic).

For $n = 3$: $f_3 = 1200\ \text{Hz}$ (second overtone, third harmonic).

The relative amplitudes of each harmonic in an actual plucked string depend on where and how the string is excited, giving each instrument its characteristic timbre.

Key Theorem (Superposition of Normal Modes). Any periodic vibration of a linear system can be expressed as a sum of its normal modes (harmonics) with appropriate amplitudes $A_n$ and phases $\phi_n$:

$$ y(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\cos(2\pi f_n t + \phi_n) $$

This result is a consequence of Fourier's theorem applied to linear wave equations. It establishes that timbre—the perceptual quality introduced above—is determined by the set of harmonic amplitudes $\{A_n\}$, and it underlies both spectral analysis and the physics of musical acoustics.

🍃 Speed Of Sound

🍃 Speed Of Sound

Definition. The speed of sound is the rate at which a mechanical pressure wave propagates through a medium. For an ideal fluid or gas, it is determined by the medium's bulk modulus $B$ and equilibrium density $\rho_0$:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{B}{\rho_0}} $$

For an ideal gas undergoing adiabatic compression—the physically correct model for audible sound—this reduces to

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_0}{\rho_0}} = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma R T}{M}} $$

where $\gamma = C_P/C_V$ is the adiabatic index, $R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant, $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvin, and $M$ is the molar mass of the gas in $\text{kg mol}^{-1}$. For dry air, $\gamma \approx 1.40$ and $M \approx 0.02896\ \text{kg mol}^{-1}$, yielding the familiar approximation $v \approx 331\sqrt{T/273}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$.

Worked Example. Find the speed of sound in dry air at $T = 20\ ^\circ\text{C} = 293\ \text{K}$.

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{(1.40)(8.314)(293)}{0.02896}} = \sqrt{\frac{3413.5}{0.02896}} \approx \sqrt{117{,}869} \approx 343\ \text{m s}^{-1} $$

This agrees with the commonly cited value and confirms that sound travels approximately $1\ \text{km}$ every $2.9\ \text{s}$ at room temperature.

Key Theorem. Because $v \propto \sqrt{T}$, the speed of sound depends on temperature but is independent of static pressure at constant temperature. This follows directly from the ideal-gas relation $P_0/\rho_0 = RT/M$: when pressure rises, density rises proportionally, so the ratio $P_0/\rho_0$ remains fixed at constant $T$. Consequently, sound travels faster in warmer air and slower in cooler air, a dependence that governs how sound beams curve as they pass through air masses of varying temperature.

🍃 Resonance Closed Tube

🍃 Resonance Closed Tube

Definition. A closed tube (closed at one end, open at the other) supports standing sound waves when its length satisfies specific resonance conditions. Because the closed end imposes a displacement node (zero air motion) and the open end imposes a displacement antinode (maximum air motion), only an odd number of quarter-wavelengths fits within the tube length $L$. The resonance condition is therefore

$$ L = \frac{(2n-1)\lambda}{4}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength and $n$ labels the harmonic. Rearranging, the resonant frequencies are

$$ f_n = \frac{(2n-1)v}{4L}, $$

where $v$ is the speed of sound in air. Only odd harmonics are present: $f_1, 3f_1, 5f_1, \ldots$ The lowest resonant frequency $f_1 = v/(4L)$ is called the fundamental.

Worked Example. A clarinet approximates a closed tube of effective length $L = 0.600\ \text{m}$. Taking the speed of sound at $20^\circ\text{C}$ to be $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$, find the fundamental frequency and the next two resonant frequencies.

$$ f_1 = \frac{v}{4L} = \frac{343}{4(0.600)} = \frac{343}{2.400} \approx 143\ \text{Hz} $$ $$ f_3 = 3f_1 \approx 429\ \text{Hz}, \qquad f_5 = 5f_1 \approx 715\ \text{Hz}. $$

Even harmonics ($2f_1, 4f_1, \ldots$) are absent. This restriction to odd harmonics gives closed-tube instruments their characteristically hollow timbre compared with open tubes, which support all harmonics.

Key Theorem. For a closed tube of length $L$, the resonant spectrum contains only odd multiples of the fundamental: $\{f_n\} = \{(2n-1)f_1 \mid n \in \mathbb{Z}^+\}$. This follows directly from the boundary conditions: the displacement must simultaneously vanish at $x = 0$ (closed end) and be extremal at $x = L$ (open end), admitting only solutions of the form $\sin\!\bigl(\frac{(2n-1)\pi x}{2L}\bigr)$.

Lab Exercise. Fill a tall graduated cylinder with water and hold a vibrating tuning fork ($f = 512\ \text{Hz}$) over the opening. Slowly lower the water level until the first resonance is heard. Measure the air-column length $L$ and verify that $L \approx v/(4f)$. Repeat to locate the second resonance at $L' \approx 3v/(4f)$ and confirm the $3\!:\!1$ ratio between the two resonant lengths.

Payoff 🎯

Musical instrument resonance stands as the natural culmination of Chapter 17 because it fuses every preceding concept — wave superposition, standing waves, boundary conditions, harmonic series, and interference — into a single coherent physical phenomenon with immediate sensory consequence. A vibrating string or air column does not merely oscillate; it selects, from the continuous spectrum of possible frequencies, precisely those modes permitted by its geometry and boundary conditions. This selectivity is not incidental: it is the mechanism by which a physical object converts broadband mechanical energy into a discrete, musically meaningful tone.

Definition. A resonant mode of a one-dimensional system of length $L$ satisfies the standing-wave condition imposed by its boundary constraints. For a string fixed at both ends, the permitted wavelengths are $\lambda_n = 2L/n$ for positive integers $n$, yielding harmonic frequencies

$$ f_n = \frac{n}{2L}\sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $T$ is string tension and $\mu$ is linear mass density. For an open cylindrical pipe, the same harmonic series holds with $v_s/2L$ replacing $\sqrt{T/\mu}/2L$; a stopped pipe produces only odd harmonics, $f_n = nv_s/4L$ for odd $n$.

Worked example. A guitar string of length $L = 0.650\ \text{m}$, tension $T = 73.4\ \text{N}$, and linear density $\mu = 3.09 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{kg/m}$ has a fundamental frequency $f_1 = \frac{1}{2(0.650)}\sqrt{73.4/3.09 \times 10^{-3}} \approx 196\ \text{Hz}$, corresponding to the note G3. Fretting the string at the fifth position reduces the vibrating length to $L' = 0.487\ \text{m}$, raising the fundamental to $f_1' \approx 261\ \text{Hz}$ (C4, middle C), confirming that $f \propto L^{-1}$.

Key theorem. The quality factor $Q = f_0 / \Delta f$ quantifies how sharply a resonator responds near its natural frequency $f_0$, where $\Delta f$ is the half-power bandwidth. High-$Q$ resonators — well-crafted instrument bodies, tuned organ pipes — sustain specific harmonics far longer than broadband damping would permit, producing the tonal clarity that distinguishes a violin from a struck board.
