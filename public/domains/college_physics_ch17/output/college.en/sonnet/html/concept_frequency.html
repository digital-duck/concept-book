🌱 Frequency

Definition. The frequency of a periodic phenomenon is the number of complete cycles occurring per unit time. For a wave or oscillator with period $T$ (the duration of one full cycle), frequency is defined as

$$ f = \frac{1}{T}. $$

The SI unit of frequency is the hertz (Hz), where $1\ \text{Hz} = 1\ \text{s}^{-1}$. For mechanical and sound waves propagating at speed $v$ with wavelength $\lambda$, frequency is related to these quantities by the fundamental wave relation

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Frequency characterizes how rapidly oscillation repeats and is entirely independent of amplitude — doubling the amplitude of a tuning fork does not alter its pitch, which is determined solely by $f$.

Worked Example. A sound wave in air travels at $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$ and has a measured wavelength of $\lambda = 0.686\ \text{m}$. Find its frequency and period.

Applying the wave relation:

$$ f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{343\ \text{m/s}}{0.686\ \text{m}} = 500\ \text{Hz}. $$

The period follows directly:

$$ T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{500\ \text{Hz}} = 2.00 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{s} = 2.00\ \text{ms}. $$

This frequency lies comfortably within the human audible range of approximately $20\ \text{Hz}$ to $20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$, corresponding to a musical pitch near $B_4$.

Key Theorem (Fourier's Theorem). Any periodic waveform of frequency $f_0$ can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoidal components at integer multiples $f_0, 2f_0, 3f_0, \ldots$ called harmonics:

$$ y(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \sin(2\pi n f_0 t + \phi_n). $$

This result underpins the analysis of musical timbre, electrical signals, and acoustic resonance: two instruments playing the same fundamental frequency $f_0$ differ in sound because their harmonic amplitudes $A_n$ differ.

Lab Exercise. Using a function generator and oscilloscope, produce a sinusoidal signal and record the period $T$ from the time-axis display. Compute $f = 1/T$ and compare it to the generator's dial setting. Next, connect a speaker and measure the wavelength of the emitted sound by locating successive pressure maxima with a microphone on a rail. With $v_{\text{sound}} \approx 343\ \text{m/s}$ at room temperature, verify that $f\lambda = v_{\text{sound}}$ within experimental uncertainty.
