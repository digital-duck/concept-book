🍃 Sound Intensity

🍃 Sound Intensity

Definition. Sound intensity $I$ is defined as the average power $P$ transported by a sound wave per unit area $A$ perpendicular to the direction of propagation:

$$ I = \frac{P}{A} $$

The SI unit of intensity is watts per square meter ($\text{W/m}^2$). Because sound is a longitudinal pressure wave, intensity depends on the amplitude of pressure oscillation and the medium's acoustic properties. For a sinusoidal plane wave with pressure amplitude $\Delta P_{\max}$ traveling through a medium of known density and sound speed, the intensity scales as $I \propto (\Delta P_{\max})^2$ — doubling the pressure amplitude quadruples the intensity.

Worked Example. A loudspeaker radiates sound uniformly in all directions with a total acoustic power of $P = 2.00\ \text{W}$. Find the intensity at a distance $r = 4.00\ \text{m}$ from the speaker.

Assuming spherical spreading, the wavefront at distance $r$ is a sphere of area $A = 4\pi r^2$:

$$ A = 4\pi (4.00)^2 = 4\pi (16.0) \approx 201\ \text{m}^2 $$ $$ I = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{2.00\ \text{W}}{201\ \text{m}^2} \approx 9.95 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{W/m}^2 $$

This inverse-square behavior — $I \propto 1/r^2$ — is a direct consequence of energy conservation over an expanding spherical surface.

Key Theorem (Inverse-Square Law). For a point source radiating isotropically in a lossless medium, the intensities $I_1$ and $I_2$ at distances $r_1$ and $r_2$ satisfy:

$$ \frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{r_2^2}{r_1^2} $$

This relation holds whenever absorption is negligible and no reflective boundaries are present. It provides a powerful tool for estimating intensity changes without knowing the source power explicitly — only the ratio of distances is required.

Lab Exercise. Place a calibrated sound-level meter at distances of $1.0\ \text{m}$, $2.0\ \text{m}$, and $4.0\ \text{m}$ from a small speaker driven at constant power. Record the intensity $I$ at each position, compute $I r^2$, and verify that this product remains approximately constant. Quantify any deviations and propose physical mechanisms — room reflections, air absorption, or directional emission — that could account for them.
