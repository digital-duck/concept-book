🌸 Ultrasound Therapy

🌸 Ultrasound Therapy

Definition. Ultrasound therapy is the clinical application of high-frequency mechanical waves—typically in the range of 0.8 to 3.0 MHz—to biological tissue for diagnostic or therapeutic purposes. Unlike audible sound (20 Hz to 20 kHz), ultrasonic waves propagate through soft tissue primarily as longitudinal pressure waves. The central physical quantity governing therapeutic effect is intensity, defined as the power delivered per unit area:

$$ I = \frac{P}{A} $$

Therapeutic ultrasound operates at intensities ranging from $0.5\ \text{W/cm}^2$ to $3.0\ \text{W/cm}^2$, producing two primary effects: thermal heating through viscous absorption, and mechanical agitation (cavitation). All dosimetric decisions in clinical practice reduce to controlling $I$ at the target tissue.

Worked Example. A physiotherapist applies an ultrasound transducer at $f = 1.0\ \text{MHz}$ with an output power of $P = 5.0\ \text{W}$ through a circular head of radius $r = 2.0\ \text{cm}$. Find the intensity delivered to the tissue.

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.020\ \text{m})^2 = 1.257 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2 $$ $$ I = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{5.0\ \text{W}}{1.257 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2} \approx 3.98\ \text{kW/m}^2 = 0.398\ \text{W/cm}^2 $$

This value falls just below the lower therapeutic threshold, so the clinician should increase output power or reduce the applicator area to reach effective treatment intensity without exceeding the safe upper bound.

Key Theorem (Exponential Attenuation). As an ultrasound beam propagates through tissue, intensity decreases exponentially with penetration depth $d$:

$$ I(d) = I_0\, e^{-2\mu d} $$

where $\mu$ is the tissue absorption coefficient (units: $\text{cm}^{-1}$). Higher frequencies yield larger $\mu$, restricting penetration. For soft muscle at 1 MHz, $\mu \approx 0.15\ \text{cm}^{-1}$; at 3 MHz it rises to approximately $0.45\ \text{cm}^{-1}$, limiting therapeutic reach to roughly 2–3 cm.

Lab Exercise. Using the exponential attenuation model, derive the half-intensity depth $d_{1/2}$ at which $I(d_{1/2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}I_0$. Show that $d_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{2\mu}$, then substitute the numerical values for muscle tissue at 1 MHz and 3 MHz. Discuss how your results inform the clinical choice of frequency when treating deep versus superficial tissue injuries.
