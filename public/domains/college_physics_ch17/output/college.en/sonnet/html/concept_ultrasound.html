🍃 Ultrasound

🍃 Ultrasound

Definition. Ultrasound refers to acoustic waves with frequencies above the upper threshold of human hearing, conventionally taken as $f > 20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$ (20 kHz). Unlike audible sound, ultrasonic waves occupy the frequency range from approximately $20\ \text{kHz}$ to several gigahertz ($10^9\ \text{Hz}$), depending on the application. As mechanical pressure waves, they obey the same wave equation as audible sound:

$$ v = f\lambda $$

where $v$ is the wave speed in the medium, $f$ is frequency, and $\lambda$ is wavelength. In soft biological tissue, $v \approx 1540\ \text{m/s}$; thus a $5\ \text{MHz}$ diagnostic ultrasound pulse has a wavelength of $\lambda = v/f = 1540/5{\times}10^6 \approx 0.31\ \text{mm}$, which sets the spatial resolution achievable in medical imaging.

Worked Example. A diagnostic ultrasound transducer operating at $f = 3.5\ \text{MHz}$ emits a pulse into abdominal tissue. An echo returns from an organ boundary after a round-trip time of $\Delta t = 1.4 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{s}$. Find the depth $d$ of the boundary.

The total path length traveled is $v \cdot \Delta t$, and since the pulse travels to the boundary and back, the depth is

$$ d = \frac{v \cdot \Delta t}{2} = \frac{1540 \times 1.4 \times 10^{-4}}{2} \approx 0.108\ \text{m} = 10.8\ \text{cm}. $$

This pulse-echo timing principle underlies all ultrasound imaging (sonography).

Key Theorem — Acoustic Impedance and Reflection. At the interface between two media, the fraction of ultrasound intensity reflected is governed by the acoustic impedance $Z = \rho v$, where $\rho$ is the medium density and $v$ is the wave speed already introduced above. The intensity reflection coefficient is

$$ R = \left(\frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}\right)^2. $$

When $Z_1 \approx Z_2$ (e.g., tissue–tissue interfaces), $R \approx 0$ and most energy is transmitted; when the mismatch is large (e.g., tissue–bone or tissue–air), $R \to 1$ and reflection dominates. This is why coupling gel is applied during sonography: it eliminates the air gap ($Z_{\text{air}} \operatorname{ll} Z_{\text{tissue}}$) between the transducer and skin, ensuring efficient energy transfer into the body.

Record the oscilloscope trace to measure the round-trip time $\Delta t$, then compute $v = 2d_0/\Delta t$. Compare your result to the standard value $v_{\text{water}} \approx 1480\ \text{m/s}$ at $20^\circ\text{C}$, and discuss how temperature affects wave speed through its influence on the bulk modulus of water.
