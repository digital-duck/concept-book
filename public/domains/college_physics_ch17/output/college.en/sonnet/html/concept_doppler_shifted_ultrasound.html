🌸 Doppler Shifted Ultrasound

🌸 Doppler Shifted Ultrasound

Definition. The Doppler effect describes the change in observed frequency of a wave when the source and observer are in relative motion. For ultrasound — high-frequency sound waves typically in the range of 1–20 MHz — this principle underpins a critical medical imaging technique. When an ultrasound transducer emits a wave of frequency $f_s$ toward a moving target (such as flowing blood or a beating heart wall), the reflected wave returns at a shifted frequency $f_o$. The general Doppler formula for a moving observer and moving source in a medium where sound travels at speed $v$ is:

$$ f_o = f_s \left(\frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s}\right) $$

where $v_o$ is the observer's speed (positive when moving toward the source) and $v_s$ is the source's speed (positive when moving toward the observer). In medical ultrasonography, the transducer acts simultaneously as source and detector, so the relevant quantity is the Doppler shift $\Delta f = f_o - f_s$, which for a reflector moving at speed $v_r$ directly toward the transducer simplifies to:

$$ \Delta f = \frac{2 f_s v_r}{v} $$

The factor of 2 arises because the wave undergoes two Doppler shifts — once on the way to the reflector and once on the return path.

Worked Example. A Doppler ultrasound device operates at $f_s = 5.00\,\text{MHz}$. The speed of sound in tissue is approximately $v = 1540\,\text{m/s}$. Blood flows directly toward the transducer at $v_r = 0.250\,\text{m/s}$. The Doppler shift is:

$$ \Delta f = \frac{2 \times (5.00 \times 10^6\,\text{Hz}) \times (0.250\,\text{m/s})}{1540\,\text{m/s}} \approx 1623\,\text{Hz} \approx 1.62\,\text{kHz} $$

This shift falls within the audible range, which is why early Doppler instruments could be monitored by ear.

Key Theorem. For a reflector moving at angle $\theta$ relative to the beam axis, the effective component of velocity is $v_r \cos\theta$, giving:

$$ \Delta f = \frac{2 f_s v_r \cos\theta}{v} $$

At $\theta = 90°$, no frequency shift is detected, illustrating that only the component of motion along the beam axis contributes to the Doppler signal.

Lab Exercise. Using a 3.00 MHz transducer and tissue sound speed of 1540 m/s, calculate the Doppler shift produced by a vessel wall moving at $0.100\,\text{m/s}$ at an insonation angle of $60°$. Then determine the minimum detectable velocity if the instrument can resolve frequency shifts as small as $50\,\text{Hz}$.
