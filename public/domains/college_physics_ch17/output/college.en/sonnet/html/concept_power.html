🌱 Power

🌱 Power

Definition. Power is the rate at which work is done, or equivalently, the rate at which energy is transferred or converted. For a process delivering work $W$ over a time interval $\Delta t$, the average power is

$$ \bar{P} = \frac{W}{\Delta t}. $$

The instantaneous power is the limiting case as $\Delta t \to 0$:

$$ P = \frac{dW}{dt}. $$

Since $dW = \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r}$, this may be written

$$ P = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v}, $$

where $\vec{v}$ is the instantaneous velocity of the point of force application. The SI unit of power is the watt (W), defined as $1\,\text{W} = 1\,\text{J}\,\text{s}^{-1}$. A legacy unit, the horsepower, satisfies $1\,\text{hp} = 746\,\text{W}$.

Worked Example. A motor lifts a $250\,\text{kg}$ elevator cab at a constant velocity of $1.80\,\text{m s}^{-1}$. Determine the mechanical power delivered by the motor.

Because the cab moves at constant velocity, the net force is zero, so the motor's upward tension $T$ exactly balances gravity:

$$ T = mg = (250\,\text{kg})(9.80\,\text{m s}^{-2}) = 2450\,\text{N}. $$

Since $\vec{T}$ and $\vec{v}$ are parallel,

$$ P = Tv = (2450\,\text{N})(1.80\,\text{m s}^{-1}) = 4410\,\text{W} \approx 4.41\,\text{kW}. $$

Key Theorem — Work–Energy Theorem in Power Form. For a particle of mass $m$, the net power equals the time rate of change of kinetic energy:

$$ P_{\text{net}} = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\right) = mv\frac{dv}{dt}. $$

This follows directly from Newton's second law and the definition of kinetic energy. The result is central to engine and motor analysis: whatever net power is supplied to an object determines how rapidly its kinetic energy changes, independent of the specific force configuration.

Lab Exercise. Using a force sensor and a motion detector, apply a measured horizontal force to a cart on a low-friction track and record both force and velocity as functions of time. Compute the instantaneous power $P(t) = F(t)\,v(t)$ at several instants and integrate numerically over a $5\,\text{s}$ interval to obtain the total work done. Compare this value to the change in kinetic energy $\Delta KE = \tfrac{1}{2}m v_f^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}m v_i^2$ and assess whether the work–energy theorem holds within experimental uncertainty.
