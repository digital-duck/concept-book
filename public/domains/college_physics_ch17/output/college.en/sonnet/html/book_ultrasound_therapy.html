🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the magnitude of the normal force $F_\perp$ exerted on a surface divided by the area $A$ over which that force is distributed:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; at any point within a static fluid, it acts equally in all directions (Pascal's principle). In a fluid column of uniform density $\rho$ under gravitational acceleration $g$, the gauge pressure at depth $h$ below the free surface is:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface. This linear dependence on depth arises because each additional fluid layer contributes its weight per unit area to the layers beneath it.

Worked Example. A rectangular tank is filled with water ($\rho = 1000\,\text{kg/m}^3$) to a depth of $h = 3.0\,\text{m}$. The atmospheric pressure at the surface is $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Find the absolute pressure at the bottom of the tank.

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1000)(9.81)(3.0) $$ $$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 2.943 \times 10^4 = 1.307 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa} $$

The gauge pressure (excess above atmospheric) is simply $\rho g h = 2.94 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa} \approx 0.29\,\text{atm}$.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. Any change in pressure applied to an enclosed incompressible fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if the applied surface pressure changes by $\Delta P$, then at every interior point the pressure also changes by $\Delta P$. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small piston area $A_1$ produces an equal pressure increase throughout the fluid, generating a large force $F_2 = P \cdot A_2$ over a larger piston area $A_2$.

Laboratory Exercise. Using a U-tube manometer partially filled with water, apply a known gauge pressure to one arm by connecting it to a sealed reservoir at a measured height. Record the height difference $\Delta h$ between the two arms and verify that $\Delta P = \rho g \Delta h$. Repeat with a denser fluid (e.g., glycerin, $\rho \approx 1260\,\text{kg/m}^3$) and compare the height differences for the same applied pressure, confirming the inverse dependence of $\Delta h$ on $\rho$.

🌱 Simple Harmonic Motion

🌱 Simple Harmonic Motion

Definition. Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a special class of periodic motion in which the restoring force on an object is directly proportional to its displacement from equilibrium and directed opposite to that displacement. Mathematically, this condition is expressed as

$$ F = -kx, $$

where $k > 0$ is the spring constant (or, more generally, the stiffness coefficient) and $x$ is the displacement from the equilibrium position. Applying Newton's second law, $F = ma$, yields the equation of motion

$$ m\ddot{x} + kx = 0, $$

whose general solution is

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), $$

with angular frequency $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$, amplitude $A$, and phase constant $\phi$ determined by initial conditions. The period and frequency follow immediately:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T}. $$

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 0.50\ \text{kg}$ is attached to a horizontal spring with $k = 200\ \text{N/m}$. At $t = 0$ the block is displaced $x_0 = 0.10\ \text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest.

Find the angular frequency, period, and position at $t = 0.30\ \text{s}$.

Step 1: $\omega = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{200/0.50} = 20\ \text{rad/s}$.

Step 2: $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/20 \approx 0.314\ \text{s}$.

Step 3: Since $v(0) = 0$ and $x(0) = x_0$, we identify $A = 0.10\ \text{m}$ and $\phi = 0$. Therefore

$$ x(0.30) = 0.10\cos(20 \times 0.30) = 0.10\cos(6.0) \approx 0.10 \times 0.960 = 0.096\ \text{m}. $$

Key Theorem. Because the spring force is conservative, the mechanical energy conservation principle established in classical mechanics applies directly to SHM:

$$ E = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2 = \text{const.} $$

This result, rather than introducing new physics, is the familiar kinetic-plus-potential energy balance evaluated for a linear restoring force. It immediately yields the maximum speed $v_{\max} = A\omega$ at $x = 0$, and confirms that the object momentarily stops ($v = 0$) at the turning points $x = \pm A$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a mass on a vertical spring and measure the period for three different masses. Plot $T^2$ versus $m$ and extract $k$ from the slope. Compare with the value obtained from a static force–extension measurement. Identify two potential sources of systematic error in each method.

🌱 Wave

🌱 Wave

Definition. A wave is a disturbance that propagates through a medium (or through free space, in the case of electromagnetic waves), transferring energy and momentum without the net transport of matter. Mathematically, any function of the form

$$ y(x, t) = A\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

describes a sinusoidal traveling wave moving in the positive $x$-direction, where $A$ is the amplitude, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, and $\phi$ is the initial phase. The wave speed is related to these quantities by

$$ v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \lambda f. $$

Waves are classified as transverse (displacement perpendicular to propagation, e.g., light on a string) or longitudinal (displacement parallel to propagation, e.g., sound in air).

Worked Example. A sinusoidal wave on a stretched string is described by $y(x,t) = 0.020\sin(6.28x - 31.4t)$ meters, where $x$ is in meters and $t$ in seconds. Identify the amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave speed.

  • Amplitude: $A = 0.020\ \text{m}$
  • Wave number: $k = 6.28\ \text{rad/m}$, so $\lambda = 2\pi/k = 1.00\ \text{m}$
  • Angular frequency: $\omega = 31.4\ \text{rad/s}$, so $f = \omega/2\pi = 5.00\ \text{Hz}$
  • Wave speed: $v = \omega/k = 31.4/6.28 = 5.00\ \text{m/s}$

Key Theorem — Wave Equation. Any wave satisfying the linear wave equation

$$ \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} $$

obeys the superposition principle: if $y_1$ and $y_2$ are both solutions, then $y_1 + y_2$ is also a solution. This underpins phenomena such as interference and standing waves. For a transverse wave on a string under tension $T$ with linear mass density $\mu$, the wave speed is $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$, confirming that $v$ depends only on the medium's physical properties.

Using a function generator and a stretched string of known linear mass density $\mu = 5.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{kg/m}$, set the tension to $T = 12\ \text{N}$ and drive the string at $f = 15\ \text{Hz}$. Predict the wavelength using $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$ and $\lambda = v/f$, then measure the node spacing in the resulting standing wave pattern to verify your prediction experimentally. Quantify any discrepancy and identify its likely sources.

🍃 Sound

🍃 Sound

Definition. Sound is a mechanical, longitudinal wave that propagates through a compressible medium by alternating compressions and rarefactions of the medium's constituent particles. Unlike electromagnetic waves, sound requires a material medium and cannot travel through a vacuum. A sound wave is fully characterized by a single primitive quantity—frequency $f$ (Hz), the number of complete oscillation cycles per second—together with derived quantities: wavelength $\lambda$ (m), amplitude $A$ (Pa for pressure waves), and propagation speed $v$ (m/s). These are related by the fundamental wave equation:

$$ v = f\lambda $$

The speed of sound in an ideal gas is given by:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_0}{\rho}} $$

where $\gamma$ is the adiabatic index (approximately 1.40 for diatomic gases such as air), $P_0$ is the equilibrium pressure, and $\rho$ is the mass density. For dry air at $T = 20\,^\circ\text{C}$, this yields $v \approx 343\,\text{m/s}$.

Worked Example. A tuning fork vibrating at $f = 440\,\text{Hz}$ (concert A) is struck in air at $20\,^\circ\text{C}$. Determine the wavelength of the sound wave produced.

Using $v = f\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343\,\text{m/s}}{440\,\text{Hz}} \approx 0.780\,\text{m} $$

This wavelength falls within the range of typical room dimensions, which is why standing-wave resonances are audible in enclosed spaces.

Key Theorem: Newton–Laplace Correction. Newton originally computed the speed of sound assuming an isothermal process, obtaining $v = \sqrt{P_0/\rho}$, which underestimates the measured value by roughly 15%. Laplace corrected this by recognizing that compressions and rarefactions in a sound wave occur too rapidly for heat exchange with the surroundings, making the process adiabatic rather than isothermal. Replacing $P_0$ with $\gamma P_0$ yields the accurate expression above. This correction reflects a fundamental principle: the thermodynamic process governing wave propagation determines the appropriate elastic modulus.

Lab Exercise. Using a resonance tube apparatus open at one end and closed at the other, strike a tuning fork of known frequency $f$ and slide the tube until the first resonance is detected (tube length $L = \lambda/4$). Measure $L$ with a ruler and compute $v = 4Lf$. Compare your result to the theoretical value $v = 331\sqrt{1 + T/273}\,\text{m/s}$ (with $T$ in degrees Celsius) and calculate the percent error. Repeat at three different temperatures to verify the temperature dependence.

🌱 Frequency

🌱 Frequency

Definition. The frequency of a periodic phenomenon is the number of complete cycles occurring per unit time. For a wave or oscillator with period $T$ (the duration of one full cycle), frequency is defined as

$$ f = \frac{1}{T}. $$

The SI unit of frequency is the hertz (Hz), where $1\ \text{Hz} = 1\ \text{s}^{-1}$. For mechanical and sound waves propagating at speed $v$ with wavelength $\lambda$, frequency is related to these quantities by the fundamental wave relation

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Frequency characterizes how rapidly oscillation repeats and is entirely independent of amplitude — doubling the amplitude of a tuning fork does not alter its pitch, which is determined solely by $f$.

Worked Example. A sound wave in air travels at $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$ and has a measured wavelength of $\lambda = 0.686\ \text{m}$. Find its frequency and period.

Applying the wave relation:

$$ f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{343\ \text{m/s}}{0.686\ \text{m}} = 500\ \text{Hz}. $$

The period follows directly:

$$ T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{500\ \text{Hz}} = 2.00 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{s} = 2.00\ \text{ms}. $$

This frequency lies comfortably within the human audible range of approximately $20\ \text{Hz}$ to $20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$, corresponding to a musical pitch near $B_4$.

Key Theorem (Fourier's Theorem). Any periodic waveform of frequency $f_0$ can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoidal components at integer multiples $f_0, 2f_0, 3f_0, \ldots$ called harmonics:

$$ y(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \sin(2\pi n f_0 t + \phi_n). $$

This result underpins the analysis of musical timbre, electrical signals, and acoustic resonance: two instruments playing the same fundamental frequency $f_0$ differ in sound because their harmonic amplitudes $A_n$ differ.

Lab Exercise. Using a function generator and oscilloscope, produce a sinusoidal signal and record the period $T$ from the time-axis display. Compute $f = 1/T$ and compare it to the generator's dial setting. Next, connect a speaker and measure the wavelength of the emitted sound by locating successive pressure maxima with a microphone on a rail. With $v_{\text{sound}} \approx 343\ \text{m/s}$ at room temperature, verify that $f\lambda = v_{\text{sound}}$ within experimental uncertainty.

🌱 Amplitude

🌱 Amplitude

Definition. The amplitude of a periodic oscillation is the maximum magnitude of displacement from the equilibrium position. For a particle undergoing simple harmonic motion (SHM), the displacement as a function of time is

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi_0), $$

where $A > 0$ is the amplitude, $\omega$ is the angular frequency, and $\phi_0$ is the initial phase. The amplitude is not the range of the oscillation but rather the half-range: the motion spans the interval $[-A, A]$, giving a total excursion of $2A$. Dimensionally, amplitude carries the same units as the displacement it measures — meters for translational oscillations, radians for angular ones.

The mechanical energy of an ideal SHM system (spring constant $k$, mass $m$) is entirely determined by $A$:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2. $$

This relation makes amplitude the single most important parameter characterizing the intensity of an oscillation.

Worked Example. A $0.50\,\text{kg}$ block attached to a spring with $k = 200\,\text{N/m}$ is displaced $8.0\,\text{cm}$ from equilibrium and released from rest. Find the amplitude and the total mechanical energy.

Because the block is released from rest at maximum displacement, the release point coincides with the turning point, so

$$ A = 8.0\,\text{cm} = 0.080\,\text{m}. $$

The total energy is

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.080)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.0064) = 0.64\,\text{J}. $$

At equilibrium ($x = 0$) this energy is entirely kinetic. The maximum speed follows from energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}mv_{\max}^2 = E$, giving $v_{\max} = \sqrt{2E/m} = \sqrt{2(0.64)/0.50} = 1.6\,\text{m/s}$.

Key Theorem. For undamped SHM, the amplitude is a conserved quantity: it remains constant for all time because no energy leaves the system. Formally, since $E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \text{const}$, any combination of potential and kinetic energy at any phase yields the same $A$. This contrasts sharply with damped oscillations, where $A(t) = A_0 e^{-\gamma t}$ decays exponentially.

Lab Exercise. Mount a spring vertically, hang a known mass, and displace it by measured amounts $A = 2, 4, 6, 8\,\text{cm}$. Record the period $T$ and the maximum speed using a motion sensor. Verify that $T$ is independent of $A$ and that $v_{\max}$ scales linearly with $A$, confirming $E \propto A^2$.

🍃 Hearing

🍃 Hearing

Definition. Hearing is the physiological process by which the auditory system converts mechanical pressure waves in air into neural signals interpreted by the brain as sound. A sound wave is a longitudinal pressure disturbance characterized by its frequency $f$ (measured in hertz, Hz). The human ear responds to a range of intensities, quantified by the sound level

$$ \beta = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{I}{I_0}\right) \text{ dB}, $$

where $I$ is the wave intensity (power per unit area, $\text{W/m}^2$) and $I_0 = 10^{-12}\ \text{W/m}^2$ is the reference intensity corresponding to the threshold of human hearing. The audible frequency range for a healthy young adult spans approximately $20\ \text{Hz}$ to $20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$, while the threshold of pain occurs near $130\ \text{dB}$.

Worked Example. A concert speaker produces a sound intensity of $I = 1.0 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{W/m}^2$ at a listener 3.0 m away. Determine the sound level in decibels.

$$ \beta = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{1.0 \times 10^{-2}}{1.0 \times 10^{-12}}\right) = 10 \log_{10}(10^{10}) = 10 \times 10 = 100\ \text{dB}. $$

This level is well above the threshold of hearing ($0\ \text{dB}$) and approaches the range where prolonged exposure causes cochlear damage (typically above $85\ \text{dB}$).

Key Theorem — Inverse-Square Law for Sound Intensity. For a point source radiating uniformly into free space, intensity falls off as

$$ I \propto \frac{1}{r^2}, $$

so that $I_1 r_1^2 = I_2 r_2^2$. Consequently, doubling the distance from a source reduces intensity by a factor of four, decreasing the sound level by approximately $6\ \text{dB}$.

Lab Exercise. Using a calibrated sound-level meter, measure $\beta$ at distances $r = 1.0,\ 2.0,\ 4.0\ \text{m}$ from a small loudspeaker driven at constant power. Record the measured levels and compare the observed decrease per doubling of distance to the theoretical $6\ \text{dB}$ prediction. Calculate the source intensity at each position and verify consistency with the inverse-square law. Discuss any deviations and attribute them to reflections, directivity, or ambient noise.

🌱 Power

🌱 Power

Definition. Power is the rate at which work is done, or equivalently, the rate at which energy is transferred or converted. For a process delivering work $W$ over a time interval $\Delta t$, the average power is

$$ \bar{P} = \frac{W}{\Delta t}. $$

The instantaneous power is the limiting case as $\Delta t \to 0$:

$$ P = \frac{dW}{dt}. $$

Since $dW = \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r}$, this may be written

$$ P = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v}, $$

where $\vec{v}$ is the instantaneous velocity of the point of force application. The SI unit of power is the watt (W), defined as $1\,\text{W} = 1\,\text{J}\,\text{s}^{-1}$. A legacy unit, the horsepower, satisfies $1\,\text{hp} = 746\,\text{W}$.

Worked Example. A motor lifts a $250\,\text{kg}$ elevator cab at a constant velocity of $1.80\,\text{m s}^{-1}$. Determine the mechanical power delivered by the motor.

Because the cab moves at constant velocity, the net force is zero, so the motor's upward tension $T$ exactly balances gravity:

$$ T = mg = (250\,\text{kg})(9.80\,\text{m s}^{-2}) = 2450\,\text{N}. $$

Since $\vec{T}$ and $\vec{v}$ are parallel,

$$ P = Tv = (2450\,\text{N})(1.80\,\text{m s}^{-1}) = 4410\,\text{W} \approx 4.41\,\text{kW}. $$

Key Theorem — Work–Energy Theorem in Power Form. For a particle of mass $m$, the net power equals the time rate of change of kinetic energy:

$$ P_{\text{net}} = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\right) = mv\frac{dv}{dt}. $$

This follows directly from Newton's second law and the definition of kinetic energy. The result is central to engine and motor analysis: whatever net power is supplied to an object determines how rapidly its kinetic energy changes, independent of the specific force configuration.

Lab Exercise. Using a force sensor and a motion detector, apply a measured horizontal force to a cart on a low-friction track and record both force and velocity as functions of time. Compute the instantaneous power $P(t) = F(t)\,v(t)$ at several instants and integrate numerically over a $5\,\text{s}$ interval to obtain the total work done. Compare this value to the change in kinetic energy $\Delta KE = \tfrac{1}{2}m v_f^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}m v_i^2$ and assess whether the work–energy theorem holds within experimental uncertainty.

🍃 Sound Intensity

🍃 Sound Intensity

Definition. Sound intensity $I$ is defined as the average power $P$ transported by a sound wave per unit area $A$ perpendicular to the direction of propagation:

$$ I = \frac{P}{A} $$

The SI unit of intensity is watts per square meter ($\text{W/m}^2$). Because sound is a longitudinal pressure wave, intensity depends on the amplitude of pressure oscillation and the medium's acoustic properties. For a sinusoidal plane wave with pressure amplitude $\Delta P_{\max}$ traveling through a medium of known density and sound speed, the intensity scales as $I \propto (\Delta P_{\max})^2$ — doubling the pressure amplitude quadruples the intensity.

Worked Example. A loudspeaker radiates sound uniformly in all directions with a total acoustic power of $P = 2.00\ \text{W}$. Find the intensity at a distance $r = 4.00\ \text{m}$ from the speaker.

Assuming spherical spreading, the wavefront at distance $r$ is a sphere of area $A = 4\pi r^2$:

$$ A = 4\pi (4.00)^2 = 4\pi (16.0) \approx 201\ \text{m}^2 $$ $$ I = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{2.00\ \text{W}}{201\ \text{m}^2} \approx 9.95 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{W/m}^2 $$

This inverse-square behavior — $I \propto 1/r^2$ — is a direct consequence of energy conservation over an expanding spherical surface.

Key Theorem (Inverse-Square Law). For a point source radiating isotropically in a lossless medium, the intensities $I_1$ and $I_2$ at distances $r_1$ and $r_2$ satisfy:

$$ \frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{r_2^2}{r_1^2} $$

This relation holds whenever absorption is negligible and no reflective boundaries are present. It provides a powerful tool for estimating intensity changes without knowing the source power explicitly — only the ratio of distances is required.

Lab Exercise. Place a calibrated sound-level meter at distances of $1.0\ \text{m}$, $2.0\ \text{m}$, and $4.0\ \text{m}$ from a small speaker driven at constant power. Record the intensity $I$ at each position, compute $I r^2$, and verify that this product remains approximately constant. Quantify any deviations and propose physical mechanisms — room reflections, air absorption, or directional emission — that could account for them.

🍃 Ultrasound

🍃 Ultrasound

Definition. Ultrasound refers to acoustic waves with frequencies above the upper threshold of human hearing, conventionally taken as $f > 20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$ (20 kHz). Unlike audible sound, ultrasonic waves occupy the frequency range from approximately $20\ \text{kHz}$ to several gigahertz ($10^9\ \text{Hz}$), depending on the application. As mechanical pressure waves, they obey the same wave equation as audible sound:

$$ v = f\lambda $$

where $v$ is the wave speed in the medium, $f$ is frequency, and $\lambda$ is wavelength. In soft biological tissue, $v \approx 1540\ \text{m/s}$; thus a $5\ \text{MHz}$ diagnostic ultrasound pulse has a wavelength of $\lambda = v/f = 1540/5{\times}10^6 \approx 0.31\ \text{mm}$, which sets the spatial resolution achievable in medical imaging.

Worked Example. A diagnostic ultrasound transducer operating at $f = 3.5\ \text{MHz}$ emits a pulse into abdominal tissue. An echo returns from an organ boundary after a round-trip time of $\Delta t = 1.4 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{s}$. Find the depth $d$ of the boundary.

The total path length traveled is $v \cdot \Delta t$, and since the pulse travels to the boundary and back, the depth is

$$ d = \frac{v \cdot \Delta t}{2} = \frac{1540 \times 1.4 \times 10^{-4}}{2} \approx 0.108\ \text{m} = 10.8\ \text{cm}. $$

This pulse-echo timing principle underlies all ultrasound imaging (sonography).

Key Theorem — Acoustic Impedance and Reflection. At the interface between two media, the fraction of ultrasound intensity reflected is governed by the acoustic impedance $Z = \rho v$, where $\rho$ is the medium density and $v$ is the wave speed already introduced above. The intensity reflection coefficient is

$$ R = \left(\frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}\right)^2. $$

When $Z_1 \approx Z_2$ (e.g., tissue–tissue interfaces), $R \approx 0$ and most energy is transmitted; when the mismatch is large (e.g., tissue–bone or tissue–air), $R \to 1$ and reflection dominates. This is why coupling gel is applied during sonography: it eliminates the air gap ($Z_{\text{air}} \operatorname{ll} Z_{\text{tissue}}$) between the transducer and skin, ensuring efficient energy transfer into the body.

Lab Exercise. Using a function generator and piezoelectric transducer, direct a $40\ \text{kHz}$ ultrasonic pulse at a flat reflector submerged in water at a known distance $d_0$. Record the oscilloscope trace to measure the round-trip time $\Delta t$, then compute $v = 2d_0/\Delta t$. Compare your result to the standard value $v_{\text{water}} \approx 1480\ \text{m/s}$ at $20^\circ\text{C}$, and discuss how temperature affects wave speed through its influence on the bulk modulus of water.

🌸 Ultrasound Therapy

🌸 Ultrasound Therapy

Definition. Ultrasound therapy is the clinical application of high-frequency mechanical waves—typically in the range of 0.8 to 3.0 MHz—to biological tissue for diagnostic or therapeutic purposes. Unlike audible sound (20 Hz to 20 kHz), ultrasonic waves propagate through soft tissue primarily as longitudinal pressure waves. The central physical quantity governing therapeutic effect is intensity, defined as the power delivered per unit area:

$$ I = \frac{P}{A} $$

Therapeutic ultrasound operates at intensities ranging from $0.5\ \text{W/cm}^2$ to $3.0\ \text{W/cm}^2$, producing two primary effects: thermal heating through viscous absorption, and mechanical agitation (cavitation). All dosimetric decisions in clinical practice reduce to controlling $I$ at the target tissue.

Worked Example. A physiotherapist applies an ultrasound transducer at $f = 1.0\ \text{MHz}$ with an output power of $P = 5.0\ \text{W}$ through a circular head of radius $r = 2.0\ \text{cm}$. Find the intensity delivered to the tissue.

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.020\ \text{m})^2 = 1.257 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2 $$ $$ I = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{5.0\ \text{W}}{1.257 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2} \approx 3.98\ \text{kW/m}^2 = 0.398\ \text{W/cm}^2 $$

This value falls just below the lower therapeutic threshold, so the clinician should increase output power or reduce the applicator area to reach effective treatment intensity without exceeding the safe upper bound.

Key Theorem (Exponential Attenuation). As an ultrasound beam propagates through tissue, intensity decreases exponentially with penetration depth $d$:

$$ I(d) = I_0\, e^{-2\mu d} $$

where $\mu$ is the tissue absorption coefficient (units: $\text{cm}^{-1}$). Higher frequencies yield larger $\mu$, restricting penetration. For soft muscle at 1 MHz, $\mu \approx 0.15\ \text{cm}^{-1}$; at 3 MHz it rises to approximately $0.45\ \text{cm}^{-1}$, limiting therapeutic reach to roughly 2–3 cm.

Lab Exercise. Using the exponential attenuation model, derive the half-intensity depth $d_{1/2}$ at which $I(d_{1/2}) = \tfrac{1}{2}I_0$. Show that $d_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{2\mu}$, then substitute the numerical values for muscle tissue at 1 MHz and 3 MHz. Discuss how your results inform the clinical choice of frequency when treating deep versus superficial tissue injuries.

Payoff 🎯

Ultrasound therapy represents the culmination of wave physics applied to biological systems, uniting the principles of mechanical oscillation, impedance matching, energy transfer, and nonlinear wave propagation into a coherent clinical and investigative tool. Throughout Chapter 17, the study of sound has progressed from idealized traveling waves through media to the quantitative description of intensity, frequency response, and boundary behavior. Ultrasound therapy is the natural endpoint of this progression because it demands mastery of every intermediate concept: frequency determines tissue penetration depth, intensity governs thermal and mechanical dose, and acoustic impedance mismatch at tissue boundaries dictates how energy is deposited or reflected.

Achieved Outcome. In therapeutic ultrasound, a transducer converts electrical energy into mechanical waves at frequencies typically between $0.8\,\text{MHz}$ and $3\,\text{MHz}$. The acoustic intensity $I = \frac{P}{A}$ delivered to a target volume produces two primary bioeffects. Thermal effects arise because soft tissue absorbs acoustic energy, raising local temperature according to $\Delta T = \frac{\alpha I t}{\rho c}$, where $\alpha$ is the absorption coefficient, $\rho$ is tissue density, and $c$ is specific heat capacity. Non-thermal (mechanical) effects — cavitation and acoustic streaming — arise when oscillating pressure cycles nucleate and collapse microbubbles, generating localized shear forces that accelerate cellular repair.

Connection to Application Domains. The framework developed here extends directly into multiple fields. In physical rehabilitation, therapists exploit the thermal mode to increase collagen extensibility in scar tissue, translating the $I$-versus-depth attenuation law into precise treatment protocols. In diagnostic imaging, the same transducer physics underpins pulse-echo sonography, where impedance contrast $Z = \rho v$ at organ boundaries produces the reflections that form anatomical images. In oncology, high-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU) concentrates wave energy at a focal point — an application of constructive interference and Huygens's principle — achieving ablative temperatures exceeding $56\,{}^{\circ}\text{C}$ without incision. In drug delivery research, sonoporation exploits cavitation-induced membrane permeabilization to enhance uptake of therapeutic agents into cells.

Invitation. Each of these domains rewards deeper inquiry, but focused ultrasound ablation in oncology offers perhaps the most striking demonstration of wave physics at clinical scale. Consider exploring how phased-array transducer geometry and electronic beam-steering allow surgeons to destroy deep-seated tumors with millimeter precision — an engineering achievement built entirely on the wave equations studied in this chapter.
