🍃 Fundamental Frequency

🍃 Fundamental Frequency

Definition. The fundamental frequency (also called the first harmonic) is the lowest resonant frequency at which a system vibrates when disturbed. For a standing wave on a string of length $L$ fixed at both ends, the fundamental arises when exactly one half-wavelength fits within the string:

$$ \lambda_1 = 2L $$

Combined with the wave speed relation $v = f\lambda$, the fundamental frequency is

$$ f_1 = \frac{v}{2L} $$

where $v$ is the transverse wave speed. Every higher resonant frequency (overtone) is an integer multiple of this value: $f_n = n f_1$, $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$

Worked Example. A guitar string has length $L = 0.650\,\text{m}$ and is held under tension $T = 120\,\text{N}$. Its linear mass density (mass per unit length) is $\mu = 5.38\times10^{-3}\,\text{kg/m}$.

  1. Find the wave speed using $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$: $v = \sqrt{120/(5.38\times10^{-3})} = \sqrt{2.23\times10^{4}} \approx 149\,\text{m/s}$.
  2. Apply the fundamental formula: $f_1 = v/(2L) = 149/(2\times0.650) \approx 115\,\text{Hz}$.

This falls within the range of the B$_2$ note, consistent with bass guitar registers.

Key Theorem (Harmonic Series). For any one-dimensional resonator with boundary conditions that support standing waves, the allowed resonant frequencies form a discrete harmonic series $\{nf_1 : n \in \mathbb{Z}^+\}$. The fundamental $f_1$ is uniquely determined by the geometry (length $L$) and the medium (wave speed $v$). Altering $T$ or $\mu$ shifts the entire series proportionally, since $f_1 \propto \sqrt{T/\mu}$.

Lab Exercise. Attach one end of a string (with known $\mu$) to a mechanical oscillator and pass the other end over a frictionless pulley, suspending a hanging mass $M$. Systematically vary $M$ while keeping $L$ fixed, and record the mass at which the string resonates in its fundamental mode (single antinode at center). Plot $f_1$ versus $\sqrt{Mg}$ and extract the slope to determine $\mu$ experimentally. Compare your result to the directly measured value and compute the percent discrepancy.
