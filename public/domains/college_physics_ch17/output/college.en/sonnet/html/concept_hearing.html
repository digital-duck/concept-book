🍃 Hearing

🍃 Hearing

Definition. Hearing is the physiological process by which the auditory system converts mechanical pressure waves in air into neural signals interpreted by the brain as sound. A sound wave is a longitudinal pressure disturbance characterized by its frequency $f$ (measured in hertz, Hz). The human ear responds to a range of intensities, quantified by the sound level

$$ \beta = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{I}{I_0}\right) \text{ dB}, $$

where $I$ is the wave intensity (power per unit area, $\text{W/m}^2$) and $I_0 = 10^{-12}\ \text{W/m}^2$ is the reference intensity corresponding to the threshold of human hearing. The audible frequency range for a healthy young adult spans approximately $20\ \text{Hz}$ to $20{,}000\ \text{Hz}$, while the threshold of pain occurs near $130\ \text{dB}$.

Worked Example. A concert speaker produces a sound intensity of $I = 1.0 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{W/m}^2$ at a listener 3.0 m away. Determine the sound level in decibels.

$$ \beta = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{1.0 \times 10^{-2}}{1.0 \times 10^{-12}}\right) = 10 \log_{10}(10^{10}) = 10 \times 10 = 100\ \text{dB}. $$

This level is well above the threshold of hearing ($0\ \text{dB}$) and approaches the range where prolonged exposure causes cochlear damage (typically above $85\ \text{dB}$).

Key Theorem — Inverse-Square Law for Sound Intensity. For a point source radiating uniformly into free space, intensity falls off as

$$ I \propto \frac{1}{r^2}, $$

so that $I_1 r_1^2 = I_2 r_2^2$. Consequently, doubling the distance from a source reduces intensity by a factor of four, decreasing the sound level by approximately $6\ \text{dB}$.

Lab Exercise. Using a calibrated sound-level meter, measure $\beta$ at distances $r = 1.0,\ 2.0,\ 4.0\ \text{m}$ from a small loudspeaker driven at constant power. Record the measured levels and compare the observed decrease per doubling of distance to the theoretical $6\ \text{dB}$ prediction. Calculate the source intensity at each position and verify consistency with the inverse-square law. Discuss any deviations and attribute them to reflections, directivity, or ambient noise.
