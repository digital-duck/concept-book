🍃 Standing Wave

🍃 Standing Wave

Definition. A standing wave is a vibrational pattern produced by the superposition of two traveling waves of equal amplitude and frequency propagating in opposite directions along the same medium. Unlike a traveling wave, a standing wave does not transport energy along the medium; instead, energy oscillates locally between kinetic and potential forms. The displacement of a standing wave on a string is given by

$$ y(x, t) = 2A\sin(kx)\cos(\omega t), $$

where $A$ is the amplitude of each component wave, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, and $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency. Points where $\sin(kx) = 0$ are called nodes — positions of permanent zero displacement. Points where $|\sin(kx)| = 1$ are called antinodes — positions of maximum displacement amplitude $2A$. Adjacent nodes are separated by $\lambda/2$, as are adjacent antinodes.

Worked Example. A string of length $L = 0.80\text{ m}$ is fixed at both ends and supports a standing wave with three antinodes. Determine the wavelength and, given a wave speed $v = 240\text{ m/s}$, find the frequency.

Three antinodes correspond to the third harmonic ($n = 3$). For a string fixed at both ends, the condition for standing waves is

$$ L = n\frac{\lambda}{2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

Solving for $\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{2L}{n} = \frac{2(0.80)}{3} \approx 0.533\text{ m}. $$

The frequency follows from $v = f\lambda$:

$$ f = \frac{v}{\lambda} = \frac{240}{0.533} \approx 450\text{ Hz}. $$

Key Theorem (Harmonic Series). For a string of length $L$ fixed at both ends with wave speed $v$, the allowed frequencies form a harmonic series — a discrete set of normal modes in which each frequency is an integer multiple of the lowest allowed frequency:

$$ f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

The lowest mode, $f_1 = v/(2L)$, is the fundamental frequency; every higher harmonic satisfies $f_n = n f_1$. The discretization of allowed frequencies is the physical basis for musical pitch in stringed instruments: only wavelengths that fit an integer number of half-wavelengths within the string length can sustain a standing wave.

Lab Exercise. Attach one end of a 1.2 m string to a mechanical oscillator and the other end over a pulley to a hanging mass. Adjust the driving frequency until you observe the second harmonic ($n = 2$). Measure the node-to-node distance and verify that $L = \lambda$ for this mode. Repeat for $n = 3$ and confirm that the frequency ratio $f_3/f_2 = 3/2$.
