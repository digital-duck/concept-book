🍃 Acoustic Impedance

🍃 Acoustic Impedance

Definition. The acoustic impedance of a medium quantifies its resistance to the propagation of sound waves. For a plane wave in a homogeneous, non-dissipative medium, the characteristic acoustic impedance $Z$ is defined as:

$$ Z = \rho v $$

where $\rho$ is the equilibrium mass density of the medium (kg/m³) and $v$ is the speed of sound in that medium (m/s). The SI unit of acoustic impedance is the pascal-second per meter (Pa·s/m), also called the rayl (Rayl). Both $\rho$ and $v$ are familiar quantities from earlier in the course; acoustic impedance combines them into a single material property that governs wave behavior at boundaries.

Acoustic impedance plays the same conceptual role in acoustics that electrical impedance plays in circuit theory: it determines how much of an incident wave is transmitted versus reflected when a wave crosses from one medium into another.

Worked Example. Consider the interface between air and water at 20 °C. For air, $\rho_1 = 1.21\ \text{kg/m}^3$ and $v_1 = 343\ \text{m/s}$, giving $Z_1 = (1.21)(343) \approx 415\ \text{Rayl}$. For water, $\rho_2 = 998\ \text{kg/m}^3$ and $v_2 = 1480\ \text{m/s}$, giving $Z_2 = (998)(1480) \approx 1.48 \times 10^6\ \text{Rayl}$. The impedance mismatch ratio is enormous ($Z_2/Z_1 \approx 3600$), which explains why sound is almost entirely reflected at an air–water surface.

Key Theorem — Reflection and Transmission Coefficients. At a planar boundary between two media with impedances $Z_1$ and $Z_2$, the pressure reflection coefficient $r$ and transmission coefficient $\tau$ for a normally incident wave are:

$$ r = \frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}, \qquad \tau = \frac{2Z_2}{Z_2 + Z_1} $$

The intensity reflection coefficient is $R = r^2$, and conservation of energy requires $R + T = 1$, where $T = \frac{Z_1}{Z_2}\tau^2$.

Lab Exercise. Using a signal generator and piezoelectric transducer, measure the amplitude of ultrasonic pulses transmitted through successive layers of air, cork, and water. For each interface, compute the predicted intensity transmission coefficient from the formula above and compare with the measured amplitude ratio squared. Identify which interface produces the greatest signal loss and propose one method—such as an impedance-matching gel layer—to improve transmission efficiency.
