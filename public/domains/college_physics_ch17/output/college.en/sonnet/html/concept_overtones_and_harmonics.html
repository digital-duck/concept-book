🍃 Overtones And Harmonics

🍃 Overtones And Harmonics

Definition. When a mechanical system—such as a vibrating string or an air column—supports standing waves, it does so at a discrete set of frequencies. The lowest such frequency is the fundamental (first harmonic), $f_1$. The harmonics are all integer multiples of the fundamental:

$$ f_n = n f_1, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

The overtones are the harmonics above the fundamental, indexed by their order above it: the first overtone is $f_2$, the second overtone is $f_3$, and so on. For an ideal string fixed at both ends or an open pipe, every harmonic is present. For a closed pipe (open at one end, closed at the other), only odd harmonics are supported: $f_n = n f_1$ with $n = 1, 3, 5, \ldots$

Worked Example. A guitar string of length $L = 0.65\ \text{m}$ is fixed at both ends. The wave speed on the string is $v = 520\ \text{m/s}$. The condition for standing waves requires $L = n\lambda/2$, giving $\lambda_n = 2L/n$. The harmonic frequencies are therefore:

$$ f_n = \frac{v}{\lambda_n} = \frac{nv}{2L} $$

For $n = 1$: $\displaystyle f_1 = \frac{(1)(520)}{2(0.65)} = 400\ \text{Hz}$ (fundamental).

For $n = 2$: $f_2 = 800\ \text{Hz}$ (first overtone, second harmonic).

For $n = 3$: $f_3 = 1200\ \text{Hz}$ (second overtone, third harmonic).

The relative amplitudes of each harmonic in an actual plucked string depend on where and how the string is excited, giving each instrument its characteristic timbre.

Key Theorem (Superposition of Normal Modes). Any periodic vibration of a linear system can be expressed as a sum of its normal modes (harmonics) with appropriate amplitudes $A_n$ and phases $\phi_n$:

$$ y(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\cos(2\pi f_n t + \phi_n) $$

This result is a consequence of Fourier's theorem applied to linear wave equations. It establishes that timbre—the perceptual quality introduced above—is determined by the set of harmonic amplitudes $\{A_n\}$, and it underlies both spectral analysis and the physics of musical acoustics.

Lab Exercise. Using a sonometer (monochord), set the string length to $L = 0.80\ \text{m}$ and adjust tension until $f_1 = 250\ \text{Hz}$. Lightly touch the string at its midpoint and pluck it to isolate the second harmonic. Measure $f_2$ with a frequency analyzer and verify that $f_2 = 2f_1 = 500\ \text{Hz}$. Repeat by touching at $L/3$ to excite $f_3$, and tabulate your results against the theoretical predictions.
