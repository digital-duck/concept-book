🌱 Wavelength

🌱 Wavelength

Definition. The wavelength of a periodic wave is the spatial distance over which the wave's shape repeats exactly once. For a sinusoidal wave propagating in one dimension, the displacement field takes the form

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{\lambda} x - \omega t + \phi_0\right), $$

where $A$ is the amplitude, $\omega$ is the angular frequency, $\phi_0$ is the initial phase, and $\lambda$ (the Greek letter lambda) denotes the wavelength. Equivalently, $\lambda$ is the distance between any two successive points that are in phase — for instance, crest to crest or trough to trough. The SI unit of wavelength is the metre (m).

The wavelength is related to wave speed $v$ and frequency $f$ by the fundamental wave relation:

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Since $f = 1/T$ where $T$ is the period, this can also be written $\lambda = vT$.

Worked Example. A sound wave in air at $20\,^\circ\text{C}$ has a frequency of $440\,\text{Hz}$ (concert A). The speed of sound in air at this temperature is approximately $v = 343\,\text{m/s}$. Find the wavelength.

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343\,\text{m/s}}{440\,\text{Hz}} = \frac{343}{440}\,\text{m} \approx 0.780\,\text{m}. $$

This result places the wavelength on the order of a typical human arm-span, which helps explain why sound diffracts readily around ordinary objects.

Key Theorem (Non-Dispersive Wave Relation). In a non-dispersive medium, the wave speed $v$ is independent of frequency. Consequently, $\lambda \propto 1/f$: doubling the frequency halves the wavelength, and halving the frequency doubles it. This inverse proportionality is a direct consequence of $v = f\lambda$ with $v$ held constant. It means that high-pitched sounds (large $f$) have short wavelengths, while low-pitched sounds (small $f$) have long wavelengths — a fact with direct implications for how sound interacts with objects and apertures of different physical sizes.

Lab Exercise. Using a ripple tank or simulation, generate surface waves at two frequencies $f_1 = 5\,\text{Hz}$ and $f_2 = 10\,\text{Hz}$. Measure the corresponding wavelengths $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ directly from the wave pattern. Compute the ratio $\lambda_1/\lambda_2$ and compare it to the theoretical prediction from $v = f\lambda$, assuming the wave speed remains constant. Discuss any deviation and propose a physical explanation.
