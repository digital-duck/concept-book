🍃 Resonance Closed Tube

🍃 Resonance Closed Tube

Definition. A closed tube (closed at one end, open at the other) supports standing sound waves when its length satisfies specific resonance conditions. Because the closed end imposes a displacement node (zero air motion) and the open end imposes a displacement antinode (maximum air motion), only an odd number of quarter-wavelengths fits within the tube length $L$. The resonance condition is therefore

$$ L = \frac{(2n-1)\lambda}{4}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength and $n$ labels the harmonic. Rearranging, the resonant frequencies are

$$ f_n = \frac{(2n-1)v}{4L}, $$

where $v$ is the speed of sound in air. Only odd harmonics are present: $f_1, 3f_1, 5f_1, \ldots$ The lowest resonant frequency $f_1 = v/(4L)$ is called the fundamental.

Worked Example. A clarinet approximates a closed tube of effective length $L = 0.600\ \text{m}$. Taking the speed of sound at $20^\circ\text{C}$ to be $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$, find the fundamental frequency and the next two resonant frequencies.

$$ f_1 = \frac{v}{4L} = \frac{343}{4(0.600)} = \frac{343}{2.400} \approx 143\ \text{Hz} $$ $$ f_3 = 3f_1 \approx 429\ \text{Hz}, \qquad f_5 = 5f_1 \approx 715\ \text{Hz}. $$

Even harmonics ($2f_1, 4f_1, \ldots$) are absent. This restriction to odd harmonics gives closed-tube instruments their characteristically hollow timbre compared with open tubes, which support all harmonics.

Key Theorem. For a closed tube of length $L$, the resonant spectrum contains only odd multiples of the fundamental: $\{f_n\} = \{(2n-1)f_1 \mid n \in \mathbb{Z}^+\}$. This follows directly from the boundary conditions: the displacement must simultaneously vanish at $x = 0$ (closed end) and be extremal at $x = L$ (open end), admitting only solutions of the form $\sin\!\bigl(\frac{(2n-1)\pi x}{2L}\bigr)$.

Lab Exercise. Fill a tall graduated cylinder with water and hold a vibrating tuning fork ($f = 512\ \text{Hz}$) over the opening. Slowly lower the water level until the first resonance is heard. Measure the air-column length $L$ and verify that $L \approx v/(4f)$. Repeat to locate the second resonance at $L' \approx 3v/(4f)$ and confirm the $3\!:\!1$ ratio between the two resonant lengths.
