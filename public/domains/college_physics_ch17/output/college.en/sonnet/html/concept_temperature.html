🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar thermodynamic state variable that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. For an ideal monatomic gas, the relationship between absolute temperature $T$ and mean translational kinetic energy $\langle KE \rangle$ per molecule is given by the equipartition theorem:

$$ \langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2} k_B T $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{J K}^{-1}$ is the Boltzmann constant. Temperature is measured on the absolute (Kelvin) scale, defined so that $T = 0\ \text{K}$ corresponds to the theoretical minimum energy state (absolute zero). Conversion from Celsius is given by $T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273.15$.

Worked Example. A sample of nitrogen gas ($\text{N}_2$) is held at room temperature, $T = 25\ °\text{C} = 298.15\ \text{K}$. Determine the root-mean-square (rms) speed of a nitrogen molecule, given its molar mass $M = 28.0\ \text{g mol}^{-1} = 0.0280\ \text{kg mol}^{-1}$.

The rms speed is derived from the equipartition result applied to translational kinetic energy:

$$ \frac{1}{2} m v_{\text{rms}}^2 = \frac{3}{2} k_B T \implies v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_B T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}} $$

where $R = N_A k_B = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant. Substituting:

$$ v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3(8.314)(298.15)}{0.0280}} = \sqrt{265{,}450}\ \approx\ 515\ \text{m s}^{-1} $$

This result illustrates that molecular speeds at ambient temperature are on the order of hundreds of meters per second.

Key Theorem (Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics). If system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $B$, and system $B$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then systems $A$ and $C$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This transitivity property establishes temperature as a well-defined, measurable quantity and underpins the logical consistency of thermometry.

Lab Exercise. Using a constant-volume gas thermometer containing $n = 0.010\ \text{mol}$ of an ideal gas, you record pressures of $P_1 = 75.0\ \text{kPa}$ at the ice point and $P_2 = 102.4\ \text{kPa}$ at an unknown temperature $T_x$. Apply the ideal gas law at constant volume, $P \propto T$, to determine $T_x$ in kelvin and convert to Celsius. Verify your result by checking whether $T_x/T_1 = P_2/P_1$ holds to three significant figures.
