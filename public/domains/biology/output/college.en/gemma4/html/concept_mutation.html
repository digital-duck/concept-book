🍃 Mutation

🍃 Mutation

Mutation refers to any permanent change in the nucleotide sequence of an organism's genome, whether occurring in DNA or RNA. These alterations represent the ultimate source of all genetic variation, driving evolution. Mutations can be classified by their scale: point mutations (single base pair changes), small insertions or deletions ($\text{indels}$), or large chromosomal rearrangements (translocations, duplications). At a molecular level, these changes arise primarily due to errors during DNA replication (e.g., polymerase slippage) or through exposure to environmental mutagens (such as UV radiation or chemical agents). The resulting change in the genetic code can range from being neutral (having no effect on fitness) to deleterious (reducing fitness) or beneficial (increasing fitness), fundamentally altering the organism's phenotype and genotype.

Consider a worked example involving a single point mutation, specifically a missense mutation. Suppose a gene segment contains the following codon sequence: $\text{GCT}-\text{TTA}-\text{CAA}$. The amino acids encoded are Glycine-Leucine-Glutamine. If the second nucleotide of the third codon undergoes a transversion from Adenine ($\text{A}$) to Guanine ($\text{G}$), the mutation changes the sequence to $\text{GCT}-\text{TTA}-\text{CGA}$. This single base pair change results in a shift from Glutamine (Q) to Arginine (R). Because this substitution alters the resulting amino acid, it is classified as a missense mutation and may significantly change the protein's function or structure.
