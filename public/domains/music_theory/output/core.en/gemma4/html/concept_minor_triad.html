🍃 Minor Triad

When analyzing harmony in music theory, we often encounter chords that evoke a specific emotional quality—a feeling of melancholy or introspection. The minor triad ($\text{m}$) is fundamental to achieving this sound. Understanding its precise structure allows us to predict and analyze harmonic movement with confidence. It moves beyond simply knowing notes; it provides the structural blueprint for an entire tonality's mood.

Definition: A minor triad is a three-note chord built by stacking intervals of a major second (whole step) on top of a root, followed by a minor third interval. If $R$ is the root note and $M_3$ is the minor third, the structure is defined as:

$$ \text{Minor Triad} = \{ R, \text{minor\_third}, \text{perfect\_fifth} \} $$

In terms of semitones (semitone distance from the root): $\{ 0, 3 \text{ semitones}, 7 \text{ semitones} \}$.

Worked Example: Let's construct a minor triad starting on F. The root is $F$. The minor third interval above $F$ is $A\flat$, which is 3 semitones up ($F \to E \to F\# \to G \to A\flat$). The perfect fifth above $F$ is $C$. Thus, the notes forming an $\text{F}$ minor triad are $\{ F, A\flat, C\}$.

Why it's true (Justification): Unlike a major triad, which contains two whole steps and one whole step interval ($\{0, 4, 7\}$ semitones), the defining characteristic of the minor triad is the presence of the minor third. This reduction from $4$ semitones to $3$ semitones (the distance between the root and the third) fundamentally alters the harmonic tension, creating a distinct, darker quality that defines the entire mode or key center.
