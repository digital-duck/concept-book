🍃 Major Scale

Have you ever noticed that certain keys sound inherently "bright" or stable? This feeling of resolution and cheerfulness is rooted in one of music's most fundamental structures: the major scale. Understanding this pattern allows us to predict and understand the harmonic bedrock of Western music, providing both a map and a formula for constructing melodies and chords.

The major scale is defined by a specific sequence of intervals that relate successive notes. In terms of semitones (half steps) and whole tones (whole steps), the structure must adhere to the pattern: Whole-Whole-Half-Whole-Whole-Whole-Half. If we denote this using interval notation, the formula is $\text{W} - \text{W} - \text{H} - \text{W} - \text{W} - \text{W} - \text{H}$.

To apply this definition, let's work through the key of C Major. Starting on C, we move:

  1. C $\rightarrow$ D (Whole step)
  2. D $\rightarrow$ E (Whole step)
  3. E $\rightarrow$ F (Half step)
  4. F $\rightarrow$ G (Whole step)
  5. G $\rightarrow$ A (Whole step)
  6. A $\rightarrow$ B (Whole step)
  7. B $\rightarrow$ C (Half step, octave) The resulting notes are $\{C, D, E, F, G, A, B\}$, confirming the robust pattern of whole and half steps that defines this tonal center.
