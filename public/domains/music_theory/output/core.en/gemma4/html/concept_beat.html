🌱 Beat

Have you ever observed two musical instruments played simultaneously, creating a faint, rhythmic pulsing in the combined sound? This phenomenon, often heard when tuning an instrument or plucking nearby strings, is called beating. It is one of the most audible examples of wave interference and provides a powerful physical method for accurately comparing frequencies. Understanding beats helps us move beyond simply observing sound to quantifying the subtle interactions between waves.

Definition: Beating refers to the periodic variation in the loudness of a sound or vibration that results from the superposition (the combination) of two or more sound waves with slightly different frequencies. The beat frequency ($f_{beat}$) is defined as the absolute difference between the individual frequencies of the sources involved. Mathematically, if $f_1$ and $f_2$ are the frequencies of the two sources, the beat frequency is given by:

$$ f_{\text{beat}} = |f_1 - f_2| $$

The frequency $f_{beat}$ represents how many times per second the loudness peaks (or dips) as the waves constructively and destructively interfere.

Worked Example: Suppose a physicist generates two sound waves using transducers: Source A vibrates at $50 \text{ Hz}$, and Source B vibrates slightly slower, at $48 \text{ Hz}$. To find the beat frequency, we apply the definition:

$$ f_{\text{beat}} = |50 \text{ Hz} - 48 \text{ Hz}| = 2 \text{ Hz} $$

This means that the combined sound will fluctuate in volume (getting louder and softer) exactly 2 times every second.
