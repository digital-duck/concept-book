🍃 Perfect Fifth

When composing music or analyzing intervals, certain ratios sound exceptionally stable and consonant. One of the most foundational and defining intervals in Western harmony is the perfect fifth ($\text{P}5$). It gives us a sense of structural completeness—like two elements fitting together perfectly to create a solid foundation. From Renaissance polyphony to modern jazz chords, understanding the $\text{P}5$ is key to grasping harmonic relationships.

Definition: The perfect fifth is an interval spanning exactly seven semitones (or $7/12$ of an octave) and has a precise frequency ratio of $3:2$. Because its ratio is derived from the simplest integer ratios found in nature (the relationship between the fundamental frequency and the third harmonic), it is considered mathematically pure and inherently stable.

Worked Example: Consider starting on Middle C ($C_4$). The note that forms a perfect fifth above it is G ($G_4$). We can confirm this by counting semitones: $C \to C\# \to D \to D\# \to E \to F \to F\# \to G$. This represents exactly seven steps, corresponding to the ratio $3:2$.

Why It's True (Justification): The stability of the $\text{P}5$ is rooted in harmonic physics. When a string vibrates at frequency $f$, its overtones (harmonics) appear at integer multiples of $f$: $1f, 2f, 3f, 4f, \dots$. If we pluck a note and listen for the second overtone ($2f$), that tone will sound exactly an octave above the fundamental. However, if we listen for the third overtone ($3f$), that tone corresponds precisely to the $\text{P}5$ ratio (since $3f / 1f = 3/1$; and dividing by $2f$ gives $3/2$). The appearance of this simple $3:2$ relationship in the natural overtones is what makes the perfect fifth so universally consonant.
