🍃 Melody

Have you ever listened to a piece of music that instantly evokes strong feelings, or perhaps remembered a specific moment? That emotional core—the memorable line that makes you hum along—is what we call melody. While it feels purely artistic, the structure underlying a compelling tune is surprisingly mathematical. A melody isn't just random notes; it is an organized sequence of pitches and durations over time, forming a cohesive narrative for the ear to follow.

Definition: Formally, a melody is a horizontal succession of musical tones. It can be defined as a function $M(t) = \langle p_i, d_i \rangle_{i=1}^N$, where $p_i$ is the pitch (frequency) at step $i$, and $d_i$ is the duration of that pitch. The overall melodic contour is determined by the interplay between these discrete pitches and their temporal relationship.

Worked Example: Consider a simple, ascending three-note motif: C4 $\rightarrow$ E4 $\rightarrow$ G4 (assuming quarter note durations). We can represent this melody $M_{example}$ using pitch indices relative to an octave base ($f_0$). If C4 is pitch 1 and the interval between pitches is measured in semitones, the sequence of pitches might be:

$$ M_{example} = \langle p_1=1, d_1; p_2=3, d_2; p_3=5, d_3 \rangle $$

The overall melodic shape (contour) increases monotonically across these three steps.

Why it's true: The perceived 'singing-ness' of a melody comes from its controlled variation in pitch and rhythm. If the sequence of pitches $p_i$ changes too rapidly or unpredictably, the brain struggles to map a coherent contour. Effective melodies often exhibit predictable patterns (like arpeggiation or step-wise movement) punctuated by specific leaps that define its character. The mathematical constraints ensure that the resulting signal is perceived as structured, rather than chaotic noise.

Practice Problem: Given a sequence of pitches $P = \langle 4, 7, 5 \rangle$ and corresponding durations $D = \langle 1/2, 1/2, 1 \rangle$. Sketch the melodic contour on a staff. Does this melody exhibit a net upward or downward movement in pitch?
