🍃 Octave

When you sing a note and then hear that same melody sung by your parent—the higher version—you have experienced an octave. This phenomenon is fundamental not just to music theory, but to understanding how physical waves scale in frequency. The concept of the octave represents the simplest and most powerful interval relationship: doubling the pitch.

Formally, an octave defines a specific frequency ratio. If we start with a base frequency $f$, the corresponding note one octave higher has a frequency exactly twice that amount, $2f$. Conversely, the note one octave lower is found at $\frac{1}{2}f$. This relationship means that octaves are defined multiplicatively; they represent powers of two in terms of frequency ratios. Since pitch perception is logarithmic, this ratio appears as a simple doubling on a linear scale.

Consider the common reference frequency $A_4$, tuned to 440 Hz. If we move up one octave from $A_4$ to $A'_5$, we are not simply adding some arbitrary number; we are multiplying by two. The resulting frequency is $A'_5 = 2 \times 440 \, \text{Hz} = 880 \, \text{Hz}$. This consistency allows scientists and engineers to predict the frequencies of nearly any related pitch across an entire scale structure.

The reason this ratio must be exactly two stems from the physics of wave propagation. For a given medium (like air) and constant speed $v$, frequency ($f$) and wavelength ($\lambda$) are linked by $v = f\lambda$. When you double the frequency, $f' = 2f$, the wavelength must also precisely double, $\lambda' = 2\lambda$, to maintain the same wave speed. This stable physical relationship is what grounds the musical definition of the octave in observable physics.
