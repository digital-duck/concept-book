🍃 Dominant Seventh

🍃 Dominant Seventh

Have you ever heard a chord that sounds like it must resolve? That unmistakable musical pull toward stability is often the work of the dominant seventh chord ($\text{V}^7$). Historically, this chord has been crucial in Western harmony because it contains an inherent tension—a built-in desire to move somewhere else. Understanding how this specific combination of notes creates that powerful gravitational pull is key to mastering harmonic movement and writing convincingly functional music.

The $\text{V}^7$ is a four-note extension derived from the dominant triad ($\text{V}$). If we denote the root as $R$, the chord structure consists of four intervals: the Root, Major Third ($\text{M}3$), Perfect Fifth ($\text{P}5$), and Minor Seventh ($\text{m}7$). Its general formula is $\{R, \text{M}3, \text{P}5, \text{m}7\}$.

Consider $\text{C}$ Major. The dominant chord (the fifth scale degree) is $\text{G}$. Therefore, the dominant seventh chord is $\text{G}^7$. Its notes are $\text{G}$ (Root), $\text{B}$ ($\text{M}3$), $\text{D}$ ($\text{P}5$), and $\text{F}$ ($\text{m}7$). This sequence of tones—$\text{G}-\text{B}-\text{D}-\text{F}$—is the blueprint for countless progressions, providing a strong harmonic anchor point that typically resolves upward or downward to the tonic chord ($\text{I}$).
