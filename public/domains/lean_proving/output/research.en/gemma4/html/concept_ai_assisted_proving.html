🍃 AI Assisted Proving

Definition: An AI-assisted proof system, $\mathcal{A}$, is a computational framework designed to augment human mathematical reasoning by automating or suggesting components of a formal derivation. Given an asserted statement $P$ and an existing set of axioms $\Gamma$, $\mathcal{A}$ operates within a formalized logical environment (e.g., Coq, Lean) to generate candidate proofs $\pi'$, which are then subjected to verification against the underlying proof kernel. The fundamental distinction from fully automated theorem proving is that $\mathcal{A}$ manages the search space and suggests high-level structural arguments rather than completing low-level logical steps autonomously. Formally, $\mathcal{A}$ utilizes a guided inference primitive, allowing it to prioritize candidate lemmas based not merely on syntactic completeness but also on observed semantic proximity to the target goal $P$, thereby focusing the proof search within relevant branches of the deduction space.

Theorem: If a mathematical statement $P$ is provable within a formal system $\mathcal{L}$, then an AI-assisted proof system $\mathcal{A}$ can generate a sequence of suggested intermediate lemmas $\{l_1, l_2, \dots, l_k\}$ such that the concatenation of these lemmas and the original axioms $\Gamma$ yields a verifiable derivation $\pi: \Gamma \cup \{l_i\} \vdash P$. Furthermore, if $\mathcal{L}$ is sound and complete regarding $P$, then $\mathcal{A}$, under sufficient computational resources, will converge to this sequence.

Proof: Let $P$ be provable in $\mathcal{L}$. By Tarski's Undefinability Theorem, the set of statements provable in $\mathcal{L}$ is recursively enumerable. The efficiency of $\mathcal{A}$ relies on leveraging machine learning models (e.g., large language models trained on proof structures) to navigate this search space. Specifically, $\mathcal{A}$ frames $P$ as a goal state and iteratively proposes intermediate goals (lemmas). Each proposed lemma $l_i$ is then checked for provability using standard decision procedures or specialized automated provers. The incorporation of the guided inference primitive allows $\mathcal{A}$ to prune irrelevant branches by assessing semantic connections between $l_i$ and the remaining proof goal, significantly reducing the combinatorial complexity inherent in general proof search. Since $P$ is provable, this guided process guarantees that $\mathcal{A}$ will eventually guide the user to a sequence of verifiable steps culminating in the derivation $\pi$.

Remark: The effectiveness of $\mathcal{A}$ critically depends on its ability to manage the 'insight gap'—the human capacity for non-obvious structural connections. While modern systems excel at checking syntactical validity and managing complex dependencies (e.g., handling type theory constraints in Lean), they currently struggle with generating fundamentally novel axioms or radically restructuring existing mathematical fields without explicit prompting or directional guidance from a researcher. The necessity of the guided inference primitive underscores that $\mathcal{A}$ functions best as an interactive scaffold, transforming the proof search into a computationally manageable process rather than acting as an autonomous oracle for foundational mathematics.
