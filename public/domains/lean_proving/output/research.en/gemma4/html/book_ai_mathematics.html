🌱 Proof

Definition. A formal proof $\mathcal{P}$ of a proposition $Q$ within a given axiomatic system $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{L})$ is a finite sequence of well-formed formulas (WFFs) $\{F_1, F_2, \ldots, F_k\}$ such that each formula $F_i$ is either an axiom drawn from $\mathcal{A}$, or is derivable from preceding formulas by the application of one or more established inference rules (e.g., Modus Ponens, Generalization). The sequence must conclude with $Q$, and every step in the derivation must be logically justified by the underlying rules of deduction, thereby establishing that $\mathcal{A} \vdash Q$.

Theorem. If a proposition $Q$ is provable within a consistent axiomatic system $\mathcal{A}$, then $Q$ is true (relative to the intended model structure). Formally, consistency implies that $\text{Prov}_{\mathcal{A}}(Q) \implies Q$, where $\text{Prov}_{\mathcal{A}}$ denotes derivability in $\mathcal{A}$.

Proof. The core mechanism establishing this theorem relies on the assumption of consistency. If an axiomatic system $\mathcal{A}$ is consistent, it means that $A \operatorname{land} \operatorname{neg} A$ is not provable for any statement $A$. Gödel's Completeness Theorem (as generalized by Hilbert and Birkhoff) provides the necessary link: for sufficiently expressive formal systems (e.g., first-order logic), derivability ($\vdash$) is equivalent to logical truth ($\models$). If we assume $\mathcal{A} \vdash Q$, this means $Q$ can be derived purely structurally from the axioms. Because the system is consistent, any derivation path that leads to a contradiction must fail; therefore, the resulting proposition $Q$ must hold universally within the intended semantics of the model structure $\mathfrak{M}$.

Remark. The concept of 'proof' highlights the distinction between validity (truth in all models) and provability (derivable within a specific formal system). Systems capable of formalizing arithmetic are subject to limitations, as demonstrated by Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems. These theorems show that if $\mathcal{A}$ is sufficiently powerful ($\text{e.g.}, \mathcal{A} = \text{PA}$) and consistent, there exist true propositions $Q$ such that $\mathcal{A} \not\vdash Q$, thereby necessitating the consideration of meta-mathematical techniques or extensions to address their truth status.

🍃 Theorem Statements

The concept of a theorem statement requires defining its structural components relative to underlying axiomatic systems. Formally, let $\mathcal{S}$ be a structured language (e.g., first-order logic $L$), and let $\Sigma$ be a set of axioms within $\mathcal{S}$. A theorem statement is an assertion $\phi \in L$ that claims logical consequence from the axiom set $\Sigma$.

Definition 2.1 (Theorem Statement): Given an axiomatic system $(\Sigma, \mathcal{S})$, a sentence $\phi$ is deemed a theorem if and only if $\Sigma \models \phi$, meaning that $\phi$ must be true under every model $\mathcal{M}$ that satisfies all axioms in $\Sigma$. The set of all such sentences constitutes the theory $T = \{\phi \in L \mid \Sigma \models \phi\}$.

Theorem 2.2 (Completeness and Consistency): An axiomatic system $(\Sigma, \mathcal{S})$ is consistent if and only if it is incapable of proving both a sentence $\phi$ and its negation $\operatorname{neg}\phi$. Furthermore, for formal systems satisfying certain computability constraints (e.g., first-order logic), the set $T$ forms a recursively enumerable theory.

Proof Sketch: The consistency condition ($\Sigma \not\models \text{False}$) is crucial because it ensures that the underlying semantics do not collapse to triviality. The equivalence between $\Sigma \models \phi$ and provability ($\Sigma \vdash \phi$) in standard formal systems (like Hilbert-style calculi) is established by Gödel's Completeness Theorem, which guarantees that every logically valid statement is derivable from the axioms.

Remark: The study of theorem statements often intersects with model theory, specifically concerning the relationship between syntactic provability ($\Sigma \vdash \phi$) and semantic truth ($\Sigma \models \phi$). Theories where this equivalence fails or requires added constraints (like in incompleteness theorems) are central areas of advanced research. For instance, arithmetic theories require careful consideration of their consistency status relative to stronger axioms.

For any two tactic modes $M_A$ and $M_B$, if $M_A \vdash M_B$ denotes a valid transition from $M_A$ to $M_B$, then the computational completeness of the subsystem defined by $M_B$ must be strictly greater than or equal to that of $M_A$: $\text{Comp}(M_B) \ge \text{Comp}(M_A)$. Proof Sketch. The computational completeness $\text{Comp}(M)$ measures the expressive power required to deduce all consequences within mode $M$. If $\text{Comp}(M_B) < \text{Comp}(M_A)$, there must exist a theorem provable in $M_A$ but unprovable in $M_B$, contradicting the assumption of a valid transition within an extended proof context (see [Hyland & Pitts, 1987] for related concepts in type theory). The uniqueness of $\{M_{min}\}$ relies on demonstrating that any mode not essential for maintaining $\text{Comp}$ relative to its neighbors introduces unnecessary computational overhead without increasing the overall deductive power.

Remark. This concept formalizes and generalizes the notion of restricted inference systems found in formal logic and proof mining. In functional settings, $M$ can be interpreted as a context-sensitive refinement of the underlying type system, effectively constraining the allowed functors or constructors to ensure properties such as termination or specific structural guarantees (e.g., transitioning from general lambda calculus deduction to linear logic deduction). The study of mode transitions, especially in relation to enabling primitives $\mathcal{E}$, is crucial for developing modular and highly optimized interactive theorem provers capable of managing computational resources precisely.

Remark. 🍃 Inductive Types

Inductive types provide the fundamental mechanism in type theory for defining complex algebraic data structures. Formally, an inductive type $A$ is defined by specifying a set of constructors $\{C_i\}$ that dictate how elements are synthesized from arguments drawn from previously defined types (the sorts). If $A$ is defined by a collection of constructors $\vec{C}: T_{1} \rightarrow \dots \rightarrow T_{n} \rightarrow A$, then any element $a : A$ must be constructed via the application of these designated constructors to arguments. This structural constraint ensures that every value possesses a unique decomposition relative to its generating basis types, establishing a canonical representation for elements of $A$.

The central result concerning inductive definitions is the Principle of Structural Recursion (or Induction): For any type $A$ defined by $\{C_i\}$, and given an arbitrary set of mapping functions $\{f_i: \text{Domain}(C_i) \rightarrow B\}$ into a target type $B$, there exists a unique total function $r: A \rightarrow B$. This map $r$ is characterized by the property that for any element constructed as $a := C(x_1, \dots, x_n)$, its value must be computed by applying the corresponding function $f$ to the components: $r(a) = f(x_1, \dots, x_n)$.

The proof of this principle relies critically on the inherent well-foundedness induced by the construction method. Since every element $a : A$ is built from base types through a finite sequence of constructor applications, the structural dependency graph formed by the elements of $A$ must be a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). The unique existence and definition of $r$ are established via transfinite induction over the structure of $A$, ensuring that the function definition proceeds correctly for all possible paths without encountering circular dependencies. This guarantees both type safety and termination for any computation defined by such recursion.

Significantly, inductive types provide a constructive model corresponding precisely to initial algebra semantics. They admit canonical models where equality is decidable (i.e., $a = b$ if and only if their unique structural decomposition matches), which is crucial for formal verification in proof assistants. A major generalization of this concept involves co-inductive types, which define structures not through construction but via observation (or initial coalgebra semantics). These are typically applied to modeling potentially infinite processes or reactive systems that require techniques such as greatest fixed points rather than least fixed points.

By construction, any deviation from $\mathcal{T}^*$ (i.e., using $\mathcal{T}'$) means the system has a better recovery path available. Since the set of tactics is finite and the state transitions are Markovian (or can be modeled as such for bounded failure regions), repeated application of non-optimal tactics will lead to either an infinite loop or convergence to a suboptimal state, violating the minimum utility constraint $U_{min}$ over time. Therefore, the optimal tactic must satisfy the condition defined by the minimization objective across all viable actions in $\mathcal{T}_{set}$.

Remark (Generalization and Connections) The concept of automation tactics generalizes classical concepts from robust control theory, particularly Model Predictive Control (MPC), where the "tactic selection" acts as a meta-optimization layer that selects an appropriate cost function or constraint set for the underlying MPC instance. Furthermore, in reinforcement learning contexts, $\mathcal{T}^*$ can be viewed as selecting the optimal high-level policy transition within a hierarchical RL framework, significantly reducing the effective search space compared to pure end-to-end learning.

🍃 Formalization Gap

🍃 Formalization Gap

Definition. Let $\mathcal{A}$ be an informal domain of human knowledge or practice (e.g., ethical reasoning, common sense, intuition). A formalization gap exists when $\mathcal{A}$ cannot be fully mapped to a sufficiently expressive and tractable Formal System $\mathcal{F}$, such that the truth value $\text{Truth}_{\mathcal{A}}(P)$ for an assertion $P \in \mathcal{A}$ does not uniquely correspond to the provability or semantic truth value $\text{Truth}_{\mathcal{F}}(P)$ within $\mathcal{F}$. This gap manifests as a failure of fidelity, meaning there exists at least one instance where $\mathcal{A} \not\equiv \mathcal{F}$, regardless of the chosen axioms for $\mathcal{F}$ (see related work on incompleteness in semantics).

Theorem. If an informal domain $\mathcal{A}$ exhibits non-monotonic reasoning or context-dependent truth assignment, then any attempt to model $\mathcal{A}$ using a standard first-order logic ($\mathcal{L}_1$) will necessarily encounter a formalization gap that cannot be bridged solely by expanding the axiomatization of $\mathcal{L}_1$. Specifically, $\text{Gap}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{L}_1) \neq \emptyset$ if $\mathcal{A}$ requires mechanisms beyond standard satisfiability semantics.

Proof Sketch. The failure stems from the inherent limitations of classical logic in capturing defeasible knowledge and implicit context. Consider a canonical example: common sense entailment, where $\text{P}(x)$ implies $\text{Q}(x)$, but this implication $P \implies Q$ only holds under specific environmental constraints $E$. Because first-order languages require implications to be globally defined (i.e., $P \models Q$), they cannot naturally encode the scope restriction $\{P \Rightarrow_{E} Q\}$. To formalize such a constraint requires moving beyond $\mathcal{L}_1$, typically necessitating modal logics, non-monotonic logics (such as default logic), or circumscription, which fundamentally alters the semantics and computational complexity of the system. Therefore, the gap is structurally necessary unless the initial domain $\mathcal{A}$ is voluntarily restricted to be monotonic and context-independent.

Remark. The identification, rather than elimination, of this formalization gap is often viewed as a critical step in AI theory (e.g., McCarthy’s critique of computational realism). While some research attempts to close the gap through highly expressive logics or probabilistic frameworks (e.g., Bayesian Networks), these methods often trade completeness for tractability. The persistence of the gap suggests that capturing human reasoning may require conceptual shifts rather than merely syntactic extensions, pointing toward a potential need for higher-order systems or meta-level representations beyond current standard formalisms.

🍃 Ai Assisted Proving

🍃 AI Assisted Proving

Definition: An AI-assisted proof system, $\mathcal{A}$, is a computational framework designed to augment human mathematical reasoning by automating or suggesting components of a formal derivation. Given an asserted statement $P$ and an existing set of axioms $\Gamma$, $\mathcal{A}$ operates within a formalized logical environment (e.g., Coq, Lean) to generate candidate proofs $\pi'$, which are then subjected to verification against the underlying proof kernel. The fundamental distinction from fully automated theorem proving is that $\mathcal{A}$ manages the search space and suggests high-level structural arguments rather than completing low-level logical steps autonomously. Formally, $\mathcal{A}$ utilizes a guided inference primitive, allowing it to prioritize candidate lemmas based not merely on syntactic completeness but also on observed semantic proximity to the target goal $P$, thereby focusing the proof search within relevant branches of the deduction space.

Theorem: If a mathematical statement $P$ is provable within a formal system $\mathcal{L}$, then an AI-assisted proof system $\mathcal{A}$ can generate a sequence of suggested intermediate lemmas $\{l_1, l_2, \dots, l_k\}$ such that the concatenation of these lemmas and the original axioms $\Gamma$ yields a verifiable derivation $\pi: \Gamma \cup \{l_i\} \vdash P$. Furthermore, if $\mathcal{L}$ is sound and complete regarding $P$, then $\mathcal{A}$, under sufficient computational resources, will converge to this sequence.

Proof: Let $P$ be provable in $\mathcal{L}$. By Tarski's Undefinability Theorem, the set of statements provable in $\mathcal{L}$ is recursively enumerable. The efficiency of $\mathcal{A}$ relies on leveraging machine learning models (e.g., large language models trained on proof structures) to navigate this search space. Specifically, $\mathcal{A}$ frames $P$ as a goal state and iteratively proposes intermediate goals (lemmas). Each proposed lemma $l_i$ is then checked for provability using standard decision procedures or specialized automated provers. The incorporation of the guided inference primitive allows $\mathcal{A}$ to prune irrelevant branches by assessing semantic connections between $l_i$ and the remaining proof goal, significantly reducing the combinatorial complexity inherent in general proof search. Since $P$ is provable, this guided process guarantees that $\mathcal{A}$ will eventually guide the user to a sequence of verifiable steps culminating in the derivation $\pi$.

Remark: The effectiveness of $\mathcal{A}$ critically depends on its ability to manage the 'insight gap'—the human capacity for non-obvious structural connections. While modern systems excel at checking syntactical validity and managing complex dependencies (e.g., handling type theory constraints in Lean), they currently struggle with generating fundamentally novel axioms or radically restructuring existing mathematical fields without explicit prompting or directional guidance from a researcher. The necessity of the guided inference primitive underscores that $\mathcal{A}$ functions best as an interactive scaffold, transforming the proof search into a computationally manageable process rather than acting as an autonomous oracle for foundational mathematics.
