🌱 Voltage

Definition. Voltage, or electric potential difference, between two points A and B in an electric field is defined as the work done per unit positive charge by an external agent moving a test charge from A to B against the electric force:

$$ V_{AB} = V_A - V_B = \frac{W_{A \to B}}{q} $$

where $W_{A\to B}$ is the work done by the external agent and $q$ is the magnitude of the test charge. The SI unit of voltage is the volt (V), where $1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C}$. Equivalently, the electric potential at a point P due to a point charge $Q$ at distance $r$ is:

$$ V = \frac{kQ}{r} $$

where $k = 8.99 \times 10^9\,\text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant. Voltage is a scalar quantity, making superposition straightforward: the total potential at a point due to multiple charges is the algebraic sum of individual contributions.

Worked Example. Two point charges, $Q_1 = +3.0\,\mu\text{C}$ and $Q_2 = -1.5\,\mu\text{C}$, are fixed at positions $x = 0$ and $x = 4.0\,\text{cm}$, respectively. Find the electric potential at the midpoint $x = 2.0\,\text{cm}$.

The distance from each charge to the midpoint is $r = 0.020\,\text{m}$.

$$ V_1 = \frac{kQ_1}{r} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(3.0\times10^{-6})}{0.020} = +1.35\times10^6\,\text{V} $$ $$ V_2 = \frac{kQ_2}{r} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(-1.5\times10^{-6})}{0.020} = -6.74\times10^5\,\text{V} $$ $$ V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 = 1.35\times10^6 - 6.74\times10^5 \approx +6.8\times10^5\,\text{V} $$

Key Theorem. The relationship between electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and potential $V$ in one dimension is:

$$ E_x = -\frac{dV}{dx} $$

This states that the electric field points in the direction of decreasing potential and has magnitude equal to the spatial rate of change of potential. Equipotential surfaces are always perpendicular to field lines, and no work is done moving a charge along an equipotential.

Lab Exercise. Using a voltmeter, measure the potential difference between three pairs of points surrounding a dipole charge configuration on a conducting paper sheet. Map at least five equipotential lines, then sketch the corresponding electric field lines. Verify that the field lines intersect your equipotentials at right angles and estimate the electric field magnitude between adjacent equipotentials using $E \approx -\Delta V / \Delta x$.
