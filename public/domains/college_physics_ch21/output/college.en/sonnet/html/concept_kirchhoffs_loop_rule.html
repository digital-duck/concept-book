🍃 Kirchhoffs Loop Rule

🍃 Kirchhoff's Loop Rule

Definition. Kirchhoff's Loop Rule (also called Kirchhoff's Voltage Law, or KVL) states that the algebraic sum of all potential differences around any closed loop in a circuit is equal to zero:

$$ \sum_{i} \Delta V_i = 0 $$

This principle follows directly from the conservation of energy. As a charge carrier traverses a complete loop and returns to its starting point, the net work done by the electric field on the charge must be zero—no energy is created or destroyed. Potential rises occur across EMF sources (batteries, generators), while potential drops occur across resistive elements due to Ohm's Law, $\Delta V = -IR$.

Sign Convention. When traversing a loop in a chosen direction: (1) crossing a battery from negative to positive terminal gives $+\mathcal{E}$; crossing from positive to negative gives $-\mathcal{E}$. (2) Crossing a resistor in the direction of assumed current flow gives $-IR$; crossing against the current gives $+IR$.

Worked Example. Consider a single-loop circuit containing a battery of EMF $\mathcal{E} = 12\,\text{V}$ with internal resistance $r = 1\,\Omega$, and two external resistors $R_1 = 3\,\Omega$ and $R_2 = 8\,\Omega$ in series. Traversing the loop in the direction of current $I$:

$$ \mathcal{E} - Ir - IR_1 - IR_2 = 0 $$ $$ 12 - I(1) - I(3) - I(8) = 0 $$ $$ 12 = 12I \implies I = 1\,\text{A} $$

The terminal voltage of the battery is $V_{ab} = \mathcal{E} - Ir = 12 - 1 = 11\,\text{V}$.

Key Theorem. For any planar circuit with $b$ branches and $n$ nodes, exactly $b - n + 1$ independent loop equations can be written using KVL. Combined with $n - 1$ independent node equations from Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL), this system is sufficient to solve for all unknown currents and voltages in the network. KCL states that the algebraic sum of currents entering any node equals zero, complementing KVL to form a complete framework for circuit analysis.

Lab Exercise. Construct a circuit with $\mathcal{E} = 12\,\text{V}$, internal resistance $r = 1\,\Omega$, and three series resistors $R_1 = 2\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 4\,\Omega$, and $R_3 = 5\,\Omega$. Apply KVL to write a single loop equation and solve for the current $I$. Measure the voltage drop across each resistor with a multimeter and confirm that the sum of all measured drops equals the battery EMF, verifying $\sum \Delta V = 0$ around the loop.
