🍃 Ohms Law

🍃 Ohms Law

Definition. Ohm's Law states that the current $I$ through a conductor between two points is directly proportional to the potential difference $V$ across those points, provided the temperature and other physical conditions remain constant. Mathematically,

$$ V = IR $$

where $V$ is the voltage in volts (V), $I$ is the current in amperes (A), and $R$ is the resistance in ohms ($\Omega$). A conductor that obeys this relationship is called an ohmic material; one for which $R$ is not constant (e.g., a diode) is non-ohmic. Resistance itself arises from collisions between conduction electrons and the ionic lattice of the material, and for a uniform conductor of length $L$, cross-sectional area $A$, and resistivity $\rho$,

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A}. $$

Worked Example. A copper wire of resistivity $\rho = 1.68 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}$, length $L = 2.00\ \text{m}$, and diameter $d = 1.00\ \text{mm}$ is connected to a $9.00\ \text{V}$ battery. Find the current through the wire.

Step 1 — Resistance:

$$ A = \pi\!\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^{2} = \pi(0.500\times10^{-3})^{2} = 7.854\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}^{2} $$ $$ R = (1.68\times10^{-8})\frac{2.00}{7.854\times10^{-7}} = 4.28\times10^{-2}\ \Omega $$

Step 2 — Current:

$$ I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{9.00}{4.28\times10^{-2}} \approx 210\ \text{A} $$

This large current reflects copper's very low resistivity; in practice, such a wire would require a high-current source and appropriate heat dissipation.

Key Theorem. For a network of resistors in series, the equivalent resistance is $R_{\text{eq}} = \sum_i R_i$, while for resistors in parallel,

$$ \frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \sum_i \frac{1}{R_i}. $$

These results follow directly from applying Ohm's Law alongside Kirchhoff's voltage and current laws, and they underpin the analysis of all linear DC circuits.

Lab Exercise. Assemble a simple circuit with a variable dc power supply, an ammeter, a voltmeter, and three resistors ($100\ \Omega$, $220\ \Omega$, $470\ \Omega$) first in series, then in parallel. For each configuration, record $V$ and $I$ at five supply voltages between $1\ \text{V}$ and $10\ \text{V}$. Plot $V$ versus $I$ and extract $R_{\text{eq}}$ from the slope. Compare your measured values against the theoretical predictions and discuss sources of systematic error, including contact resistance and ammeter loading.
