🍃 Capacitor Charging

🍃 Capacitor Charging

Definition. When a capacitor of capacitance $C$ is connected in series with a resistor $R$ and a DC source of emf $\mathcal{E}$, charge accumulates on the capacitor plates over time. The instantaneous charge $q(t)$ obeys the first-order linear differential equation obtained by applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the loop:

$$ \mathcal{E} - \frac{q}{C} - iR = 0, \quad i = \frac{dq}{dt} $$

Substituting and rearranging gives

$$ R\frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{q}{C} = \mathcal{E}. $$

Solving with initial condition $q(0) = 0$ yields

$$ q(t) = C\mathcal{E}\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad \tau = RC, $$

where $\tau$ is the time constant. The voltage across the capacitor and the charging current follow directly:

$$ V_C(t) = \mathcal{E}\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad i(t) = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R}e^{-t/\tau}. $$

Worked Example. A $12\,\text{V}$ battery charges a $C = 470\,\mu\text{F}$ capacitor through $R = 10\,\text{k}\Omega$. Find (a) the time constant, (b) the voltage across the capacitor at $t = 5\,\text{s}$, and (c) the initial charging current.

(a) $\tau = RC = (10\times10^{3})(470\times10^{-6}) = 4.70\,\text{s}$.

(b) $V_C(5) = 12\!\left(1 - e^{-5/4.70}\right) = 12\!\left(1 - e^{-1.064}\right) \approx 12(1 - 0.345) \approx 7.86\,\text{V}$.

(c) $i(0) = \mathcal{E}/R = 12/(10^4) = 1.2\,\text{mA}$.

Key Theorem. At $t = \tau$, the capacitor has reached $1 - e^{-1} \approx 63.2\%$ of its final charge $Q_\infty = C\mathcal{E}$. The capacitor is considered fully charged after approximately $5\tau$, at which point $V_C \approx 99.3\%\,\mathcal{E}$ and the current is negligibly small. The time constant $\tau = RC$ therefore serves as the single characteristic scale governing the entire transient response: a larger resistance slows the current and extends charging; a larger capacitance increases the total charge required and likewise extends the process. Altering either component changes $\tau$ proportionally, while the shape of the exponential approach to equilibrium remains universal.

Lab Exercise. Assemble an RC circuit with $\mathcal{E} = 9\,\text{V}$, $R = 22\,\text{k}\Omega$, and $C = 100\,\mu\text{F}$. Record $V_C$ at ten-second intervals using a digital multimeter. Plot $\ln\!\left(1 - V_C/\mathcal{E}\right)$ versus $t$ and extract $\tau$ from the slope. Compare your experimental value to the theoretical prediction $\tau = RC = 2.2\,\text{s}$ and account for any systematic discrepancies arising from tolerance in the component values.
