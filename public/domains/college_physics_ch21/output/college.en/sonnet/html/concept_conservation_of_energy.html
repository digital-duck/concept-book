🌱 Conservation Of Energy

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

Definition. The principle of conservation of energy states that the total energy of an isolated system remains constant over time. Energy may be transformed from one form to another — kinetic, potential, thermal, electromagnetic — but cannot be created or destroyed. In the context of electric circuits, this principle manifests as Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL): the algebraic sum of all potential differences around any closed loop equals zero,

$$ \sum_i \Delta V_i = 0. $$

Equivalently, the electrical energy supplied by sources in a loop equals the energy dissipated by resistive elements. Power delivered to a resistor is $P = I^2 R$. Over a time interval $\Delta t$, energy conservation requires

$$ \mathcal{E} I \,\Delta t = \sum_j I^2 R_j \,\Delta t, $$

where $\mathcal{E}$ is the electromotive force (EMF) of the source — the energy per unit charge it supplies to the circuit.

Worked Example. Consider a single-loop circuit containing a battery of EMF $\mathcal{E} = 12\,\text{V}$ and internal resistance $r = 1\,\Omega$, connected to an external resistor $R = 5\,\Omega$. The current is

$$ I = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R + r} = \frac{12}{6} = 2\,\text{A}. $$

The power supplied by the battery is $P_{\text{source}} = \mathcal{E} I = 12 \times 2 = 24\,\text{W}$. Power dissipated in $R$ is $P_R = I^2 R = 4 \times 5 = 20\,\text{W}$, and in $r$ is $P_r = I^2 r = 4 \times 1 = 4\,\text{W}$. Summing the dissipated powers: $20 + 4 = 24\,\text{W}$, confirming energy conservation.

Key Theorem. For any closed loop in a resistive network, KVL follows directly from the path-independence of electric potential. Because the electrostatic force is conservative, the work done per unit charge around any closed path satisfies $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = 0$, which is the microscopic foundation of KVL and, by extension, of energy conservation in circuits.

Lab Exercise. Construct a two-loop circuit with $\mathcal{E}_1 = 9\,\text{V}$, $\mathcal{E}_2 = 6\,\text{V}$, and resistors $R_1 = 2\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 3\,\Omega$, $R_3 = 4\,\Omega$. Use a multimeter to measure the voltage drop across each element. Apply KVL to each loop and verify that the sum of measured potential differences around every loop is within $\pm 0.1\,\text{V}$ of zero. Calculate the total power delivered by both sources and compare it to the total power dissipated; record and explain any discrepancy.
