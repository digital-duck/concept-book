🍃 Electromotive Force

🍃 Electromotive Force

Definition. The electromotive force (EMF), denoted $\mathcal{E}$, of a source is the work done per unit charge by non-electrostatic forces (chemical, mechanical, or electromagnetic) in moving a positive test charge completely around a closed circuit. Formally,

$$ \mathcal{E} = \frac{dW_{\text{non-elec}}}{dq} $$

with SI unit of volts (V = J/C). Despite its name, EMF is not a force but an energy-per-charge quantity, entirely analogous to potential difference. A real source also possesses internal resistance $r$, so the terminal voltage — the potential difference available at its output terminals — is

$$ V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E} - Ir $$

where $I$ is the current drawn. When $I = 0$ (open circuit), $V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E}$; under load, the terminal voltage is reduced by the resistive drop $Ir$ across the source itself.

Worked Example. A battery rated $\mathcal{E} = 12.0\ \text{V}$ has internal resistance $r = 0.50\ \Omega$ and drives current through an external resistor $R = 5.50\ \Omega$. Find the current and terminal voltage.

Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the single loop:

$$ \mathcal{E} = I(R + r) \implies I = \frac{12.0}{5.50 + 0.50} = \frac{12.0}{6.00} = 2.00\ \text{A} $$

The terminal voltage is then

$$ V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E} - Ir = 12.0 - (2.00)(0.50) = 11.0\ \text{V} $$

The 1.0 V drop across the internal resistance represents power $P_r = I^2 r = 2.0\ \text{W}$ dissipated inside the source.

Key Theorem. For a source with EMF $\mathcal{E}$ and internal resistance $r$ connected to external resistance $R$, maximum power is delivered to the external circuit when $R = r$ (the maximum power transfer theorem). Under this condition,

$$ P_{\max} = \frac{\mathcal{E}^2}{4r} $$

This result follows directly from differentiating $P = I^2 R = \mathcal{E}^2 R/(R+r)^2$ with respect to $R$ and setting $dP/dR = 0$.

Laboratory Exercise. Connect a 9-V battery to a decade resistance box set to several values between $1\ \Omega$ and $100\ \Omega$. For each $R$, measure both the current $I$ with an ammeter and the terminal voltage $V_{\text{terminal}}$ with a voltmeter placed directly at the battery terminals. Plot $V_{\text{terminal}}$ versus $I$; the $y$-intercept gives $\mathcal{E}$ and the (negative) slope gives $r$. Compare the graphically determined $r$ to the manufacturer's specification and discuss how increasing current load affects the terminal voltage.
