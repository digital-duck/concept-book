🍃 Capacitor Discharging

🍃 Capacitor Discharging

Definition. Capacitor discharging describes the process by which a charged capacitor releases its stored energy through a resistor, with the charge, voltage, and current decaying exponentially over time. In a simple RC circuit consisting of a capacitor $C$ and resistor $R$, once the external voltage source is removed, Kirchhoff's voltage law yields:

$$ V_C(t) = V_0 \, e^{-t/\tau} $$

where $V_0$ is the initial voltage across the capacitor and $\tau = RC$ is the time constant of the circuit. The charge and current follow analogous forms:

$$ Q(t) = Q_0 \, e^{-t/\tau}, \qquad I(t) = \frac{V_0}{R} \, e^{-t/\tau} $$

The time constant $\tau$ governs the rate of decay: after one time constant, the voltage falls to $e^{-1} \approx 36.8\%$ of its initial value; after five time constants, the capacitor is considered effectively discharged.

Worked Example. A $200\,\mu\text{F}$ capacitor is charged to $12\,\text{V}$ and then connected to a $50\,\text{k}\Omega$ resistor. Determine the time constant and the voltage after $10\,\text{s}$.

$$ \tau = RC = (50 \times 10^3\,\Omega)(200 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{F}) = 10\,\text{s} $$ $$ V(10\,\text{s}) = 12 \cdot e^{-10/10} = 12 \cdot e^{-1} \approx 12 \times 0.368 = 4.42\,\text{V} $$

The initial discharge current is $I_0 = V_0/R = 12/(50\times10^3) = 0.24\,\text{mA}$, decaying with the same time constant.

Key Theorem. The energy dissipated in the resistor during a complete discharge equals the energy originally stored in the capacitor. Starting from $U = \tfrac{1}{2}CV_0^2$, integrating the instantaneous power $P(t) = I^2 R$ over all time confirms:

$$ \int_0^{\infty} I^2 R \, dt = \int_0^{\infty} \frac{V_0^2}{R} e^{-2t/\tau} dt = \frac{1}{2}CV_0^2 $$

This result underscores that RC circuits are purely dissipative during discharge — no energy is returned to a source.

Lab Exercise. Assemble a series RC circuit with $C = 100\,\mu\text{F}$ and $R = 10\,\text{k}\Omega$. Charge the capacitor to $9\,\text{V}$, then disconnect the supply and record $V_C$ at intervals of $1\,\text{s}$ for $15\,\text{s}$. Plot $\ln(V_C)$ versus $t$; the slope should equal $-1/\tau$. Compare the experimental time constant to the theoretical value $\tau = 1\,\text{s}$ and account for any discrepancy due to component tolerances.
