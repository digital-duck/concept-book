🍃 RC Circuit

An RC circuit is a series network of a resistor ($R$, in ohms) and a capacitor ($C$, in farads) driven by an EMF source $\mathcal{E}$. Because the capacitor voltage builds as charge accumulates, the circuit response is inherently time-dependent: the charge and current evolve exponentially rather than settling instantaneously as in a purely resistive network.

Definition. Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the loop gives the governing equation:

$$ \mathcal{E} - \frac{q}{C} - R\frac{dq}{dt} = 0 $$

With the initial condition $q(0) = 0$, the solution is:

$$ q(t) = C\mathcal{E}\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad \tau \equiv RC $$

The quantity $\tau = RC$ (seconds) is the time constant: the single new primitive this section introduces. It sets the characteristic timescale of the exponential approach to equilibrium. After one time constant the capacitor holds approximately $63.2\%$ of its final charge $Q_{\max} = C\mathcal{E}$; the current $I(t) = (\mathcal{E}/R)\,e^{-t/\tau}$ decays on the same scale.

Worked Example. Given $\mathcal{E} = 12\,\text{V}$, $R = 4.7\,\text{k}\Omega$, $C = 100\,\mu\text{F}$:

$$ \tau = (4.7 \times 10^3)(100 \times 10^{-6}) = 0.47\,\text{s} $$

The maximum charge is $Q_{\max} = (100 \times 10^{-6})(12) = 1.2\,\text{mC}$. At $t = \tau$, $q = 1.2(1 - e^{-1}) \approx 0.758\,\text{mC}$. The initial current is $I(0) = 12/(4.7 \times 10^3) \approx 2.55\,\text{mA}$, falling exponentially toward zero. Both observables are entirely determined by $\tau$.

Key Theorem. The energy stored in the capacitor at full charge is $U_C = \frac{1}{2}C\mathcal{E}^2$, and the energy dissipated resistively during the charging process is also $\frac{1}{2}C\mathcal{E}^2$, independent of $R$. Consequently, regardless of how large or small $\tau$ is, exactly half the source energy is stored and half is dissipated as heat.

Lab Exercise. Assemble a series RC circuit with $R = 10\,\text{k}\Omega$, $C = 47\,\mu\text{F}$, and a $9\,\text{V}$ source ($\tau_{\text{theory}} = 0.47\,\text{s}$). Record the capacitor voltage at intervals of $\tau/2$ until $V_C$ plateaus. Fit the data to $V_C(t) = \mathcal{E}(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$ to extract the experimental time constant, then compute the percent error relative to $\tau_{\text{theory}}$ and identify plausible sources of systematic deviation.
