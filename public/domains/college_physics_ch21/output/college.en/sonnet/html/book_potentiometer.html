🌱 Electric Current

🌱 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional surface. If a net charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface in a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}. $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limit

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt}. $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of conventional current is taken to be the direction in which positive charges flow, even though in metallic conductors it is free electrons (negative charges) that move in the opposite direction. Current is a scalar quantity with an associated direction along a conductor.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries $3.2 \times 10^{19}$ electrons past a cross-section every second. Determine the magnitude of the current.

Each electron carries charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, so the total charge transferred per second is

$$ \Delta Q = (3.2 \times 10^{19})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}) = 5.13\,\text{C}. $$

Since $\Delta t = 1\,\text{s}$,

$$ I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} = \frac{5.13\,\text{C}}{1\,\text{s}} \approx 5.1\,\text{A}. $$

Conventional current flows opposite to the electron drift, but its magnitude is $5.1\,\text{A}$.

Key Theorem — Current Conservation in a Series Path. For any cross-section of an unbranched conductor in steady state, the current $I$ is the same regardless of where along the conductor the measurement is taken. This follows directly from charge conservation: charge cannot accumulate inside a conductor in steady state, so the same quantity $\Delta Q$ must pass every cross-section in each interval $\Delta t$. Consequently, $I$ is a property of the branch, not of the particular cross-section chosen, which justifies treating current as a single scalar value throughout a series element.

🌱 Voltage

🌱 Voltage

Definition. Voltage, or electric potential difference, between two points A and B in an electric field is defined as the work done per unit positive charge by an external agent moving a test charge from A to B against the electric force:

$$ V_{AB} = V_A - V_B = \frac{W_{A \to B}}{q} $$

where $W_{A\to B}$ is the work done by the external agent and $q$ is the magnitude of the test charge. The SI unit of voltage is the volt (V), where $1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C}$. Equivalently, the electric potential at a point P due to a point charge $Q$ at distance $r$ is:

$$ V = \frac{kQ}{r} $$

where $k = 8.99 \times 10^9\,\text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant. Voltage is a scalar quantity, making superposition straightforward: the total potential at a point due to multiple charges is the algebraic sum of individual contributions.

Worked Example. Two point charges, $Q_1 = +3.0\,\mu\text{C}$ and $Q_2 = -1.5\,\mu\text{C}$, are fixed at positions $x = 0$ and $x = 4.0\,\text{cm}$, respectively. Find the electric potential at the midpoint $x = 2.0\,\text{cm}$.

The distance from each charge to the midpoint is $r = 0.020\,\text{m}$.

$$ V_1 = \frac{kQ_1}{r} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(3.0\times10^{-6})}{0.020} = +1.35\times10^6\,\text{V} $$ $$ V_2 = \frac{kQ_2}{r} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(-1.5\times10^{-6})}{0.020} = -6.74\times10^5\,\text{V} $$ $$ V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 = 1.35\times10^6 - 6.74\times10^5 \approx +6.8\times10^5\,\text{V} $$

Key Theorem. The relationship between electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and potential $V$ in one dimension is:

$$ E_x = -\frac{dV}{dx} $$

This states that the electric field points in the direction of decreasing potential and has magnitude equal to the spatial rate of change of potential. Equipotential surfaces are always perpendicular to field lines, and no work is done moving a charge along an equipotential.

🌱 Conservation Of Charge

🌱 Conservation Of Charge

Definition. The conservation of electric charge is a fundamental law of nature stating that the total electric charge of an isolated system remains constant over time. Formally, if a system is isolated from external charge exchange, then

$$ Q_{\text{total}} = \sum_i q_i = \text{constant}, $$

where $q_i$ denotes the charge of each constituent particle or object. Charge is neither created nor destroyed; it may only be transferred between objects or transformed between particle–antiparticle pairs, always in equal and opposite amounts. Electric charge is quantized in integer multiples of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, and every observed process respects this conservation law without exception.

Worked Example. A neutral rubber rod is rubbed against a neutral wool cloth. After contact, the rod acquires a net charge of $q_{\text{rod}} = -8.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Determine the charge remaining on the cloth.

Since both objects were initially neutral, the total charge of the system before contact is

$$ Q_{\text{initial}} = 0 + 0 = 0. $$

By conservation of charge, the total charge after contact must satisfy

$$ Q_{\text{final}} = q_{\text{rod}} + q_{\text{cloth}} = 0. $$

Solving,

$$ q_{\text{cloth}} = -q_{\text{rod}} = +8.0\ \mu\text{C}. $$

The cloth carries $+8.0\ \mu\text{C}$, consistent with electrons being transferred from wool to rubber.

Key Theorem. Conservation of charge is a consequence of gauge invariance in electromagnetism. By Noether's theorem, the invariance of the electromagnetic Lagrangian under a global $U(1)$ phase transformation $\psi \to e^{i\alpha}\psi$ yields a conserved Noether current $J^\mu$, whose time component gives the conserved charge $Q = \int J^0\, d^3x$. This connection elevates charge conservation from an empirical rule to a deep symmetry principle of nature. The corresponding local continuity equation,

$$ \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0, $$

expresses charge conservation in differential form: any decrease in local charge density must be accompanied by a diverging current density.

Lab Exercise. Charge an aluminum foil strip by contact with a charged glass rod, then touch it momentarily to an identical uncharged strip while both are suspended on insulating threads. Measure the deflection angle of each strip using a protractor and estimate the charge on each from Coulomb's law. Verify that the sum of the two measured charges equals the original charge on the first strip, confirming conservation of charge within experimental uncertainty.

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

Definition. The principle of conservation of energy states that the total energy of an isolated system remains constant over time. Energy may be transformed from one form to another — kinetic, potential, thermal, electromagnetic — but cannot be created or destroyed. In the context of electric circuits, this principle manifests as Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL): the algebraic sum of all potential differences around any closed loop equals zero,

$$ \sum_i \Delta V_i = 0. $$

Equivalently, the electrical energy supplied by sources in a loop equals the energy dissipated by resistive elements. Power delivered to a resistor is $P = I^2 R$. Over a time interval $\Delta t$, energy conservation requires

$$ \mathcal{E} I \,\Delta t = \sum_j I^2 R_j \,\Delta t, $$

where $\mathcal{E}$ is the electromotive force (EMF) of the source — the energy per unit charge it supplies to the circuit.

Worked Example. Consider a single-loop circuit containing a battery of EMF $\mathcal{E} = 12\,\text{V}$ and internal resistance $r = 1\,\Omega$, connected to an external resistor $R = 5\,\Omega$. The current is

$$ I = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R + r} = \frac{12}{6} = 2\,\text{A}. $$

The power supplied by the battery is $P_{\text{source}} = \mathcal{E} I = 12 \times 2 = 24\,\text{W}$. Power dissipated in $R$ is $P_R = I^2 R = 4 \times 5 = 20\,\text{W}$, and in $r$ is $P_r = I^2 r = 4 \times 1 = 4\,\text{W}$. Summing the dissipated powers: $20 + 4 = 24\,\text{W}$, confirming energy conservation.

Key Theorem. For any closed loop in a resistive network, KVL follows directly from the path-independence of electric potential. Because the electrostatic force is conservative, the work done per unit charge around any closed path satisfies $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = 0$, which is the microscopic foundation of KVL and, by extension, of energy conservation in circuits.

Lab Exercise. Construct a two-loop circuit with $\mathcal{E}_1 = 9\,\text{V}$, $\mathcal{E}_2 = 6\,\text{V}$, and resistors $R_1 = 2\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 3\,\Omega$, $R_3 = 4\,\Omega$. Use a multimeter to measure the voltage drop across each element. Apply KVL to each loop and verify that the sum of measured potential differences around every loop is within $\pm 0.1\,\text{V}$ of zero. Calculate the total power delivered by both sources and compare it to the total power dissipated; record and explain any discrepancy.

🍃 Kirchhoffs Junction Rule

🍃 Kirchhoff's Junction Rule

Definition

Kirchhoff's Junction Rule, also called the Current Law (KCL), states that the algebraic sum of all electric currents entering and leaving any junction (node) in an electrical circuit equals zero. Mathematically:

$$ \sum_{k} I_k = 0 $$

where currents directed into the junction are taken as positive and currents directed out of the junction are taken as negative (or vice versa, provided the convention is applied consistently). This rule is a direct consequence of the conservation of electric charge: charge cannot accumulate at a node in steady-state conditions, so the rate at which charge arrives must equal the rate at which it departs.

Worked Example

Consider a junction where three wires meet. Wire 1 carries a current $I_1 = 3\,\text{A}$ into the junction, wire 2 carries $I_2 = 5\,\text{A}$ into the junction, and wire 3 carries current $I_3$ away from the junction. Applying the Junction Rule with the convention that inward currents are positive:

$$ I_1 + I_2 - I_3 = 0 $$ $$ 3 + 5 - I_3 = 0 $$ $$ I_3 = 8\,\text{A} $$

The current leaving through wire 3 is $8\,\text{A}$, consistent with charge conservation.

Key Theorem

For any circuit with $n$ nodes, the Junction Rule applied at each node yields $n$ equations; however, only $n - 1$ of these equations are linearly independent. The redundant equation arises because each branch current appears in exactly two nodal equations (once with a positive sign, once with a negative sign), making the sum of all nodal equations identically zero. In practice, one junction equation is always discarded when solving a system, and the remaining equations are combined with Kirchhoff's Voltage Law equations to form a complete, solvable system.

Construct a circuit containing a $9\,\text{V}$ battery connected to three parallel resistors: $R_1 = 10\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 20\,\Omega$, and $R_3 = 30\,\Omega$. Using a multimeter, measure the current through each branch and record your values as $I_1$, $I_2$, and $I_3$. 🍃 Kirchhoffs Loop Rule

🍃 Kirchhoff's Loop Rule

Definition. Kirchhoff's Loop Rule (also called Kirchhoff's Voltage Law, or KVL) states that the algebraic sum of all potential differences around any closed loop in a circuit is equal to zero:

$$ \sum_{i} \Delta V_i = 0 $$

This principle follows directly from the conservation of energy. As a charge carrier traverses a complete loop and returns to its starting point, the net work done by the electric field on the charge must be zero—no energy is created or destroyed. Potential rises occur across EMF sources (batteries, generators), while potential drops occur across resistive elements due to Ohm's Law, $\Delta V = -IR$.

Sign Convention. When traversing a loop in a chosen direction: (1) crossing a battery from negative to positive terminal gives $+\mathcal{E}$; crossing from positive to negative gives $-\mathcal{E}$. (2) Crossing a resistor in the direction of assumed current flow gives $-IR$; crossing against the current gives $+IR$.

Worked Example. Consider a single-loop circuit containing a battery of EMF $\mathcal{E} = 12\,\text{V}$ with internal resistance $r = 1\,\Omega$, and two external resistors $R_1 = 3\,\Omega$ and $R_2 = 8\,\Omega$ in series. Traversing the loop in the direction of current $I$:

$$ \mathcal{E} - Ir - IR_1 - IR_2 = 0 $$ $$ 12 - I(1) - I(3) - I(8) = 0 $$ $$ 12 = 12I \implies I = 1\,\text{A} $$

The terminal voltage of the battery is $V_{ab} = \mathcal{E} - Ir = 12 - 1 = 11\,\text{V}$.

Key Theorem. For any planar circuit with $b$ branches and $n$ nodes, exactly $b - n + 1$ independent loop equations can be written using KVL. Combined with $n - 1$ independent node equations from Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL), this system is sufficient to solve for all unknown currents and voltages in the network. KCL states that the algebraic sum of currents entering any node equals zero, complementing KVL to form a complete framework for circuit analysis.

Lab Exercise. Construct a circuit with $\mathcal{E} = 12\,\text{V}$, internal resistance $r = 1\,\Omega$, and three series resistors $R_1 = 2\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 4\,\Omega$, and $R_3 = 5\,\Omega$. Apply KVL to write a single loop equation and solve for the current $I$. Measure the voltage drop across each resistor with a multimeter and confirm that the sum of all measured drops equals the battery EMF, verifying $\sum \Delta V = 0$ around the loop.

🌱 Resistance

🌱 Resistance

Definition. Electrical resistance is the opposition a material offers to the flow of electric current. For a conductor carrying current $I$ under an applied potential difference $V$, the resistance $R$ is defined by Ohm's Law:

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} $$

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm ($\Omega$), where $1\,\Omega = 1\,\text{V}/\text{A}$. Resistance arises microscopically from collisions between conduction electrons and the ionic lattice of the material; each collision transfers kinetic energy to the lattice as thermal energy, producing Joule heating. For a uniform conductor of length $L$, cross-sectional area $A$, and resistivity $\rho$, resistance is given by:

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} $$

Resistivity $\rho$ is an intrinsic material property (units: $\Omega\cdot\text{m}$) that characterizes how strongly a material opposes current flow, depending on temperature, composition, and microstructure.

Worked Example. A copper wire ($\rho = 1.72\times10^{-8}\,\Omega\cdot\text{m}$) has length $L = 2.00\,\text{m}$ and circular cross-section of diameter $d = 1.00\,\text{mm}$. Find its resistance.

First, compute the cross-sectional area:

$$ A = \pi\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi\left(5.00\times10^{-4}\right)^2 = 7.854\times10^{-7}\,\text{m}^2 $$

Then apply the resistivity formula:

$$ R = \rho\frac{L}{A} = (1.72\times10^{-8})\frac{2.00}{7.854\times10^{-7}} = 4.38\times10^{-2}\,\Omega \approx 43.8\,\text{m}\Omega $$

This small value confirms that copper is an excellent conductor, making it suitable for household wiring.

Key Theorem. For a network of resistors in series, total resistance is additive: $R_{\text{series}} = \sum_i R_i$. For a parallel combination, the reciprocal resistances add: $R_{\text{parallel}}^{-1} = \sum_i R_i^{-1}$. These results follow directly from Kirchhoff's voltage and current laws and enable systematic analysis of arbitrarily complex resistive circuits.

Investigate how substituting a tungsten lamp for the resistor produces a nonlinear $V$–$I$ curve, demonstrating that resistivity—and hence resistance—is not constant for all materials under all conditions.

🍃 Galvanometer

🍃 Galvanometer

Definition. A galvanometer is a sensitive electromagnetic instrument used to detect and measure small electric currents. Its operation relies on the torque exerted on a current-carrying coil placed within a uniform magnetic field. When current $I$ passes through a rectangular coil of $N$ turns, each of area $A$, situated in a radial magnetic field of magnitude $B$, the magnetic torque is

$$ \tau_{mag} = NIAB. $$

This torque is opposed by a restoring torque from a calibrated spiral spring, given by $\tau_{spring} = \kappa\theta$, where $\kappa$ is the spring constant and $\theta$ is the angular deflection. At equilibrium,

$$ NIAB = \kappa\theta \implies \theta = \frac{NIAB}{\kappa}. $$

Since $N$, $A$, $B$, and $\kappa$ are fixed for a given instrument, the deflection $\theta$ is directly proportional to the current $I$, enabling quantitative measurement.

Worked Example. A galvanometer has $N = 50$ turns, coil area $A = 2.0 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2$, magnetic field $B = 0.20\ \text{T}$, and spring constant $\kappa = 4.0 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{N·m/rad}$. Find the deflection when $I = 1.0\ \mu\text{A} = 1.0 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{A}$.

$$ \theta = \frac{NIAB}{\kappa} = \frac{(50)(1.0\times10^{-6})(2.0\times10^{-4})(0.20)}{4.0\times10^{-8}} = \frac{2.0\times10^{-9}}{4.0\times10^{-8}} = 0.050\ \text{rad} \approx 2.9°. $$

This small but measurable deflection confirms the instrument's sensitivity to microampere-level currents.

Key Theorem — Current Sensitivity. The current sensitivity $S_I$ of a galvanometer is defined as the deflection per unit current:

$$ S_I = \frac{\theta}{I} = \frac{NAB}{\kappa}. $$

To maximize sensitivity, designers increase $N$, $A$, or $B$, or use a weaker spring (smaller $\kappa$). A galvanometer can be converted into an ammeter by connecting a low-resistance shunt in parallel, allowing it to measure larger currents without altering its fundamental operating principle.

Lab Exercise. Using a galvanometer with coil resistance $R_g = 25\ \Omega$ and full-scale deflection current $I_g = 1.0\ \text{mA}$, determine the shunt resistance $R_s$ needed to convert it into an ammeter with full-scale deflection at $I_{fs} = 10\ \text{mA}$. The shunt carries the remaining current $I_s = I_{fs} - I_g = 9.0\ \text{mA}$. Apply the condition that the voltage across the shunt equals the voltage across the coil:

$$ I_s R_s = I_g R_g \implies R_s = \frac{I_g R_g}{I_s} = \frac{(1.0\times10^{-3})(25)}{9.0\times10^{-3}} \approx 2.8\ \Omega. $$

🍃 Kirchhoffs Rules

🍃 Kirchhoff's Rules

Definition. Kirchhoff's Rules are two conservation laws that govern the behavior of electrical currents and voltages in circuit networks that cannot be reduced by simple series-parallel combinations.

Kirchhoff's Junction Rule (KCL): At any node (junction) in a circuit, the algebraic sum of all currents entering and leaving is zero:

$$ \sum_k I_k = 0 $$

This rule is a direct consequence of charge conservation — charge neither accumulates at nor drains from a junction in steady state.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Rule (KVL): Around any closed loop in a circuit, the algebraic sum of all potential differences (EMFs and resistive drops) is zero:

$$ \sum_k \mathcal{E}_k - \sum_k I_k R_k = 0 $$

This rule follows from conservation of energy: the work done per unit charge around a closed path in an electrostatic field must vanish.

Sign Convention. Traversing a resistor in the direction of assumed current yields a drop $-IR$; against the current yields $+IR$. Traversing a battery from $-$ to $+$ terminal yields $+\mathcal{E}$; from $+$ to $-$ yields $-\mathcal{E}$.

Worked Example. Consider a two-loop circuit with $\mathcal{E}_1 = 12\,\text{V}$, $\mathcal{E}_2 = 6\,\text{V}$, $R_1 = 4\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 2\,\Omega$, $R_3 = 3\,\Omega$. Assign currents $I_1$, $I_2$, $I_3$ and apply KCL at the central junction: $I_1 = I_2 + I_3$. Apply KVL to loop 1: $12 - 4I_1 - 2I_2 = 0$, and loop 2: $6 - 3I_3 + 2I_2 = 0$. Substituting $I_1 = I_2 + I_3$ and solving simultaneously yields $I_1 = 2\,\text{A}$, $I_2 = 2\,\text{A}$, $I_3 = 0\,\text{A}$.

Key Theorem. For a planar circuit with $N$ nodes and $B$ branches, independent equations number $(N-1)$ from KCL and $B - (N-1)$ from KVL, giving exactly $B$ equations for $B$ unknown currents — a system guaranteed to have a unique solution when the circuit contains only linear elements and independent sources.

Lab Exercise. Build a three-resistor bridge circuit on a breadboard with two known EMF sources. Measure all branch currents with an ammeter and all node voltages with a voltmeter. Apply Kirchhoff's Rules analytically to predict each current, then compute the percent discrepancy between predicted and measured values. Investigate how replacing one resistor with a nonlinear element (such as a diode) affects whether KVL continues to hold.

🍃 Electromotive Force

🍃 Electromotive Force

Definition. The electromotive force (EMF), denoted $\mathcal{E}$, of a source is the work done per unit charge by non-electrostatic forces (chemical, mechanical, or electromagnetic) in moving a positive test charge completely around a closed circuit. Formally,

$$ \mathcal{E} = \frac{dW_{\text{non-elec}}}{dq} $$

with SI unit of volts (V = J/C). Despite its name, EMF is not a force but an energy-per-charge quantity, entirely analogous to potential difference. A real source also possesses internal resistance $r$, so the terminal voltage — the potential difference available at its output terminals — is

$$ V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E} - Ir $$

where $I$ is the current drawn. When $I = 0$ (open circuit), $V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E}$; under load, the terminal voltage is reduced by the resistive drop $Ir$ across the source itself.

Worked Example. A battery rated $\mathcal{E} = 12.0\ \text{V}$ has internal resistance $r = 0.50\ \Omega$ and drives current through an external resistor $R = 5.50\ \Omega$. Find the current and terminal voltage.

Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the single loop:

$$ \mathcal{E} = I(R + r) \implies I = \frac{12.0}{5.50 + 0.50} = \frac{12.0}{6.00} = 2.00\ \text{A} $$

The terminal voltage is then

$$ V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E} - Ir = 12.0 - (2.00)(0.50) = 11.0\ \text{V} $$

The 1.0 V drop across the internal resistance represents power $P_r = I^2 r = 2.0\ \text{W}$ dissipated inside the source.

Key Theorem. For a source with EMF $\mathcal{E}$ and internal resistance $r$ connected to external resistance $R$, maximum power is delivered to the external circuit when $R = r$ (the maximum power transfer theorem). Under this condition,

$$ P_{\max} = \frac{\mathcal{E}^2}{4r} $$

This result follows directly from differentiating $P = I^2 R = \mathcal{E}^2 R/(R+r)^2$ with respect to $R$ and setting $dP/dR = 0$.

Laboratory Exercise. Connect a 9-V battery to a decade resistance box set to several values between $1\ \Omega$ and $100\ \Omega$. For each $R$, measure both the current $I$ with an ammeter and the terminal voltage $V_{\text{terminal}}$ with a voltmeter placed directly at the battery terminals. Plot $V_{\text{terminal}}$ versus $I$; the $y$-intercept gives $\mathcal{E}$ and the (negative) slope gives $r$. Compare the graphically determined $r$ to the manufacturer's specification and discuss how increasing current load affects the terminal voltage.

🍃 Null Measurement

🍃 Null Measurement

Definition

A null measurement is an experimental technique in which an unknown quantity is determined by adjusting a known, calibrated quantity until the measuring instrument reads exactly zero—the null condition. Rather than inferring the unknown from a deflection or reading on the detector, the experimenter drives the detector to its zero point, at which the detector draws no current and therefore does not disturb the circuit under test. This elimination of instrument loading is the defining advantage of null methods over direct-deflection methods.

The two canonical implementations in DC circuit analysis are the Wheatstone bridge (null measurement of resistance) and the potentiometer (null measurement of EMF). Both share the same logic: the circuit is balanced so that no current flows through the galvanometer branch, and the unknown is then expressed in terms of the known calibration values.

Worked Example

Consider a Wheatstone bridge with known resistors $R_1$ and $R_2$ in one voltage-divider arm and a calibrated variable resistor $R_3$ in the other. The unknown resistance $R_x$ occupies the fourth position. A galvanometer connects the two midpoints. The bridge is balanced when the galvanometer reads zero, which requires

$$ \frac{R_1}{R_2} = \frac{R_3}{R_x}. $$

Solving for the unknown,

$$ R_x = R_3 \frac{R_2}{R_1}. $$

If $R_1 = 100\ \Omega$, $R_2 = 200\ \Omega$, and the null condition is achieved at $R_3 = 150\ \Omega$, then

$$ R_x = 150\ \Omega \times \frac{200}{100} = 300\ \Omega. $$

Because the galvanometer carries zero current at balance, the reading is independent of the galvanometer's internal resistance, and no current is diverted from the branch containing $R_x$.

Key Theorem

At the null condition of a Wheatstone bridge, the potential difference across the galvanometer branch is identically zero. Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the galvanometer loop confirms that this requires $V_{AD} = V_{BD}$, which reduces directly to the bridge balance condition $R_1 R_x = R_2 R_3$. Any deviation from this product equality produces a nonzero galvanometer deflection, making the null point detectable with high sensitivity.

🌸 Potentiometer

🌸 Potentiometer

Definition. A potentiometer is a three-terminal resistive device in which a sliding contact (wiper) divides a resistor of total resistance $R$ into two segments, providing a continuously variable voltage output. If the total length of the resistive element is $L$ and the wiper is displaced a distance $x$ from one end, the resistance of each segment is

$$ R_1 = \frac{x}{L}\,R, \qquad R_2 = \frac{L - x}{L}\,R. $$

When a source of EMF $\mathcal{E}$ (with negligible internal resistance) is connected across the full element, the potential difference tapped at the wiper relative to one terminal is

$$ V_{\text{out}} = \frac{x}{L}\,\mathcal{E}. $$

This linear relationship between wiper position and output voltage is the defining property of an ideal potentiometer.

Worked Example. A potentiometer has a total resistance of $500\,\Omega$ and is connected to a $9.0\,\text{V}$ battery. The wiper is set at $30\%$ of the total length from the negative terminal. Find the output voltage and the current drawn from the source.

Solution. The output voltage is

$$ V_{\text{out}} = 0.30 \times 9.0\,\text{V} = 2.7\,\text{V}. $$

Since the full resistance $R = 500\,\Omega$ remains across the source, the current drawn is

$$ I = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R} = \frac{9.0\,\text{V}}{500\,\Omega} = 18\,\text{mA}, $$

independent of wiper position when no load is connected.

Key Theorem — Null Method of EMF Comparison. In the classical Poggendorff potentiometer circuit, an unknown EMF $\mathcal{E}_x$ is balanced against the drop across a length $x_0$ of the wire when a sensitive galvanometer reads zero. At balance, no current flows through the galvanometer branch, so

$$ \mathcal{E}_x = \frac{x_0}{L}\,\mathcal{E}_{\text{ref}}, $$

where $\mathcal{E}_{\text{ref}}$ is a known standard cell. Because the galvanometer current vanishes at null, the measurement is independent of the internal resistances of either cell—a significant accuracy advantage over direct voltmeter methods.

Payoff 🎯

The potentiometer stands as the natural culminating instrument of electrostatic and circuit theory because it fuses the abstract principles of potential difference, Ohm's law, and resistive networks into a single, precise, and experimentally powerful device. Unlike a voltmeter, which draws current and therefore perturbs the circuit under measurement, the potentiometer operates at the null-balance condition—it measures electromotive force (EMF) by finding the point at which no current flows through the galvanometer branch. At this equilibrium, the unknown EMF $\varepsilon_x$ is related to the standard EMF $\varepsilon_s$ and the balance lengths $\ell_x$ and $\ell_s$ by

$$ \frac{\varepsilon_x}{\varepsilon_s} = \frac{\ell_x}{\ell_s}. $$

This ratio rests entirely on the uniformity of the resistance wire and the stability of the driver cell, making the measurement independent of the internal resistance of the source being tested—an elegance that no simple voltmeter can claim.

Worked Example. A standard Weston cell with $\varepsilon_s = 1.0183\,\text{V}$ balances at $\ell_s = 68.5\,\text{cm}$. An unknown cell balances at $\ell_x = 72.3\,\text{cm}$. Then

$$ \varepsilon_x = \varepsilon_s \cdot \frac{\ell_x}{\ell_s} = 1.0183 \times \frac{72.3}{68.5} \approx 1.0748\,\text{V}. $$

The calculation requires no knowledge of wire resistance per unit length, only the ratio of lengths—a direct consequence of the linear potential drop along a uniform resistor carrying steady current.

Key Theorem. The null-balance principle guarantees that at the balance point the terminal voltage of the unknown source equals its true EMF: since $I_{\text{galv}} = 0$, there is no voltage drop across the source's internal resistance $r$, so $V_{\text{terminal}} = \varepsilon_x - I_{\text{galv}}\,r = \varepsilon_x$. This is the formal justification for the potentiometer's superiority over direct voltmeter readings whenever internal resistance is non-negligible.

The potentiometer's null-balance logic reappears in Wheatstone bridges, lock-in amplifiers, and digital-to-analogue conversion networks. If you wish to see how this same principle scales to microelectronic precision voltage references—where the "wire" becomes a laser-trimmed resistor array on a silicon die—the study of bandgap reference circuits in analogue IC design offers a rewarding and technically rich extension of exactly the ideas developed here.
