🌱 Voltage

🌱 Voltage

Definition. Voltage, or electric potential difference, between two points A and B in an electric field is defined as the work done per unit positive charge by an external agent moving a test charge from A to B against the electric force:

$$ V_{AB} = V_A - V_B = \frac{W_{A \to B}}{q} $$

where $W_{A\to B}$ is the work done by the external agent and $q$ is the magnitude of the test charge. The SI unit of voltage is the volt (V), where $1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C}$. Equivalently, the electric potential at a point P due to a point charge $Q$ at distance $r$ is:

$$ V = \frac{kQ}{r} $$

where $k = 8.99 \times 10^9\,\text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant. Voltage is a scalar quantity, making superposition straightforward: the total potential at a point due to multiple charges is the algebraic sum of individual contributions.

Worked Example. Two point charges, $Q_1 = +3.0\,\mu\text{C}$ and $Q_2 = -1.5\,\mu\text{C}$, are fixed at positions $x = 0$ and $x = 4.0\,\text{cm}$, respectively. Find the electric potential at the midpoint $x = 2.0\,\text{cm}$.

The distance from each charge to the midpoint is $r = 0.020\,\text{m}$.

$$ V_1 = \frac{kQ_1}{r} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(3.0\times10^{-6})}{0.020} = +1.35\times10^6\,\text{V} $$ $$ V_2 = \frac{kQ_2}{r} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(-1.5\times10^{-6})}{0.020} = -6.74\times10^5\,\text{V} $$ $$ V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 = 1.35\times10^6 - 6.74\times10^5 \approx +6.8\times10^5\,\text{V} $$

Key Theorem. The relationship between electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and potential $V$ in one dimension is:

$$ E_x = -\frac{dV}{dx} $$

This states that the electric field points in the direction of decreasing potential and has magnitude equal to the spatial rate of change of potential. Equipotential surfaces are always perpendicular to field lines, and no work is done moving a charge along an equipotential.

🌱 Electric Current

🌱 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional surface. If a net charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface in a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}. $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limit

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt}. $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of conventional current is taken to be the direction in which positive charges flow, even though in metallic conductors it is free electrons (negative charges) that move in the opposite direction. Current is a scalar quantity with an associated direction along a conductor.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries $3.2 \times 10^{19}$ electrons past a cross-section every second. Determine the magnitude of the current.

Each electron carries charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, so the total charge transferred per second is

$$ \Delta Q = (3.2 \times 10^{19})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}) = 5.13\,\text{C}. $$

Since $\Delta t = 1\,\text{s}$,

$$ I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} = \frac{5.13\,\text{C}}{1\,\text{s}} \approx 5.1\,\text{A}. $$

Conventional current flows opposite to the electron drift, but its magnitude is $5.1\,\text{A}$.

Key Theorem — Current Conservation in a Series Path. For any cross-section of an unbranched conductor in steady state, the current $I$ is the same regardless of where along the conductor the measurement is taken. This follows directly from charge conservation: charge cannot accumulate inside a conductor in steady state, so the same quantity $\Delta Q$ must pass every cross-section in each interval $\Delta t$. Consequently, $I$ is a property of the branch, not of the particular cross-section chosen, which justifies treating current as a single scalar value throughout a series element.

🌱 Resistance

🌱 Resistance

Definition. Electrical resistance is the opposition a material offers to the flow of electric current. For a conductor carrying current $I$ under an applied potential difference $V$, the resistance $R$ is defined by Ohm's Law:

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} $$

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm ($\Omega$), where $1\,\Omega = 1\,\text{V}/\text{A}$. Resistance arises microscopically from collisions between conduction electrons and the ionic lattice of the material; each collision transfers kinetic energy to the lattice as thermal energy, producing Joule heating. For a uniform conductor of length $L$, cross-sectional area $A$, and resistivity $\rho$, resistance is given by:

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} $$

Resistivity $\rho$ is an intrinsic material property (units: $\Omega\cdot\text{m}$) that characterizes how strongly a material opposes current flow, depending on temperature, composition, and microstructure.

Worked Example. A copper wire ($\rho = 1.72\times10^{-8}\,\Omega\cdot\text{m}$) has length $L = 2.00\,\text{m}$ and circular cross-section of diameter $d = 1.00\,\text{mm}$. Find its resistance.

First, compute the cross-sectional area:

$$ A = \pi\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi\left(5.00\times10^{-4}\right)^2 = 7.854\times10^{-7}\,\text{m}^2 $$

Then apply the resistivity formula:

$$ R = \rho\frac{L}{A} = (1.72\times10^{-8})\frac{2.00}{7.854\times10^{-7}} = 4.38\times10^{-2}\,\Omega \approx 43.8\,\text{m}\Omega $$

This small value confirms that copper is an excellent conductor, making it suitable for household wiring.

Key Theorem. For a network of resistors in series, total resistance is additive: $R_{\text{series}} = \sum_i R_i$. For a parallel combination, the reciprocal resistances add: $R_{\text{parallel}}^{-1} = \sum_i R_i^{-1}$. These results follow directly from Kirchhoff's voltage and current laws and enable systematic analysis of arbitrarily complex resistive circuits.

🌱 Capacitance

🌱 Capacitance

Definition. A capacitor is a device that stores electric charge and energy in an electric field between two conducting surfaces. The capacitance $C$ of a capacitor is defined as the ratio of the magnitude of charge $Q$ stored on either conductor to the magnitude of the potential difference $V$ between them:

$$ C = \frac{Q}{V} $$

The SI unit of capacitance is the farad (F), where $1\ \text{F} = 1\ \text{C/V}$. Capacitance is a geometric property—it depends on the shape, size, and separation of the conductors, as well as the permittivity of the medium between them, not on $Q$ or $V$ individually.

For a parallel-plate capacitor with plate area $A$, plate separation $d$, and vacuum (or air) between the plates, the electric field is uniform and given by $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0$, where $\sigma = Q/A$ is the surface charge density. The potential difference is $V = Ed = Qd/(\varepsilon_0 A)$, which yields the standard result:

$$ C = \varepsilon_0 \frac{A}{d} $$

where $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{F/m}$ is the permittivity of free space.

Worked Example. A parallel-plate capacitor has square plates of side $0.10\ \text{m}$ separated by $d = 1.0\ \text{mm} = 1.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}$. Find the capacitance and the charge stored when connected to a $12\ \text{V}$ battery.

Solution. The plate area is $A = (0.10)^2 = 0.010\ \text{m}^2$. Then:

$$ C = \varepsilon_0 \frac{A}{d} = (8.85 \times 10^{-12})\frac{0.010}{1.0 \times 10^{-3}} = 88.5\ \text{pF} $$

The stored charge is:

$$ Q = CV = (88.5 \times 10^{-12})(12) = 1.06\ \text{nC} $$

Key Theorem. The energy $U$ stored in a capacitor is:

$$ U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{QV}{2} $$

This result follows directly from integrating the work done to transfer incremental charge $dq$ against the growing potential $q/C$: $U = \int_0^Q (q/C)\,dq = Q^2/(2C)$. The three equivalent forms are useful depending on which pair of quantities—$C$, $Q$, $V$—is known.

🍃 Ohms Law

🍃 Ohms Law

Definition. Ohm's Law states that the current $I$ through a conductor between two points is directly proportional to the potential difference $V$ across those points, provided the temperature and other physical conditions remain constant. Mathematically,

$$ V = IR $$

where $V$ is the voltage in volts (V), $I$ is the current in amperes (A), and $R$ is the resistance in ohms ($\Omega$). A conductor that obeys this relationship is called an ohmic material; one for which $R$ is not constant (e.g., a diode) is non-ohmic. Resistance itself arises from collisions between conduction electrons and the ionic lattice of the material, and for a uniform conductor of length $L$, cross-sectional area $A$, and resistivity $\rho$,

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A}. $$

Worked Example. A copper wire of resistivity $\rho = 1.68 \times 10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}$, length $L = 2.00\ \text{m}$, and diameter $d = 1.00\ \text{mm}$ is connected to a $9.00\ \text{V}$ battery. Find the current through the wire.

Step 1 — Resistance:

$$ A = \pi\!\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^{2} = \pi(0.500\times10^{-3})^{2} = 7.854\times10^{-7}\ \text{m}^{2} $$ $$ R = (1.68\times10^{-8})\frac{2.00}{7.854\times10^{-7}} = 4.28\times10^{-2}\ \Omega $$

Step 2 — Current:

$$ I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{9.00}{4.28\times10^{-2}} \approx 210\ \text{A} $$

This large current reflects copper's very low resistivity; in practice, such a wire would require a high-current source and appropriate heat dissipation.

Key Theorem. For a network of resistors in series, the equivalent resistance is $R_{\text{eq}} = \sum_i R_i$, while for resistors in parallel,

$$ \frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \sum_i \frac{1}{R_i}. $$

These results follow directly from applying Ohm's Law alongside Kirchhoff's voltage and current laws, and they underpin the analysis of all linear DC circuits.

🍃 Rc Circuit

🍃 RC Circuit

An RC circuit is a series network of a resistor ($R$, in ohms) and a capacitor ($C$, in farads) driven by an EMF source $\mathcal{E}$. Because the capacitor voltage builds as charge accumulates, the circuit response is inherently time-dependent: the charge and current evolve exponentially rather than settling instantaneously as in a purely resistive network.

Definition. Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the loop gives the governing equation:

$$ \mathcal{E} - \frac{q}{C} - R\frac{dq}{dt} = 0 $$

With the initial condition $q(0) = 0$, the solution is:

$$ q(t) = C\mathcal{E}\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad \tau \equiv RC $$

The quantity $\tau = RC$ (seconds) is the time constant: the single new primitive this section introduces. It sets the characteristic timescale of the exponential approach to equilibrium. After one time constant the capacitor holds approximately $63.2\%$ of its final charge $Q_{\max} = C\mathcal{E}$; the current $I(t) = (\mathcal{E}/R)\,e^{-t/\tau}$ decays on the same scale.

Worked Example. Given $\mathcal{E} = 12\,\text{V}$, $R = 4.7\,\text{k}\Omega$, $C = 100\,\mu\text{F}$:

$$ \tau = (4.7 \times 10^3)(100 \times 10^{-6}) = 0.47\,\text{s} $$

The maximum charge is $Q_{\max} = (100 \times 10^{-6})(12) = 1.2\,\text{mC}$. At $t = \tau$, $q = 1.2(1 - e^{-1}) \approx 0.758\,\text{mC}$. The initial current is $I(0) = 12/(4.7 \times 10^3) \approx 2.55\,\text{mA}$, falling exponentially toward zero. Both observables are entirely determined by $\tau$.

Key Theorem. The energy stored in the capacitor at full charge is $U_C = \frac{1}{2}C\mathcal{E}^2$, and the energy dissipated resistively during the charging process is also $\frac{1}{2}C\mathcal{E}^2$, independent of $R$. Consequently, regardless of how large or small $\tau$ is, exactly half the source energy is stored and half is dissipated as heat.

🍃 Rc Time Constant

🍃 RC Time Constant

Definition. When a resistor of resistance $R$ (in ohms) is connected in series with a capacitor of capacitance $C$ (in farads), the system possesses a characteristic timescale called the RC time constant, defined as

$$ \tau = RC. $$

The quantity $\tau$ has units of seconds ($\Omega \cdot \mathrm{F} = \mathrm{s}$) and governs the rate at which charge accumulates on the capacitor. During charging through a voltage source $\mathcal{E}$, the voltage across the capacitor evolves as

$$ V_C(t) = \mathcal{E}\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right). $$

All other time-dependent quantities in the circuit—current, charge, voltage across the resistor—are likewise parameterized by $\tau$, making it the single most descriptive quantity of an RC circuit's transient response.

Worked Example. A $12\,\mathrm{V}$ battery is connected in series with a $4.7\,\mathrm{k\Omega}$ resistor and a $100\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ capacitor. Find $\tau$, the voltage across the capacitor at $t = \tau$, and the time required for $V_C$ to reach $10\,\mathrm{V}$.

Step 1: Compute the time constant.

$$ \tau = RC = (4700\,\Omega)(100 \times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{F}) = 0.47\,\mathrm{s}. $$

Step 2: Voltage at $t = \tau$.

$$ V_C(\tau) = 12\!\left(1 - e^{-1}\right) \approx 12(0.6321) \approx 7.59\,\mathrm{V}. $$

At exactly one time constant, the capacitor has charged to approximately $63.2\%$ of the supply voltage—a direct consequence of the factor $1 - e^{-1}$.

Step 3: Time to reach $10\,\mathrm{V}$.

$$ 10 = 12(1 - e^{-t/\tau}) \implies e^{-t/\tau} = \frac{1}{6} \implies t = \tau\ln 6 \approx (0.47)(1.792) \approx 0.842\,\mathrm{s}. $$

Key Theorem. After $n$ time constants, the capacitor voltage differs from its final value $\mathcal{E}$ by a factor of $e^{-n}$. Consequently, after $5\tau$ the voltage is within $0.67\%$ of $\mathcal{E}$, which is conventionally taken as "fully charged" in engineering practice. This exponential convergence is a direct implication of the first-order linear differential equation governing the circuit, whose solution is uniquely determined by $\tau$.

Compare your result to the theoretical prediction $\tau_{\mathrm{th}} = RC$ and compute the percent discrepancy, accounting for component tolerances.

🍃 Electromotive Force

🍃 Electromotive Force

Definition. The electromotive force (EMF), denoted $\mathcal{E}$, of a source is the work done per unit charge by non-electrostatic forces (chemical, mechanical, or electromagnetic) in moving a positive test charge completely around a closed circuit. Formally,

$$ \mathcal{E} = \frac{dW_{\text{non-elec}}}{dq} $$

with SI unit of volts (V = J/C). Despite its name, EMF is not a force but an energy-per-charge quantity, entirely analogous to potential difference. A real source also possesses internal resistance $r$, so the terminal voltage — the potential difference available at its output terminals — is

$$ V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E} - Ir $$

where $I$ is the current drawn. When $I = 0$ (open circuit), $V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E}$; under load, the terminal voltage is reduced by the resistive drop $Ir$ across the source itself.

Worked Example. A battery rated $\mathcal{E} = 12.0\ \text{V}$ has internal resistance $r = 0.50\ \Omega$ and drives current through an external resistor $R = 5.50\ \Omega$. Find the current and terminal voltage.

Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the single loop:

$$ \mathcal{E} = I(R + r) \implies I = \frac{12.0}{5.50 + 0.50} = \frac{12.0}{6.00} = 2.00\ \text{A} $$

The terminal voltage is then

$$ V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E} - Ir = 12.0 - (2.00)(0.50) = 11.0\ \text{V} $$

The 1.0 V drop across the internal resistance represents power $P_r = I^2 r = 2.0\ \text{W}$ dissipated inside the source.

Key Theorem. For a source with EMF $\mathcal{E}$ and internal resistance $r$ connected to external resistance $R$, maximum power is delivered to the external circuit when $R = r$ (the maximum power transfer theorem). Under this condition,

$$ P_{\max} = \frac{\mathcal{E}^2}{4r} $$

This result follows directly from differentiating $P = I^2 R = \mathcal{E}^2 R/(R+r)^2$ with respect to $R$ and setting $dP/dR = 0$.

🍃 Capacitor Charging

🍃 Capacitor Charging

Definition. When a capacitor of capacitance $C$ is connected in series with a resistor $R$ and a DC source of emf $\mathcal{E}$, charge accumulates on the capacitor plates over time. The instantaneous charge $q(t)$ obeys the first-order linear differential equation obtained by applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the loop:

$$ \mathcal{E} - \frac{q}{C} - iR = 0, \quad i = \frac{dq}{dt} $$

Substituting and rearranging gives

$$ R\frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{q}{C} = \mathcal{E}. $$

Solving with initial condition $q(0) = 0$ yields

$$ q(t) = C\mathcal{E}\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad \tau = RC, $$

where $\tau$ is the time constant. The voltage across the capacitor and the charging current follow directly:

$$ V_C(t) = \mathcal{E}\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad i(t) = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R}e^{-t/\tau}. $$

Worked Example. A $12\,\text{V}$ battery charges a $C = 470\,\mu\text{F}$ capacitor through $R = 10\,\text{k}\Omega$. Find (a) the time constant, (b) the voltage across the capacitor at $t = 5\,\text{s}$, and (c) the initial charging current.

(a) $\tau = RC = (10\times10^{3})(470\times10^{-6}) = 4.70\,\text{s}$.

(b) $V_C(5) = 12\!\left(1 - e^{-5/4.70}\right) = 12\!\left(1 - e^{-1.064}\right) \approx 12(1 - 0.345) \approx 7.86\,\text{V}$.

(c) $i(0) = \mathcal{E}/R = 12/(10^4) = 1.2\,\text{mA}$.

Key Theorem. At $t = \tau$, the capacitor has reached $1 - e^{-1} \approx 63.2\%$ of its final charge $Q_\infty = C\mathcal{E}$. The capacitor is considered fully charged after approximately $5\tau$, at which point $V_C \approx 99.3\%\,\mathcal{E}$ and the current is negligibly small. The time constant $\tau = RC$ therefore serves as the single characteristic scale governing the entire transient response: a larger resistance slows the current and extends charging; a larger capacitance increases the total charge required and likewise extends the process. Altering either component changes $\tau$ proportionally, while the shape of the exponential approach to equilibrium remains universal.

🍃 Capacitor Discharging

🍃 Capacitor Discharging

Definition. Capacitor discharging describes the process by which a charged capacitor releases its stored energy through a resistor, with the charge, voltage, and current decaying exponentially over time. In a simple RC circuit consisting of a capacitor $C$ and resistor $R$, once the external voltage source is removed, Kirchhoff's voltage law yields:

$$ V_C(t) = V_0 \, e^{-t/\tau} $$

where $V_0$ is the initial voltage across the capacitor and $\tau = RC$ is the time constant of the circuit. The charge and current follow analogous forms:

$$ Q(t) = Q_0 \, e^{-t/\tau}, \qquad I(t) = \frac{V_0}{R} \, e^{-t/\tau} $$

The time constant $\tau$ governs the rate of decay: after one time constant, the voltage falls to $e^{-1} \approx 36.8\%$ of its initial value; after five time constants, the capacitor is considered effectively discharged.

Worked Example. A $200\,\mu\text{F}$ capacitor is charged to $12\,\text{V}$ and then connected to a $50\,\text{k}\Omega$ resistor. Determine the time constant and the voltage after $10\,\text{s}$.

$$ \tau = RC = (50 \times 10^3\,\Omega)(200 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{F}) = 10\,\text{s} $$ $$ V(10\,\text{s}) = 12 \cdot e^{-10/10} = 12 \cdot e^{-1} \approx 12 \times 0.368 = 4.42\,\text{V} $$

The initial discharge current is $I_0 = V_0/R = 12/(50\times10^3) = 0.24\,\text{mA}$, decaying with the same time constant.

Key Theorem. The energy dissipated in the resistor during a complete discharge equals the energy originally stored in the capacitor. Starting from $U = \tfrac{1}{2}CV_0^2$, integrating the instantaneous power $P(t) = I^2 R$ over all time confirms:

$$ \int_0^{\infty} I^2 R \, dt = \int_0^{\infty} \frac{V_0^2}{R} e^{-2t/\tau} dt = \frac{1}{2}CV_0^2 $$

This result underscores that RC circuits are purely dissipative during discharge — no energy is returned to a source.

🌸 Rc Timing Applications

🌸 Rc Timing Applications

Definition. An RC circuit consists of a resistor $R$ and capacitor $C$ connected in series with a voltage source. When the source is switched on, the capacitor charges exponentially; when disconnected, it discharges similarly. The governing differential equation for charging is

$$ V_C(t) = V_0\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), $$

and for discharging,

$$ V_C(t) = V_0\, e^{-t/\tau}, $$

where $V_0$ is the initial (or supply) voltage and $\tau = RC$ is the time constant, measured in seconds when $R$ is in ohms and $C$ in farads. After one time constant, a charging capacitor reaches approximately $63.2\%$ of $V_0$; after five time constants, it is effectively fully charged ($\approx 99.3\%$).

Worked Example. Suppose a camera flash circuit uses $R = 1.0\,\mathrm{k\Omega}$ and $C = 800\,\mathrm{\mu F}$. The time constant is

$$ \tau = RC = (1.0 \times 10^3\,\Omega)(800 \times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{F}) = 0.80\,\mathrm{s}. $$

If the capacitor must charge to at least $90\%$ of the $300\,\mathrm{V}$ supply before the flash fires, find the minimum wait time. Setting $V_C = 0.90 V_0$:

$$ 0.90 = 1 - e^{-t/0.80} \implies e^{-t/0.80} = 0.10 \implies t = -0.80\ln(0.10) \approx 1.84\,\mathrm{s}. $$

The photographer must wait roughly $1.84\,\mathrm{s}$ between flashes.

Key Theorem. The energy stored in a fully charged capacitor is $U = \tfrac{1}{2}CV_0^2$, and the total energy dissipated in the resistor during a complete charge cycle equals $U$ as well — regardless of $R$. Consequently, reducing $R$ speeds charging but does not reduce energy loss; it only increases peak current $I_0 = V_0/R$.

Compare your experimental $\tau$ with the theoretical value $\tau = 1.0\,\mathrm{s}$ and compute the percent error.

Payoff 🎯

RC Timing Applications represents the natural culmination of circuit analysis in College Physics 2E Ch21 because it transforms abstract exponential mathematics into measurable, controllable physical behavior. Every prior concept in the chapter—charge accumulation, voltage–current relationships in capacitors, and the time-domain response of resistive networks—converges here into a single, unified framework for designing systems that govern when things happen.

Definition and Core Result

An RC circuit driven by a step input voltage $V_0$ charges its capacitor according to

$$ V_C(t) = V_0\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad \tau = RC, $$

where $\tau$ is the time constant. The voltage reaches approximately $63.2\%$ of $V_0$ at $t = \tau$, and is considered fully charged after $5\tau$. By selecting $R$ and $C$ precisely, a designer specifies $\tau$ and thereby controls the elapsed time between an input event and a threshold crossing—the essential mechanism of a timer.

Worked Example

Suppose a circuit requires a 2.0 ms delay before triggering a downstream comparator set at $V_{\text{th}} = 0.75\, V_0$. Solving for the trigger time:

$$ 0.75 = 1 - e^{-t/\tau} \implies t = -\tau \ln(0.25) \approx 1.386\,\tau. $$

Setting $1.386\,\tau = 2.0\,\text{ms}$ gives $\tau \approx 1.44\,\text{ms}$. Choosing $C = 0.1\,\mu\text{F}$, one obtains $R = \tau/C \approx 14.4\,\text{k}\Omega$—standard resistor values suffice.

Key Theorem

The threshold-crossing time scales logarithmically with the fractional trigger level $f = V_{\text{th}}/V_0$:

$$ t_{\text{trigger}} = -RC\ln(1 - f). $$

This relation guarantees that arbitrarily long or short delays are achievable by varying a single passive component, without altering the circuit topology.

Lab Exercise

Assemble an RC circuit with $R = 10\,\text{k}\Omega$ and $C = 100\,\mu\text{F}$. Apply a 5 V step and record $V_C(t)$ at 100 ms intervals using a digital multimeter. Plot your measurements against the theoretical curve $V_C(t) = 5(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$, extract $\tau$ from the data, and compare it with the nominal value $RC = 1.0\,\text{s}$. Quantify any discrepancy and attribute it to component tolerance.
