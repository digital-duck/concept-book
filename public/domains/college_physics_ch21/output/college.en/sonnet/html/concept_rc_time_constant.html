🍃 Rc Time Constant

🍃 RC Time Constant

Definition. When a resistor of resistance $R$ (in ohms) is connected in series with a capacitor of capacitance $C$ (in farads), the system possesses a characteristic timescale called the RC time constant, defined as

$$ \tau = RC. $$

The quantity $\tau$ has units of seconds ($\Omega \cdot \mathrm{F} = \mathrm{s}$) and governs the rate at which charge accumulates on the capacitor. During charging through a voltage source $\mathcal{E}$, the voltage across the capacitor evolves as

$$ V_C(t) = \mathcal{E}\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right). $$

All other time-dependent quantities in the circuit—current, charge, voltage across the resistor—are likewise parameterized by $\tau$, making it the single most descriptive quantity of an RC circuit's transient response.

Worked Example. A $12\,\mathrm{V}$ battery is connected in series with a $4.7\,\mathrm{k\Omega}$ resistor and a $100\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ capacitor. Find $\tau$, the voltage across the capacitor at $t = \tau$, and the time required for $V_C$ to reach $10\,\mathrm{V}$.

Step 1: Compute the time constant.

$$ \tau = RC = (4700\,\Omega)(100 \times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{F}) = 0.47\,\mathrm{s}. $$

Step 2: Voltage at $t = \tau$.

$$ V_C(\tau) = 12\!\left(1 - e^{-1}\right) \approx 12(0.6321) \approx 7.59\,\mathrm{V}. $$

At exactly one time constant, the capacitor has charged to approximately $63.2\%$ of the supply voltage—a direct consequence of the factor $1 - e^{-1}$.

Step 3: Time to reach $10\,\mathrm{V}$.

$$ 10 = 12(1 - e^{-t/\tau}) \implies e^{-t/\tau} = \frac{1}{6} \implies t = \tau\ln 6 \approx (0.47)(1.792) \approx 0.842\,\mathrm{s}. $$

Key Theorem. After $n$ time constants, the capacitor voltage differs from its final value $\mathcal{E}$ by a factor of $e^{-n}$. Consequently, after $5\tau$ the voltage is within $0.67\%$ of $\mathcal{E}$, which is conventionally taken as "fully charged" in engineering practice. This exponential convergence is a direct implication of the first-order linear differential equation governing the circuit, whose solution is uniquely determined by $\tau$.

Lab Exercise. Assemble an RC circuit with $R = 10\,\mathrm{k\Omega}$ and $C = 47\,\mu\mathrm{F}$. Using a voltage probe and data-acquisition interface, record $V_C(t)$ during charging from a $5\,\mathrm{V}$ source. Fit the data to $V_C(t) = A(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$ and extract $\tau_{\mathrm{exp}}$. Compare your result to the theoretical prediction $\tau_{\mathrm{th}} = RC$ and compute the percent discrepancy, accounting for component tolerances.
