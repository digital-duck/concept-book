🍃 Kirchhoffs Junction Rule

🍃 Kirchhoff's Junction Rule

Definition

Kirchhoff's Junction Rule, also called the Current Law (KCL), states that the algebraic sum of all electric currents entering and leaving any junction (node) in an electrical circuit equals zero. Mathematically:

$$ \sum_{k} I_k = 0 $$

where currents directed into the junction are taken as positive and currents directed out of the junction are taken as negative (or vice versa, provided the convention is applied consistently). This rule is a direct consequence of the conservation of electric charge: charge cannot accumulate at a node in steady-state conditions, so the rate at which charge arrives must equal the rate at which it departs.

Worked Example

Consider a junction where three wires meet. Wire 1 carries a current $I_1 = 3\,\text{A}$ into the junction, wire 2 carries $I_2 = 5\,\text{A}$ into the junction, and wire 3 carries current $I_3$ away from the junction. Applying the Junction Rule with the convention that inward currents are positive:

$$ I_1 + I_2 - I_3 = 0 $$ $$ 3 + 5 - I_3 = 0 $$ $$ I_3 = 8\,\text{A} $$

The current leaving through wire 3 is $8\,\text{A}$, consistent with charge conservation.

Key Theorem

For any circuit with $n$ nodes, the Junction Rule applied at each node yields $n$ equations; however, only $n - 1$ of these equations are linearly independent. The redundant equation arises because each branch current appears in exactly two nodal equations (once with a positive sign, once with a negative sign), making the sum of all nodal equations identically zero. In practice, one junction equation is always discarded when solving a system, and the remaining equations are combined with Kirchhoff's Voltage Law equations to form a complete, solvable system.

Lab Exercise

Construct a circuit containing a $9\,\text{V}$ battery connected to three parallel resistors: $R_1 = 10\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 20\,\Omega$, and $R_3 = 30\,\Omega$. Using a multimeter, measure the current through each branch and record your values as $I_1$, $I_2$, and $I_3$. At the main junction where all three branches meet, verify that $I_1 + I_2 + I_3$ equals the total current $I_\text{total}$ drawn from the battery. Quantify any experimental discrepancy and identify possible sources of error such as measurement uncertainty introduced by the multimeter's own internal resistance when inserted in series with each branch.
