🌸 Voltmeter

🌸 Voltmeter

Definition. A voltmeter is an instrument that measures the electric potential difference (voltage) between two points in a circuit. An ideal voltmeter has infinite internal resistance, ensuring that it draws no current from the branch under measurement and thus does not disturb the circuit's operating conditions. In practice, analog voltmeters achieve high but finite resistance through a galvanometer connected in series with a large multiplier resistor, while digital voltmeters employ high-impedance operational amplifier inputs.

The governing principle follows directly from Ohm's Law. A galvanometer of full-scale deflection current $I_g$ and coil resistance $r_g$ can be converted into a voltmeter for a maximum voltage $V_{\max}$ by inserting a series multiplier resistance $R_m$ satisfying

$$ V_{\max} = I_g (R_m + r_g). $$

Solving for the multiplier:

$$ R_m = \frac{V_{\max}}{I_g} - r_g. $$

Worked Example. A galvanometer has $I_g = 50\,\mu\text{A}$ and $r_g = 100\,\Omega$. Determine the series resistance required to construct a voltmeter with a full-scale reading of $V_{\max} = 10\,\text{V}$.

$$ R_m = \frac{10}{50 \times 10^{-6}} - 100 = 200{,}000 - 100 = 199{,}900\,\Omega \approx 200\,\text{k}\Omega. $$

The total voltmeter resistance is $R_V = R_m + r_g = 200\,\text{k}\Omega$, confirming that the meter draws only $50\,\mu\text{A}$ at full scale — negligible compared to typical branch currents.

Key Theorem (Loading Error). When a voltmeter of finite resistance $R_V$ is placed across a resistor $R$ in a circuit with Thévenin source $V_{Th}$ and Thévenin resistance $R_{Th}$, the measured voltage is

$$ V_{\text{meas}} = V_{Th} \cdot \frac{R \,\|\, R_V}{R_{Th} + R \,\|\, R_V}, $$

where $R \,\|\, R_V = \frac{R R_V}{R + R_V}$. The fractional loading error decreases as $R_V / R \to \infty$, which is why maximizing $R_V$ is the central design objective.

Lab Exercise. Assemble a voltage divider using $R_1 = 10\,\text{k}\Omega$ and $R_2 = 10\,\text{k}\Omega$ powered by a $9\,\text{V}$ source. Calculate the ideal output voltage across $R_2$. Then measure it with a voltmeter rated at $R_V = 20\,\text{k}\Omega$ and compute the percentage loading error. Compare your theoretical prediction to the experimental reading and discuss how replacing the voltmeter with one rated at $R_V = 1\,\text{M}\Omega$ reduces this discrepancy.
