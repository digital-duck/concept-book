🍃 Null Measurement

🍃 Null Measurement

Definition

A null measurement is an experimental technique in which an unknown quantity is determined by adjusting a known, calibrated quantity until the measuring instrument reads exactly zero—the null condition. Rather than inferring the unknown from a deflection or reading on the detector, the experimenter drives the detector to its zero point, at which the detector draws no current and therefore does not disturb the circuit under test. This elimination of instrument loading is the defining advantage of null methods over direct-deflection methods.

The two canonical implementations in DC circuit analysis are the Wheatstone bridge (null measurement of resistance) and the potentiometer (null measurement of EMF). Both share the same logic: the circuit is balanced so that no current flows through the galvanometer branch, and the unknown is then expressed in terms of the known calibration values.

Worked Example

Consider a Wheatstone bridge with known resistors $R_1$ and $R_2$ in one voltage-divider arm and a calibrated variable resistor $R_3$ in the other. The unknown resistance $R_x$ occupies the fourth position. A galvanometer connects the two midpoints. The bridge is balanced when the galvanometer reads zero, which requires

$$ \frac{R_1}{R_2} = \frac{R_3}{R_x}. $$

Solving for the unknown,

$$ R_x = R_3 \frac{R_2}{R_1}. $$

If $R_1 = 100\ \Omega$, $R_2 = 200\ \Omega$, and the null condition is achieved at $R_3 = 150\ \Omega$, then

$$ R_x = 150\ \Omega \times \frac{200}{100} = 300\ \Omega. $$

Because the galvanometer carries zero current at balance, the reading is independent of the galvanometer's internal resistance, and no current is diverted from the branch containing $R_x$.

Key Theorem

At the null condition of a Wheatstone bridge, the potential difference across the galvanometer branch is identically zero. Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law around the galvanometer loop confirms that this requires $V_{AD} = V_{BD}$, which reduces directly to the bridge balance condition $R_1 R_x = R_2 R_3$. Any deviation from this product equality produces a nonzero galvanometer deflection, making the null point detectable with high sensitivity.

Lab Exercise

Assemble a Wheatstone bridge using two fixed resistors ($R_1 = 470\ \Omega$, $R_2 = 1\,\mathrm{k}\Omega$), a decade resistance box as $R_3$, and an unknown carbon resistor as $R_x$. Adjust $R_3$ until the galvanometer reads zero, then calculate $R_x$ from the balance condition. Compare the null-measurement result with an ohmmeter reading and discuss sources of discrepancy, including contact resistance and ohmmeter loading effects.
