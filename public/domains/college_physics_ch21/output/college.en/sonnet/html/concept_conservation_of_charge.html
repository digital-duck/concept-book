🌱 Conservation Of Charge

🌱 Conservation Of Charge

Definition. The conservation of electric charge is a fundamental law of nature stating that the total electric charge of an isolated system remains constant over time. Formally, if a system is isolated from external charge exchange, then

$$ Q_{\text{total}} = \sum_i q_i = \text{constant}, $$

where $q_i$ denotes the charge of each constituent particle or object. Charge is neither created nor destroyed; it may only be transferred between objects or transformed between particle–antiparticle pairs, always in equal and opposite amounts. Electric charge is quantized in integer multiples of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, and every observed process respects this conservation law without exception.

Worked Example. A neutral rubber rod is rubbed against a neutral wool cloth. After contact, the rod acquires a net charge of $q_{\text{rod}} = -8.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Determine the charge remaining on the cloth.

Since both objects were initially neutral, the total charge of the system before contact is

$$ Q_{\text{initial}} = 0 + 0 = 0. $$

By conservation of charge, the total charge after contact must satisfy

$$ Q_{\text{final}} = q_{\text{rod}} + q_{\text{cloth}} = 0. $$

Solving,

$$ q_{\text{cloth}} = -q_{\text{rod}} = +8.0\ \mu\text{C}. $$

The cloth carries $+8.0\ \mu\text{C}$, consistent with electrons being transferred from wool to rubber.

Key Theorem. Conservation of charge is a consequence of gauge invariance in electromagnetism. By Noether's theorem, the invariance of the electromagnetic Lagrangian under a global $U(1)$ phase transformation $\psi \to e^{i\alpha}\psi$ yields a conserved Noether current $J^\mu$, whose time component gives the conserved charge $Q = \int J^0\, d^3x$. This connection elevates charge conservation from an empirical rule to a deep symmetry principle of nature. The corresponding local continuity equation,

$$ \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0, $$

expresses charge conservation in differential form: any decrease in local charge density must be accompanied by a diverging current density.

Lab Exercise. Charge an aluminum foil strip by contact with a charged glass rod, then touch it momentarily to an identical uncharged strip while both are suspended on insulating threads. Measure the deflection angle of each strip using a protractor and estimate the charge on each from Coulomb's law. Verify that the sum of the two measured charges equals the original charge on the first strip, confirming conservation of charge within experimental uncertainty.
