Distance

When you check a map app for directions, the straight-line distance between two points is almost never the route you drive — yet that straight line is the most fundamental measurement in all of geometry. Understanding distance precisely lets us quantify closeness, define circles, and eventually build calculus.

Definition. Given two points $P = (x_1, y_1)$ and $Q = (x_2, y_2)$ in the Euclidean plane, the distance between them is

$$d(P, Q) = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.$$

Worked example. Find the distance between $A = (1, 2)$ and $B = (4, 6)$.

$$d(A, B) = \sqrt{(4 - 1)^2 + (6 - 2)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5.$$

Why it's true. The formula is a direct application of the Pythagorean theorem. Draw a right triangle whose hypotenuse connects $A$ to $B$. The horizontal leg has length $|x_2 - x_1| = 3$ and the vertical leg has length $|y_2 - y_1| = 4$. By the Pythagorean theorem, the hypotenuse satisfies $c^2 = 3^2 + 4^2 = 25$, so $c = 5$. This argument works for any two points: the coordinate differences give the legs, and the theorem gives the hypotenuse — which is exactly the straight-line distance.

Notice that the formula always returns a non-negative number, and swapping the two points doesn't change the result — both facts follow from squaring the differences before taking the square root. These properties match our intuition: a distance is never negative, and the distance from $A$ to $B$ is the same as from $B$ to $A$.

Because the formula relies only on coordinate differences, it generalizes cleanly: in three dimensions you add a $(z_2 - z_1)^2$ term under the radical, in four dimensions another term, and so on. But the core idea — sum the squared differences, then take the square root — remains the Pythagorean theorem at work.

Practice problem. A triangle has vertices at $P = (0, 0)$, $Q = (3, 0)$, and $R = (0, 4)$. Compute all three side lengths and confirm that no single side is longer than the sum of the other two.

Point

Every map begins with a single pin. Before you can draw a route, measure a distance, or outline a region, you need a way to say where. In geometry, that role belongs to the point — the simplest and most fundamental object in the entire subject.

Definition. A point is a location in space that has no size, no shape, and no dimension. It has position and nothing else. We label points with capital letters and represent them as dots on the page, but the dot is only a visual aid — a true point is infinitely small.

Formally, in a coordinate system, a point in the plane is an ordered pair $P = (x, y)$ where $x, y \in \mathbb{R}$. In three-dimensional space, a point is an ordered triple $P = (x, y, z)$.

Worked Example. Consider the point $A = (3, 5)$. This tells us $A$ sits exactly 3 units along the horizontal axis and 5 units along the vertical axis from the origin $O = (0, 0)$. The point $A$ occupies that single location — it contains no length, no area, nothing to measure except its coordinates.

Now consider $B = (3, 5)$ labeled differently. Despite the different letter, $A$ and $B$ are the same point because they share identical coordinates. A point's identity is its position: two points are equal if and only if their corresponding coordinates match. Conversely, $C = (5, 3)$ is a different point from $A$, because order matters in an ordered pair — $(3, 5) \neq (5, 3)$.

Why it's true. Euclid opens the Elements with: "A point is that which has no part." This is not a theorem but an axiom — an irreducible starting agreement. We accept it because every geometric object (lines, planes, solids) can be described as collections of points, yet no geometric object can be decomposed into anything simpler than a point. It is the atom of geometry.

Practice Problem. A point $P$ in the plane has coordinates $(a,\; 2a - 1)$. If $P$ is the same point as $Q = (4, 7)$, find $a$ and verify that both coordinates match.

Circle

A manhole cover, a coin, the rim of a coffee cup — circles appear everywhere because they solve a fundamental problem: enclosing the most area with the least boundary. Understanding the circle precisely is the gateway to trigonometry, analytic geometry, and calculus.

Definition. A circle is the set of all points in a plane that are equidistant from a fixed point called the center. That fixed distance is the radius $r$. If the center is at $(h, k)$, the circle is the set

$$\{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : (x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2\}.$$

This equation is called the standard form. It captures the circle completely: the center and radius are read directly from the equation, and every point on the curve is exactly $r$ units from $(h, k)$.

Worked example. Find the center and radius of the circle given by

$$x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0.$$

We complete the square in each variable:

$$(x^2 - 6x + 9) + (y^2 + 4y + 4) = 12 + 9 + 4,$$ $$(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25.$$

Matching this against $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$, we read off center $(3, -2)$ and $r^2 = 25$, giving $r = 5$.

Why the equation works. The standard form is a direct application of the Euclidean distance formula. The distance from any point $(x, y)$ to the center $(h, k)$ is $\sqrt{(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2}$. Setting this equal to $r$ and squaring both sides eliminates the radical, producing exactly the standard-form equation. Every point satisfying the equation lies at distance $r$ from the center — no more, no less — which is precisely the geometric definition of a circle. Conversely, any point closer or farther than $r$ fails the equation and lies off the curve. The algebra and the geometry say the same thing.

Practice problem. A circle has center $(-1, 3)$ and passes through the point $(2, 7)$. Determine the radius and write the equation in standard form.

Payoff

A wheel rolls, a planet orbits, a ripple spreads — and behind each of these stands one idea: the circle. Every point at the same distance from a center. That single constraint, elegant and absolute, generates the most symmetric figure in the plane and unlocks an extraordinary range of mathematics and engineering.

Definition. A circle is the set of all points in a plane at a fixed distance $r$ (the radius) from a fixed point $C$ (the center):

$$\mathcal{C} = \{P \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \|P - C\| = r\}.$$

In Cartesian coordinates with $C = (h, k)$, this becomes $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$.

Worked example. Find the circumference and area of a circle with radius $r = 5$. The circumference is $C = 2\pi r = 10\pi \approx 31.42$ units. The area is $A = \pi r^2 = 25\pi \approx 78.54$ square units. These formulas emerge from the constant-distance property: $\pi$ itself is defined as the ratio of any circle's circumference to its diameter.

Why this is the natural endpoint. The circle sits at the intersection of every thread we have built. Distance gives us the radius. Angles give us positions along the arc. Coordinates let us write its equation. Symmetry explains why a circle has the largest area for a given perimeter — an isoperimetric optimality that no other shape achieves. The constant $\pi$ bridges arithmetic and geometry, appearing whenever rotation, periodicity, or curvature enters a problem. In short, the circle is where measurement, algebra, and geometric reasoning converge.

Where circles lead. Wrap a number line around a circle and you discover trigonometry — sine and cosine as coordinates of a point at angle $\theta$. Let a circle's radius vary and you enter conic sections: ellipses, parabolas, hyperbolas. Track a point on a rolling circle and you meet cycloids, the curves that solve the brachistochrone problem. In physics, circular motion underpins orbital mechanics and wave theory. In engineering, every gear, bearing, and antenna pattern begins with the geometry of $r$.

Practice problem. A circular pond has area $50\,\text{m}^2$. Find its radius and the length of a fence enclosing it.

Pick one application — trigonometry, orbital mechanics, or conic sections — and trace how the circle's constant-radius property generates its core ideas. The circle is not an ending; it is the launchpad.
