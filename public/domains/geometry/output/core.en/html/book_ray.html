Concept Book: Ray

Geometry · Generated by SPL

Payoff

The ray is the minimal geometric object that simultaneously encodes position and direction — two of the three primitives on which virtually all of Euclidean geometry, linear algebra, and physics rest (the third being distance). Every construction encountered in this book — from the angle between two vectors to the notion of a half-space in convex analysis — can be expressed cleanly once the concept of a ray is fully in hand.

Definition (Ray). Let $P \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a fixed point and $\mathbf{d} \in \mathbb{R}^n \setminus \{\mathbf{0}\}$ a direction vector. The ray emanating from $P$ in direction $\mathbf{d}$ is the set

$$ \mathcal{R}(P,\,\mathbf{d}) \;=\; \{\, P + t\,\mathbf{d} \;\mid\; t \geq 0 \,\}. $$

Choosing $t = 0$ returns the endpoint $P$; as $t \to \infty$ the ray extends without bound. Crucially, $\mathcal{R}(P, \mathbf{d}) = \mathcal{R}(P, \lambda\mathbf{d})$ for any $\lambda > 0$, so only the direction class $[\mathbf{d}]$ — not the magnitude — carries geometric content.

Worked Example. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, let $P = (1,\,2)$ and $\mathbf{d} = (3,\,-1)$. A point $Q$ lies on $\mathcal{R}(P,\mathbf{d})$ if and only if $Q = (1+3t,\;2-t)$ for some $t \geq 0$. The ray crosses the $x$-axis when $2 - t = 0$, giving $t^{} = 2$ and crossing point $Q^{} = (7,\,0)$. This ray–hyperplane intersection — solve the parametric equation, check $t^{*} \geq 0$ — is the core subroutine underlying rendering pipelines, collision detection, and linear-programming pivots alike.

Key Theorem (Cone Decomposition). A set $C \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is a convex cone if and only if it is a union of rays through the origin:

$$ C = \bigcup_{\mathbf{v} \in C} \mathcal{R}(\mathbf{0},\,\mathbf{v}). $$

Proof sketch. ($\Rightarrow$) For any $\mathbf{v} \in C$ and $t > 0$, the conic property gives $t\mathbf{v} \in C$, so $\mathcal{R}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{v}) \subset C$. ($\Leftarrow$) Any union of rays through the origin is closed under positive scaling; convexity follows from forming conic combinations of two such rays. $\square$

This theorem is the bridge from elementary geometry to Farkas's lemma, dual cones, and the optimality conditions of linear programming — all developed in the chapters ahead.

Lab Cell (SymPy).

from sympy import symbols, Matrix, solve

t = symbols('t', nonneg=True)
P, d = Matrix([1, 2]), Matrix([3, -1])
Q = P + t * d

t_star = solve(Q[1], t)[0]          # x-axis crossing
print("t* =", t_star)               # 2
print("Q* =", Q.subs(t, t_star).T)  # [7, 0]

Of the paths that open from here, none is more immediately rewarding than ray tracing in computer graphics, where Whitted's 1980 algorithm reduces global illumination to a recursive tree of ray–surface intersections — each one a direct instance of the parametric equation above.
