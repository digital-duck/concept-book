Concept Book: Coordinate Plane

Geometry · Generated by SPL

Payoff

Definition

The coordinate plane (or Cartesian plane) is the set $\mathbb{R}^2 = \{(x, y) \mid x, y \in \mathbb{R}\}$, equipped with the standard basis $\{\mathbf{e}_1 = (1,0),\, \mathbf{e}_2 = (0,1)\}$ and the Euclidean metric $d\bigl((x_1,y_1),(x_2,y_2)\bigr) = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$. Every point, curve, and transformation in the plane is fully determined by two real numbers — a radical compression of geometric intuition into algebraic precision.

This is why the coordinate plane is the natural culmination of the concepts that precede it. Real numbers supply the continuum; functions supply the rules of dependence; vectors supply direction and magnitude; linear transformations supply the language of change. The coordinate plane is the stage on which all of these actors perform simultaneously.

Worked Example

Consider the line $\ell$ passing through $P_1 = (1, 2)$ and $P_2 = (4, 6)$. The direction vector is $\mathbf{v} = P_2 - P_1 = (3, 4)$, with length $\|\mathbf{v}\| = 5$. The parametric form $\ell(t) = (1 + 3t,\; 2 + 4t)$ places every point on $\ell$ in correspondence with $t \in \mathbb{R}$, converting a geometric object into an algebraic one — distance becomes arithmetic, intersection becomes equation-solving.

Key Theorem

Theorem (Rigid Motions of $\mathbb{R}^2$). Every distance-preserving map $T : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ is a composition of at most three reflections, and can be written as $T(\mathbf{x}) = R\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{t}$, where $R \in O(2)$ (an orthogonal matrix) and $\mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{R}^2$. Proof sketch: any isometry fixing the origin is linear and norm-preserving, hence orthogonal; translation accounts for the remaining degree of freedom. $\square$

Application: Computer Graphics

Computer graphics inherits the coordinate plane wholesale. Every sprite, polygon, and camera frustum lives in $\mathbb{R}^2$ or its homogeneous extension $\mathbb{P}^2$. Rotation by angle $\theta$ is the matrix $R_\theta = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$; translation by $(t_x, t_y)$ becomes a single matrix multiply in homogeneous coordinates, enabling GPU pipelines to chain transforms with constant-time multiplication.

Lab Cell (SymPy)

from sympy import symbols, cos, sin, Matrix, pi, simplify

theta = symbols('theta')
R = Matrix([[cos(theta), -sin(theta)],
            [sin(theta),  cos(theta)]])
p = Matrix([3, 0])                        # point on x-axis
rotated = simplify(R * p)
print("Rotated point:", rotated)          # [3cos θ, 3sin θ]
print("Norm preserved:", simplify((R*p).norm()))  # 3

The coordinate plane is not merely a backdrop — it is the first complete language for space. To see how rotation matrices grow into the quaternion algebra driving every modern 3-D game engine, turn to Chapter 9: Computer Graphics.
