🍃 Trigonometric Ratios

Imagine you’re a surveyor tasked with determining the height of a tall building. You can't simply climb it! Instead, you use angles and distances – tools that form the foundation of trigonometry. These ratios provide a way to relate the sides of right-angled triangles to their angles, allowing us to solve for unknown lengths and angles.

Definition: Trigonometric ratios are ratios of the lengths of the sides of a right-angled triangle based on one of its acute angles. The three primary trigonometric ratios are sine (sin), cosine (cos), and tangent (tan). Let’s denote an angle within the right-angled triangle as $\theta$.

  • Sine: $\sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$ – This represents the ratio of the length of the side opposite to the angle $\theta$ to the longest side (the hypotenuse).
  • Cosine: $\cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$ – The cosine is the ratio of the adjacent side (to the angle $\theta$) to the hypotenuse.
  • Tangent: $\tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} $ - This represents the ratio of the length of the side opposite to the angle $\theta$ to the adjacent side.

    • Worked Example: Let’s say we have a right-angled triangle where the angle $\theta = 30^\circ$, and the hypotenuse has a length of $10$. We want to find the length of the side opposite to $\theta$. Using sine: $\sin(30^\circ) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{10}$ $\frac{1}{2} = \frac{\text{opposite}}{10}$ Therefore, the opposite side has a length of $5$.

    Why it's true: These ratios arise from fundamental geometric principles. The sine, cosine, and tangent functions are defined based on the definitions of similar triangles formed within the right-angled triangle. The angles in a right-angled triangle always sum to 90 degrees, which allows us to establish these proportional relationships precisely.

    Practice Problem: A ladder leaning against a wall makes an angle of $60^\circ$ with the ground. If the foot of the ladder is 4 meters away from the base of the wall, how high up the wall does the ladder reach?
