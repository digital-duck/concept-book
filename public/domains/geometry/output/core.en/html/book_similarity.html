Concept Book: Similarity

Payoff

Definition

Two figures $F$ and $F'$ are similar ($F \sim F'$) if there exists a bijective map $\phi: F \to F'$ that is a composition of isometries and a uniform scaling by factor $k > 0$, such that for every pair of points $P, Q \in F$,

$$|{\phi(P)\,\phi(Q)}| = k\,|PQ|.$$

Similarity is the natural endpoint of this textbook because it is the coarsest equivalence relation that preserves shape. Congruence (covered earlier) preserves shape and size; similarity relaxes the size constraint while retaining every angle and every ratio of lengths. This single relaxation is what makes similarity the operative notion in virtually every field that must reason about scaled representations of real objects.

Worked Example

Let $\triangle ABC$ have sides $a = 3$, $b = 4$, $c = 5$ and let $\triangle A'B'C'$ have sides $a' = 6$, $b' = 8$, $c' = 10$. Since $a'/a = b'/b = c'/c = 2$, the Side-Side-Side (SSS) similarity criterion confirms $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle A'B'C'$ with ratio $k = 2$. The angles are identical: $\angle C = \angle C' = 90°$. The area scales by $k^2 = 4$: if $[ABC] = 6$, then $[A'B'C'] = 24$.

Key Theorem

Theorem (AA Similarity). If two angles of one triangle are congruent to two angles of another, the triangles are similar.

Proof sketch. Let $\angle A = \angle A'$ and $\angle B = \angle B'$. By the angle-sum identity $\angle C = \pi - \angle A - \angle B = \angle C'$. Map $\triangle ABC$ to $\triangle A'B'C'$ by aligning vertex $A$ with $A'$ and rotating until $AB \parallel A'B'$; the required scaling factor is $k = A'B' / AB$. All three side ratios equal $k$ by the properties of parallel transversals. $\blacksquare$

Connections to Application Domains

Architecture and construction. Every blueprint is a similar image of the building it describes. A floor plan drawn at scale $1:50$ satisfies $k = 1/50$; load calculations use the fact that structural cross-sections scale as $k^2$ while volumes (and thus material costs) scale as $k^3$.

Computer graphics. The model-view-projection pipeline applies a sequence of similarity transforms to three-dimensional meshes. Uniform scaling of a mesh by $k$ is a similarity; non-uniform scaling breaks similarity but can be decomposed into a similarity followed by a shear. Level-of-detail algorithms exploit the fact that a model and its lower-resolution proxy are approximately similar, bounding geometric error by $\varepsilon = (1 - k)\,\text{diam}(F)$.

Art and design. The golden ratio $\varphi = (1 + \sqrt{5})/2$ generates a self-similar rectangle: removing a square from a $\varphi \times 1$ rectangle leaves a $1 \times 1/\varphi$ rectangle similar to the original. This recursive similarity underlies the aesthetic proportions found in classical architecture, typography, and compositional grids.

Lab Cell (SymPy)

from sympy import symbols, sqrt, simplify, Rational

k = symbols('k', positive=True)

# Verify golden-ratio self-similarity
phi = (1 + sqrt(5)) / 2
ratio_after_cut = 1 / (phi - 1)           # side ratio of remaining rectangle
print(simplify(ratio_after_cut - phi))     # should print 0

# Scale a right triangle and confirm area ratio
a, b = Rational(3), Rational(4)
area_original = a * b / 2
area_scaled   = (2*a) * (2*b) / 2
print(area_scaled / area_original)         # should print 4

Similarity is, in this sense, the hinge between abstract geometry and the physical world. Every discipline above reduces a complex real object to a scaled, angle-preserving proxy — and then reasons confidently in that proxy.
