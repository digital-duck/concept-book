Concept Book: Polygon

Geometry · Generated by SPL

Payoff

A polygon is a closed planar figure bounded by a finite sequence of line segments $e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n$, where each segment $e_i$ connects vertex $v_i$ to $v_{i+1}$ (indices modulo $n$), and no two non-adjacent edges intersect. The integer $n \geq 3$ is the polygon's order. Every concept encountered in this book — point, line, angle, congruence, similarity, area — was, in a precise sense, preparation for this object.

The polygon is not merely one more figure; it is the universal approximator of planar shape. Any smooth closed curve $\gamma$ in the plane can be approximated to arbitrary precision by an inscribed polygon: subdivide $\gamma$ into $n$ arcs of equal arc-length and connect successive subdivision points. As $n \to \infty$, the perimeter of the inscribed polygon converges to the arc-length of $\gamma$, and its area converges to the area enclosed by $\gamma$. This is the geometric fact underlying every numerical integration rule and every mesh-based simulation.

Worked example. Let $\gamma$ be the unit circle. An inscribed regular $n$-gon has vertices at $v_k = (\cos\frac{2\pi k}{n},\, \sin\frac{2\pi k}{n})$. Its perimeter is $P_n = n \cdot 2\sin\frac{\pi}{n}$. Since $\lim_{n\to\infty} n\sin\frac{\pi}{n} = \pi$, we obtain $\lim_{n\to\infty} P_n = 2\pi$, recovering the circumference of the unit circle from purely polygonal data.

Key theorem (Shoelace formula). For a simple polygon with vertices $(x_1,y_1),\ldots,(x_n,y_n)$ listed in counterclockwise order,

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\left|\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i y_{i+1} - x_{i+1} y_i)\right|,$$

where indices are taken modulo $n$. The proof follows directly from Green's theorem applied to the vector field $\mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{2}(-y, x)$, whose curl is identically $1$.

from sympy import Rational, Abs
from sympy.geometry import Point, Polygon

# Regular hexagon inscribed in the unit circle
hex_pts = [Point(Rational(1,1)*__import__('sympy').cos(k*__import__('sympy').pi/3),
                 __import__('sympy').sin(k*__import__('sympy').pi/3))
           for k in range(6)]
hex_poly = Polygon(*hex_pts)
print("Area:", hex_poly.area)          # 3*sqrt(3)/2
print("Perimeter:", hex_poly.perimeter)  # 6

Every branch of mathematics and computation that must represent shape — computational geometry, finite-element analysis, computer graphics, geographic information systems — begins precisely here. The polygon is where abstraction meets the physical world: a mesh is a tiling by triangles (3-gons); a convex hull is a polygon enclosing a point set; a Voronoi cell is a convex polygon dual to a data point. Having mastered polygons, you are invited now to venture into triangulation: the art of decomposing any polygon into triangles, which is the algorithmic heart of everything from GPU rendering pipelines to structural stress analysis in civil engineering.
