🍃 Bremsstrahlung

🍃 Bremsstrahlung

Definition. Bremsstrahlung (German: braking radiation) is electromagnetic radiation emitted when a charged particle undergoes acceleration—most commonly deceleration—due to interaction with the Coulomb field of an atomic nucleus. When an electron of kinetic energy $K_0$ enters a material and is deflected by a nucleus of charge $Ze$, it loses kinetic energy $\Delta K$, which is radiated as a photon of frequency $\nu$ satisfying

$$ h\nu = \Delta K. $$

Because the deceleration can take any value from zero up to a complete stop, the emitted spectrum is continuous. The maximum photon energy occurs when the electron surrenders all its kinetic energy in a single collision:

$$ h\nu_{\max} = K_0 = eV, $$

where $V$ is the accelerating potential used to produce the electron beam. This upper limit defines the Duane–Hunt short-wavelength cutoff:

$$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV} = \frac{1240 \text{ eV·nm}}{eV}. $$

Worked example. An X-ray tube operates at $V = 40.0\text{ kV}$. Find the shortest wavelength in its bremsstrahlung spectrum.

$$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV} = \frac{1240\text{ eV·nm}}{40{,}000\text{ eV}} = 0.0310\text{ nm} = 31.0\text{ pm}. $$

This falls in the hard X-ray range, confirming that higher tube voltages shift the cutoff toward shorter, more penetrating wavelengths.

Key theorem (Duane–Hunt Law). The minimum wavelength of bremsstrahlung radiation produced by electrons accelerated through potential $V$ is

$$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV}, $$

independent of the target material. This result provided one of the earliest direct measurements of Planck's constant $h$ from purely macroscopic electrical quantities, reinforcing the photon model of radiation.

Lab exercise. Set up a simulated X-ray tube at three voltages: $20\text{ kV}$, $40\text{ kV}$, and $80\text{ kV}$. For each voltage, (a) calculate $\lambda_{\min}$ using the Duane–Hunt Law, (b) sketch the expected continuous spectrum indicating the cutoff and the broad intensity peak near $\lambda \approx 1.5\,\lambda_{\min}$, and (c) determine how doubling the voltage affects $\lambda_{\min}$. Compare your sketches and verify that the short-wavelength cutoff scales inversely with voltage, consistent with the prediction that $\lambda_{\min} V = hc/e = \text{const}$.
