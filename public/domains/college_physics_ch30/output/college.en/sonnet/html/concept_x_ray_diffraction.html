🌸 X Ray Diffraction

🌸 X Ray Diffraction

Definition. X-ray diffraction is the elastic scattering of X-rays by the periodic atomic planes of a crystalline solid, producing constructive or destructive interference patterns that reveal the crystal's lattice geometry. Because X-ray wavelengths ($\lambda \sim 0.01$–$10\,\text{nm}$) are comparable to interatomic spacings ($d \sim 0.1$–$1\,\text{nm}$), crystals act as three-dimensional diffraction gratings. The governing relation is Bragg's Law:

$$ 2d\sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $d$ is the interplanar spacing, $\theta$ is the glancing angle (measured from the reflecting plane, not the normal), and $m$ is the diffraction order. Constructive interference occurs only when the path-length difference between rays reflected from adjacent planes equals an integer number of wavelengths.

Worked Example. A beam of X-rays with $\lambda = 0.154\,\text{nm}$ (Cu $K_\alpha$ radiation) strikes a sodium chloride crystal. The principal planes have spacing $d = 0.282\,\text{nm}$. Find the first-order Bragg angle.

$$ \sin\theta = \frac{m\lambda}{2d} = \frac{(1)(0.154\,\text{nm})}{2(0.282\,\text{nm})} = 0.273 $$ $$ \theta = \arcsin(0.273) \approx 15.8° $$

The detector should therefore be positioned to receive the reflected beam at a glancing angle of $15.8°$ relative to the crystal surface, or equivalently at a scattering angle $2\theta \approx 31.6°$ from the incident direction.

Key Theorem — von Laue Condition. A more general formulation states that diffraction occurs when the change in wave vector $\Delta\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{k}_f - \mathbf{k}_i$ equals a reciprocal-lattice vector $\mathbf{G}$:

$$ \Delta\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{G} $$

Bragg's law is a one-dimensional projection of this vector condition onto a single family of planes. The full von Laue condition is essential for indexing diffraction patterns from crystals with complex unit cells, and it establishes the equivalence between real-space periodicity and momentum-space peak positions.

Lab Exercise. Using a powder diffractometer with $\lambda = 0.179\,\text{nm}$, an experimenter records peaks at $2\theta = 38.2°$, $44.4°$, and $64.6°$. Calculate the interplanar spacings $d$ for each peak using Bragg's law ($m = 1$), and identify which cubic planes (Miller indices $hkl$ with $d_{hkl} = a/\sqrt{h^2+k^2+l^2}$) they correspond to, given the lattice parameter $a = 0.405\,\text{nm}$ for aluminum.
