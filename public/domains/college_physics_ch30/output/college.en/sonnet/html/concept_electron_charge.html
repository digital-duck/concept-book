🍃 Electron Charge

Definition. The electron charge, denoted $e$, is the fundamental quantum of electric charge carried by a single electron. Its accepted value is

$$ e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C} $$

where C denotes coulombs. The electron carries a charge of $-e$ (negative by convention), while the proton carries $+e$. All observable electric charges are integer multiples of $e$, making it the elementary charge unit. This quantization of charge is a foundational principle of atomic and subatomic physics, establishing that charge cannot be subdivided below this threshold in ordinary matter.

Worked Example. Consider a conducting sphere that has lost $3.0 \times 10^{12}$ electrons. Determine the net charge on the sphere.

Each lost electron removes a charge of $-e$, so removing $n$ electrons increases the net charge by $+ne$. Thus:

$$ Q = n \cdot e = (3.0 \times 10^{12})(1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) $$ $$ Q = 4.81 \times 10^{-7} \text{ C} = 0.481 \; \mu\text{C} $$

The sphere carries a net positive charge of approximately $0.48 \; \mu\text{C}$, consistent with a deficit of electrons.

Key Theorem — Charge Quantization. Millikan's oil-drop experiment (1909–1913) provided the first precise measurement of $e$ and established quantization empirically. In that experiment, the equilibrium condition for a charged oil droplet suspended in a uniform electric field $E$ is:

$$ qE = mg $$

where $m$ is the droplet mass and $g$ is gravitational acceleration. By varying the field and measuring multiple droplets, Millikan found that the charge $q$ on any droplet was always an integer multiple of a smallest value, which he identified as $e$. Modern quantum electrodynamics explains charge quantization through gauge symmetry and the topology of the gauge group, but the experimental result stands independent of theory.

Lab Exercise. Using a parallel-plate capacitor with plate separation $d = 5.0 \text{ mm}$ and voltage $V = 49 \text{ V}$, calculate the electric field $E = V/d$ between the plates. Then determine the mass of a singly charged oil droplet (charge $= e$) that would be held in equilibrium. Compare your result to a droplet radius of approximately $1 \; \mu\text{m}$ using $m = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \rho$ with $\rho = 900 \text{ kg/m}^3$, and comment on whether equilibrium is physically achievable.
