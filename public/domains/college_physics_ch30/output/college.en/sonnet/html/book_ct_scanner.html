🌱 Atom

🌱 Atom

Definition. An atom is the smallest electrically neutral unit of an element that retains the chemical identity of that element. Each atom consists of a dense, positively charged nucleus—containing $Z$ protons (atomic number) and $N$ neutrons (mass number $A = Z + N$)—surrounded by a cloud of $Z$ electrons. The nuclear radius scales approximately as $r \approx r_0 A^{1/3}$, where $r_0 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-15}\ \text{m}$, making the nucleus roughly $10^5$ times smaller than the atomic radius ($\sim 10^{-10}\ \text{m}$). Electrons occupy discrete energy levels characterized by the principal quantum number $n$, which determines both the orbital radius and the energy of the electron in the hydrogen atom.

Worked Example. Consider a hydrogen atom ($Z = 1$) with its single electron in the $n = 2$ state. The Bohr model gives the orbital radius:

$$ r_n = n^2 a_0, \quad a_0 = 0.529\ \text{Å} $$

so $r_2 = 4a_0 = 2.116\ \text{Å}$. The corresponding energy is:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2} $$

yielding $E_2 = -3.4\ \text{eV}$. When this electron transitions to the ground state ($n = 1$, $E_1 = -13.6\ \text{eV}$), the emitted photon carries energy:

$$ \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = 10.2\ \text{eV} $$

corresponding to a wavelength $\lambda = hc/\Delta E \approx 121.6\ \text{nm}$, the Lyman-$\alpha$ line in the ultraviolet.

Key Theorem (Bohr Quantization). Stable electron orbits satisfy the angular momentum condition $L = m_e v r = n\hbar$, where $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ and $\hbar = h/2\pi$. Combined with the Coulomb force providing centripetal acceleration:

$$ \frac{ke^2}{r^2} = \frac{m_e v^2}{r} $$

these two conditions uniquely determine the quantized radii and energies above, predicting the hydrogen spectrum to high accuracy.

🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how a particle participates in electromagnetic interactions. Charge is quantized: every observable free charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$. By convention, the proton carries charge $+e$ and the electron carries charge $-e$. The SI unit of charge is the coulomb (C). The total electric charge of an isolated system is conserved — a principle called conservation of charge — meaning charge can neither be created nor destroyed, only transferred between objects.

The electrostatic force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is given by Coulomb's Law:

$$ \vec{F} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2$ is the Coulomb constant, related to the permittivity of free space by $k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$.

Worked Example. Two small spheres carry charges $q_1 = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and $q_2 = -1.5\ \mu\text{C}$ and are placed $0.20\ \text{m}$ apart. Find the magnitude and character of the force.

$$ |\vec{F}| = k_e \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(1.5 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ |\vec{F}| = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{4.5 \times 10^{-12}}{0.040} \approx 1.01\ \text{N} $$

Because the charges are opposite in sign, the force is attractive.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Charge). For any closed system, $\sum q_i = \text{constant}$. In pair production, for example, a photon creates an electron–positron pair $(q = -e,\ q = +e)$; the net charge remains zero, consistent with the photon's zero charge.

🍃 Electron

🍃 Electron

Definition. The electron is a fundamental subatomic particle with a rest mass of $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, an electric charge of $q_e = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, and an intrinsic spin quantum number of $s = \tfrac{1}{2}$. As a lepton, it does not participate in the strong nuclear interaction. In atomic structure, electrons occupy discrete energy levels determined by their quantum state; the Pauli exclusion principle prevents two electrons in the same atom from sharing an identical quantum state, directly governing the shell structure underlying the periodic table. Both quantum numbers and the exclusion principle are treated as established background here; this section focuses on the wave nature of the electron as the key new idea.

Worked Example. Consider a hydrogen atom with its electron in the ground state ($n = 1$). The total energy is given by the Bohr formula:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2} $$

For $n = 1$: $E_1 = -13.6\ \text{eV}$. If the electron absorbs a photon and transitions to $n = 3$, the required photon energy is:

$$ \Delta E = E_3 - E_1 = -\frac{13.6}{9}\ \text{eV} - (-13.6\ \text{eV}) = 13.6\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{9}\right)\!\text{eV} = 12.09\ \text{eV} $$

The corresponding wavelength is:

$$ \lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{12.09 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 102.6\ \text{nm} $$

This ultraviolet photon belongs to the Lyman series, consistent with observed hydrogen spectra.

Key Theorem (de Broglie Relation). An electron with momentum $p = m_e v$ possesses an associated wavelength:

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{m_e v} $$

This wave-particle duality was confirmed experimentally by Davisson and Germer in 1927, who observed diffraction of electrons from a nickel crystal lattice. The relation justifies the Bohr quantization condition $2\pi r = n\lambda$ and underpins the full quantum mechanical treatment of atomic orbitals.

Lab Exercise. Using a cathode ray tube with accelerating potential $V$, measure the deflection radius $r$ of an electron beam in a perpendicular magnetic field $B$. 🌱 Photon

🌱 Photon

Definition. A photon is the elementary quantum of electromagnetic radiation — a massless, chargeless particle that carries energy and momentum in discrete packets. For monochromatic radiation of frequency $\nu$, each photon carries energy

$$ E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}, $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant, $c$ is the speed of light in vacuum, and $\lambda$ is the wavelength. The photon's relativistic momentum is

$$ p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{h\nu}{c}. $$

Although the photon has zero rest mass, it propagates at speed $c$ in vacuum and carries angular momentum (spin) of $\hbar$, consistent with its classification as a spin-1 boson.

Worked Example. Ultraviolet light illuminates a metallic surface in a photoelectric experiment. The incident radiation has wavelength $\lambda = 200\ \text{nm}$ and the work function of the metal is $\phi = 4.70\ \text{eV}$. Find the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted electrons.

First, compute the photon energy:

$$ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.626\times10^{-34})(3.00\times10^8)}{200\times10^{-9}} = 9.94\times10^{-19}\ \text{J} = 6.21\ \text{eV}. $$

Applying Einstein's photoelectric equation,

$$ K_{\max} = E - \phi = 6.21\ \text{eV} - 4.70\ \text{eV} = 1.51\ \text{eV}. $$

Key Theorem (Einstein's Photoelectric Law). When a photon of energy $h\nu$ is absorbed by a bound electron in a material with work function $\phi$, the maximum kinetic energy of the ejected photoelectron is

$$ K_{\max} = h\nu - \phi, $$

provided $h\nu \geq \phi$; otherwise no emission occurs regardless of light intensity. This result — confirmed experimentally by Millikan — established that energy exchange between radiation and matter is quantized at the single-photon level, earning Einstein the 1921 Nobel Prize in Physics.

Extract the work function $\phi$ from the intercept and compare with the accepted value for your electrode material.

🍃 Bremsstrahlung

🍃 Bremsstrahlung

Definition. Bremsstrahlung (German: braking radiation) is electromagnetic radiation emitted when a charged particle undergoes acceleration—most commonly deceleration—due to interaction with the Coulomb field of an atomic nucleus. When an electron of kinetic energy $K_0$ enters a material and is deflected by a nucleus of charge $Ze$, it loses kinetic energy $\Delta K$, which is radiated as a photon of frequency $\nu$ satisfying

$$ h\nu = \Delta K. $$

Because the deceleration can take any value from zero up to a complete stop, the emitted spectrum is continuous. The maximum photon energy occurs when the electron surrenders all its kinetic energy in a single collision:

$$ h\nu_{\max} = K_0 = eV, $$

where $V$ is the accelerating potential used to produce the electron beam. This upper limit defines the Duane–Hunt short-wavelength cutoff:

$$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV} = \frac{1240 \text{ eV·nm}}{eV}. $$

Worked example. An X-ray tube operates at $V = 40.0\text{ kV}$. Find the shortest wavelength in its bremsstrahlung spectrum.

$$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV} = \frac{1240\text{ eV·nm}}{40{,}000\text{ eV}} = 0.0310\text{ nm} = 31.0\text{ pm}. $$

This falls in the hard X-ray range, confirming that higher tube voltages shift the cutoff toward shorter, more penetrating wavelengths.

Key theorem (Duane–Hunt Law). The minimum wavelength of bremsstrahlung radiation produced by electrons accelerated through potential $V$ is

$$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV}, $$

independent of the target material. This result provided one of the earliest direct measurements of Planck's constant $h$ from purely macroscopic electrical quantities, reinforcing the photon model of radiation.

Lab exercise. Set up a simulated X-ray tube at three voltages: $20\text{ kV}$, $40\text{ kV}$, and $80\text{ kV}$. For each voltage, (a) calculate $\lambda_{\min}$ using the Duane–Hunt Law, (b) sketch the expected continuous spectrum indicating the cutoff and the broad intensity peak near $\lambda \approx 1.5\,\lambda_{\min}$, and (c) determine how doubling the voltage affects $\lambda_{\min}$. Compare your sketches and verify that the short-wavelength cutoff scales inversely with voltage, consistent with the prediction that $\lambda_{\min} V = hc/e = \text{const}$.

🌸 Ct Scanner

🌸 CT Scanner

Definition. A computed tomography (CT) scanner is a diagnostic imaging device that reconstructs a three-dimensional map of internal tissue density by measuring the attenuation of X-ray beams transmitted through a patient from multiple projection angles. The physical basis is the Beer–Lambert law: for a monoenergetic X-ray beam of initial intensity $I_0$ traversing a path of length $L$ through a medium of linear attenuation coefficient $\mu$,

$$ I = I_0 \, e^{-\mu L}. $$

When the beam passes through heterogeneous tissue, the exponent becomes a line integral, $\int \mu(x,y)\, ds$, and the reconstruction problem is to recover the two-dimensional function $\mu(x,y)$ from a complete set of such projections—a mathematical task accomplished by filtered back-projection or iterative algorithms based on the Radon transform.

Worked Example. A CT beam enters a patient along a horizontal path and passes sequentially through 3.0 cm of soft tissue ($\mu_1 = 0.20\ \text{cm}^{-1}$) and 1.5 cm of compact bone ($\mu_2 = 0.48\ \text{cm}^{-1}$). Find the transmitted fraction $I/I_0$.

$$ \ln\!\left(\frac{I}{I_0}\right) = -\!\left(\mu_1 d_1 + \mu_2 d_2\right) = -\!\left[(0.20)(3.0) + (0.48)(1.5)\right] = -\!(0.60 + 0.72) = -1.32 $$ $$ \frac{I}{I_0} = e^{-1.32} \approx 0.267. $$

Roughly 27% of the beam exits; the detector records this reduction, contributing one data point to the sinogram from which the image is reconstructed.

Key Theorem. The Fourier Slice Theorem states that the one-dimensional Fourier transform of a projection at angle $\theta$ equals the two-dimensional Fourier transform of $\mu(x,y)$ evaluated along a line through the origin at that same angle. Formally, if $P_\theta(t)$ is the projection, then $\hat{P}_\theta(\omega) = \hat{\mu}(\omega\cos\theta,\, \omega\sin\theta)$. This theorem is the theoretical foundation of all frequency-domain reconstruction methods and guarantees that a sufficient angular sampling of projections uniquely determines $\mu(x,y)$.

Payoff 🎯

The computed tomography (CT) scanner stands as the natural culmination of Chapter 30's exploration of atomic and nuclear physics because it synthesizes X-ray production, photon attenuation, and quantum detection into a single diagnostic instrument capable of resolving three-dimensional internal structure without surgical intervention. Where earlier sections established the photoelectric effect, Bremsstrahlung radiation, and the exponential attenuation law $I = I_0 e^{-\mu x}$, the CT scanner deploys all three simultaneously: a rotating X-ray tube generates a polychromatic beam, tissue attenuates it according to its linear attenuation coefficient $\mu$, and a detector ring records transmitted intensity. Tomographic reconstruction then inverts the measured projections into a spatial map of $\mu(x, y, z)$, yielding grayscale images in Hounsfield units $\text{HU} = 1000 \times \frac{\mu_{\text{tissue}} - \mu_{\text{water}}}{\mu_{\text{water}}}$.

Worked Example. A 120 kVp CT beam passes through 20 cm of soft tissue ($\mu \approx 0.20\ \text{cm}^{-1}$) and 2 cm of cortical bone ($\mu \approx 0.48\ \text{cm}^{-1}$). The transmitted fraction is:

$$ \frac{I}{I_0} = e^{-(0.20)(20)} e^{-(0.48)(2)} = e^{-4.0} \cdot e^{-0.96} \approx 0.018 \cdot 0.383 \approx 0.007 $$

Roughly 0.7 % of photons survive, and the detector's measured deficit encodes the sum $\int \mu\, dl$ along that ray — one entry in the sinogram that filtered back-projection will invert.

Key Theorem. The Fourier Slice Theorem guarantees that the one-dimensional Fourier transform of a parallel projection at angle $\theta$ equals a central slice through the two-dimensional Fourier transform of the object $f(x,y)$ at the same angle. This mathematical identity is what makes exact reconstruction possible from finitely many projections, connecting the physics of attenuation to the algorithm of image formation.
