🌱 Atom

🌱 Atom

Definition. An atom is the smallest electrically neutral unit of an element that retains the chemical identity of that element. Each atom consists of a dense, positively charged nucleus—containing $Z$ protons (atomic number) and $N$ neutrons (mass number $A = Z + N$)—surrounded by a cloud of $Z$ electrons. The nuclear radius scales approximately as $r \approx r_0 A^{1/3}$, where $r_0 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-15}\ \text{m}$, making the nucleus roughly $10^5$ times smaller than the atomic radius ($\sim 10^{-10}\ \text{m}$). Electrons occupy discrete energy levels characterized by the principal quantum number $n$, which determines both the orbital radius and the energy of the electron in the hydrogen atom.

Worked Example. Consider a hydrogen atom ($Z = 1$) with its single electron in the $n = 2$ state. The Bohr model gives the orbital radius:

$$ r_n = n^2 a_0, \quad a_0 = 0.529\ \text{Å} $$

so $r_2 = 4a_0 = 2.116\ \text{Å}$. The corresponding energy is:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2} $$

yielding $E_2 = -3.4\ \text{eV}$. When this electron transitions to the ground state ($n = 1$, $E_1 = -13.6\ \text{eV}$), the emitted photon carries energy:

$$ \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = 10.2\ \text{eV} $$

corresponding to a wavelength $\lambda = hc/\Delta E \approx 121.6\ \text{nm}$, the Lyman-$\alpha$ line in the ultraviolet.

Key Theorem (Bohr Quantization). Stable electron orbits satisfy the angular momentum condition $L = m_e v r = n\hbar$, where $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ and $\hbar = h/2\pi$. Combined with the Coulomb force providing centripetal acceleration:

$$ \frac{ke^2}{r^2} = \frac{m_e v^2}{r} $$

these two conditions uniquely determine the quantized radii and energies above, predicting the hydrogen spectrum to high accuracy.

to identify each transition and calculate the Rydberg constant from your measurements. Compare your experimental value with the accepted value and estimate the percent error.

🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how a particle participates in electromagnetic interactions. Charge is quantized: every observable free charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$. By convention, the proton carries charge $+e$ and the electron carries charge $-e$. The SI unit of charge is the coulomb (C). The total electric charge of an isolated system is conserved — a principle called conservation of charge — meaning charge can neither be created nor destroyed, only transferred between objects.

The electrostatic force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is given by Coulomb's Law:

$$ \vec{F} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2$ is the Coulomb constant, related to the permittivity of free space by $k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$.

Worked Example. Two small spheres carry charges $q_1 = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and $q_2 = -1.5\ \mu\text{C}$ and are placed $0.20\ \text{m}$ apart. Find the magnitude and character of the force.

$$ |\vec{F}| = k_e \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(1.5 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ |\vec{F}| = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{4.5 \times 10^{-12}}{0.040} \approx 1.01\ \text{N} $$

Because the charges are opposite in sign, the force is attractive.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Charge). For any closed system, $\sum q_i = \text{constant}$. In pair production, for example, a photon creates an electron–positron pair $(q = -e,\ q = +e)$; the net charge remains zero, consistent with the photon's zero charge.

🍃 Atomic Nucleus

🍃 Atomic Nucleus

Definition. The atomic nucleus is the dense, positively charged core of an atom, composed of protons and neutrons (collectively called nucleons) bound together by the strong nuclear force. For a nucleus with atomic number $Z$ (proton count) and mass number $A$ (total nucleon count), the neutron number is $N = A - Z$. The nucleus is conventionally denoted $^A_Z\text{X}$, where X is the chemical symbol. Nuclear radii follow the empirical relation

$$ r = r_0 A^{1/3}, \quad r_0 \approx 1.2 \text{ fm}, $$

where $1 \text{ fm} = 10^{-15} \text{ m}$. This scaling implies that nuclear volume is proportional to $A$, reflecting an approximately constant nucleon density of roughly $2.3 \times 10^{17} \text{ kg/m}^3$.

Worked Example. Estimate the radius and density of the iron-56 nucleus, $^{56}_{26}\text{Fe}$.

Using the radius formula:

$$ r = (1.2 \text{ fm})(56)^{1/3} = (1.2 \text{ fm})(3.83) \approx 4.6 \text{ fm}. $$

The nuclear mass is approximately $56 \times 1.67 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg} = 9.35 \times 10^{-26} \text{ kg}$. The volume is:

$$ V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 = \frac{4}{3}\pi (4.6 \times 10^{-15})^3 \approx 4.1 \times 10^{-43} \text{ m}^3. $$

The density is therefore $\rho \approx 2.3 \times 10^{17} \text{ kg/m}^3$, confirming that all nuclei share nearly the same density regardless of $A$.

Key Theorem: Nuclear Binding Energy. The total mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of its constituent nucleon masses. This mass defect $\Delta m$ corresponds to the binding energy $B$ via Einstein's relation:

$$ B = \Delta m \, c^2 = \bigl[Z m_p + N m_n - m_\text{nuc}\bigr]c^2. $$

The binding energy per nucleon, $B/A$, peaks near $A = 56$ (iron), explaining why both fission of heavy nuclei and fusion of light nuclei release energy.

🌱 Coulomb Force

🌱 Coulomb Force

Definition. The Coulomb force is the electrostatic interaction between two point charges. For charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by a distance $r$ in vacuum, the magnitude of the force exerted by each charge on the other is given by Coulomb's Law:

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N \cdot m^2 \cdot C^{-2}}$ is Coulomb's constant, related to the permittivity of free space by $k_e = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$, with $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \mathrm{C^2 \cdot N^{-1} \cdot m^{-2}}$. The force is directed along the line connecting the two charges: repulsive when the charges share the same sign, attractive when opposite. In vector form,

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\,\hat{r}_{12} $$

where $\hat{r}_{12}$ is the unit vector pointing from charge 1 toward charge 2.

Worked Example. Two charges, $q_1 = +3.0 \, \mu\mathrm{C}$ and $q_2 = -5.0 \, \mu\mathrm{C}$, are placed $0.20 \, \mathrm{m}$ apart. Find the magnitude of the Coulomb force between them.

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(5.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-11}}{4.0 \times 10^{-2}} = (8.988 \times 10^9)(3.75 \times 10^{-10}) \approx 3.4 \, \mathrm{N} $$

Since the charges are opposite in sign, the force is attractive.

Key Theorem — Superposition Principle. When more than two charges are present, the net force on any single charge is the vector sum of the individual pairwise Coulomb forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\mathrm{net}} = \sum_{i \neq j} \vec{F}_{ij} $$

This linearity is not obvious from first principles; it is an empirically validated property of the electromagnetic field that holds precisely in the classical regime.

🌱 Electromagnetic Radiation

🌱 Electromagnetic Radiation

Definition. Electromagnetic radiation consists of self-propagating oscillations of mutually perpendicular electric and magnetic fields that travel through vacuum at the universal constant $c = 2.998 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}$. Unlike mechanical waves, electromagnetic waves require no medium; the changing electric field $\mathbf{E}$ induces a changing magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, and vice versa, sustaining propagation indefinitely. For a plane wave traveling in the $+x$ direction, the fields take the form

$$ \mathbf{E}(x,t) = E_0 \sin(kx - \omega t)\,\hat{j}, \qquad \mathbf{B}(x,t) = B_0 \sin(kx - \omega t)\,\hat{k}, $$

where $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, and the amplitudes satisfy $E_0 = cB_0$. The wave carries energy described by the Poynting vector $\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0}\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B}$, whose time-averaged magnitude gives the intensity $I = \frac{E_0^2}{2\mu_0 c}$.

Worked Example. A radio transmitter produces a sinusoidal electromagnetic wave with frequency $f = 100 \, \text{MHz}$ and electric-field amplitude $E_0 = 50 \, \text{V/m}$. Find the wavelength, the magnetic-field amplitude, and the wave intensity.

Wavelength:

$$ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{2.998 \times 10^8}{1.00 \times 10^8} = 3.00 \, \text{m}. $$

Magnetic amplitude:

$$ B_0 = \frac{E_0}{c} = \frac{50}{2.998 \times 10^8} = 1.67 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T}. $$

Intensity:

$$ I = \frac{E_0^2}{2\mu_0 c} = \frac{(50)^2}{2(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(2.998 \times 10^8)} = 3.32 \, \text{W/m}^2. $$

Key Theorem (Maxwell's Prediction). Maxwell's equations in vacuum yield a wave equation for each field component, from which the speed of propagation is derived analytically:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}}. $$

Substituting $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A}$ and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{C}^2/\text{N·m}^2$ reproduces the measured speed of light, unifying optics and electromagnetism into a single theoretical framework.

Verify that $\lambda = 2d$ and compute $c = f\lambda$. Compare your result to the accepted value and estimate the dominant sources of uncertainty in your measurement.

🍃 Electron

🍃 Electron

Definition. The electron is a fundamental subatomic particle with a rest mass of $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, an electric charge of $q_e = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, and an intrinsic spin quantum number of $s = \tfrac{1}{2}$. As a lepton, it does not participate in the strong nuclear interaction. In atomic structure, electrons occupy discrete energy levels determined by their quantum state; the Pauli exclusion principle prevents two electrons in the same atom from sharing an identical quantum state, directly governing the shell structure underlying the periodic table. Both quantum numbers and the exclusion principle are treated as established background here; this section focuses on the wave nature of the electron as the key new idea.

Worked Example. Consider a hydrogen atom with its electron in the ground state ($n = 1$). The total energy is given by the Bohr formula:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2} $$

For $n = 1$: $E_1 = -13.6\ \text{eV}$. If the electron absorbs a photon and transitions to $n = 3$, the required photon energy is:

$$ \Delta E = E_3 - E_1 = -\frac{13.6}{9}\ \text{eV} - (-13.6\ \text{eV}) = 13.6\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{9}\right)\!\text{eV} = 12.09\ \text{eV} $$

The corresponding wavelength is:

$$ \lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{12.09 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 102.6\ \text{nm} $$

This ultraviolet photon belongs to the Lyman series, consistent with observed hydrogen spectra.

Key Theorem (de Broglie Relation). An electron with momentum $p = m_e v$ possesses an associated wavelength:

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{m_e v} $$

This wave-particle duality was confirmed experimentally by Davisson and Germer in 1927, who observed diffraction of electrons from a nickel crystal lattice. The relation justifies the Bohr quantization condition $2\pi r = n\lambda$ and underpins the full quantum mechanical treatment of atomic orbitals.

Lab Exercise. Using a cathode ray tube with accelerating potential $V$, measure the deflection radius $r$ of an electron beam in a perpendicular magnetic field $B$. Apply force balance $qvB = m_e v^2/r$ and energy conservation $qV = \tfrac{1}{2}m_e v^2$ to obtain $e/m_e = 2V/(B^2 r^2)$, then compare your result to the accepted value $1.759 \times 10^{11}\ \text{C/kg}$.

🍃 Atomic Spectra

🍃 Atomic Spectra

Definition

When atoms absorb or emit electromagnetic radiation, they do so only at discrete, characteristic wavelengths — a phenomenon known as atomic spectra. For emission spectra, a gas of atoms excited by heat or electrical discharge releases photons as electrons transition from higher to lower energy levels. For absorption spectra, atoms illuminated by broadband light absorb only those wavelengths that correspond to allowed upward transitions, leaving dark lines against a continuous background. The emitted or absorbed photon energy satisfies:

$$ E_{\text{photon}} = hf = E_i - E_f $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant, $f$ is the photon frequency, and $E_i$, $E_f$ are the initial and final electron energy levels. For the hydrogen atom, the quantized energy levels are:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

Worked Example

Find the wavelength of the photon emitted when a hydrogen electron transitions from $n = 3$ to $n = 2$ (the H$\alpha$ line of the Balmer series).

$$ \Delta E = E_3 - E_2 = -\frac{13.6}{9}\ \text{eV} - \left(-\frac{13.6}{4}\ \text{eV}\right) = 1.89\ \text{eV} $$

Converting: $\Delta E = 1.89 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J} = 3.03 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J}$

$$ \lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{3.03 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 656\ \text{nm} $$

This falls in the red region of the visible spectrum, consistent with the observed H$\alpha$ emission.

Key Theorem: The Rydberg Formula

The complete set of hydrogen spectral lines is described by the Rydberg formula:

$$ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left(\frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2}\right), \quad n_i > n_f $$

where $R_H = 1.097 \times 10^7\ \text{m}^{-1}$ is the Rydberg constant. Spectral series are named by their terminal level $n_f$: Lyman ($n_f = 1$, ultraviolet), Balmer ($n_f = 2$, visible), and Paschen ($n_f = 3$, infrared). The Rydberg formula can be derived directly from the Bohr energy levels by applying the photon energy condition $hf = E_i - E_f$ and substituting $c = f\lambda$.

🌱 Classical Angular Momentum

🌱 Classical Angular Momentum

Definition. The classical angular momentum of a particle is a vector quantity defined relative to a chosen origin. For a particle with linear momentum $\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}$ located at position $\mathbf{r}$ from the origin, the angular momentum is

$$ \mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p} = m(\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{v}). $$

The magnitude is $L = rp\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{p}$. The direction is given by the right-hand rule, perpendicular to the plane containing $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{p}$. The SI unit of angular momentum is $\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s}$, equivalently $\text{J}\cdot\text{s}$.

For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis with moment of inertia $I$ and angular velocity $\omega$, the scalar form is

$$ L = I\omega. $$

Worked Example. An electron in the Bohr model of hydrogen moves in a circular orbit of radius $r_1 = 0.529\times10^{-10}\ \text{m}$ with speed $v_1 = 2.19\times10^6\ \text{m/s}$. The electron's mass is $m_e = 9.109\times10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$. Because the velocity is tangential ($\theta = 90°$), the orbital angular momentum magnitude is

$$ L = m_e v_1 r_1 = (9.109\times10^{-31})(2.19\times10^6)(0.529\times10^{-10}) $$ $$ L \approx 1.055\times10^{-34}\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s} = \hbar. $$

This result, $L = \hbar$, represents the ground-state angular momentum in the Bohr model and foreshadows the quantization condition $L = n\hbar$ for the $n$-th orbit.

Key Theorem — Conservation of Angular Momentum. The net torque on a system equals the time rate of change of its angular momentum:

$$ \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt}. $$

When $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} = \mathbf{0}$, angular momentum is conserved: $\mathbf{L} = \text{constant}$. This conservation law is the classical foundation upon which quantum mechanical angular momentum quantization is built.

Lab Exercise. Mount a bicycle wheel on a freely rotating platform. Measure the wheel's angular velocity $\omega_i$ with the axle horizontal. Tilt the axle to vertical, observing platform rotation. Record the platform's angular velocity $\omega_f$ and use $I_{\text{wheel}}\omega_i = (I_{\text{wheel}} + I_{\text{platform}})\omega_f$ to verify conservation of angular momentum, thereby confirming that no net external torque acted on the system during the tilt.

🌱 Planck Constant

🌱 Planck Constant

Definition. The Planck constant, denoted $h$, is a fundamental physical constant that sets the scale of quantum effects. It relates the energy $E$ of a photon to the frequency $\nu$ of its associated electromagnetic radiation:

$$ E = h\nu $$

Its measured value is $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$. The constant emerged from Max Planck's 1900 resolution of the ultraviolet catastrophe: classical physics predicted that a blackbody would radiate infinite energy at high frequencies, but Planck showed that assuming energy is exchanged in discrete quanta $E = h\nu$ produced a spectrum in agreement with experiment. The reduced Planck constant $\hbar = h/(2\pi) = 1.055 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is the one new derived quantity introduced here; it appears naturally in wave mechanics wherever angles are measured in radians rather than cycles.

Worked Example. A hydrogen atom emits a photon when an electron transitions from the $n = 3$ to the $n = 2$ energy level. The energy of the emitted photon is:

$$ E = E_3 - E_2 = \left(-\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{9}\right) - \left(-\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{4}\right) = 13.6\ \text{eV}\left(\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{9}\right) = 13.6 \times \frac{5}{36}\ \text{eV} \approx 1.89\ \text{eV} $$

Converting to joules: $E = 1.89 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J} = 3.03 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J}$. The photon's frequency is then:

$$ \nu = \frac{E}{h} = \frac{3.03 \times 10^{-19}}{6.626 \times 10^{-34}} \approx 4.57 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz} $$

This corresponds to a wavelength $\lambda = c/\nu \approx 656\ \text{nm}$, the red H$\alpha$ line of the Balmer series — in excellent agreement with spectroscopic measurement.

Key Theorem (Planck–Einstein Relation). For any photon, energy and frequency are strictly proportional: $E = h\nu = \hbar\omega$, where $\omega = 2\pi\nu$ is the angular frequency expressible in terms of $\hbar$ already introduced above. This relation establishes $h$ as the fundamental bridge between the wave and particle descriptions of light, and underpins the quantization of energy that defines quantum mechanics.

🍃 Planetary Model

🍃 Planetary Model

Definition. The planetary model of the atom, proposed by Ernest Rutherford in 1911 following his gold-foil scattering experiments, posits that an atom consists of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center, around which negatively charged electrons orbit at relatively large distances — analogous to planets orbiting the Sun. The nucleus contains nearly all the atomic mass but occupies only a tiny fraction of the atomic volume (nuclear radius $r_n \sim 10^{-15}$ m versus atomic radius $r_a \sim 10^{-10}$ m, a ratio of $10^{-5}$). In this classical picture, the Coulomb electrostatic attraction between the electron (charge $-e$) and nucleus (charge $+Ze$) provides the centripetal force necessary for circular orbit:

$$ \frac{Ze^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} = \frac{m_e v^2}{r} $$

where $r$ is the orbital radius, $m_e$ is the electron mass, and $v$ is the orbital speed.

Worked Example. Estimate the orbital speed of the electron in hydrogen ($Z = 1$) at the Bohr radius $r = a_0 = 5.29 \times 10^{-11}$ m. Solving the force-balance equation for $v$:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 m_e a_0}} $$

Substituting $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C, $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}$ kg, and $\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0} = 2.307 \times 10^{-28}$ N·m²:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{2.307 \times 10^{-28}}{(9.109 \times 10^{-31})(5.29 \times 10^{-11})}} \approx 2.19 \times 10^6 \text{ m/s} $$

This is approximately $0.73\%$ of the speed of light, justifying a non-relativistic treatment.

Key Theorem (Classical Instability). Despite its intuitive appeal, the planetary model is fundamentally untenable within classical electrodynamics. An accelerating charge radiates electromagnetic energy (Larmor radiation) at the rate $P = \frac{e^2 a^2}{6\pi\epsilon_0 c^3}$, where $a$ is the centripetal acceleration. As the electron loses energy, it spirals inward and would collapse into the nucleus in approximately $\tau \approx 10^{-11}$ s — contradicting the observed stability of atoms. This failure motivated Bohr's introduction of quantized orbits in 1913.

🍃 Bohr Model

🍃 Bohr Model

Definition. The Bohr model, proposed by Niels Bohr in 1913, describes the hydrogen atom as a nucleus orbited by an electron constrained to discrete circular orbits. The model rests on two postulates: (1) electrons occupy stationary states without radiating energy, and (2) radiation is emitted or absorbed only when an electron transitions between states, with the photon energy equal to the difference in orbital energies. Both postulates treat previously established physics — Coulomb's law and the photon energy relation $E = hf$ — as given. The single new primitive the Bohr model introduces is the quantization of angular momentum:

$$ L = m_e v r = n\hbar, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $\hbar = h/2\pi$ and $n$ is the principal quantum number. Everything else follows from this condition combined with known mechanics. Requiring the Coulomb force to supply centripetal acceleration and substituting the quantization condition yields the allowed orbital radii $r_n = n^2 a_0$, with $a_0 = \hbar^2/(m_e k e^2) \approx 0.0529\text{ nm}$, and the energy levels:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\text{ eV}}{n^2} $$

Worked Example. Calculate the wavelength of the photon emitted when a hydrogen electron transitions from $n = 4$ to $n = 2$.

The photon energy equals the orbital energy difference:

$$ \Delta E = E_4 - E_2 = -\frac{13.6}{16} + \frac{13.6}{4} = -0.85 + 3.40 = 2.55\text{ eV} $$

Converting: $\Delta E = 2.55 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \approx 4.09 \times 10^{-19}$ J. Using $E = hc/\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} = \frac{(6.626\times10^{-34})(3.00\times10^8)}{4.09\times10^{-19}} \approx 486\text{ nm} $$

This corresponds to the $H_\beta$ line of the Balmer series, in excellent agreement with observation.

Key Theorem (Rydberg Formula). The angular momentum quantization condition implies that all hydrogen spectral lines satisfy:

$$ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H\!\left(\frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2}\right), \quad R_H = 1.097\times10^7\text{ m}^{-1} $$

where $R_H$ is derived entirely from fundamental constants, a striking predictive success of the model.

🍃 Quantized Orbits

🍃 Quantized Orbits

Definition. In Bohr's model of the hydrogen atom, electrons do not occupy arbitrary circular orbits but are restricted to discrete radii at which the electron's angular momentum is an integer multiple of the reduced Planck constant $\hbar = h/2\pi$. The quantization condition is

$$ L = m_e v_n r_n = n\hbar, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $m_e$ is the electron mass, $v_n$ is the orbital speed, $r_n$ is the orbital radius, and $n$ is the principal quantum number. Combining this condition with the requirement that the Coulomb force supplies the centripetal acceleration yields the Bohr radius formula

$$ r_n = n^2 a_0, \quad a_0 = \frac{\hbar^2}{m_e k_e e^2} \approx 0.0529 \text{ nm} $$

where $k_e$ is the Coulomb constant and $e$ is the elementary charge. Each allowed orbit corresponds to a definite total mechanical energy

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}. $$

Worked Example. Determine the radius and total energy of the $n = 3$ orbit in hydrogen.

Radius:

$$ r_3 = 3^2 \times 0.0529 \text{ nm} = 9 \times 0.0529 \text{ nm} = 0.476 \text{ nm.} $$

Energy:

$$ E_3 = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{9} = -1.51 \text{ eV.} $$

The negative sign confirms a bound state; the electron must absorb at least $1.51\text{ eV}$ to escape from this orbit to infinity.

Key Theorem. The de Broglie relation $\lambda = h/(m_e v_n)$ gives quantized orbits a wave-mechanical foundation: a stable orbit exists precisely when an integer number of electron wavelengths fits around the circumference,

$$ 2\pi r_n = n\lambda = n\frac{h}{m_e v_n}, $$

which is algebraically equivalent to Bohr's angular-momentum condition. This correspondence demonstrates that quantized orbits are not an arbitrary postulate but a resonance condition for standing matter waves.

Lab Exercise. Using the relation $\Delta E = hf$, calculate the wavelength of the photon emitted when an electron transitions from $n = 4$ to $n = 2$. Verify that the result falls within the visible spectrum (Balmer series) and compare your computed value to the experimentally observed $H_\beta$ line at $486.1\text{ nm}$.

🍃 Space Quantization

🍃 Space Quantization

Definition. When an atom is placed in an external magnetic field $\vec{B}$, the orbital angular momentum vector $\vec{L}$ does not adopt arbitrary orientations. Instead, the component of $\vec{L}$ along the field direction (conventionally the $z$-axis) is restricted to the discrete values

$$ L_z = m_\ell \hbar, \quad m_\ell = -\ell,\, -\ell+1,\, \ldots,\, +\ell, $$

where $\ell$ is the orbital quantum number and $\hbar = h/2\pi$ is the reduced Planck constant. This phenomenon is called space quantization: the spatial orientation of the angular momentum vector is quantized, not continuous. Because the magnitude of the orbital angular momentum is $L = \hbar\sqrt{\ell(\ell+1)}$, the polar angle $\theta$ between $\vec{L}$ and $\hat{z}$ satisfies

$$ \cos\theta = \frac{m_\ell}{\sqrt{\ell(\ell+1)}}, $$

yielding $2\ell + 1$ permitted orientations for each value of $\ell$.

Worked Example. Consider an electron in the $3d$ subshell, for which $\ell = 2$. The allowed magnetic quantum numbers are $m_\ell \in \{-2, -1, 0, +1, +2\}$, giving five discrete orientations. The magnitude of the angular momentum is $L = \hbar\sqrt{2(3)} = \hbar\sqrt{6} \approx 2.58 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$. For $m_\ell = 1$, the projection is $L_z = \hbar$, so the polar angle is

$$ \theta = \arccos\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\right) \approx 65.9°. $$

The five orientations are symmetrically distributed about $\theta = 90°$ but are never aligned with or against $\vec{B}$ (since $m_\ell = \pm\ell$ gives $\cos\theta \neq \pm 1$ for $\ell \geq 1$).

Key Theorem — Stern–Gerlach Result. Space quantization has direct experimental consequences: an inhomogeneous magnetic field exerts a force $F_z = \mu_z (\partial B/\partial z)$ on the atom, where the $z$-component of the magnetic moment $\mu_z = -m_\ell \mu_B$ ($\mu_B = e\hbar/2m_e$ is the Bohr magneton). Because $m_\ell$ takes only integer values, a beam of atoms is deflected into $2\ell + 1$ discrete spots rather than a continuous smear — the original 1922 Stern–Gerlach observation that confirmed space quantization.

🍃 Principal Quantum Number

🍃 Principal Quantum Number

Definition. The principal quantum number, denoted $n$, is a positive integer ($n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$) that specifies the energy level and mean orbital radius of an electron in a hydrogen-like atom. It arises naturally from the boundary conditions imposed on the radial part of the Schrödinger equation for a Coulomb potential. The allowed energies of the hydrogen atom are given exactly by

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

The ground state ($n = 1$) has energy $E_1 = -13.6$ eV. As $n \to \infty$, $E_n \to 0$, corresponding to a free (unbound) electron. The associated mean orbital radius scales as $r_n = n^2 a_0$, where $a_0 \approx 0.0529$ nm is the Bohr radius.

Worked Example. An electron in a hydrogen atom transitions from $n = 3$ to $n = 2$. Determine the wavelength of the emitted photon.

First, compute the energy of each level:

$$ E_3 = -\frac{13.6}{9} \approx -1.511 \text{ eV}, \qquad E_2 = -\frac{13.6}{4} = -3.400 \text{ eV}. $$

The energy released is

$$ \Delta E = E_3 - E_2 = (-1.511) - (-3.400) = 1.889 \text{ eV}. $$

Converting to joules: $\Delta E = 1.889 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \approx 3.027 \times 10^{-19}$ J. Using $E = hc/\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{3.027 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 656 \text{ nm}. $$

This is the H$\alpha$ line in the Balmer series, visible as red light.

Key Theorem (Rydberg Formula). For any transition between levels $n_i > n_f$ in hydrogen, the emitted photon wavenumber is

$$ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left(\frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2}\right), $$

where $R_H = 1.097 \times 10^7$ m$^{-1}$ is the Rydberg constant. This formula unifies all spectral series (Lyman, Balmer, Paschen) under a single expression parameterized solely by $n$.

Lab Exercise. Using a hydrogen discharge tube and diffraction grating spectrometer, measure the wavelengths of at least three visible Balmer series lines. For each line, identify $n_i$ and $n_f$, compute the theoretical wavelength from the Rydberg formula, and calculate the percent discrepancy. Estimate $R_H$ from your data and compare it to the accepted value.

🍃 Angular Momentum Quantum Number

🍃 Angular Momentum Quantum Number

Definition. In the quantum mechanical model of the hydrogen atom, each electron state is characterized by a set of quantum numbers. The angular momentum quantum number (also called the azimuthal quantum number), denoted $\ell$, specifies the magnitude of the electron's orbital angular momentum. For a given principal quantum number $n$, the allowed values of $\ell$ are

$$ \ell = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, n-1. $$

The magnitude of the orbital angular momentum is not $\ell\hbar$ but rather

$$ L = \sqrt{\ell(\ell+1)}\,\hbar, $$

where $\hbar = h/2\pi$ is the reduced Planck constant. The subshells corresponding to $\ell = 0, 1, 2, 3$ are conventionally labeled $s$, $p$, $d$, and $f$, respectively.

Worked Example. Consider an electron in the $n = 3$ shell. The permitted values of $\ell$ are $0$, $1$, and $2$, corresponding to the $3s$, $3p$, and $3d$ subshells. For the $3d$ state ($\ell = 2$), the magnitude of the orbital angular momentum is

$$ L = \sqrt{2(2+1)}\,\hbar = \sqrt{6}\,\hbar \approx 2.58 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}. $$

Note that a classical orbit with the same energy would yield a different angular momentum—this quantization is a purely quantum mechanical result with no classical analogue.

Key Theorem (Quantization of $L^2$). Solving the time-independent Schrödinger equation in spherical coordinates via separation of variables shows that the operator $\hat{L}^2$ has eigenvalues $\ell(\ell+1)\hbar^2$. This follows from the requirement that the polar angular wave function $\Theta(\theta)$ remain finite and single-valued at $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = \pi$, which restricts $\ell$ to non-negative integers. The degeneracy of each subshell—the number of distinct $m_\ell$ projections—is $2\ell + 1$, giving a total of $n^2$ degenerate states per principal level (excluding spin).

Payoff 🎯

The periodic table stands as one of the most consequential organizational achievements in the history of science — not merely a chart of elements, but a compressed encoding of quantum mechanics made visible. Each position in the table is determined by the solution to the Schrödinger equation for a many-electron atom: the atomic number $Z$ fixes the nuclear charge, and the aufbau principle, governed by the Pauli exclusion principle and Hund's rules, fills the available orbitals in order of increasing energy. The result is that an element's chemical and physical behavior — its ionization energy, electronegativity, atomic radius, and valence — emerges directly from its electron configuration $[\text{core}]\, ns^a\, np^b\, nd^c \ldots$. The table makes quantum structure legible at a glance.

To see this concretely, consider sodium ($Z = 11$). Its ground-state configuration is $1s^2\, 2s^2\, 2p^6\, 3s^1$. The single valence electron in the $3s$ orbital is shielded from the nucleus by ten inner electrons, yielding an effective nuclear charge $Z_{\text{eff}} \approx 2.2$. The ionization energy is therefore much lower than that of neon ($Z = 10$), which has a complete $n = 2$ shell: $E_i(\text{Na}) \approx 5.1\,\text{eV}$ versus $E_i(\text{Ne}) \approx 21.6\,\text{eV}$. This quantitative gap explains sodium's chemical reactivity and places it unambiguously at the head of the alkali metals.

The key theorem underlying the table's predictive power is the shell-closure theorem: when all subshells of a given principal quantum number $n$ are completely filled, the resulting configuration is spherically symmetric and exceptionally stable. Noble gases satisfy this condition, accounting for their chemical inertness. Elements one position beyond a noble gas carry a single loosely bound electron; those one position before carry a single vacancy, or hole — and both extremes define the most reactive families in the table.

As a laboratory exercise, measure the emission spectrum of hydrogen and two alkali metals (lithium and sodium) using a diffraction-grating spectrometer. Record the principal series wavelengths and use the Rydberg formula $\frac{1}{\lambda} = R_\infty Z_{\text{eff}}^2\!\left(\frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2}\right)$ to extract $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for each transition. Compare the fitted values with theoretical shielding estimates; the agreement quantifies how well a one-electron model captures the physics of multi-electron atoms.

The periodic table, then, is not an endpoint but a launching pad. Every discipline from semiconductor engineering to biochemistry to astrophysical spectroscopy draws on the column-and-row logic encoded here. If you wish to see exactly how quantum shell structure drives the conductivity of silicon, the magnetism of iron, or the color of transition-metal complexes, follow the thread into solid-state physics — the periodic table will be your map.
