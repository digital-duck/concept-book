🍃 Space Quantization

🍃 Space Quantization

Definition. When an atom is placed in an external magnetic field $\vec{B}$, the orbital angular momentum vector $\vec{L}$ does not adopt arbitrary orientations. Instead, the component of $\vec{L}$ along the field direction (conventionally the $z$-axis) is restricted to the discrete values

$$ L_z = m_\ell \hbar, \quad m_\ell = -\ell,\, -\ell+1,\, \ldots,\, +\ell, $$

where $\ell$ is the orbital quantum number and $\hbar = h/2\pi$ is the reduced Planck constant. This phenomenon is called space quantization: the spatial orientation of the angular momentum vector is quantized, not continuous. Because the magnitude of the orbital angular momentum is $L = \hbar\sqrt{\ell(\ell+1)}$, the polar angle $\theta$ between $\vec{L}$ and $\hat{z}$ satisfies

$$ \cos\theta = \frac{m_\ell}{\sqrt{\ell(\ell+1)}}, $$

yielding $2\ell + 1$ permitted orientations for each value of $\ell$.

Worked Example. Consider an electron in the $3d$ subshell, for which $\ell = 2$. The allowed magnetic quantum numbers are $m_\ell \in \{-2, -1, 0, +1, +2\}$, giving five discrete orientations. The magnitude of the angular momentum is $L = \hbar\sqrt{2(3)} = \hbar\sqrt{6} \approx 2.58 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$. For $m_\ell = 1$, the projection is $L_z = \hbar$, so the polar angle is

$$ \theta = \arccos\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\right) \approx 65.9°. $$

The five orientations are symmetrically distributed about $\theta = 90°$ but are never aligned with or against $\vec{B}$ (since $m_\ell = \pm\ell$ gives $\cos\theta \neq \pm 1$ for $\ell \geq 1$).

Key Theorem — Stern–Gerlach Result. Space quantization has direct experimental consequences: an inhomogeneous magnetic field exerts a force $F_z = \mu_z (\partial B/\partial z)$ on the atom, where the $z$-component of the magnetic moment $\mu_z = -m_\ell \mu_B$ ($\mu_B = e\hbar/2m_e$ is the Bohr magneton). Because $m_\ell$ takes only integer values, a beam of atoms is deflected into $2\ell + 1$ discrete spots rather than a continuous smear — the original 1922 Stern–Gerlach observation that confirmed space quantization.

Lab Exercise. Silver atoms ($\ell = 0$ for the $5s$ valence electron, but $s = 1/2$) pass through an inhomogeneous field with gradient $\partial B/\partial z = 15\ \text{T/m}$ over a path length of $3.5\ \text{cm}$. Given that the $z$-component of the spin magnetic moment is $\pm\mu_B$, calculate the transverse deflection of each beam spot at a detector $20\ \text{cm}$ beyond the magnet. Assume an atomic speed of $570\ \text{m/s}$ and silver mass $1.79 \times 10^{-25}\ \text{kg}$.
