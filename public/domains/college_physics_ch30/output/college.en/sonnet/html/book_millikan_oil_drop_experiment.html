🌱 Atom

🌱 Atom

Definition. An atom is the smallest electrically neutral unit of an element that retains the chemical identity of that element. Each atom consists of a dense, positively charged nucleus—containing $Z$ protons (atomic number) and $N$ neutrons (mass number $A = Z + N$)—surrounded by a cloud of $Z$ electrons. The nuclear radius scales approximately as $r \approx r_0 A^{1/3}$, where $r_0 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-15}\ \text{m}$, making the nucleus roughly $10^5$ times smaller than the atomic radius ($\sim 10^{-10}\ \text{m}$). Electrons occupy discrete energy levels characterized by the principal quantum number $n$, which determines both the orbital radius and the energy of the electron in the hydrogen atom.

Worked Example. Consider a hydrogen atom ($Z = 1$) with its single electron in the $n = 2$ state. The Bohr model gives the orbital radius:

$$ r_n = n^2 a_0, \quad a_0 = 0.529\ \text{Å} $$

so $r_2 = 4a_0 = 2.116\ \text{Å}$. The corresponding energy is:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2} $$

yielding $E_2 = -3.4\ \text{eV}$. When this electron transitions to the ground state ($n = 1$, $E_1 = -13.6\ \text{eV}$), the emitted photon carries energy:

$$ \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = 10.2\ \text{eV} $$

corresponding to a wavelength $\lambda = hc/\Delta E \approx 121.6\ \text{nm}$, the Lyman-$\alpha$ line in the ultraviolet.

Key Theorem (Bohr Quantization). Stable electron orbits satisfy the angular momentum condition $L = m_e v r = n\hbar$, where $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ and $\hbar = h/2\pi$. Combined with the Coulomb force providing centripetal acceleration:

$$ \frac{ke^2}{r^2} = \frac{m_e v^2}{r} $$

these two conditions uniquely determine the quantized radii and energies above, predicting the hydrogen spectrum to high accuracy.

🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how a particle participates in electromagnetic interactions. Charge is quantized: every observable free charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$. By convention, the proton carries charge $+e$ and the electron carries charge $-e$. The SI unit of charge is the coulomb (C). The total electric charge of an isolated system is conserved — a principle called conservation of charge — meaning charge can neither be created nor destroyed, only transferred between objects.

The electrostatic force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is given by Coulomb's Law:

$$ \vec{F} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2$ is the Coulomb constant, related to the permittivity of free space by $k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$.

Worked Example. Two small spheres carry charges $q_1 = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and $q_2 = -1.5\ \mu\text{C}$ and are placed $0.20\ \text{m}$ apart. Find the magnitude and character of the force.

$$ |\vec{F}| = k_e \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(1.5 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ |\vec{F}| = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{4.5 \times 10^{-12}}{0.040} \approx 1.01\ \text{N} $$

Because the charges are opposite in sign, the force is attractive.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Charge). For any closed system, $\sum q_i = \text{constant}$. In pair production, for example, a photon creates an electron–positron pair $(q = -e,\ q = +e)$; the net charge remains zero, consistent with the photon's zero charge.

🍃 Electron

🍃 Electron

Definition. The electron is a fundamental subatomic particle with a rest mass of $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, an electric charge of $q_e = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, and an intrinsic spin quantum number of $s = \tfrac{1}{2}$. As a lepton, it does not participate in the strong nuclear interaction. In atomic structure, electrons occupy discrete energy levels determined by their quantum state; the Pauli exclusion principle prevents two electrons in the same atom from sharing an identical quantum state, directly governing the shell structure underlying the periodic table. Both quantum numbers and the exclusion principle are treated as established background here; this section focuses on the wave nature of the electron as the key new idea.

Worked Example. Consider a hydrogen atom with its electron in the ground state ($n = 1$). The total energy is given by the Bohr formula:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2} $$

For $n = 1$: $E_1 = -13.6\ \text{eV}$. If the electron absorbs a photon and transitions to $n = 3$, the required photon energy is:

$$ \Delta E = E_3 - E_1 = -\frac{13.6}{9}\ \text{eV} - (-13.6\ \text{eV}) = 13.6\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{9}\right)\!\text{eV} = 12.09\ \text{eV} $$

The corresponding wavelength is:

$$ \lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{12.09 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 102.6\ \text{nm} $$

This ultraviolet photon belongs to the Lyman series, consistent with observed hydrogen spectra.

Key Theorem (de Broglie Relation). An electron with momentum $p = m_e v$ possesses an associated wavelength:

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{m_e v} $$

This wave-particle duality was confirmed experimentally by Davisson and Germer in 1927, who observed diffraction of electrons from a nickel crystal lattice. The relation justifies the Bohr quantization condition $2\pi r = n\lambda$ and underpins the full quantum mechanical treatment of atomic orbitals.

🍃 Electron Charge

🍃 Electron Charge

Definition. The electron charge, denoted $e$, is the fundamental quantum of electric charge carried by a single electron. Its accepted value is

$$ e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C} $$

where C denotes coulombs. The electron carries a charge of $-e$ (negative by convention), while the proton carries $+e$. All observable electric charges are integer multiples of $e$, making it the elementary charge unit. This quantization of charge is a foundational principle of atomic and subatomic physics, establishing that charge cannot be subdivided below this threshold in ordinary matter.

Worked Example. Consider a conducting sphere that has lost $3.0 \times 10^{12}$ electrons. Determine the net charge on the sphere.

Each lost electron removes a charge of $-e$, so removing $n$ electrons increases the net charge by $+ne$. Thus:

$$ Q = n \cdot e = (3.0 \times 10^{12})(1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) $$ $$ Q = 4.81 \times 10^{-7} \text{ C} = 0.481 \; \mu\text{C} $$

The sphere carries a net positive charge of approximately $0.48 \; \mu\text{C}$, consistent with a deficit of electrons.

Key Theorem — Charge Quantization. Millikan's oil-drop experiment (1909–1913) provided the first precise measurement of $e$ and established quantization empirically. In that experiment, the equilibrium condition for a charged oil droplet suspended in a uniform electric field $E$ is:

$$ qE = mg $$

where $m$ is the droplet mass and $g$ is gravitational acceleration. By varying the field and measuring multiple droplets, Millikan found that the charge $q$ on any droplet was always an integer multiple of a smallest value, which he identified as $e$. Modern quantum electrodynamics explains charge quantization through gauge symmetry and the topology of the gauge group, but the experimental result stands independent of theory.

🌸 Millikan Oil Drop Experiment

🌸 Millikan Oil Drop Experiment

Definition. The Millikan oil drop experiment (1909) provides a direct measurement of the elementary charge $e$. Tiny oil droplets are sprayed into a chamber between two horizontal parallel plates separated by distance $d$ and maintained at potential difference $V$, producing a uniform electric field $E = V/d$. A droplet of mass $m$ carrying charge $q$ experiences gravitational force $F_g = mg$ downward and electric force $F_E = qE$ upward (when the field is oriented appropriately). By adjusting $V$ until the droplet is stationary, one achieves mechanical equilibrium:

$$ qE = mg \implies q = \frac{mgd}{V}. $$

The droplet mass is determined by switching off the field and measuring the terminal velocity $v_t$ under viscous drag. Stokes' law gives the drag force $F_d = 6\pi\eta r v_t$, where $\eta$ is the air viscosity and $r$ is the droplet radius. At terminal velocity, $F_d = mg$, so:

$$ r = \sqrt{\frac{9\eta v_t}{2\rho g}}, \qquad m = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \rho, $$

where $\rho$ is the oil density.

Worked Example. Suppose a droplet reaches terminal velocity $v_t = 1.20 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m/s}$ with the field off. Given $\eta = 1.81 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{Pa·s}$, $\rho = 900\ \text{kg/m}^3$, compute:

$$ r = \sqrt{\frac{9(1.81\times10^{-5})(1.20\times10^{-4})}{2(900)(9.81)}} \approx 1.00 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{m}. $$

Then $m = \frac{4}{3}\pi (10^{-6})^3 (900) \approx 3.77 \times 10^{-15}\ \text{kg}$. With $d = 5.00\ \text{mm}$ and $V = 1155\ \text{V}$ for equilibrium:

$$ q = \frac{(3.77\times10^{-15})(9.81)(5.00\times10^{-3})}{1155} \approx 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C} = e. $$

Key Theorem. Millikan's critical finding was that every measured charge was an integer multiple of $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, establishing charge quantization: $q = ne$, $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$.

Payoff 🎯

The Millikan Oil Drop Experiment stands as one of the most precise and consequential measurements in the history of physics: the direct determination of the elementary charge $e$. By balancing gravitational, buoyant, and electric forces on microscopic oil droplets suspended between charged capacitor plates, Robert Millikan demonstrated in 1909–1913 that electric charge is not continuous but exists only in discrete integer multiples of a fundamental unit. This experiment is the natural endpoint of a course in modern physics because it transforms the electron from a theoretical construct into a measurable, quantifiable entity—one whose charge underpins every electromagnetic phenomenon studied throughout the course.

Experimental Principle. Consider a droplet of mass $m$ and charge $q = ne$ (where $n$ is an integer) suspended motionless between plates separated by distance $d$ under voltage $V$. Force balance gives:

$$ qE = mg \implies q = \frac{mgd}{V} $$

By varying $V$ and observing terminal velocities under Stokes drag, one extracts $q$ for many droplets. The greatest common divisor of all measured charges converges to $e \approx 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$.

Key Theorem: Quantization of Charge. Millikan's data established rigorously that charge is quantized: every observable charge $Q$ satisfies $Q = ne$ for some $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. This is not merely an empirical regularity—it is required by gauge invariance in quantum electrodynamics and is equivalent to the existence of magnetic monopoles in certain unified theories. The value of $e$, now defined exactly in SI units, anchors the ampere, the faraday, and the fine-structure constant $\alpha = e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 \hbar c)$.

Using Stokes' law $F = 6\pi\eta r v$ with $\eta = 1.81 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{Pa·s}$, determine $q$ and find $n$.

The measurement of $e$ is not a historical artifact—it is the gateway to understanding atomic spectra, semiconductor physics, and the quantum Hall effect. If you wish to follow one thread deeper, consider exploring how the quantized charge couples to the hydrogen atom's energy levels through the Bohr model: there, $e$ appears in every energy eigenvalue $E_n = -me^4/(8\epsilon_0^2 h^2 n^2)$, and the entire structure of atomic physics unfolds from this single constant you have now measured.
