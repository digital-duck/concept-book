🌱 Planck Constant

🌱 Planck Constant

Definition. The Planck constant, denoted $h$, is a fundamental physical constant that sets the scale of quantum effects. It relates the energy $E$ of a photon to the frequency $\nu$ of its associated electromagnetic radiation:

$$ E = h\nu $$

Its measured value is $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$. The constant emerged from Max Planck's 1900 resolution of the ultraviolet catastrophe: classical physics predicted that a blackbody would radiate infinite energy at high frequencies, but Planck showed that assuming energy is exchanged in discrete quanta $E = h\nu$ produced a spectrum in agreement with experiment. The reduced Planck constant $\hbar = h/(2\pi) = 1.055 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is the one new derived quantity introduced here; it appears naturally in wave mechanics wherever angles are measured in radians rather than cycles.

Worked Example. A hydrogen atom emits a photon when an electron transitions from the $n = 3$ to the $n = 2$ energy level. The energy of the emitted photon is:

$$ E = E_3 - E_2 = \left(-\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{9}\right) - \left(-\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{4}\right) = 13.6\ \text{eV}\left(\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{9}\right) = 13.6 \times \frac{5}{36}\ \text{eV} \approx 1.89\ \text{eV} $$

Converting to joules: $E = 1.89 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J} = 3.03 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J}$. The photon's frequency is then:

$$ \nu = \frac{E}{h} = \frac{3.03 \times 10^{-19}}{6.626 \times 10^{-34}} \approx 4.57 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz} $$

This corresponds to a wavelength $\lambda = c/\nu \approx 656\ \text{nm}$, the red H$\alpha$ line of the Balmer series — in excellent agreement with spectroscopic measurement.

Key Theorem (Planck–Einstein Relation). For any photon, energy and frequency are strictly proportional: $E = h\nu = \hbar\omega$, where $\omega = 2\pi\nu$ is the angular frequency expressible in terms of $\hbar$ already introduced above. This relation establishes $h$ as the fundamental bridge between the wave and particle descriptions of light, and underpins the quantization of energy that defines quantum mechanics.

Lab Exercise. Using a diffraction grating spectrometer and a set of colored LEDs with known threshold voltages $V_\text{th}$, measure the stopping potential for each color. Plot $eV_\text{th}$ versus $\nu$ (obtained from the grating measurement) and determine the slope. Verify that the slope equals $h$ within experimental uncertainty, and compare your result to the accepted value $6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$.
