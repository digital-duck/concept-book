🍃 Electron

🍃 Electron

Definition. The electron is a fundamental subatomic particle with a rest mass of $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, an electric charge of $q_e = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, and an intrinsic spin quantum number of $s = \tfrac{1}{2}$. As a lepton, it does not participate in the strong nuclear interaction. In atomic structure, electrons occupy discrete energy levels determined by their quantum state; the Pauli exclusion principle prevents two electrons in the same atom from sharing an identical quantum state, directly governing the shell structure underlying the periodic table. Both quantum numbers and the exclusion principle are treated as established background here; this section focuses on the wave nature of the electron as the key new idea.

Worked Example. Consider a hydrogen atom with its electron in the ground state ($n = 1$). The total energy is given by the Bohr formula:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2} $$

For $n = 1$: $E_1 = -13.6\ \text{eV}$. If the electron absorbs a photon and transitions to $n = 3$, the required photon energy is:

$$ \Delta E = E_3 - E_1 = -\frac{13.6}{9}\ \text{eV} - (-13.6\ \text{eV}) = 13.6\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{9}\right)\!\text{eV} = 12.09\ \text{eV} $$

The corresponding wavelength is:

$$ \lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{12.09 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 102.6\ \text{nm} $$

This ultraviolet photon belongs to the Lyman series, consistent with observed hydrogen spectra.

Key Theorem (de Broglie Relation). An electron with momentum $p = m_e v$ possesses an associated wavelength:

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{m_e v} $$

This wave-particle duality was confirmed experimentally by Davisson and Germer in 1927, who observed diffraction of electrons from a nickel crystal lattice. The relation justifies the Bohr quantization condition $2\pi r = n\lambda$ and underpins the full quantum mechanical treatment of atomic orbitals.

Lab Exercise. Using a cathode ray tube with accelerating potential $V$, measure the deflection radius $r$ of an electron beam in a perpendicular magnetic field $B$. Apply force balance $qvB = m_e v^2/r$ and energy conservation $qV = \tfrac{1}{2}m_e v^2$ to obtain $e/m_e = 2V/(B^2 r^2)$, then compare your result to the accepted value $1.759 \times 10^{11}\ \text{C/kg}$.
