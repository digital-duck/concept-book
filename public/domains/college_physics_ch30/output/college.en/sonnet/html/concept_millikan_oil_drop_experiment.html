🌸 Millikan Oil Drop Experiment

🌸 Millikan Oil Drop Experiment

Definition. The Millikan oil drop experiment (1909) provides a direct measurement of the elementary charge $e$. Tiny oil droplets are sprayed into a chamber between two horizontal parallel plates separated by distance $d$ and maintained at potential difference $V$, producing a uniform electric field $E = V/d$. A droplet of mass $m$ carrying charge $q$ experiences gravitational force $F_g = mg$ downward and electric force $F_E = qE$ upward (when the field is oriented appropriately). By adjusting $V$ until the droplet is stationary, one achieves mechanical equilibrium:

$$ qE = mg \implies q = \frac{mgd}{V}. $$

The droplet mass is determined by switching off the field and measuring the terminal velocity $v_t$ under viscous drag. Stokes' law gives the drag force $F_d = 6\pi\eta r v_t$, where $\eta$ is the air viscosity and $r$ is the droplet radius. At terminal velocity, $F_d = mg$, so:

$$ r = \sqrt{\frac{9\eta v_t}{2\rho g}}, \qquad m = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \rho, $$

where $\rho$ is the oil density.

Worked Example. Suppose a droplet reaches terminal velocity $v_t = 1.20 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m/s}$ with the field off. Given $\eta = 1.81 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{Pa·s}$, $\rho = 900\ \text{kg/m}^3$, compute:

$$ r = \sqrt{\frac{9(1.81\times10^{-5})(1.20\times10^{-4})}{2(900)(9.81)}} \approx 1.00 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{m}. $$

Then $m = \frac{4}{3}\pi (10^{-6})^3 (900) \approx 3.77 \times 10^{-15}\ \text{kg}$. With $d = 5.00\ \text{mm}$ and $V = 1155\ \text{V}$ for equilibrium:

$$ q = \frac{(3.77\times10^{-15})(9.81)(5.00\times10^{-3})}{1155} \approx 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C} = e. $$

Key Theorem. Millikan's critical finding was that every measured charge was an integer multiple of $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, establishing charge quantization: $q = ne$, $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$.

Lab Exercise. Using a virtual Millikan apparatus, record equilibrium voltages for five different droplets and their corresponding terminal velocities. For each, compute $q$ and determine $n$ by dividing by $e$. Verify that $q/n$ yields a consistent value of $e$ across all trials, and calculate the percent deviation from the accepted value $1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$.
