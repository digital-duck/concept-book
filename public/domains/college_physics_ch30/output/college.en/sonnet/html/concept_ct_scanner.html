🌸 Ct Scanner

🌸 CT Scanner

Definition. A computed tomography (CT) scanner is a diagnostic imaging device that reconstructs a three-dimensional map of internal tissue density by measuring the attenuation of X-ray beams transmitted through a patient from multiple projection angles. The physical basis is the Beer–Lambert law: for a monoenergetic X-ray beam of initial intensity $I_0$ traversing a path of length $L$ through a medium of linear attenuation coefficient $\mu$,

$$ I = I_0 \, e^{-\mu L}. $$

When the beam passes through heterogeneous tissue, the exponent becomes a line integral, $\int \mu(x,y)\, ds$, and the reconstruction problem is to recover the two-dimensional function $\mu(x,y)$ from a complete set of such projections—a mathematical task accomplished by filtered back-projection or iterative algorithms based on the Radon transform.

Worked Example. A CT beam enters a patient along a horizontal path and passes sequentially through 3.0 cm of soft tissue ($\mu_1 = 0.20\ \text{cm}^{-1}$) and 1.5 cm of compact bone ($\mu_2 = 0.48\ \text{cm}^{-1}$). Find the transmitted fraction $I/I_0$.

$$ \ln\!\left(\frac{I}{I_0}\right) = -\!\left(\mu_1 d_1 + \mu_2 d_2\right) = -\!\left[(0.20)(3.0) + (0.48)(1.5)\right] = -\!(0.60 + 0.72) = -1.32 $$ $$ \frac{I}{I_0} = e^{-1.32} \approx 0.267. $$

Roughly 27% of the beam exits; the detector records this reduction, contributing one data point to the sinogram from which the image is reconstructed.

Key Theorem. The Fourier Slice Theorem states that the one-dimensional Fourier transform of a projection at angle $\theta$ equals the two-dimensional Fourier transform of $\mu(x,y)$ evaluated along a line through the origin at that same angle. Formally, if $P_\theta(t)$ is the projection, then $\hat{P}_\theta(\omega) = \hat{\mu}(\omega\cos\theta,\, \omega\sin\theta)$. This theorem is the theoretical foundation of all frequency-domain reconstruction methods and guarantees that a sufficient angular sampling of projections uniquely determines $\mu(x,y)$.

Lab Exercise. Using an online CT simulation tool, acquire projections of a phantom (two cylinders of differing densities) at $N = 8$, $N = 36$, and $N = 180$ equally spaced angles. Reconstruct each image via filtered back-projection. Quantify the streak artifact intensity as a function of $N$ and explain, in terms of the Fourier Slice Theorem, why finer angular sampling reduces reconstruction error.
