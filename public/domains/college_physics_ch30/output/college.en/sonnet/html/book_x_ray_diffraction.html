🌱 Atom

🌱 Atom

Definition. An atom is the smallest electrically neutral unit of an element that retains the chemical identity of that element. Each atom consists of a dense, positively charged nucleus—containing $Z$ protons (atomic number) and $N$ neutrons (mass number $A = Z + N$)—surrounded by a cloud of $Z$ electrons. The nuclear radius scales approximately as $r \approx r_0 A^{1/3}$, where $r_0 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-15}\ \text{m}$, making the nucleus roughly $10^5$ times smaller than the atomic radius ($\sim 10^{-10}\ \text{m}$). Electrons occupy discrete energy levels characterized by the principal quantum number $n$, which determines both the orbital radius and the energy of the electron in the hydrogen atom.

Worked Example. Consider a hydrogen atom ($Z = 1$) with its single electron in the $n = 2$ state. The Bohr model gives the orbital radius:

$$ r_n = n^2 a_0, \quad a_0 = 0.529\ \text{Å} $$

so $r_2 = 4a_0 = 2.116\ \text{Å}$. The corresponding energy is:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2} $$

yielding $E_2 = -3.4\ \text{eV}$. When this electron transitions to the ground state ($n = 1$, $E_1 = -13.6\ \text{eV}$), the emitted photon carries energy:

$$ \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = 10.2\ \text{eV} $$

corresponding to a wavelength $\lambda = hc/\Delta E \approx 121.6\ \text{nm}$, the Lyman-$\alpha$ line in the ultraviolet.

Key Theorem (Bohr Quantization). Stable electron orbits satisfy the angular momentum condition $L = m_e v r = n\hbar$, where $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ and $\hbar = h/2\pi$. Combined with the Coulomb force providing centripetal acceleration:

$$ \frac{ke^2}{r^2} = \frac{m_e v^2}{r} $$

these two conditions uniquely determine the quantized radii and energies above, predicting the hydrogen spectrum to high accuracy.

$$ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H\!\left(\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{n^2}\right), \quad R_H = 1.097 \times 10^7\ \text{m}^{-1} $$

to identify each transition and calculate the Rydberg constant from your measurements. Compare your experimental value with the accepted value and estimate the percent error.

🌱 Electromagnetic Radiation

🌱 Electromagnetic Radiation

Definition. Electromagnetic radiation consists of self-propagating oscillations of mutually perpendicular electric and magnetic fields that travel through vacuum at the universal constant $c = 2.998 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}$. Unlike mechanical waves, electromagnetic waves require no medium; the changing electric field $\mathbf{E}$ induces a changing magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, and vice versa, sustaining propagation indefinitely. For a plane wave traveling in the $+x$ direction, the fields take the form

$$ \mathbf{E}(x,t) = E_0 \sin(kx - \omega t)\,\hat{j}, \qquad \mathbf{B}(x,t) = B_0 \sin(kx - \omega t)\,\hat{k}, $$

where $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, and the amplitudes satisfy $E_0 = cB_0$. The wave carries energy described by the Poynting vector $\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0}\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B}$, whose time-averaged magnitude gives the intensity $I = \frac{E_0^2}{2\mu_0 c}$.

Worked Example. A radio transmitter produces a sinusoidal electromagnetic wave with frequency $f = 100 \, \text{MHz}$ and electric-field amplitude $E_0 = 50 \, \text{V/m}$. Find the wavelength, the magnetic-field amplitude, and the wave intensity.

Wavelength:

$$ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{2.998 \times 10^8}{1.00 \times 10^8} = 3.00 \, \text{m}. $$

Magnetic amplitude:

$$ B_0 = \frac{E_0}{c} = \frac{50}{2.998 \times 10^8} = 1.67 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T}. $$

Intensity:

$$ I = \frac{E_0^2}{2\mu_0 c} = \frac{(50)^2}{2(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(2.998 \times 10^8)} = 3.32 \, \text{W/m}^2. $$

Key Theorem (Maxwell's Prediction). Maxwell's equations in vacuum yield a wave equation for each field component, from which the speed of propagation is derived analytically:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}}. $$

Substituting $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A}$ and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{C}^2/\text{N·m}^2$ reproduces the measured speed of light, unifying optics and electromagnetism into a single theoretical framework.

Verify that $\lambda = 2d$ and compute $c = f\lambda$. Compare your result to the accepted value and estimate the dominant sources of uncertainty in your measurement.

🌸 X Ray Diffraction

🌸 X Ray Diffraction

Definition. X-ray diffraction is the elastic scattering of X-rays by the periodic atomic planes of a crystalline solid, producing constructive or destructive interference patterns that reveal the crystal's lattice geometry. Because X-ray wavelengths ($\lambda \sim 0.01$–$10\,\text{nm}$) are comparable to interatomic spacings ($d \sim 0.1$–$1\,\text{nm}$), crystals act as three-dimensional diffraction gratings. The governing relation is Bragg's Law:

$$ 2d\sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $d$ is the interplanar spacing, $\theta$ is the glancing angle (measured from the reflecting plane, not the normal), and $m$ is the diffraction order. Constructive interference occurs only when the path-length difference between rays reflected from adjacent planes equals an integer number of wavelengths.

Worked Example. A beam of X-rays with $\lambda = 0.154\,\text{nm}$ (Cu $K_\alpha$ radiation) strikes a sodium chloride crystal. The principal planes have spacing $d = 0.282\,\text{nm}$. Find the first-order Bragg angle.

$$ \sin\theta = \frac{m\lambda}{2d} = \frac{(1)(0.154\,\text{nm})}{2(0.282\,\text{nm})} = 0.273 $$ $$ \theta = \arcsin(0.273) \approx 15.8° $$

The detector should therefore be positioned to receive the reflected beam at a glancing angle of $15.8°$ relative to the crystal surface, or equivalently at a scattering angle $2\theta \approx 31.6°$ from the incident direction.

Key Theorem — von Laue Condition. A more general formulation states that diffraction occurs when the change in wave vector $\Delta\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{k}_f - \mathbf{k}_i$ equals a reciprocal-lattice vector $\mathbf{G}$:

$$ \Delta\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{G} $$

Bragg's law is a one-dimensional projection of this vector condition onto a single family of planes. The full von Laue condition is essential for indexing diffraction patterns from crystals with complex unit cells, and it establishes the equivalence between real-space periodicity and momentum-space peak positions.

Lab Exercise. Using a powder diffractometer with $\lambda = 0.179\,\text{nm}$, an experimenter records peaks at $2\theta = 38.2°$, $44.4°$, and $64.6°$. Calculate the interplanar spacings $d$ for each peak using Bragg's law ($m = 1$), and identify which cubic planes (Miller indices $hkl$ with $d_{hkl} = a/\sqrt{h^2+k^2+l^2}$) they correspond to, given the lattice parameter $a = 0.405\,\text{nm}$ for aluminum.

Payoff 🎯

X-ray diffraction stands as the culminating technique of this chapter because it transforms the abstract wave nature of electromagnetic radiation into a precise, quantitative tool for probing the atomic architecture of matter. The phenomenon arises when X-rays, whose wavelengths ($\lambda \approx 0.01$–$10\ \text{nm}$) are commensurate with interatomic spacings, scatter coherently from the periodic planes of a crystal lattice. Constructive interference occurs only when the path-length difference between reflections from successive planes satisfies the Bragg condition:

$$ 2d\sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $d$ is the interplanar spacing and $\theta$ is the glancing angle. This single equation, simple in form yet profound in consequence, links a measurable angle to an atomic-scale distance.

Worked Example. Consider a sodium chloride crystal with $d = 0.282\ \text{nm}$ illuminated by X-rays of wavelength $\lambda = 0.154\ \text{nm}$ (Cu $K_\alpha$ line). For first-order diffraction ($m = 1$):

$$ \sin\theta = \frac{\lambda}{2d} = \frac{0.154}{2(0.282)} = 0.273 \implies \theta \approx 15.8°. $$

A detector rotated to $2\theta \approx 31.6°$ from the incident beam records the intensity maximum, confirming both the crystal spacing and the X-ray wavelength independently.

Key Theorem (Bragg's Law as a Phase Condition). The Bragg condition is equivalent to requiring that the scattering vector $\vec{q} = \vec{k}_f - \vec{k}_i$ coincide with a reciprocal-lattice vector $\vec{G}$. This Laue formulation generalizes Bragg's Law to arbitrarily oriented crystals and underpins modern crystallographic software, demonstrating that diffraction patterns encode the full three-dimensional Fourier transform of the electron density.

X-ray diffraction is not a terminal concept but a gateway. In materials science it reveals grain structure and phase composition of alloys. In structural biology, rotating-crystal and powder methods reconstructed the double-helix geometry of DNA and the active sites of enzymes. In solid-state physics, diffraction maps phonon dispersion and detects subtle distortions signaling superconducting transitions.
