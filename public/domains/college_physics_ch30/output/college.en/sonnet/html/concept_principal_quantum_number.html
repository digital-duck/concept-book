🍃 Principal Quantum Number

🍃 Principal Quantum Number

Definition. The principal quantum number, denoted $n$, is a positive integer ($n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$) that specifies the energy level and mean orbital radius of an electron in a hydrogen-like atom. It arises naturally from the boundary conditions imposed on the radial part of the Schrödinger equation for a Coulomb potential. The allowed energies of the hydrogen atom are given exactly by

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

The ground state ($n = 1$) has energy $E_1 = -13.6$ eV. As $n \to \infty$, $E_n \to 0$, corresponding to a free (unbound) electron. The associated mean orbital radius scales as $r_n = n^2 a_0$, where $a_0 \approx 0.0529$ nm is the Bohr radius.

Worked Example. An electron in a hydrogen atom transitions from $n = 3$ to $n = 2$. Determine the wavelength of the emitted photon.

First, compute the energy of each level:

$$ E_3 = -\frac{13.6}{9} \approx -1.511 \text{ eV}, \qquad E_2 = -\frac{13.6}{4} = -3.400 \text{ eV}. $$

The energy released is

$$ \Delta E = E_3 - E_2 = (-1.511) - (-3.400) = 1.889 \text{ eV}. $$

Converting to joules: $\Delta E = 1.889 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \approx 3.027 \times 10^{-19}$ J. Using $E = hc/\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{3.027 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 656 \text{ nm}. $$

This is the H$\alpha$ line in the Balmer series, visible as red light.

Key Theorem (Rydberg Formula). For any transition between levels $n_i > n_f$ in hydrogen, the emitted photon wavenumber is

$$ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left(\frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2}\right), $$

where $R_H = 1.097 \times 10^7$ m$^{-1}$ is the Rydberg constant. This formula unifies all spectral series (Lyman, Balmer, Paschen) under a single expression parameterized solely by $n$.

Lab Exercise. Using a hydrogen discharge tube and diffraction grating spectrometer, measure the wavelengths of at least three visible Balmer series lines. For each line, identify $n_i$ and $n_f$, compute the theoretical wavelength from the Rydberg formula, and calculate the percent discrepancy. Estimate $R_H$ from your data and compare it to the accepted value.
