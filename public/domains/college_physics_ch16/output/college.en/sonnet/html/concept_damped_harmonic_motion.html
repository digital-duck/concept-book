🍃 Damped Harmonic Motion

🍃 Damped Harmonic Motion

Definition. Damped harmonic motion describes oscillatory motion in which energy is progressively dissipated by a resistive force, causing the amplitude to decrease over time. For a mass $m$ on a spring with spring constant $k$, subject to a velocity-dependent damping force $F_d = -bv$, the single new primitive is the damping coefficient $b > 0$ (SI unit: kg/s), which characterizes the strength of the resistive medium. Newton's second law then yields:

$$ m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = 0 $$

Defining $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ (the natural angular frequency, already established for simple harmonic motion) and $\gamma = b/(2m)$ (derived from $b$), this becomes:

$$ \ddot{x} + 2\gamma\dot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0 $$

When $\gamma < \omega_0$ (underdamping), the system oscillates at a modified frequency $\omega_d = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \gamma^2}$ and the general solution is:

$$ x(t) = A_0 e^{-\gamma t} \cos(\omega_d t + \phi) $$

where $A_0$ and $\phi$ are determined by initial conditions. The factor $e^{-\gamma t}$ constitutes an exponentially decaying envelope that bounds the oscillation amplitude.

Worked Example. A 0.50 kg mass is attached to a spring ($k = 20\ \text{N/m}$) with damping coefficient $b = 1.0\ \text{kg/s}$. Find $\omega_d$ and the time for the amplitude to decay to half its initial value.

First, $\omega_0 = \sqrt{20/0.50} = \sqrt{40} \approx 6.32\ \text{rad/s}$ and $\gamma = 1.0/(2 \times 0.50) = 1.0\ \text{s}^{-1}$. Since $\gamma \operatorname{ll} \omega_0$, the system is underdamped: $\omega_d = \sqrt{40 - 1} \approx 6.24\ \text{rad/s}$. The amplitude halves when $e^{-\gamma t_{1/2}} = 1/2$, giving $t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \gamma \approx 0.693\ \text{s}$.

Key Theorem. The mechanical energy of an underdamped oscillator decays as $E(t) = \frac{1}{2}kA_0^2\, e^{-2\gamma t}$, so energy decays at twice the rate of amplitude. Larger $b$ increases $\gamma$, accelerating both amplitude and energy loss. In the limit $b \to 0$, the solution reduces exactly to simple harmonic motion with constant amplitude, confirming that undamped SHM is a special case.

Lab Exercise. Mount a 200 g mass on a spring and suspend it in a beaker of water to increase damping. Record position versus time using a motion sensor. Extract $\gamma$ by fitting the amplitude envelope to $A(t) = A_0 e^{-\gamma t}$, then estimate $b = 2m\gamma$ and compare with the free-air case to quantify how the fluid raises the damping coefficient.
