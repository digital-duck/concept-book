🍃 Natural Frequency

🍃 Natural Frequency

Definition. The natural frequency of an oscillating system is the frequency at which the system oscillates when displaced from equilibrium and released in the absence of any driving or damping forces. For a simple harmonic oscillator, the natural frequency $f_0$ is determined entirely by the system's physical parameters—its inertia and its restoring-force constant—and is independent of the amplitude of oscillation. In angular terms, the natural angular frequency is

$$ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}, $$

where $k$ is the spring constant (N/m) and $m$ is the mass (kg). The natural frequency in hertz is then

$$ f_0 = \frac{\omega_0}{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}, $$

and the corresponding natural period is $T_0 = 1/f_0$.

Worked Example. A 0.250 kg block is attached to a spring with $k = 40.0\ \text{N/m}$ on a frictionless horizontal surface. Find the natural frequency and period.

$$ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{40.0}{0.250}} = \sqrt{160} \approx 12.65\ \text{rad/s} $$ $$ f_0 = \frac{12.65}{2\pi} \approx 2.01\ \text{Hz}, \qquad T_0 = \frac{1}{2.01} \approx 0.497\ \text{s}. $$

No matter how far (within the linear regime) the block is displaced, it will always complete approximately 2.01 full oscillations per second.

Key Theorem — Resonance. When an external periodic force drives an oscillating system at a frequency equal to its natural frequency $f_0$, the system is said to be in resonance. In the undamped limit, the amplitude of oscillation grows without bound; with damping present, the steady-state amplitude is finite but reaches its maximum precisely at the natural frequency. This result has critical implications in structural and acoustic engineering: a structure subjected to periodic loading at its own natural frequency can accumulate dangerously large oscillations even under modest driving forces.

Lab Exercise. Suspend masses of 100 g, 200 g, and 300 g from the same spring. Measure the period of free oscillation for each mass using a photogate timer. Compute $f_0$ from each measurement and compare with the theoretical prediction $f_0 = (1/2\pi)\sqrt{k/m}$. Plot $f_0^2$ versus $1/m$ and verify that the slope equals $k/(4\pi^2)$.
