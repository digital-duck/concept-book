🍃 Transverse Wave

🍃 Transverse Wave

A transverse wave is a mechanical or electromagnetic disturbance in which the displacement of the medium's particles (or the oscillation of the field vectors) occurs perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. Formally, if the wave travels along the $+x$-axis, each particle of the medium oscillates along the $y$- or $z$-axis. This distinguishes transverse waves from longitudinal waves, in which particle displacement is parallel to propagation. The mathematical description of a harmonic transverse wave is given by:

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

where $A$ is the amplitude, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, $\lambda$ is the wavelength, $f$ is the frequency, and $\phi$ is the initial phase. The wave speed relates these parameters via:

$$ v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \lambda f $$

Worked Example. A transverse wave on a string is described by $y(x,t) = 0.03\sin(4\pi x - 20\pi t)$ m. Identify the amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave speed.

Comparing with the standard form: $A = 0.03$ m, $k = 4\pi$ rad/m, $\omega = 20\pi$ rad/s. Then:

$$ \lambda = \frac{2\pi}{k} = \frac{2\pi}{4\pi} = 0.50 \text{ m} $$ $$ f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{20\pi}{2\pi} = 10 \text{ Hz} $$ $$ v = \lambda f = (0.50)(10) = 5.0 \text{ m/s} $$

Key Theorem — Wave Speed on a String. For a transverse wave traveling along a stretched string under tension $F_T$ with linear mass density $\mu$ (kg/m), the wave speed is:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{F_T}{\mu}} $$

This result follows from applying Newton's second law to a small string element undergoing transverse acceleration, then taking the limit as the element length approaches zero. Increasing tension raises the restoring force and hence the propagation speed; increasing linear density raises inertia and lowers it.

Lab Exercise. Set up a string of measured length $L$ and mass $m$ between a fixed wall and a pulley loaded with hanging mass $M$, giving linear mass density $\mu = m/L$. Drive the string at several frequencies using a mechanical oscillator. For each standing-wave resonance observed, record the frequency $f_n$ and the number of antinodes $n$, compute the experimental wave speed $v = 2Lf_n/n$, and compare it to the theoretical prediction $v = \sqrt{Mg/\mu}$. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify the dominant sources of error.
