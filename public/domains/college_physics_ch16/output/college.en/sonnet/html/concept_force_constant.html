🍃 Force Constant

🍃 Force Constant

Definition. The force constant (also called the spring constant), denoted $k$, characterizes the stiffness of a restoring force that obeys Hooke's Law. For a system displaced by $x$ from its equilibrium position, the restoring force satisfies

$$ F = -kx, $$

where the negative sign indicates that the force opposes the displacement. The SI unit of $k$ is newtons per meter ($\text{N/m}$). A large value of $k$ corresponds to a stiff spring requiring substantial force for a small displacement; a small $k$ describes a compliant spring that deflects readily under modest loading.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 0.500\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface and is attached to a spring. When a horizontal force of $F = 8.00\,\text{N}$ is applied, the spring stretches by $x = 0.0400\,\text{m}$. Find $k$.

From Hooke's Law (taking magnitudes, since the direction is established by the setup):

$$ k = \frac{F}{x} = \frac{8.00\,\text{N}}{0.0400\,\text{m}} = 200\,\text{N/m}. $$

The block is then released from rest at $x = 0.0400\,\text{m}$. The angular frequency $\omega$ of the resulting simple harmonic motion is

$$ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{200}{0.500}} = 20.0\,\text{rad/s}, $$

giving a period $T = 2\pi/\omega \approx 0.314\,\text{s}$.

Key Theorem. For a mass–spring system executing simple harmonic motion, the force constant determines the period through the relation

$$ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}. $$

This result follows directly from Newton's second law applied to $F = -kx$, yielding the differential equation $\ddot{x} + (k/m)x = 0$, whose general solution is $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$, with $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$ and $A$ the amplitude.

Lab Exercise. Suspend masses of $0.100\,\text{kg}$, $0.200\,\text{kg}$, and $0.300\,\text{kg}$ in turn from a vertical spring. Measure the equilibrium displacement $x$ for each mass and plot $mg$ versus $x$. The slope of the best-fit line gives $k$ experimentally. Compare your result to the value obtained by measuring the oscillation period $T$ and solving $k = 4\pi^2 m / T^2$. Quantify any discrepancy and identify likely sources of systematic error.
