🌱 Displacement

🌱 Displacement

Definition. In classical mechanics, displacement is the vector quantity that describes the change in position of an object. If a particle occupies position $\vec{r}_i$ at time $t_i$ and position $\vec{r}_f$ at time $t_f$, its displacement is defined as

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i. $$

In one dimension along the $x$-axis this reduces to the scalar $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$, where the algebraic sign encodes direction. Displacement is categorically distinct from distance traveled: distance is the total arc length traversed along the actual path and is always non-negative, whereas displacement depends only on the endpoints and can be zero even when substantial motion has occurred.

Worked Example. A student walks $8\,\text{m}$ east, then $6\,\text{m}$ north. Taking east as $+x$ and north as $+y$, the displacement vector is

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = 8\,\hat{x} + 6\,\hat{y}\;\text{m}. $$

The magnitude is

$$ |\Delta \vec{r}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{100} = 10\,\text{m}, $$

directed at $\theta = \arctan(6/8) \approx 36.9°$ north of east. The total distance walked is $8 + 6 = 14\,\text{m}$, illustrating that distance and displacement magnitude are generally unequal.

Key Theorem. For motion described by a position function $x(t)$ that is differentiable on $[t_i, t_f]$, the displacement equals the definite integral of velocity:

$$ \Delta x = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} v(t)\, dt. $$

This follows directly from the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, since $v(t) = dx/dt$. Importantly, this integral can be negative, reflecting net displacement in the $-x$ direction, whereas the total distance is $\int_{t_i}^{t_f} |v(t)|\, dt \geq |\Delta x|$.

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor interfaced to data-acquisition software, record the position $x(t)$ of a dynamics cart on a frictionless track as it bounces between two bumpers over a $5\,\text{s}$ interval. From the recorded data: (a) compute the displacement $\Delta x$ over the full interval; (b) numerically integrate $|v(t)|$ to find total distance traveled; (c) compare the two results and explain any discrepancy in terms of the cart's direction reversals.

🌱 Equilibrium

🌱 Equilibrium

Definition. A system is in mechanical equilibrium when the net external force and the net external torque acting on it are simultaneously zero. Formally, for a rigid body, equilibrium requires

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}. $$

The first condition eliminates linear acceleration; the second eliminates angular acceleration. Together they are necessary and sufficient for a rigid body to remain in a state of unchanging motion—whether at rest or moving at constant velocity.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ and length $L = 4\,\text{m}$ is supported at its left end by a hinge and at a point $d = 3\,\text{m}$ from the left end by a vertical cable. A box of mass $M = 50\,\text{kg}$ hangs from the right end. Find the cable tension $T$ and the hinge reaction force $\vec{R}$.

Take torques about the hinge to eliminate $\vec{R}$:

$$ T\,d - mg\frac{L}{2} - Mg\,L = 0. $$

Substituting values ($g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$):

$$ T(3) = (20)(9.8)(2) + (50)(9.8)(4) = 392 + 1960 = 2352\,\text{N·m}, $$ $$ T = 784\,\text{N}. $$

Applying $\sum F_y = 0$: $\;R_y + T - mg - Mg = 0$, giving $R_y = (70)(9.8) - 784 = -98\,\text{N}$ (directed downward). Since no horizontal forces act, $R_x = 0$.

Key Theorem. The location chosen as the pivot for computing torques is arbitrary when the system is in complete equilibrium; the torque-balance equation $\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$ holds about any point. This invariance provides a powerful problem-solving strategy: selecting the pivot at an unknown force's point of application eliminates that unknown from the torque equation, simplifying the algebra considerably.

Lab Exercise. Set up a meter stick on a fulcrum at the 40 cm mark. Hang a 200 g mass at the 10 cm mark and an unknown mass $m_x$ at the 70 cm mark. Using the torque-balance condition about the fulcrum, predict $m_x$, then verify by direct measurement. Repeat the experiment with the fulcrum shifted to the 50 cm mark and confirm that the equilibrium condition remains valid at the new pivot location.

🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. A force is a vector quantity representing an interaction that, when applied to a body, alters or tends to alter the body's state of motion or shape. In SI units, force is measured in newtons (N), where $1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Newton's Second Law provides the operational definition:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass of the object and $\vec{a}$ is the resulting acceleration. Forces are additive vectors; the net force acting on a body is the vector sum of all individual forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i $$

Forces arise from fundamental interactions — gravitational, electromagnetic, and nuclear — and from their macroscopic manifestations such as tension, normal forces, and friction.

Worked Example. A crate of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 50\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -30\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$. Find the acceleration.

The net force is:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (50 - 30)\,\hat{x}\,\text{N} = 20\,\hat{x}\,\text{N} $$

Applying Newton's Second Law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{20\,\hat{x}}{20\,\text{kg}} = 1.0\,\hat{x}\,\text{m/s}^2 $$

The crate accelerates at $1.0\,\text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction.

Key Theorem — Newton's Third Law. For every force exerted by object A on object B, there exists an equal and opposite force exerted by B on A:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A} $$

This law is essential for constructing free-body diagrams correctly: third-law pairs act on different objects and therefore never cancel when analyzing the net force on a single body. Misidentifying third-law pairs is among the most common errors in mechanics.

Lab Exercise. Using a force sensor and a dynamics cart on a low-friction track, apply a series of measured horizontal forces and record the resulting accelerations. Plot $F_{\text{net}}$ versus $a$ for a fixed cart mass and verify that the slope equals $m$, confirming the linear relationship expressed in Newton's Second Law.

🍃 Periodic Motion

🍃 Periodic Motion

Definition. A system undergoes periodic motion when its state repeats itself at regular time intervals. Formally, if $x(t)$ describes the position of a particle, then the motion is periodic with period $T$ if

$$ x(t + T) = x(t) \quad \text{for all } t. $$

The smallest positive value of $T$ satisfying this relation is called the fundamental period. The frequency $f$ measures how many complete cycles occur per unit time:

$$ f = \frac{1}{T}, $$

with SI unit hertz ($1\,\text{Hz} = 1\,\text{s}^{-1}$). The angular frequency $\omega$ relates to frequency by

$$ \omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T}. $$

These three quantities — $T$, $f$, and $\omega$ — are interchangeable descriptors of the same periodic behavior.

Worked Example. A mass on a horizontal frictionless surface is attached to a spring of stiffness $k = 200\,\text{N/m}$. The mass is $m = 0.50\,\text{kg}$. Determine the period and frequency of oscillation.

For a mass–spring system, Newton's second law yields the equation of motion $m\ddot{x} = -kx$, whose solution is simple harmonic: $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$. The angular frequency is

$$ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{200}{0.50}} = \sqrt{400} = 20\,\text{rad/s}. $$

Therefore,

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi}{20} \approx 0.314\,\text{s}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T} \approx 3.18\,\text{Hz}. $$

Note that neither $T$ nor $f$ depends on the amplitude $A$; this isochronism is a defining feature of simple harmonic motion.

Key Theorem (Isochronism of SHM). For any linear restoring force $F = -kx$, the period $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$ is independent of amplitude. This follows directly from the linearity of the differential equation: scaling the initial conditions scales $x(t)$ uniformly without altering $\omega$.

Lab Exercise. Suspend a mass of $m = 0.20\,\text{kg}$ from a spring and measure the period of vertical oscillations for three amplitudes: $A = 2\,\text{cm}$, $4\,\text{cm}$, and $8\,\text{cm}$. Record five oscillations each time and compute the average period. Verify that $T$ remains constant across amplitudes to within experimental uncertainty, and use your measured $T$ to calculate the effective spring constant $k = 4\pi^2 m / T^2$.

🍃 Restoring Force

🍃 Restoring Force

A restoring force is any force that acts on a displaced object in a direction opposing that displacement, always directed toward the system's equilibrium position. Formally, if $x$ denotes displacement from equilibrium, a restoring force $F$ satisfies

$$ F = -kx $$

for some positive constant $k$, known as the spring constant (SI unit: $\text{N}\,\text{m}^{-1}$). The negative sign encodes the opposition between force direction and displacement direction. This relationship is Hooke's Law, and any system obeying it executes simple harmonic motion.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 0.500\ \text{kg}$ is attached to a horizontal spring with $k = 80.0\ \text{N}\,\text{m}^{-1}$. The block is pulled $x_0 = 0.120\ \text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest. Find the restoring force at release and at the equilibrium position.

At release ($x = 0.120\ \text{m}$):

$$ F = -kx = -(80.0)(0.120) = -9.60\ \text{N} $$

The magnitude is $9.60\ \text{N}$, directed back toward equilibrium. At equilibrium ($x = 0$):

$$ F = -(80.0)(0) = 0\ \text{N} $$

The force vanishes at equilibrium — consistent with the definition of that point as the position of zero net force.

Key Theorem. For a mass–spring system governed by $F = -kx$, Newton's second law gives

$$ m\ddot{x} = -kx \implies \ddot{x} + \omega^2 x = 0, \quad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} $$

The general solution is $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$, confirming that a linear restoring force produces sinusoidal simple harmonic motion with angular frequency $\omega$ and period $T = 2\pi/\omega$. The amplitude $A$ and phase $\phi$ are determined entirely by initial conditions.

Lab Exercise. Suspend a spring vertically and attach masses of $m = 0.100,\ 0.200,\ 0.300\ \text{kg}$ in turn. For each mass, measure the static displacement $\Delta x$ at equilibrium. Plot $mg$ versus $\Delta x$; the slope yields the experimental spring constant $k$. Compare your result to the manufacturer's value and compute the percent error. Then displace the $0.200\ \text{kg}$ mass by $5.00\ \text{cm}$, release it, and time 20 complete oscillations to determine $\omega_{\text{exp}}$. Verify that $\omega_{\text{exp}} \approx \sqrt{k/m}$ within experimental uncertainty.

🍃 Hookes Law

🍃 Hooke's Law

Definition. Hooke's Law states that the restoring force exerted by a spring is directly proportional to the displacement from its equilibrium position, provided that displacement remains within the elastic limit. Formally, if $x$ denotes the displacement from equilibrium, the restoring force $F$ satisfies

$$ F = -kx, $$

where $k > 0$ is the spring constant (or stiffness coefficient), measured in newtons per meter ($\text{N/m}$). The negative sign reflects that the force opposes the displacement: when the spring is stretched ($x > 0$), the force acts in the negative direction, and vice versa. This linear relationship holds as long as the material is not deformed beyond its elastic limit—beyond that threshold, permanent deformation occurs and the law breaks down.

Worked Example. A horizontal spring with spring constant $k = 200\ \text{N/m}$ is compressed by $x = 0.15\ \text{m}$ from its natural length. Find the magnitude and direction of the restoring force.

Applying Hooke's Law directly:

$$ F = -kx = -(200\ \text{N/m})(0.15\ \text{m}) = -30\ \text{N}. $$

The magnitude is $|F| = 30\ \text{N}$, directed opposite to the compression—that is, in the direction of elongation. If the compression defines the positive $x$-direction, the spring pushes the attached mass in the negative $x$-direction with a force of $30\ \text{N}$.

Key Theorem. When a mass $m$ is attached to a spring obeying Hooke's Law and constrained to one-dimensional motion, Newton's second law gives $m\ddot{x} = -kx$. This differential equation has the general solution

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), $$

where $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$ is the angular frequency of the resulting oscillation, $A$ is the amplitude, and $\phi$ is the phase constant determined by initial conditions. The period of oscillation is $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$, independent of amplitude—a hallmark of simple harmonic motion.

Lab Exercise. Suspend a spring vertically and attach masses of $m = 0.10,\ 0.20,\ 0.30,\ 0.40\ \text{kg}$ in turn. Measure the equilibrium extension $x$ for each load. Plot $F = mg$ versus $x$ and determine $k$ from the slope. Compare your experimental value to the manufacturer specification and calculate the percent error. Verify that the relationship remains linear across all tested masses.

Payoff 🎯

The pendulum clock represents one of the most elegant convergences of physical principle and practical engineering in the history of science. At its core, the device exploits the isochronous property of small-angle simple harmonic motion: for a simple pendulum of length $L$, the period

$$ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} $$

depends only on $L$ and the local gravitational acceleration $g$, not on the amplitude of swing (provided $\theta \operatorname{ll} 1$ rad). This remarkable independence from amplitude is precisely what makes the pendulum a reliable timekeeper. The pendulum clock is therefore the natural endpoint of Chapter 16 because it synthesizes every major theme of the chapter — restoring forces, angular frequency $\omega = \sqrt{g/L}$, energy exchange between kinetic and potential forms, and the damping-and-driving balance needed to sustain oscillation against friction — into a single, historically transformative instrument.

Worked Example. A pendulum clock is calibrated at sea level where $g = 9.80\ \text{m s}^{-2}$. Its pendulum has length $L = 0.9929\ \text{m}$, giving $T = 2\pi\sqrt{0.9929/9.80} = 2.000\ \text{s}$, so the clock ticks once per second. If the clock is transported to a mountain laboratory where $g = 9.78\ \text{m s}^{-2}$, the new period becomes $T' = 2\pi\sqrt{0.9929/9.78} \approx 2.002\ \text{s}$. The clock loses approximately $0.002\ \text{s}$ per tick, accumulating an error of roughly $86\ \text{s}$ per day — a measurable and correctable systematic shift.

Key Theorem (Isochronism). For a pendulum with amplitude $\theta_0$, the exact period is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}\left(1 + \frac{1}{16}\theta_0^2 + \cdots\right)$. The leading correction is second-order in $\theta_0$, confirming that amplitude independence holds to excellent approximation whenever $\theta_0 \lesssim 0.1\ \text{rad}$ ($\approx 6°$).

The pendulum clock also opens the door to geophysical measurement: because $T$ is sensitive to $g$, a precision pendulum can map gravitational anomalies caused by subsurface density variations, a technique foundational to modern gravimetry and resource exploration.

Lab Exercise. Using a photogate and a pendulum of adjustable length, measure $T$ for five values of $L$ between $0.25\ \text{m}$ and $1.00\ \text{m}$. Plot $T^2$ versus $L$ and extract $g$ from the slope $4\pi^2/g$. Compare your result to the accepted local value and estimate the fractional uncertainty introduced by finite amplitude.

The physics you have built throughout this chapter — from Hooke's law to driven resonance — lives inside every tick of a pendulum clock. To see how that same isochronism underlies the GPS-synchronized atomic timescales that replaced the pendulum, follow the thread into the physics of oscillators and frequency standards.
