🍃 Critical Damping

🍃 Critical Damping

Definition. A damped harmonic oscillator obeys the equation of motion

$$ m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = 0, $$

where $m$ is the mass, $b$ is the damping coefficient, and $k$ is the spring constant. Recalling the natural angular frequency $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ and the damping ratio $\zeta = b/(2\sqrt{mk})$ introduced in the preceding section, the system is said to be critically damped when $\zeta = 1$. The single new quantity this condition defines is the critical damping coefficient

$$ b_c \equiv 2\sqrt{mk} = 2m\omega_0, $$

which sets the precise value of $b$ required to achieve critical damping. At this value the characteristic equation $m\lambda^2 + b\lambda + k = 0$ yields a repeated real root $\lambda = -\omega_0$, producing the general solution

$$ x(t) = (C_1 + C_2 t)\,e^{-\omega_0 t}. $$

This solution returns to equilibrium faster than any other damping regime without oscillating, making $b_c$ the boundary between underdamping ($\zeta < 1$, oscillatory) and overdamping ($\zeta > 1$, sluggish non-oscillatory).

Worked Example. A door closer has $m = 2\,\text{kg}$, $k = 8\,\text{N/m}$, and is released from rest at $x_0 = 0.5\,\text{m}$. Determine $b_c$ and the position as a function of time.

First, $\omega_0 = \sqrt{8/2} = 2\,\text{rad/s}$, so $b_c = 2(2)(2) = 8\,\text{N·s/m}$.

Applying initial conditions $x(0) = 0.5$ and $\dot{x}(0) = 0$ gives $C_1 = 0.5$ and $C_2 = \omega_0 C_1 = 1.0\,\text{m/s}$:

$$ x(t) = (0.5 + 1.0\,t)\,e^{-2t}\,\text{m}. $$

The door closes continuously, reaching $x = 0.01\,\text{m}$ at approximately $t \approx 2.7\,\text{s}$, without overshooting the frame.

Key Theorem. Among all non-oscillatory responses to an initial displacement, the critically damped solution minimizes the time required to return to within any prescribed neighborhood of equilibrium. Formally, for any $\epsilon > 0$ the critical solution achieves $|x(t)| < \epsilon$ at the smallest $t^*$ relative to overdamped solutions with the same initial data.

Lab Exercise. Construct a mass-spring system on an air track and measure the decay time for three values of $b$: estimated underdamped, critical, and overdamped (adjust $b$ using magnetic braking). Plot $x(t)$ for each case and fit the critically damped data to $(C_1 + C_2 t)e^{-\omega_0 t}$ to extract $\omega_0$. Compare the extracted value with $\sqrt{k/m}$ measured independently.
