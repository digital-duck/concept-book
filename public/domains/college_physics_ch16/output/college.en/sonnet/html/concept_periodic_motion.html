🍃 Periodic Motion

🍃 Periodic Motion

Definition. A system undergoes periodic motion when its state repeats itself at regular time intervals. Formally, if $x(t)$ describes the position of a particle, then the motion is periodic with period $T$ if

$$ x(t + T) = x(t) \quad \text{for all } t. $$

The smallest positive value of $T$ satisfying this relation is called the fundamental period. The frequency $f$ measures how many complete cycles occur per unit time:

$$ f = \frac{1}{T}, $$

with SI unit hertz ($1\,\text{Hz} = 1\,\text{s}^{-1}$). The angular frequency $\omega$ relates to frequency by

$$ \omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T}. $$

These three quantities — $T$, $f$, and $\omega$ — are interchangeable descriptors of the same periodic behavior.

Worked Example. A mass on a horizontal frictionless surface is attached to a spring of stiffness $k = 200\,\text{N/m}$. The mass is $m = 0.50\,\text{kg}$. Determine the period and frequency of oscillation.

For a mass–spring system, Newton's second law yields the equation of motion $m\ddot{x} = -kx$, whose solution is simple harmonic: $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$. The angular frequency is

$$ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{200}{0.50}} = \sqrt{400} = 20\,\text{rad/s}. $$

Therefore,

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi}{20} \approx 0.314\,\text{s}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T} \approx 3.18\,\text{Hz}. $$

Note that neither $T$ nor $f$ depends on the amplitude $A$; this isochronism is a defining feature of simple harmonic motion.

Key Theorem (Isochronism of SHM). For any linear restoring force $F = -kx$, the period $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$ is independent of amplitude. This follows directly from the linearity of the differential equation: scaling the initial conditions scales $x(t)$ uniformly without altering $\omega$.

Lab Exercise. Suspend a mass of $m = 0.20\,\text{kg}$ from a spring and measure the period of vertical oscillations for three amplitudes: $A = 2\,\text{cm}$, $4\,\text{cm}$, and $8\,\text{cm}$. Record five oscillations each time and compute the average period. Verify that $T$ remains constant across amplitudes to within experimental uncertainty, and use your measured $T$ to calculate the effective spring constant $k = 4\pi^2 m / T^2$.
