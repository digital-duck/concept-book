🍃 Forced Oscillation

🍃 Forced Oscillation

A forced oscillation occurs when an external periodic force is applied to an oscillating system, sustaining motion against dissipation. Unlike free oscillations—where a system vibrates at its natural frequency $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ and gradually loses energy—a driven system is continuously supplied energy by the driving force $F(t) = F_0 \cos(\omega_d t)$, where $F_0$ is the driving amplitude and $\omega_d$ is the driving frequency. The equation of motion becomes:

$$ m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = F_0 \cos(\omega_d t) $$

where $b$ is the damping coefficient. In steady state, transient behavior dies away and the system oscillates at $\omega_d$ with amplitude:

$$ A = \frac{F_0/m}{\sqrt{(\omega_0^2 - \omega_d^2)^2 + (b\omega_d/m)^2}} $$

Worked Example. A 0.50 kg mass on a spring ($k = 50\ \text{N/m}$) is driven by $F_0 = 2.0\ \text{N}$ at $\omega_d = 8.0\ \text{rad/s}$, with damping $b = 1.0\ \text{N·s/m}$. The natural frequency is $\omega_0 = \sqrt{50/0.50} = 10.0\ \text{rad/s}$. Substituting:

$$ A = \frac{2.0/0.50}{\sqrt{(100 - 64)^2 + (1.0 \cdot 8.0/0.50)^2}} = \frac{4.0}{\sqrt{(36)^2 + (16)^2}} = \frac{4.0}{\sqrt{1296 + 256}} \approx \frac{4.0}{39.5} \approx 0.101\ \text{m} $$

Amplitude–Frequency Theorem. The amplitude $A(\omega_d)$ is maximized when $\omega_d = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - b^2/(2m^2)}$. For light damping ($b \operatorname{ll} 2m\omega_0$), this peak frequency approaches $\omega_0$, and the maximum amplitude scales as $A_{\max} \approx F_0/(b\omega_0)$. Consequently, reducing damping dramatically amplifies the peak response—a fact of critical importance in structural engineering and machinery design.

Lab Exercise. Set up a mass-spring oscillator on an air track with an attached electromagnetic driver. Vary $\omega_d$ from $0.5\omega_0$ to $2.0\omega_0$ in steps of $0.1\omega_0$, recording steady-state amplitude at each frequency. Plot $A$ versus $\omega_d$ and locate the peak. Repeat for two different damping resistances and compare how the peak height and width change with $b$, verifying that a smaller $b$ produces a taller, narrower response curve.
