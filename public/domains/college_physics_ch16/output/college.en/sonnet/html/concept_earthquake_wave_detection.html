🌸 Earthquake Wave Detection

🌸 Earthquake Wave Detection

Definition. Earthquake wave detection is the systematic measurement and analysis of seismic waves—mechanical disturbances propagating through Earth's interior and along its surface following a seismic event. Seismic waves are classified into two primary bulk types: P-waves (primary or compressional waves), in which particle displacement is parallel to the propagation direction, and S-waves (secondary or shear waves), in which displacement is perpendicular to propagation. A seismometer detects ground motion by exploiting the inertia of a suspended mass: as the instrument housing moves with the ground, the mass tends to remain stationary, and the relative displacement is recorded as a function of time, producing a seismogram.

Worked Example. A seismic station records the arrival of a P-wave at $t_P = 0\,\text{s}$ and an S-wave at $t_S = 48\,\text{s}$. Given that P-wave speed in continental crust is $v_P = 6.0\,\text{km/s}$ and S-wave speed is $v_S = 3.5\,\text{km/s}$, estimate the epicentral distance $d$.

Because both waves originate at the same hypocenter simultaneously,

$$ d = v_P\, t_P = v_S\, t_S \implies t_S - t_P = d\!\left(\frac{1}{v_S} - \frac{1}{v_P}\right). $$

Solving for $d$:

$$ d = \frac{t_S - t_P}{\dfrac{1}{v_S} - \dfrac{1}{v_P}} = \frac{48}{\dfrac{1}{3.5} - \dfrac{1}{6.0}} = \frac{48}{0.2857 - 0.1667} = \frac{48}{0.1190} \approx 403\,\text{km}. $$

Key Theorem (S–P Interval Method). The epicentral distance $d$ is uniquely determined by the S–P arrival time difference and the known wave speeds:

$$ d = \frac{\Delta t_{SP}}{\dfrac{1}{v_S} - \dfrac{1}{v_P}}, \quad \Delta t_{SP} = t_S - t_P. $$

Triangulation using readings from at least three stations yields a unique epicenter location, since each station constrains $d$ to a circle of radius $d$ centered at that station; the circles intersect at the epicenter.

Lab Exercise. Using a three-axis seismometer dataset provided in your laboratory manual, identify the P- and S-wave arrival times on all three component traces. Apply the S–P interval method to compute the epicentral distance. Then, using distances from two additional simulated stations, construct circles on a regional map and locate the epicenter graphically. Compare your result with the catalog location and compute the percentage error.
