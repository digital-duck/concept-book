🌱 Displacement

🌱 Displacement

Definition. In classical mechanics, displacement is the vector quantity that describes the change in position of an object. If a particle occupies position $\vec{r}_i$ at time $t_i$ and position $\vec{r}_f$ at time $t_f$, its displacement is defined as

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i. $$

In one dimension along the $x$-axis this reduces to the scalar $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$, where the algebraic sign encodes direction. Displacement is categorically distinct from distance traveled: distance is the total arc length traversed along the actual path and is always non-negative, whereas displacement depends only on the endpoints and can be zero even when substantial motion has occurred.

Worked Example. A student walks $8\,\text{m}$ east, then $6\,\text{m}$ north. Taking east as $+x$ and north as $+y$, the displacement vector is

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = 8\,\hat{x} + 6\,\hat{y}\;\text{m}. $$

The magnitude is

$$ |\Delta \vec{r}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{100} = 10\,\text{m}, $$

directed at $\theta = \arctan(6/8) \approx 36.9°$ north of east. The total distance walked is $8 + 6 = 14\,\text{m}$, illustrating that distance and displacement magnitude are generally unequal.

Key Theorem. For motion described by a position function $x(t)$ that is differentiable on $[t_i, t_f]$, the displacement equals the definite integral of velocity:

$$ \Delta x = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} v(t)\, dt. $$

This follows directly from the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, since $v(t) = dx/dt$. Importantly, this integral can be negative, reflecting net displacement in the $-x$ direction, whereas the total distance is $\int_{t_i}^{t_f} |v(t)|\, dt \geq |\Delta x|$.

🌱 Equilibrium

🌱 Equilibrium

Definition. A system is in mechanical equilibrium when the net external force and the net external torque acting on it are simultaneously zero. Formally, for a rigid body, equilibrium requires

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}. $$

The first condition eliminates linear acceleration; the second eliminates angular acceleration. Together they are necessary and sufficient for a rigid body to remain in a state of unchanging motion—whether at rest or moving at constant velocity.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ and length $L = 4\,\text{m}$ is supported at its left end by a hinge and at a point $d = 3\,\text{m}$ from the left end by a vertical cable. A box of mass $M = 50\,\text{kg}$ hangs from the right end. Find the cable tension $T$ and the hinge reaction force $\vec{R}$.

Take torques about the hinge to eliminate $\vec{R}$:

$$ T\,d - mg\frac{L}{2} - Mg\,L = 0. $$

Substituting values ($g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$):

$$ T(3) = (20)(9.8)(2) + (50)(9.8)(4) = 392 + 1960 = 2352\,\text{N·m}, $$ $$ T = 784\,\text{N}. $$

Applying $\sum F_y = 0$: $\;R_y + T - mg - Mg = 0$, giving $R_y = (70)(9.8) - 784 = -98\,\text{N}$ (directed downward). Since no horizontal forces act, $R_x = 0$.

Key Theorem. The location chosen as the pivot for computing torques is arbitrary when the system is in complete equilibrium; the torque-balance equation $\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$ holds about any point. This invariance provides a powerful problem-solving strategy: selecting the pivot at an unknown force's point of application eliminates that unknown from the torque equation, simplifying the algebra considerably.

🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. A force is a vector quantity representing an interaction that, when applied to a body, alters or tends to alter the body's state of motion or shape. In SI units, force is measured in newtons (N), where $1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Newton's Second Law provides the operational definition:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass of the object and $\vec{a}$ is the resulting acceleration. Forces are additive vectors; the net force acting on a body is the vector sum of all individual forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i $$

Forces arise from fundamental interactions — gravitational, electromagnetic, and nuclear — and from their macroscopic manifestations such as tension, normal forces, and friction.

Worked Example. A crate of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 50\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -30\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$. Find the acceleration.

The net force is:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (50 - 30)\,\hat{x}\,\text{N} = 20\,\hat{x}\,\text{N} $$

Applying Newton's Second Law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{20\,\hat{x}}{20\,\text{kg}} = 1.0\,\hat{x}\,\text{m/s}^2 $$

The crate accelerates at $1.0\,\text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction.

Key Theorem — Newton's Third Law. For every force exerted by object A on object B, there exists an equal and opposite force exerted by B on A:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A} $$

This law is essential for constructing free-body diagrams correctly: third-law pairs act on different objects and therefore never cancel when analyzing the net force on a single body. Misidentifying third-law pairs is among the most common errors in mechanics.

🍃 Restoring Force

🍃 Restoring Force

A restoring force is any force that acts on a displaced object in a direction opposing that displacement, always directed toward the system's equilibrium position. Formally, if $x$ denotes displacement from equilibrium, a restoring force $F$ satisfies

$$ F = -kx $$

for some positive constant $k$, known as the spring constant (SI unit: $\text{N}\,\text{m}^{-1}$). The negative sign encodes the opposition between force direction and displacement direction. This relationship is Hooke's Law, and any system obeying it executes simple harmonic motion.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 0.500\ \text{kg}$ is attached to a horizontal spring with $k = 80.0\ \text{N}\,\text{m}^{-1}$. The block is pulled $x_0 = 0.120\ \text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest. Find the restoring force at release and at the equilibrium position.

At release ($x = 0.120\ \text{m}$):

$$ F = -kx = -(80.0)(0.120) = -9.60\ \text{N} $$

The magnitude is $9.60\ \text{N}$, directed back toward equilibrium. At equilibrium ($x = 0$):

$$ F = -(80.0)(0) = 0\ \text{N} $$

The force vanishes at equilibrium — consistent with the definition of that point as the position of zero net force.

Key Theorem. For a mass–spring system governed by $F = -kx$, Newton's second law gives

$$ m\ddot{x} = -kx \implies \ddot{x} + \omega^2 x = 0, \quad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} $$

The general solution is $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$, confirming that a linear restoring force produces sinusoidal simple harmonic motion with angular frequency $\omega$ and period $T = 2\pi/\omega$. The amplitude $A$ and phase $\phi$ are determined entirely by initial conditions.

🍃 Hookes Law

🍃 Hooke's Law

Definition. Hooke's Law states that the restoring force exerted by a spring is directly proportional to the displacement from its equilibrium position, provided that displacement remains within the elastic limit. Formally, if $x$ denotes the displacement from equilibrium, the restoring force $F$ satisfies

$$ F = -kx, $$

where $k > 0$ is the spring constant (or stiffness coefficient), measured in newtons per meter ($\text{N/m}$). The negative sign reflects that the force opposes the displacement: when the spring is stretched ($x > 0$), the force acts in the negative direction, and vice versa. This linear relationship holds as long as the material is not deformed beyond its elastic limit—beyond that threshold, permanent deformation occurs and the law breaks down.

Worked Example. A horizontal spring with spring constant $k = 200\ \text{N/m}$ is compressed by $x = 0.15\ \text{m}$ from its natural length. Find the magnitude and direction of the restoring force.

Applying Hooke's Law directly:

$$ F = -kx = -(200\ \text{N/m})(0.15\ \text{m}) = -30\ \text{N}. $$

The magnitude is $|F| = 30\ \text{N}$, directed opposite to the compression—that is, in the direction of elongation. If the compression defines the positive $x$-direction, the spring pushes the attached mass in the negative $x$-direction with a force of $30\ \text{N}$.

Key Theorem. When a mass $m$ is attached to a spring obeying Hooke's Law and constrained to one-dimensional motion, Newton's second law gives $m\ddot{x} = -kx$. This differential equation has the general solution

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), $$

where $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$ is the angular frequency of the resulting oscillation, $A$ is the amplitude, and $\phi$ is the phase constant determined by initial conditions. The period of oscillation is $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$, independent of amplitude—a hallmark of simple harmonic motion.

Plot $F = mg$ versus $x$ and determine $k$ from the slope. Compare your experimental value to the manufacturer specification and calculate the percent error. 🍃 Periodic Motion

🍃 Periodic Motion

Definition. A system undergoes periodic motion when its state repeats itself at regular time intervals. Formally, if $x(t)$ describes the position of a particle, then the motion is periodic with period $T$ if

$$ x(t + T) = x(t) \quad \text{for all } t. $$

The smallest positive value of $T$ satisfying this relation is called the fundamental period. The frequency $f$ measures how many complete cycles occur per unit time:

$$ f = \frac{1}{T}, $$

with SI unit hertz ($1\,\text{Hz} = 1\,\text{s}^{-1}$). The angular frequency $\omega$ relates to frequency by

$$ \omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T}. $$

These three quantities — $T$, $f$, and $\omega$ — are interchangeable descriptors of the same periodic behavior.

Worked Example. A mass on a horizontal frictionless surface is attached to a spring of stiffness $k = 200\,\text{N/m}$. The mass is $m = 0.50\,\text{kg}$. Determine the period and frequency of oscillation.

For a mass–spring system, Newton's second law yields the equation of motion $m\ddot{x} = -kx$, whose solution is simple harmonic: $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$. The angular frequency is

$$ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{200}{0.50}} = \sqrt{400} = 20\,\text{rad/s}. $$

Therefore,

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi}{20} \approx 0.314\,\text{s}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T} \approx 3.18\,\text{Hz}. $$

Note that neither $T$ nor $f$ depends on the amplitude $A$; this isochronism is a defining feature of simple harmonic motion.

Key Theorem (Isochronism of SHM). For any linear restoring force $F = -kx$, the period $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$ is independent of amplitude. This follows directly from the linearity of the differential equation: scaling the initial conditions scales $x(t)$ uniformly without altering $\omega$.

🍃 Amplitude

🍃 Amplitude

Definition. The amplitude of a periodic wave or oscillation is the maximum displacement of the oscillating quantity from its equilibrium position. For a mechanical wave or simple harmonic oscillator described by

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), $$

the amplitude $A$ is the positive real number satisfying $A = \max_t |x(t)|$. Amplitude carries the same units as the displacement itself (meters for transverse waves on a string, pascals for pressure waves in sound) and is strictly non-negative. Crucially, amplitude is independent of frequency and phase; it encodes the intensity of the oscillation, not its rate or timing.

Worked Example. A transverse wave on a string is described by

$$ y(x,t) = (2.4\times10^{-3}\,\text{m})\sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{0.65\,\text{m}}\,x - 2\pi\cdot 330\,\text{Hz}\cdot t\right). $$

Identify the amplitude. The coefficient of the sinusoidal function is $A = 2.4\times10^{-3}$ m $= 2.4$ mm. At any fixed position, the string oscillates between $+2.4$ mm and $-2.4$ mm from equilibrium; the maximum displacement magnitude is therefore $2.4$ mm regardless of $x$ or $t$. Note that $\omega = 2\pi(330)$ rad/s and the wavelength is $\lambda = 0.65$ m, but neither quantity affects $A$.

Key Theorem — Energy Proportionality. For a sinusoidal mechanical wave traveling through a medium of linear mass density $\mu$ at wave speed $v$, the average power transmitted is

$$ P_{\text{avg}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu v \omega^2 A^2. $$

This result, derived from integrating the instantaneous power $P = F_y v_y$ over one full cycle, establishes that average power scales as the square of the amplitude. Doubling the amplitude quadruples the energy transported per unit time. For the string above, with $\mu = 3.0\times10^{-3}$ kg/m and $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$, substituting $A = 2.4$ mm gives a concrete numerical prediction for $P_{\text{avg}}$.

Compute the predicted average power using the formula above. Then double the driving amplitude and repeat the measurement. 🌱 Energy

🌱 Energy

Definition. In classical mechanics and thermodynamics, energy is a scalar quantity that characterizes the capacity of a physical system to do work. For a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$, the kinetic energy is

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

The potential energy $U$ depends on the configuration of the system relative to a reference state; for example, the gravitational potential energy near Earth's surface is $U = mgh$, where $g \approx 9.8\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ and $h$ is the height above the chosen reference level. The total mechanical energy is defined as

$$ E = K + U. $$

Energy is measured in joules ($1\ \mathrm{J} = 1\ \mathrm{kg \cdot m^2/s^2}$).

Worked Example. A 2.0 kg ball is released from rest at a height $h = 5.0\ \mathrm{m}$ above the ground. Determine its speed just before impact, assuming no air resistance.

At the initial instant, $K_i = 0$ and $U_i = mgh = (2.0)(9.8)(5.0) = 98\ \mathrm{J}$, so $E = 98\ \mathrm{J}$. At impact, $h = 0$ and $U_f = 0$, so all energy is kinetic:

$$ K_f = 98\ \mathrm{J} \implies \frac{1}{2}(2.0)v^2 = 98 \implies v = \sqrt{98} \approx 9.9\ \mathrm{m/s}. $$

Key Theorem — Conservation of Mechanical Energy. For a system subject only to conservative forces (forces derivable from a potential energy function), the total mechanical energy is constant throughout the motion:

$$ E_i = E_f \implies K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f. $$

Proof sketch. The work–energy theorem states $W_\mathrm{net} = \Delta K$. For a conservative force, $W = -\Delta U$. Substituting gives $-\Delta U = \Delta K$, hence $\Delta(K + U) = 0$, confirming $E$ is constant. When non-conservative forces such as friction act, mechanical energy is not conserved; instead, the lost mechanical energy converts to thermal or internal energy, preserving the broader principle of total energy conservation.

🍃 Simple Harmonic Motion

🍃 Simple Harmonic Motion

Definition. Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a type of periodic oscillation in which the restoring force on an object is directly proportional to its displacement from equilibrium and acts in the opposite direction. Mathematically, this condition is expressed by Hooke's Law:

$$ F = -kx $$

where $k > 0$ is the spring constant (in N/m) and $x$ is the displacement from equilibrium. Applying Newton's second law yields the equation of motion:

$$ m\ddot{x} = -kx \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \ddot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0 $$

where $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ is the angular frequency (rad/s). The general solution is:

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \phi) $$

with amplitude $A$ (meters) and phase constant $\phi$ (radians) determined by initial conditions. The period and frequency follow directly from $\omega_0$:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T} $$

Worked Example. A 0.500 kg block is attached to a spring with $k = 200\ \text{N/m}$. At $t = 0$, the block is displaced $x_0 = 0.100\ \text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest.

  • Angular frequency: $\omega_0 = \sqrt{200/0.500} = 20.0\ \text{rad/s}$
  • Period: $T = 2\pi/20.0 \approx 0.314\ \text{s}$
  • Since $v(0) = 0$, the phase constant $\phi = 0$, giving $x(t) = 0.100\cos(20.0\,t)\ \text{m}$
  • Maximum speed: $v_{\max} = A\omega_0 = (0.100)(20.0) = 2.00\ \text{m/s}$, occurring at $x = 0$

Key Theorem — Conservation of Energy in SHM. The total mechanical energy of a simple harmonic oscillator is constant:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 $$

This follows from substituting $x(t)$ and $v(t) = -A\omega_0\sin(\omega_0 t + \phi)$ and applying the Pythagorean identity $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$. Kinetic and potential energy oscillate $90°$ out of phase, while total energy depends only on amplitude — a consequence of the linearity of the restoring force.

Plot $T^2$ versus $m$ and determine $k$ from the slope using $T^2 = (4\pi^2/k)\,m$. Compare your experimental $k$ with the value obtained from a static force-extension measurement and compute the percent discrepancy.

🍃 Damped Harmonic Motion

🍃 Damped Harmonic Motion

Definition. Damped harmonic motion describes oscillatory motion in which energy is progressively dissipated by a resistive force, causing the amplitude to decrease over time. For a mass $m$ on a spring with spring constant $k$, subject to a velocity-dependent damping force $F_d = -bv$, the single new primitive is the damping coefficient $b > 0$ (SI unit: kg/s), which characterizes the strength of the resistive medium. Newton's second law then yields:

$$ m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = 0 $$

Defining $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ (the natural angular frequency, already established for simple harmonic motion) and $\gamma = b/(2m)$ (derived from $b$), this becomes:

$$ \ddot{x} + 2\gamma\dot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0 $$

When $\gamma < \omega_0$ (underdamping), the system oscillates at a modified frequency $\omega_d = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \gamma^2}$ and the general solution is:

$$ x(t) = A_0 e^{-\gamma t} \cos(\omega_d t + \phi) $$

where $A_0$ and $\phi$ are determined by initial conditions. The factor $e^{-\gamma t}$ constitutes an exponentially decaying envelope that bounds the oscillation amplitude.

Worked Example. A 0.50 kg mass is attached to a spring ($k = 20\ \text{N/m}$) with damping coefficient $b = 1.0\ \text{kg/s}$. Find $\omega_d$ and the time for the amplitude to decay to half its initial value.

First, $\omega_0 = \sqrt{20/0.50} = \sqrt{40} \approx 6.32\ \text{rad/s}$ and $\gamma = 1.0/(2 \times 0.50) = 1.0\ \text{s}^{-1}$. Since $\gamma \operatorname{ll} \omega_0$, the system is underdamped: $\omega_d = \sqrt{40 - 1} \approx 6.24\ \text{rad/s}$. The amplitude halves when $e^{-\gamma t_{1/2}} = 1/2$, giving $t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \gamma \approx 0.693\ \text{s}$.

Key Theorem. The mechanical energy of an underdamped oscillator decays as $E(t) = \frac{1}{2}kA_0^2\, e^{-2\gamma t}$, so energy decays at twice the rate of amplitude. Larger $b$ increases $\gamma$, accelerating both amplitude and energy loss. In the limit $b \to 0$, the solution reduces exactly to simple harmonic motion with constant amplitude, confirming that undamped SHM is a special case.

🍃 Critical Damping

🍃 Critical Damping

Definition. A damped harmonic oscillator obeys the equation of motion

$$ m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = 0, $$

where $m$ is the mass, $b$ is the damping coefficient, and $k$ is the spring constant. Recalling the natural angular frequency $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ and the damping ratio $\zeta = b/(2\sqrt{mk})$ introduced in the preceding section, the system is said to be critically damped when $\zeta = 1$. The single new quantity this condition defines is the critical damping coefficient

$$ b_c \equiv 2\sqrt{mk} = 2m\omega_0, $$

which sets the precise value of $b$ required to achieve critical damping. At this value the characteristic equation $m\lambda^2 + b\lambda + k = 0$ yields a repeated real root $\lambda = -\omega_0$, producing the general solution

$$ x(t) = (C_1 + C_2 t)\,e^{-\omega_0 t}. $$

This solution returns to equilibrium faster than any other damping regime without oscillating, making $b_c$ the boundary between underdamping ($\zeta < 1$, oscillatory) and overdamping ($\zeta > 1$, sluggish non-oscillatory).

Worked Example. A door closer has $m = 2\,\text{kg}$, $k = 8\,\text{N/m}$, and is released from rest at $x_0 = 0.5\,\text{m}$. Determine $b_c$ and the position as a function of time.

First, $\omega_0 = \sqrt{8/2} = 2\,\text{rad/s}$, so $b_c = 2(2)(2) = 8\,\text{N·s/m}$.

Applying initial conditions $x(0) = 0.5$ and $\dot{x}(0) = 0$ gives $C_1 = 0.5$ and $C_2 = \omega_0 C_1 = 1.0\,\text{m/s}$:

$$ x(t) = (0.5 + 1.0\,t)\,e^{-2t}\,\text{m}. $$

The door closes continuously, reaching $x = 0.01\,\text{m}$ at approximately $t \approx 2.7\,\text{s}$, without overshooting the frame.

Key Theorem. Among all non-oscillatory responses to an initial displacement, the critically damped solution minimizes the time required to return to within any prescribed neighborhood of equilibrium. Formally, for any $\epsilon > 0$ the critical solution achieves $|x(t)| < \epsilon$ at the smallest $t^*$ relative to overdamped solutions with the same initial data.

Plot $x(t)$ for each case and fit the critically damped data to $(C_1 + C_2 t)e^{-\omega_0 t}$ to extract $\omega_0$. Compare the extracted value with $\sqrt{k/m}$ measured independently.

🍃 Force Constant

🍃 Force Constant

Definition. The force constant (also called the spring constant), denoted $k$, characterizes the stiffness of a restoring force that obeys Hooke's Law. For a system displaced by $x$ from its equilibrium position, the restoring force satisfies

$$ F = -kx, $$

where the negative sign indicates that the force opposes the displacement. The SI unit of $k$ is newtons per meter ($\text{N/m}$). A large value of $k$ corresponds to a stiff spring requiring substantial force for a small displacement; a small $k$ describes a compliant spring that deflects readily under modest loading.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 0.500\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface and is attached to a spring. When a horizontal force of $F = 8.00\,\text{N}$ is applied, the spring stretches by $x = 0.0400\,\text{m}$. Find $k$.

From Hooke's Law (taking magnitudes, since the direction is established by the setup):

$$ k = \frac{F}{x} = \frac{8.00\,\text{N}}{0.0400\,\text{m}} = 200\,\text{N/m}. $$

The block is then released from rest at $x = 0.0400\,\text{m}$. The angular frequency $\omega$ of the resulting simple harmonic motion is

$$ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{200}{0.500}} = 20.0\,\text{rad/s}, $$

giving a period $T = 2\pi/\omega \approx 0.314\,\text{s}$.

Key Theorem. For a mass–spring system executing simple harmonic motion, the force constant determines the period through the relation

$$ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}. $$

This result follows directly from Newton's second law applied to $F = -kx$, yielding the differential equation $\ddot{x} + (k/m)x = 0$, whose general solution is $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$, with $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$ and $A$ the amplitude.

🌸 Car Suspension

🌸 Car Suspension

Definition. A car suspension system is a mechanical assembly designed to isolate the vehicle chassis from road-induced vibrations while maintaining tire contact with the road surface. It is modeled as a damped harmonic oscillator—a sprung mass $m$ coupled to a spring of stiffness $k$ and a dashpot with damping coefficient $b$—governed by

$$ m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = F_{\text{road}}(t), $$

where $x(t)$ is vertical displacement from equilibrium and $F_{\text{road}}(t)$ represents forcing from road irregularities. The natural frequency is $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$. The single new quantity characterizing how energy is dissipated is the damping ratio

$$ \zeta = \frac{b}{2\sqrt{km}}. $$

When $\zeta < 1$ the system is underdamped and oscillates with decaying amplitude; when $\zeta > 1$ it is overdamped and returns to equilibrium without oscillating. A well-designed suspension targets the underdamped regime, balancing ride comfort against excessive bounce.

Worked Example. A vehicle corner has sprung mass $m = 400\ \text{kg}$, spring constant $k = 16{,}000\ \text{N/m}$, and damping coefficient $b = 2{,}000\ \text{N·s/m}$.

Natural frequency:

$$ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{16{,}000}{400}} = \sqrt{40} \approx 6.32\ \text{rad/s}, \quad f_0 \approx 1.0\ \text{Hz}. $$

Damping ratio:

$$ \zeta = \frac{2{,}000}{2\sqrt{16{,}000 \times 400}} = \frac{2{,}000}{5{,}060} \approx 0.40. $$

Since $0 < \zeta < 1$, the suspension is underdamped. After striking a single bump, the chassis oscillates at frequency $\omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2} \approx 5.80\ \text{rad/s}$ ($\approx 0.92\ \text{Hz}$), with amplitude decaying as $e^{-\zeta\omega_0 t} = e^{-2.53t}$—settling to within 5% of equilibrium in roughly 1.2 s.

Key Theorem. For a driven damped oscillator, the steady-state amplitude peaks at resonance frequency $\omega_r = \omega_0\sqrt{1-2\zeta^2}$. Engineers choose $\zeta \approx 0.3$–$0.5$ so that $\omega_r$ lies well above typical road excitation frequencies (0.5–2 Hz), preventing resonance amplification that would destabilize the vehicle. Higher $\zeta$ suppresses the resonance peak but increases high-frequency harshness, making the optimal range a deliberate engineering trade-off.

Payoff 🎯

Car suspension systems represent the practical culmination of oscillatory mechanics, synthesizing every major concept developed throughout the study of waves and vibrations into a single, indispensable engineering application. At its core, a suspension system is a damped harmonic oscillator: the spring (coil, leaf, or torsion bar) stores elastic potential energy when displaced, while the shock absorber (dashpot) dissipates that energy through viscous damping. The interplay between these two elements determines whether a vehicle's response to road irregularities is overdamped, underdamped, or critically damped—the last being the engineering ideal that eliminates oscillation in minimum time.

Definition. A car suspension modeled as a mass–spring–damper system obeys

$$ m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = F(t), $$

where $m$ is the effective sprung mass, $b$ the damping coefficient, $k$ the spring constant, and $F(t)$ the periodic forcing from road surface undulations. The natural frequency of the unforced system is $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$, and the damping ratio $\zeta = b/(2\sqrt{km})$ governs the transient response character.

Worked Example. A vehicle has sprung mass $m = 400\,\text{kg}$, spring constant $k = 16{,}000\,\text{N/m}$, and damping coefficient $b = 3{,}200\,\text{N·s/m}$. The natural frequency is $\omega_0 = \sqrt{16000/400} = 6.32\,\text{rad/s}$, and the damping ratio is $\zeta = 3200/(2\sqrt{16000 \times 400}) = 0.80$. Since $\zeta < 1$, the system is underdamped but well-attenuated—reaching equilibrium after approximately two to three cycles.

Key Theorem. At resonance ($\omega_\text{drive} = \omega_0$), the steady-state amplitude is $A_\text{res} = F_0/(b\omega_0)$. Adequate damping ($\zeta \approx 0.7$) simultaneously minimizes resonance amplitude and preserves responsiveness to high-frequency inputs, explaining why automotive engineers target this specific damping ratio in passenger vehicles.
