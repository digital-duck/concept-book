🌱 Displacement

🌱 Displacement

Definition. In classical mechanics, displacement is the vector quantity that describes the change in position of an object. If a particle occupies position $\vec{r}_i$ at time $t_i$ and position $\vec{r}_f$ at time $t_f$, its displacement is defined as

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i. $$

In one dimension along the $x$-axis this reduces to the scalar $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$, where the algebraic sign encodes direction. Displacement is categorically distinct from distance traveled: distance is the total arc length traversed along the actual path and is always non-negative, whereas displacement depends only on the endpoints and can be zero even when substantial motion has occurred.

Worked Example. A student walks $8\,\text{m}$ east, then $6\,\text{m}$ north. Taking east as $+x$ and north as $+y$, the displacement vector is

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = 8\,\hat{x} + 6\,\hat{y}\;\text{m}. $$

The magnitude is

$$ |\Delta \vec{r}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{100} = 10\,\text{m}, $$

directed at $\theta = \arctan(6/8) \approx 36.9°$ north of east. The total distance walked is $8 + 6 = 14\,\text{m}$, illustrating that distance and displacement magnitude are generally unequal.

Key Theorem. For motion described by a position function $x(t)$ that is differentiable on $[t_i, t_f]$, the displacement equals the definite integral of velocity:

$$ \Delta x = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} v(t)\, dt. $$

This follows directly from the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, since $v(t) = dx/dt$. Importantly, this integral can be negative, reflecting net displacement in the $-x$ direction, whereas the total distance is $\int_{t_i}^{t_f} |v(t)|\, dt \geq |\Delta x|$.

🌱 Energy

🌱 Energy

Definition. In classical mechanics and thermodynamics, energy is a scalar quantity that characterizes the capacity of a physical system to do work. For a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$, the kinetic energy is

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

The potential energy $U$ depends on the configuration of the system relative to a reference state; for example, the gravitational potential energy near Earth's surface is $U = mgh$, where $g \approx 9.8\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ and $h$ is the height above the chosen reference level. The total mechanical energy is defined as

$$ E = K + U. $$

Energy is measured in joules ($1\ \mathrm{J} = 1\ \mathrm{kg \cdot m^2/s^2}$).

Worked Example. A 2.0 kg ball is released from rest at a height $h = 5.0\ \mathrm{m}$ above the ground. Determine its speed just before impact, assuming no air resistance.

At the initial instant, $K_i = 0$ and $U_i = mgh = (2.0)(9.8)(5.0) = 98\ \mathrm{J}$, so $E = 98\ \mathrm{J}$. At impact, $h = 0$ and $U_f = 0$, so all energy is kinetic:

$$ K_f = 98\ \mathrm{J} \implies \frac{1}{2}(2.0)v^2 = 98 \implies v = \sqrt{98} \approx 9.9\ \mathrm{m/s}. $$

Key Theorem — Conservation of Mechanical Energy. For a system subject only to conservative forces (forces derivable from a potential energy function), the total mechanical energy is constant throughout the motion:

$$ E_i = E_f \implies K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f. $$

Proof sketch. The work–energy theorem states $W_\mathrm{net} = \Delta K$. For a conservative force, $W = -\Delta U$. Substituting gives $-\Delta U = \Delta K$, hence $\Delta(K + U) = 0$, confirming $E$ is constant. When non-conservative forces such as friction act, mechanical energy is not conserved; instead, the lost mechanical energy converts to thermal or internal energy, preserving the broader principle of total energy conservation.

🍃 Wave

🍃 Wave

Definition. A wave is a disturbance that propagates through a medium (or through free space, in the case of electromagnetic waves), transferring energy and momentum without net transport of matter. Mathematically, any function of the form

$$ y(x, t) = A\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

describes a transverse sinusoidal wave traveling in the positive $x$-direction, where $A$ is the amplitude, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, $\lambda$ is the wavelength, $f$ is the frequency, and $\phi$ is the phase constant. The wave speed is given by

$$ v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \lambda f. $$

Waves are broadly classified as transverse (displacement perpendicular to propagation, e.g., light, waves on a string) or longitudinal (displacement parallel to propagation, e.g., sound in air).

Worked Example. A transverse wave on a string is described by $y(x,t) = 0.030\sin(2.5x - 400t)$ meters, where $x$ is in meters and $t$ in seconds. Identify the amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave speed.

Comparing with the standard form: $A = 0.030\ \text{m}$, $k = 2.5\ \text{rad/m}$, $\omega = 400\ \text{rad/s}$.

$$ \lambda = \frac{2\pi}{k} = \frac{2\pi}{2.5} \approx 2.51\ \text{m} $$ $$ f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{400}{2\pi} \approx 63.7\ \text{Hz} $$ $$ v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \frac{400}{2.5} = 160\ \text{m/s} $$

Key Theorem — Wave Equation. Any wave satisfying the superposition principle obeys the linear wave equation:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2}. $$

One can verify that $y = A\sin(kx - \omega t)$ satisfies this equation exactly when $v = \omega/k$, confirming the self-consistency of the sinusoidal solution. This second-order PDE is fundamental: it governs mechanical waves on strings, pressure waves in fluids, and (via Maxwell's equations) electromagnetic radiation.

🍃 Periodic Motion

🍃 Periodic Motion

Definition. A system undergoes periodic motion when its state repeats itself at regular time intervals. Formally, if $x(t)$ describes the position of a particle, then the motion is periodic with period $T$ if

$$ x(t + T) = x(t) \quad \text{for all } t. $$

The smallest positive value of $T$ satisfying this relation is called the fundamental period. The frequency $f$ measures how many complete cycles occur per unit time:

$$ f = \frac{1}{T}, $$

with SI unit hertz ($1\,\text{Hz} = 1\,\text{s}^{-1}$). The angular frequency $\omega$ relates to frequency by

$$ \omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T}. $$

These three quantities — $T$, $f$, and $\omega$ — are interchangeable descriptors of the same periodic behavior.

Worked Example. A mass on a horizontal frictionless surface is attached to a spring of stiffness $k = 200\,\text{N/m}$. The mass is $m = 0.50\,\text{kg}$. Determine the period and frequency of oscillation.

For a mass–spring system, Newton's second law yields the equation of motion $m\ddot{x} = -kx$, whose solution is simple harmonic: $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$. The angular frequency is

$$ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{200}{0.50}} = \sqrt{400} = 20\,\text{rad/s}. $$

Therefore,

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi}{20} \approx 0.314\,\text{s}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T} \approx 3.18\,\text{Hz}. $$

Note that neither $T$ nor $f$ depends on the amplitude $A$; this isochronism is a defining feature of simple harmonic motion.

Key Theorem (Isochronism of SHM). For any linear restoring force $F = -kx$, the period $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$ is independent of amplitude. This follows directly from the linearity of the differential equation: scaling the initial conditions scales $x(t)$ uniformly without altering $\omega$.

🍃 Frequency

🍃 Frequency

Definition. The frequency of a periodic wave or oscillation is the number of complete cycles that occur per unit time. Formally, if $T$ denotes the period—the time required to complete one full cycle—then the frequency $f$ is defined by

$$ f = \frac{1}{T}. $$

The SI unit of frequency is the hertz (Hz), where $1\,\text{Hz} = 1\,\text{cycle}\,\text{s}^{-1} = 1\,\text{s}^{-1}$. For rotational and wave phenomena, the angular frequency $\omega$ (rad/s) is related to $f$ by

$$ \omega = 2\pi f. $$

Worked Example. A tuning fork produces a pure tone with a period of $T = 2.27 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{s}$. Determine its frequency and angular frequency.

Solution.

$$ f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{2.27 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{s}} \approx 440\,\text{Hz}. $$ $$ \omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi (440\,\text{Hz}) \approx 2.76 \times 10^{3}\,\text{rad\,s}^{-1}. $$

This corresponds to the musical note A4, the standard concert-pitch reference. The displacement of the air parcel at position $x = 0$ varies as $y(t) = A\sin(2\pi f t)$, completing 440 full oscillations every second.

Key Theorem — Wave–Speed Relation. For a traveling wave propagating through a medium, frequency $f$, wavelength $\lambda$, and wave speed $v$ satisfy

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Proof sketch. In one period $T$, the wavefront advances one wavelength $\lambda$. Since $v = \lambda/T$ and $f = 1/T$, substitution immediately yields $v = f\lambda$. This relation holds for mechanical waves (sound, water waves) and electromagnetic waves alike; for light in vacuum, $v = c$ and $\lambda = c/f$.

🍃 Wavelength

🍃 Wavelength

Definition. The wavelength of a periodic wave is the spatial period of the wave pattern — the minimum distance over which the wave repeats itself. For a sinusoidal wave propagating in the positive $x$-direction, the displacement $y$ of the medium is described by

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\,x - \frac{2\pi}{T}\,t + \phi_0\right), $$

where $A$ is the amplitude, $T$ is the period, $\phi_0$ is the initial phase, and $\lambda$ is the wavelength. Equivalently, $\lambda$ is the distance between any two successive points that are in phase — for instance, crest to crest or trough to trough. The wavelength is related to the wave speed $v$ and frequency $f$ by the fundamental relation

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

This expression follows directly from the definitions of $f$ and $\lambda$: in one period $T = 1/f$, the wave advances exactly one wavelength, so $v = \lambda/T = f\lambda$.

Worked Example. A transverse wave on a string has a frequency of $f = 440\ \text{Hz}$ and travels at $v = 176\ \text{m/s}$. Find the wavelength.

Applying the relation above:

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{176\ \text{m/s}}{440\ \text{Hz}} = 0.400\ \text{m}. $$

Thus successive crests are separated by $40.0\ \text{cm}$. If the medium were changed so that the wave speed doubled to $352\ \text{m/s}$ while the source frequency remained fixed, the wavelength would also double to $0.800\ \text{m}$, illustrating that wavelength depends on both the source and the medium, whereas frequency is set solely by the source.

Key Theorem. For a mechanical wave on a uniform string of linear mass density $\mu$ under tension $F_T$, the wave speed is

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{F_T}{\mu}}. $$

Because $v$ is independent of wavelength, all wavelengths travel at the same speed and $v = f\lambda$ holds without modification. This makes a uniform string a non-dispersive medium — a pulse launched on it retains its shape as it travels, rather than broadening over distance.

🍃 Longitudinal Wave

🍃 Longitudinal Wave

Definition. A longitudinal wave is a mechanical wave in which the displacement of the medium's particles occurs parallel to the direction of wave propagation. As the wave travels, regions of compression (increased particle density and pressure) and rarefaction (decreased particle density and pressure) alternate along the propagation axis. The general wave relations $v = f\lambda$, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$, and $\omega = 2\pi f$ carry over from the transverse-wave framework. The single new primitive introduced here is the wave speed in a fluid medium, determined by the bulk modulus $B$ and equilibrium density $\rho_0$:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{B}{\rho_0}} $$

Physically, a stiffer medium (larger $B$) transmits a disturbance faster, while a denser medium (larger $\rho_0$) resists acceleration and slows propagation. In an ideal gas the adiabatic bulk modulus equals $\gamma P_0$, so $v = \sqrt{\gamma P_0/\rho_0}$, where $\gamma$ is the adiabatic index and $P_0$ is the equilibrium pressure.

Worked Example. Dry air at $20\,°C$ has $\gamma = 1.40$, $P_0 = 1.013\times10^{5}\,\text{Pa}$, and $\rho_0 = 1.20\,\text{kg/m}^3$. Find the wave speed and the wavelength for a $440\,\text{Hz}$ tone.

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{1.40\times1.013\times10^{5}}{1.20}} = \sqrt{1.182\times10^{5}} \approx 343\,\text{m/s} $$ $$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343}{440} \approx 0.780\,\text{m} $$

The computed speed matches the standard tabulated value, confirming the adiabatic (not isothermal) model for sound in air.

Key Theorem. The time-averaged intensity of a sinusoidal longitudinal plane wave is:

$$ I = \frac{1}{2}\rho_0 v \omega^2 s_{\max}^2 $$

Once $v = \sqrt{B/\rho_0}$ is established, this result follows from integrating the kinetic and potential energy densities over one cycle. Intensity scales as the square of both amplitude and frequency, a relationship central to acoustical engineering and audiology.

Lab Exercise. Using a signal generator and two microphones placed at a known separation in a tube filled with air, measure the time delay between received signals at $500\,\text{Hz}$. From the delay and the separation distance, calculate the experimental wave speed and compare it to $v = \sqrt{\gamma P_{\text{atm}}/\rho_{\text{air}}}$, accounting for ambient temperature.

🍃 Superposition

🍃 Superposition

Definition. The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves occupy the same region of space simultaneously, the resultant displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the individual displacements produced by each wave acting alone. For two waves with displacements $y_1(x,t)$ and $y_2(x,t)$, the net displacement is

$$ y(x,t) = y_1(x,t) + y_2(x,t). $$

This principle holds exactly for linear media — those in which the restoring force is proportional to displacement — and applies to mechanical waves, sound, and electromagnetic radiation. Nonlinear media (e.g., large-amplitude waves in a strained material) violate superposition, but for the vast majority of introductory wave phenomena the assumption is valid.

Worked Example. Two sinusoidal waves travel in the same direction along a string:

$$ y_1 = A\sin(kx - \omega t), \qquad y_2 = A\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi). $$

Applying superposition and using the sum-to-product identity,

$$ y = y_1 + y_2 = 2A\cos\!\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right)\sin\!\left(kx - \omega t + \frac{\phi}{2}\right). $$

When $\phi = 0$ (waves perfectly in phase), the amplitude doubles to $2A$ — constructive interference. When $\phi = \pi$ (waves exactly out of phase), $\cos(\pi/2) = 0$ and the string remains undisturbed — destructive interference. For intermediate phase differences the amplitude takes intermediate values between $0$ and $2A$.

Key Theorem. Standing-wave formation. When two otherwise identical waves travel in opposite directions, superposition yields a standing wave:

$$ y = 2A\cos(\omega t)\sin(kx). $$

The amplitude factor $2A\cos(\omega t)$ oscillates uniformly in time while $\sin(kx)$ fixes the spatial envelope, producing nodes (permanent zeros) at $kx = n\pi$ and antinodes (maximum displacement) at $kx = (n+\tfrac{1}{2})\pi$, for integer $n$. Standing waves underlie the resonant modes of strings, organ pipes, and laser cavities.

🍃 Transverse Wave

🍃 Transverse Wave

A transverse wave is a mechanical or electromagnetic disturbance in which the displacement of the medium's particles (or the oscillation of the field vectors) occurs perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. Formally, if the wave travels along the $+x$-axis, each particle of the medium oscillates along the $y$- or $z$-axis. This distinguishes transverse waves from longitudinal waves, in which particle displacement is parallel to propagation. The mathematical description of a harmonic transverse wave is given by:

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

where $A$ is the amplitude, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, $\lambda$ is the wavelength, $f$ is the frequency, and $\phi$ is the initial phase. The wave speed relates these parameters via:

$$ v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \lambda f $$

Worked Example. A transverse wave on a string is described by $y(x,t) = 0.03\sin(4\pi x - 20\pi t)$ m. Identify the amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave speed.

Comparing with the standard form: $A = 0.03$ m, $k = 4\pi$ rad/m, $\omega = 20\pi$ rad/s. Then:

$$ \lambda = \frac{2\pi}{k} = \frac{2\pi}{4\pi} = 0.50 \text{ m} $$ $$ f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{20\pi}{2\pi} = 10 \text{ Hz} $$ $$ v = \lambda f = (0.50)(10) = 5.0 \text{ m/s} $$

Key Theorem — Wave Speed on a String. For a transverse wave traveling along a stretched string under tension $F_T$ with linear mass density $\mu$ (kg/m), the wave speed is:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{F_T}{\mu}} $$

This result follows from applying Newton's second law to a small string element undergoing transverse acceleration, then taking the limit as the element length approaches zero. Increasing tension raises the restoring force and hence the propagation speed; increasing linear density raises inertia and lowers it.

🍃 Wave Velocity

🍃 Wave Velocity

Definition. The wave velocity (or phase velocity) of a periodic wave is the speed at which a point of constant phase propagates through the medium. For a transverse or longitudinal wave characterized by frequency $f$ and wavelength $\lambda$, the wave velocity is

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Equivalently, since $f = 1/T$ where $T$ is the period, one may write $v = \lambda/T$. The wave velocity is a property of the medium and the wave type, not of the source motion; it remains constant in a uniform, non-dispersive medium regardless of amplitude or frequency.

Worked Example. A student generates water waves on the surface of a tank. They observe that crests are separated by $\lambda = 0.12\ \text{m}$ and that 5 complete crests pass a fixed point every 2.0 seconds. Determine the wave velocity.

First, extract the frequency:

$$ f = \frac{5\ \text{cycles}}{2.0\ \text{s}} = 2.5\ \text{Hz}. $$

Then apply the fundamental relation:

$$ v = f\lambda = (2.5\ \text{Hz})(0.12\ \text{m}) = 0.30\ \text{m/s}. $$

The wave travels at $0.30\ \text{m/s}$ across the tank surface, independent of wave height.

Key Theorem. For a transverse wave on a stretched string under tension $F_T$, the wave velocity is given by

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{F_T}{\mu}}, $$

where $\mu$ (kg/m) is the linear mass density of the string. This result follows from applying Newton's second law to an infinitesimal string element and demonstrates that wave speed depends solely on the mechanical properties of the medium. Increasing tension raises the restoring force and thus increases $v$; increasing linear mass density raises inertia and decreases $v$. This theorem generalizes: in all classical wave media, $v = \sqrt{\text{(elastic property)}/\text{(inertial property)}}$.

Lab Exercise. Clamp a string of measured length $L$ between two fixed supports. 🌸 Earthquake Wave Detection

🌸 Earthquake Wave Detection

Definition. Earthquake wave detection is the systematic measurement and analysis of seismic waves—mechanical disturbances propagating through Earth's interior and along its surface following a seismic event. Seismic waves are classified into two primary bulk types: P-waves (primary or compressional waves), in which particle displacement is parallel to the propagation direction, and S-waves (secondary or shear waves), in which displacement is perpendicular to propagation. A seismometer detects ground motion by exploiting the inertia of a suspended mass: as the instrument housing moves with the ground, the mass tends to remain stationary, and the relative displacement is recorded as a function of time, producing a seismogram.

Worked Example. A seismic station records the arrival of a P-wave at $t_P = 0\,\text{s}$ and an S-wave at $t_S = 48\,\text{s}$. Given that P-wave speed in continental crust is $v_P = 6.0\,\text{km/s}$ and S-wave speed is $v_S = 3.5\,\text{km/s}$, estimate the epicentral distance $d$.

Because both waves originate at the same hypocenter simultaneously,

$$ d = v_P\, t_P = v_S\, t_S \implies t_S - t_P = d\!\left(\frac{1}{v_S} - \frac{1}{v_P}\right). $$

Solving for $d$:

$$ d = \frac{t_S - t_P}{\dfrac{1}{v_S} - \dfrac{1}{v_P}} = \frac{48}{\dfrac{1}{3.5} - \dfrac{1}{6.0}} = \frac{48}{0.2857 - 0.1667} = \frac{48}{0.1190} \approx 403\,\text{km}. $$

Key Theorem (S–P Interval Method). The epicentral distance $d$ is uniquely determined by the S–P arrival time difference and the known wave speeds:

$$ d = \frac{\Delta t_{SP}}{\dfrac{1}{v_S} - \dfrac{1}{v_P}}, \quad \Delta t_{SP} = t_S - t_P. $$

Triangulation using readings from at least three stations yields a unique epicenter location, since each station constrains $d$ to a circle of radius $d$ centered at that station; the circles intersect at the epicenter.

Payoff 🎯

Earthquake Wave Detection represents the culmination of wave mechanics as developed throughout this chapter: it is the discipline in which abstract principles of oscillation, superposition, wave speed, and medium-dependent propagation are unified into a coherent physical instrument for probing the interior of the Earth. By the time a student fully grasps how seismic data are recorded and interpreted, they have operationalized every major concept from periodic motion through standing waves and energy transport. The detection problem is not merely an application—it is the proof that the theoretical framework is complete enough to decode signals that have traveled thousands of kilometers through materials no human has ever directly sampled.

The essential measurement begins at the seismograph. A suspended inertial mass remains stationary while the ground beneath it oscillates; the relative displacement is recorded as a function of time. For a horizontal-component instrument, the equation of motion of the mass-spring system under ground acceleration $\ddot{x}_g(t)$ is

$$ m\ddot{u} + c\dot{u} + ku = -m\ddot{x}_g(t), $$

where $u$ is the displacement of the mass relative to the frame, $c$ is the damping coefficient, and $k$ is the spring constant. The natural frequency $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ is designed to lie well below the frequencies of interest so that $u \approx -\ddot{x}_g / \omega_0^2$, making the instrument respond proportionally to ground acceleration.

A central theorem of seismology is the P–S arrival-time theorem: the difference in arrival times of the compressional ($P$) and shear ($S$) waves at a single station satisfies

$$ \Delta t = d\!\left(\frac{1}{v_S} - \frac{1}{v_P}\right), $$

where $d$ is the epicentral distance and $v_P$, $v_S$ are the respective wave speeds in the crust. Because $v_P \approx 1.7\, v_S$ in typical rock, a measurement of $\Delta t$ immediately yields $d$, enabling triangulation of the earthquake's epicenter from three or more stations.

As a laboratory exercise, examine a publicly available seismogram from the IRIS Earthquake Browser. Identify the $P$- and $S$-wave onsets by locating the two distinct amplitude bursts; measure $\Delta t$ in seconds; apply the formula above using $v_P = 6.0\ \text{km/s}$ and $v_S = 3.5\ \text{km/s}$; and compare your computed epicentral distance to the catalogued value. Discrepancies arise from lateral velocity heterogeneity—an invitation to explore seismic tomography, where the very deviations from this simple model are inverted to produce three-dimensional images of Earth's mantle. If that prospect intrigues you, the next natural step is to study how travel-time residuals across a global network are used to reconstruct subsurface structure—a problem that connects wave physics directly to geodynamics, mineral physics, and the long-term evolution of our planet.
