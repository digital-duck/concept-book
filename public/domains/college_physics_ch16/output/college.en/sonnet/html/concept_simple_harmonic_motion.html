🍃 Simple Harmonic Motion

🍃 Simple Harmonic Motion

Definition. Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a type of periodic oscillation in which the restoring force on an object is directly proportional to its displacement from equilibrium and acts in the opposite direction. Mathematically, this condition is expressed by Hooke's Law:

$$ F = -kx $$

where $k > 0$ is the spring constant (in N/m) and $x$ is the displacement from equilibrium. Applying Newton's second law yields the equation of motion:

$$ m\ddot{x} = -kx \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \ddot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0 $$

where $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ is the angular frequency (rad/s). The general solution is:

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \phi) $$

with amplitude $A$ (meters) and phase constant $\phi$ (radians) determined by initial conditions. The period and frequency follow directly from $\omega_0$:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T} $$

Worked Example. A 0.500 kg block is attached to a spring with $k = 200\ \text{N/m}$. At $t = 0$, the block is displaced $x_0 = 0.100\ \text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest.

  • Angular frequency: $\omega_0 = \sqrt{200/0.500} = 20.0\ \text{rad/s}$
  • Period: $T = 2\pi/20.0 \approx 0.314\ \text{s}$
  • Since $v(0) = 0$, the phase constant $\phi = 0$, giving $x(t) = 0.100\cos(20.0\,t)\ \text{m}$
  • Maximum speed: $v_{\max} = A\omega_0 = (0.100)(20.0) = 2.00\ \text{m/s}$, occurring at $x = 0$

Key Theorem — Conservation of Energy in SHM. The total mechanical energy of a simple harmonic oscillator is constant:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 $$

This follows from substituting $x(t)$ and $v(t) = -A\omega_0\sin(\omega_0 t + \phi)$ and applying the Pythagorean identity $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$. Kinetic and potential energy oscillate $90°$ out of phase, while total energy depends only on amplitude — a consequence of the linearity of the restoring force.

Lab Exercise. Suspend a known mass $m$ from a spring and measure the period $T$ of oscillation for five different masses. Plot $T^2$ versus $m$ and determine $k$ from the slope using $T^2 = (4\pi^2/k)\,m$. Compare your experimental $k$ with the value obtained from a static force-extension measurement and compute the percent discrepancy.
