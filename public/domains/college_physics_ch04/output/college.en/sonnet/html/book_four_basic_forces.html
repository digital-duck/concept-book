­¤ī▒ Force

­¤ī▒ Force

Definition. A force is a vector quantity representing an interaction between two objects that causes, or tends to cause, a change in the motion of either object. Formally, force $\vec{F}$ is defined through Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass of the object (in kilograms) and $\vec{a}$ is its acceleration (in $\text{m/s}^2$). The SI unit of force is the newton: $1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Because force is a vector, the net force is the vector sum of all individual forces acting on the object:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i $$

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 5.0\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\,\hat{x}\,\text{N} + 3\,\hat{y}\,\text{N}$. Find the net force and the resulting acceleration.

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (12 - 4)\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y} = 8\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y}\;\text{N} $$

The magnitude is $|\vec{F}_{\text{net}}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{73} \approx 8.54\,\text{N}$, directed at angle $\theta = \arctan(3/8) \approx 20.6┬░$ above the $x$-axis. The acceleration follows directly:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y}}{5.0} = 1.6\,\hat{x} + 0.6\,\hat{y}\;\text{m/s}^2 $$

Key Theorem (Newton's Third Law). For every force exerted by object A on object B, there exists an equal and opposite force exerted by B on A:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A} $$

These action-reaction pairs always act on different objects and therefore do not cancel in any single free-body diagram. This principle is essential for analyzing systems of interacting bodies.

Lab Exercise. Using a force sensor and a dynamics cart on a low-friction track, apply a series of measured horizontal forces and record the resulting accelerations. Plot $a$ versus $F_{\text{net}}$ and determine the slope. Verify that the slope equals $1/m$ and confirm Newton's second law within experimental uncertainty. Repeat with additional mass added to the cart and comment on how the slope changes.

­¤īĖ Four Basic Forces

Definition

Modern physics recognizes four fundamental interactions that govern all physical phenomena in the universe. These are the gravitational force, the electromagnetic force, the strong nuclear force, and the weak nuclear force. Each force is characterized by its relative strength, range, and the particles (mediators) through which it acts.

Force Relative Strength Range Mediator
Strong nuclear $1$ $\sim 10^{-15}$ m Gluons
Electromagnetic $10^{-2}$ $\infty$ Photons
Weak nuclear $10^{-13}$ $\sim 10^{-18}$ m $W^\pm$, $Z^0$ bosons
Gravitational $10^{-38}$ $\infty$ Gravitons (theoretical)

The gravitational force between two masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ separated by distance $r$ is given by Newton's law:

$$ F_g = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} $$

where $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{N┬Ęm}^2/\text{kg}^2$. The electromagnetic force between two charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ follows Coulomb's law:

$$ F_e = k_e\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} $$

where $k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9\ \text{N┬Ęm}^2/\text{C}^2$.

Worked Example

Compare the gravitational and electromagnetic forces between a proton and an electron separated by $r = 5.29 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{m}$ (the Bohr radius).

Using $m_p = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$, $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, and $|q| = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$:

$$ F_g = \frac{(6.674\times10^{-11})(1.673\times10^{-27})(9.109\times10^{-31})}{(5.29\times10^{-11})^2} \approx 3.63 \times 10^{-47}\ \text{N} $$ $$ F_e = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(1.602\times10^{-19})^2}{(5.29\times10^{-11})^2} \approx 8.23 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{N} $$

The ratio $F_e/F_g \approx 2.27 \times 10^{39}$ confirms that electromagnetism dominates gravity at the atomic scale by roughly 39 orders of magnitude.

Key Theorem

The unification principle holds that at sufficiently high energies, the electromagnetic and weak forces merge into a single electroweak force ŌĆö a result confirmed experimentally at CERN through detection of the $W^\pm$ and $Z^0$ bosons. Theorists seek a Grand Unified Theory (GUT) encompassing the strong force and, ultimately, a Theory of Everything incorporating gravity.

The study of College Physics 2E, Chapter 4 builds systematically from Newton's laws of motion toward an understanding of the forces that govern all physical interactions. That arc finds its natural endpoint in the Four Basic Forces: gravitation, electromagnetism, the strong nuclear force, and the weak nuclear force. These four interactions are not merely a catalog of phenomena ŌĆö they represent the complete ontology of physical causation. Every push, pull, chemical bond, radioactive decay, and gravitational orbit reduces, at the fundamental level, to one of these four interactions acting through an exchange of force-carrying particles or fields. Recognizing this unifying structure transforms physics from a collection of disconnected rules into a coherent framework.

Formal Statement. The four fundamental interactions are distinguished by their mediating bosons, their coupling strengths (relative to the strong force, normalized to 1), their range, and the classes of matter they affect:

Force Mediator Relative Strength Range
Strong Gluon ($g$) $1$ $\sim 10^{-15}\ \text{m}$
Electromagnetic Photon ($\gamma$) $10^{-2}$ $\infty$
Weak $W^{\pm},\ Z^{0}$ $10^{-6}$ $\sim 10^{-18}\ \text{m}$
Gravitational Graviton (theoretical) $10^{-38}$ $\infty$

The electromagnetic force obeys Coulomb's inverse-square law, $\mathbf{F} = k_e \dfrac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r}$, while gravitation follows the structurally identical $\mathbf{F} = -G\dfrac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\hat{r}$. This formal parallel is not coincidental ŌĆö it motivated Einstein's geometric unification of gravity and points toward ongoing efforts to unify all four forces.

Worked Example. Consider a proton confined in a nucleus of radius $r \approx 1.0 \times 10^{-15}\ \text{m}$. The electrostatic repulsion between two protons separated by $r$ is:

$$ F_E = k_e \frac{e^2}{r^2} = (8.99 \times 10^9)\ \frac{(1.6\times10^{-19})^2}{(10^{-15})^2} \approx 230\ \text{N} $$

This enormous repulsion is overcome by the strong force, which operates at the same scale but with coupling strength $\sim 100\times$ greater, binding nucleons into stable nuclei. The weak force, by contrast, mediates beta decay ŌĆö a neutron converting to a proton by emitting a $W^-$ boson ŌĆö operating at ranges far smaller than the nucleus itself.

Key Theorem. The Standard Model of particle physics encodes the electromagnetic, strong, and weak interactions under the gauge symmetry group $SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$. Each force arises from demanding that the Lagrangian remain invariant under local gauge transformations, producing the mediating bosons as a mathematical necessity rather than an empirical postulate.

Laboratory Exercise. Using a torsion balance (Cavendish apparatus), measure the gravitational constant $G$ by recording the equilibrium deflection $\theta$ of a dumbbell attracted to two large lead spheres. Apply the torque balance $\kappa\theta = G\dfrac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\ell$ to extract $G$, then compare its magnitude to $k_e$ to appreciate the $10^{36}$-fold hierarchy between gravitational and electromagnetic coupling strengths.

The Four Basic Forces show that nature, despite its apparent complexity, is governed by just four interactions ŌĆö each with a precise mathematical structure, a defined range, and a class of phenomena it controls. If this unification captivates you, pursue electromagnetism next: Maxwell's equations reveal how a single force field unites electricity, magnetism, and light itself into one elegant theory.
