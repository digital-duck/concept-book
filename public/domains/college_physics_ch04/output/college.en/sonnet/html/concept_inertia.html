🍃 Inertia

🍃 Inertia

Definition. Inertia is the intrinsic tendency of an object to resist any change in its state of motion. An object at rest remains at rest, and an object moving at constant velocity continues at that velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force. Inertia is not a force; it is a property of matter. Quantitatively, inertia is measured by an object's inertial mass $m$, defined operationally through Newton's Second Law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

A larger mass $m$ requires a greater net force to produce the same acceleration $\vec{a}$, directly reflecting greater inertia. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg).

Worked Example. Two boxes rest on a frictionless surface. Box A has mass $m_A = 2.0\ \text{kg}$ and Box B has mass $m_B = 8.0\ \text{kg}$. The same horizontal force $F = 12\ \text{N}$ is applied to each separately. Find the acceleration of each box and interpret the result in terms of inertia.

Applying Newton's Second Law to each:

$$ a_A = \frac{F}{m_A} = \frac{12\ \text{N}}{2.0\ \text{kg}} = 6.0\ \text{m/s}^2 $$ $$ a_B = \frac{F}{m_B} = \frac{12\ \text{N}}{8.0\ \text{kg}} = 1.5\ \text{m/s}^2 $$

Box B accelerates at only one-quarter the rate of Box A under the identical applied force. This factor-of-four reduction in acceleration directly reflects Box B's fourfold greater inertia: more mass means a stronger resistance to the imposed change in motion.

Key Theorem (Newton's First Law — Law of Inertia). If the net force on an object is zero, $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{0}$, then the object's acceleration is zero and its velocity remains constant:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{0} \implies \vec{a} = \vec{0} \implies \vec{v} = \text{constant} $$

This theorem defines the conditions under which inertia alone governs motion and underpins the entire Newtonian framework.

Lab Exercise. Place objects of different masses (e.g., 0.5 kg, 1.0 kg, 2.0 kg) on a low-friction track. Apply a constant force using a hanging mass connected by a string over a pulley. Measure the resulting acceleration with a motion sensor for each object. Verify that the product $ma$ remains constant across trials and construct a plot of $a$ versus $1/m$ to confirm the linear relationship predicted by Newton's Second Law.
