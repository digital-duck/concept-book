🍃 Newtons First Law

🍃 Newton's First Law

Definition. Newton's First Law of Motion, often called the law of inertia, states that an object remains in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by a net external force. Formally, if the vector sum of all forces acting on an object satisfies $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, then the object's velocity $\vec{v}$ remains constant — meaning both its magnitude (speed) and direction are unchanged. This includes the special case $\vec{v} = \vec{0}$, in which the object remains at rest. The law applies strictly in inertial reference frames, which are frames that are themselves not accelerating.

Worked Example. A hockey puck slides across a frictionless ice surface with velocity $\vec{v} = 3.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}$. The only forces acting on it are gravity $\vec{F}_g = -mg\,\hat{y}$ and the normal force $\vec{N} = +mg\,\hat{y}$ from the ice. Computing the net force:

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{N} + \vec{F}_g = mg\,\hat{y} - mg\,\hat{y} = \vec{0} $$

Since the net force is zero, Newton's First Law predicts that the puck continues moving at $3.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}$ indefinitely. In practice, real ice is not perfectly frictionless, but the example illustrates the idealized principle: absent a net force, no change in motion occurs.

Key Theorem — Equivalence of Inertial Frames. Any reference frame moving at constant velocity relative to a known inertial frame is itself inertial. If Newton's First Law holds in frame $S$, it holds equally in frame $S'$ moving with constant velocity $\vec{V}$ relative to $S$. This follows directly from the Galilean transformation: $\vec{v}' = \vec{v} - \vec{V}$, which preserves the constancy of velocity when $\vec{V}$ is constant. Consequently, there is no preferred inertial frame — a foundational insight that later motivated special relativity.

Lab Exercise. Place a coin on a sheet of paper resting on a smooth table. Rapidly pull the paper horizontally from beneath the coin. Observe that the coin remains approximately stationary while the paper slides away. The dominant contact interaction during the pull is friction between the coin and paper: estimate this frictional force by measuring the coin's small displacement $d$ over the pull duration $t$, then computing the average acceleration $a = 2d/t^2$ and applying $F = ma$. Identify at what pull speeds or surface conditions this friction becomes non-negligible, and explain why the approximation of zero net force on the coin breaks down in those cases.
