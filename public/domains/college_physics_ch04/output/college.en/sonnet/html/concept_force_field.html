­¤īĖ Force Field

Definition. A force field is a vector-valued function $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r})$ that assigns to every point $\mathbf{r}$ in space a force vector representing the force that would be exerted on a test particle placed at that location. Formally, for a gravitational field near a massive body $M$,

$$ \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{r}) = -\frac{GM}{r^2}\hat{r}, $$

where $G$ is the universal gravitational constant and $\hat{r}$ is the outward radial unit vector. The field exists independently of whether a test mass is present; it encodes the influence of the source throughout the surrounding region. Similarly, an electric field $\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r})$ due to a point charge $Q$ is

$$ \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2}\hat{r}. $$

The force on a test particle of mass $m$ or charge $q$ is then $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{g}$ or $\mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{E}$, respectively.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$) is placed $r = 0.50\ \text{m}$ from a fixed charge $Q = 2.0\ \mu\text{C} = 2.0 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{C}$. Find the magnitude of the electric field at the proton's location and the force on the proton.

$$ E = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(2.0\times10^{-6})}{(0.50)^2} = \frac{1.798\times10^4}{0.25} \approx 7.2\times10^4\ \text{N/C}. $$ $$ F = qE = (1.60\times10^{-19})(7.2\times10^4) \approx 1.15\times10^{-14}\ \text{N}. $$

The direction is radially outward from $Q$ (repulsive, like charges).

Key Theorem (Superposition Principle). For a system of $n$ source charges $Q_1, Q_2, \ldots, Q_n$, the total electric field at position $\mathbf{r}$ is the vector sum of the individual fields:

$$ \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{Q_i}{r_i^2}\hat{r}_i, $$

where $r_i = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_i|$. This linearity is a fundamental experimental fact, not derivable from more basic principles, and it underlies the entire framework of classical electromagnetism.

Sketch the resulting field-line pattern and identify any points where $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{0}$.
