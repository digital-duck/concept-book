🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. A force is a vector quantity representing an interaction between two objects that causes, or tends to cause, a change in the motion of either object. Formally, force $\vec{F}$ is defined through Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass of the object (in kilograms) and $\vec{a}$ is its acceleration (in $\text{m/s}^2$). The SI unit of force is the newton: $1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Because force is a vector, the net force is the vector sum of all individual forces acting on the object:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i $$

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 5.0\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\,\hat{x}\,\text{N} + 3\,\hat{y}\,\text{N}$. Find the net force and the resulting acceleration.

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (12 - 4)\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y} = 8\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y}\;\text{N} $$

The magnitude is $|\vec{F}_{\text{net}}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{73} \approx 8.54\,\text{N}$, directed at angle $\theta = \arctan(3/8) \approx 20.6°$ above the $x$-axis. The acceleration follows directly:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y}}{5.0} = 1.6\,\hat{x} + 0.6\,\hat{y}\;\text{m/s}^2 $$

Key Theorem (Newton's Third Law). For every force exerted by object A on object B, there exists an equal and opposite force exerted by B on A:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A} $$

These action-reaction pairs always act on different objects and therefore do not cancel in any single free-body diagram. This principle is essential for analyzing systems of interacting bodies.

Repeat with additional mass added to the cart and comment on how the slope changes.

🌱 Dynamics

🌱 Dynamics

Definition. Dynamics is the branch of classical mechanics concerned with the relationship between the forces acting on a body and the resulting motion of that body. It extends the purely descriptive scope of kinematics by introducing the concept of cause: forces produce changes in motion, and Newton's three laws of motion provide the quantitative framework governing these interactions. The central object of analysis is the equation of motion, derived by applying Newton's Second Law,

$$ \sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}, $$

where $\sum \vec{F}$ is the net external force on a particle of mass $m$ and $\vec{a}$ is its resulting acceleration. This vector equation encodes three scalar equations in Cartesian coordinates: $\sum F_x = ma_x$, $\sum F_y = ma_y$, and $\sum F_z = ma_z$.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 5.0\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A horizontal force $F_1 = 20\ \text{N}$ is applied to the right, and a second force $F_2 = 8\ \text{N}$ acts to the left. Determine the acceleration of the block.

Constructing a free-body diagram, the net horizontal force is

$$ \sum F_x = F_1 - F_2 = 20\ \text{N} - 8\ \text{N} = 12\ \text{N}. $$

Applying Newton's Second Law:

$$ a_x = \frac{\sum F_x}{m} = \frac{12\ \text{N}}{5.0\ \text{kg}} = 2.4\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

The block accelerates to the right at $2.4\ \text{m/s}^2$.

Key Theorem: Newton's Second Law (Impulse–Momentum Form). For a particle of constant mass subject to a net force $\vec{F}(t)$, the impulse $\vec{J}$ delivered over a time interval $[t_1, t_2]$ equals the change in linear momentum:

$$ \vec{J} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{F}\, dt = \Delta \vec{p} = m\vec{v}_f - m\vec{v}_i. $$

This formulation reveals that dynamics governs not merely instantaneous acceleration but the cumulative transfer of momentum. When the net force is constant, the integral reduces immediately to $\vec{F}\,\Delta t = m\Delta\vec{v}$, consistent with $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$.

🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. Mass is a scalar, intrinsic property of matter that quantifies its resistance to acceleration. In Newtonian mechanics, this inertial role of mass is expressed by Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

where $m$ is the mass of the object, $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$ is the net external force acting upon it, and $\vec{a}$ is the resulting acceleration. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg). Mass is an invariant under classical transformations: it does not depend on the object's velocity, position, or reference frame in the non-relativistic regime. A deeper empirical fact—confirmed to extraordinary precision by Eötvös-type experiments—is that an object's resistance to acceleration is identical to its response to gravitational fields. This equivalence of inertial and gravitational mass is the single new primitive introduced here, and its significance extends far beyond classical mechanics.

Worked Example. A hockey puck of mass $m = 0.170\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless ice surface. A player applies a constant horizontal force $F = 5.00\,\text{N}$ for $t = 3.00\,\text{s}$. Find the puck's final speed.

Solution. Applying Newton's second law along the direction of motion:

$$ a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{5.00\,\text{N}}{0.170\,\text{kg}} \approx 29.4\,\text{m/s}^2 $$

Starting from rest ($v_0 = 0$), the kinematic relation $v = v_0 + at$ gives:

$$ v = 0 + (29.4\,\text{m/s}^2)(3.00\,\text{s}) \approx 88.2\,\text{m/s} $$

The mass directly governs the magnitude of acceleration produced by a given force.

Key Theorem (Equivalence of Inertial and Gravitational Mass). For any object, $m_{\text{inertial}} = m_{\text{gravitational}}$. This equivalence implies that in a uniform gravitational field $\vec{g}$, every object undergoes identical free-fall acceleration regardless of mass:

$$ \vec{F}_g = m_{\text{gravitational}}\,\vec{g} = m_{\text{inertial}}\,\vec{a} \implies \vec{a} = \vec{g} $$

Einstein later elevated this result to the foundational postulate of general relativity, the Equivalence Principle.

🍃 System Of Interest

🍃 System Of Interest

Definition. In Newtonian mechanics, the system of interest is the precisely delimited object or collection of objects whose motion is to be analyzed. All other matter in the universe constitutes the environment. Forces exerted on the system by the environment are external forces; forces that system constituents exert on one another are internal forces. Newton's second law applies exclusively to external forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}} = m\vec{a} $$

where $m$ is the total mass of the system and $\vec{a}$ is the acceleration of its center of mass. Internal forces, by Newton's third law, cancel in pairs and produce zero net effect on the system's translational motion.

The choice of system is a modeling decision, not a physical fact. Redrawing the system boundary changes which forces are classified as internal versus external, and therefore changes the equation of motion written for that system.

Worked Example. A 60 kg person stands on a 20 kg skateboard. A horizontal rope exerts a 40 N force on the person.

  • System: person alone ($m = 60\ \text{kg}$). External forces on the person include the rope tension ($T = 40\ \text{N}$) and the horizontal contact force from the skateboard ($F_{\text{board}}$). Applying Newton's second law horizontally: $T + F_{\text{board}} = 60a$.

  • System: person + skateboard ($m = 80\ \text{kg}$). The contact force between person and board is now internal and vanishes from the equation. The only horizontal external force is the rope: $40 = 80a$, giving $a = 0.50\ \text{m/s}^2$.

The second choice eliminates the unknown internal contact force, illustrating how a judicious system boundary simplifies analysis.

Key Theorem. Superposition of external forces. For a system of $N$ particles, the net external force equals the sum of all forces exerted by agents outside the boundary:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net, ext}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec{F}_{i,\text{ext}} $$

Internal force pairs $\vec{f}_{ij} = -\vec{f}_{ji}$ sum to zero by Newton's third law, so they never appear in the system's equation of motion.

🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the vector quantity that describes the rate of change of an object's position with respect to time. For a particle whose position vector is $\vec{r}(t)$, the instantaneous velocity is defined as the time derivative

$$ \vec{v}(t) = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t}, $$

where $\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}(t + \Delta t) - \vec{r}(t)$ is the displacement over the interval $\Delta t$. Unlike speed, which is the scalar magnitude $|\vec{v}|$, velocity carries directional information and can be positive, negative, or zero depending on the chosen coordinate system. In one dimension along the $x$-axis, velocity reduces to $v_x = dx/dt$, with sign indicating direction.

Worked Example. A car travels along a straight highway. Its position (in meters) as a function of time (in seconds) is given by $x(t) = 4t^2 - 12t + 9$. Find the velocity at $t = 3\,\text{s}$ and determine when the car is momentarily at rest.

Differentiating: $v_x(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 8t - 12$. At $t = 3\,\text{s}$:

$$ v_x(3) = 8(3) - 12 = 12\,\text{m/s}. $$

Setting $v_x = 0$: $8t - 12 = 0 \Rightarrow t = 1.5\,\text{s}$. The car reverses direction at $t = 1.5\,\text{s}$, confirming that $x(t)$ has a minimum there.

Key Theorem (Mean Value Theorem applied to kinematics). If a particle moves with continuous velocity $v_x(t)$ over an interval $[t_1, t_2]$, then there exists at least one instant $t^* \in (t_1, t_2)$ such that

$$ v_x(t^*) = \frac{x(t_2) - x(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1} = \bar{v}_x, $$

where $\bar{v}_x$ is the average velocity over the interval. This guarantees that instantaneous velocity equals the average velocity at some point during the motion, bridging the two concepts rigorously.

🌱 Acceleration

🌱 Acceleration

Definition. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time. For a particle whose velocity $\vec{v}$ is a differentiable function of time $t$, the instantaneous acceleration is defined as

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = \frac{d^2\vec{r}}{dt^2}, $$

where $\vec{r}$ is the position vector. In one dimension, acceleration reduces to $a = dv/dt$. Because velocity is a vector, acceleration arises whenever the speed, the direction of motion, or both change. The SI unit of acceleration is metres per second squared ($\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$).

Worked Example. A car traveling along a straight highway increases its speed uniformly from $v_0 = 20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ to $v_f = 35\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ over an interval $\Delta t = 5.0\ \mathrm{s}$. The average acceleration is

$$ \bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_f - v_0}{\Delta t} = \frac{35 - 20}{5.0} = 3.0\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}. $$

Because the acceleration is constant, $\bar{a}$ equals the instantaneous acceleration throughout the interval. The distance covered is obtained from the kinematic relation $\Delta x = v_0 \Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}a(\Delta t)^2$:

$$ \Delta x = (20)(5.0) + \tfrac{1}{2}(3.0)(5.0)^2 = 100 + 37.5 = 137.5\ \mathrm{m}. $$

Key Theorem (Kinematic Equations for Constant Acceleration). When acceleration $a$ is constant, the equations of motion take the closed form

$$ v = v_0 + at, \qquad \Delta x = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2, \qquad v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x. $$

These three relations are not independent; any two can be derived from the definition $a = dv/dt$ together with $v = dx/dt$. They hold exactly under constant acceleration and serve as first-order approximations when acceleration varies slowly compared with the timescale of interest.

🍃 Inertia

🍃 Inertia

Definition. Inertia is the intrinsic tendency of an object to resist any change in its state of motion. An object at rest remains at rest, and an object moving at constant velocity continues at that velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force. Inertia is not a force; it is a property of matter. Quantitatively, inertia is measured by an object's inertial mass $m$, defined operationally through Newton's Second Law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

A larger mass $m$ requires a greater net force to produce the same acceleration $\vec{a}$, directly reflecting greater inertia. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg).

Worked Example. Two boxes rest on a frictionless surface. Box A has mass $m_A = 2.0\ \text{kg}$ and Box B has mass $m_B = 8.0\ \text{kg}$. The same horizontal force $F = 12\ \text{N}$ is applied to each separately. Find the acceleration of each box and interpret the result in terms of inertia.

Applying Newton's Second Law to each:

$$ a_A = \frac{F}{m_A} = \frac{12\ \text{N}}{2.0\ \text{kg}} = 6.0\ \text{m/s}^2 $$ $$ a_B = \frac{F}{m_B} = \frac{12\ \text{N}}{8.0\ \text{kg}} = 1.5\ \text{m/s}^2 $$

Box B accelerates at only one-quarter the rate of Box A under the identical applied force. This factor-of-four reduction in acceleration directly reflects Box B's fourfold greater inertia: more mass means a stronger resistance to the imposed change in motion.

Key Theorem (Newton's First Law — Law of Inertia). If the net force on an object is zero, $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{0}$, then the object's acceleration is zero and its velocity remains constant:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{0} \implies \vec{a} = \vec{0} \implies \vec{v} = \text{constant} $$

This theorem defines the conditions under which inertia alone governs motion and underpins the entire Newtonian framework.

🍃 Net External Force

🍃 Net External Force

Definition. The net external force acting on a system is the vector sum of all forces exerted on that system by agents outside its boundary. If a system experiences forces $\vec{F}_1, \vec{F}_2, \ldots, \vec{F}_n$ from external sources, the net external force is

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \vec{F}_i. $$

Forces exchanged between constituents within the system—internal forces—cancel in Newton's-third-law pairs and therefore contribute nothing to $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$. It is the net external force alone that governs translational acceleration through Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}. $$

Worked Example. A crate of mass $m = 20\text{ kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two workers push it simultaneously: Worker A applies $\vec{F}_A = 80\text{ N}$ due east, and Worker B applies $\vec{F}_B = 60\text{ N}$ due north. The normal force and weight are equal and opposite, canceling vertically and leaving no contribution to horizontal motion. Resolving the remaining forces into components,

$$ F_{\text{net},x} = 80\text{ N}, \qquad F_{\text{net},y} = 60\text{ N}. $$

The magnitude of the net external force is

$$ F_{\text{net}} = \sqrt{(80)^2 + (60)^2} = \sqrt{10{,}000} = 100\text{ N}, $$

directed at $\theta = \arctan(60/80) \approx 36.9°$ north of east. The resulting acceleration is

$$ a = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{100\text{ N}}{20\text{ kg}} = 5.0\text{ m/s}^2, $$

pointing in the same direction as $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$.

Key Theorem (Newton's Second Law for a System). For any collection of particles treated as a single system of total mass $M$, the net external force equals $M$ times the acceleration of the center of mass:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net,ext}} = M\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}. $$

This holds regardless of the complexity of internal interactions, since those forces cancel pairwise and drop out of the sum entirely.

🍃 Free Body Diagram

🍃 Free Body Diagram

Definition. A free body diagram (FBD) is a schematic representation of a single object — isolated from its surroundings — in which every external force acting on that object is drawn as a vector arrow originating at the object's center of mass (or point of application). The diagram excludes internal forces and forces exerted by the object on other bodies. Formally, if an object of mass $m$ experiences $n$ external forces $\vec{F}_1, \vec{F}_2, \ldots, \vec{F}_n$, the net force is

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \vec{F}_i, $$

and Newton's second law requires $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}$. The FBD makes this summation explicit and systematic.

Worked Example. A crate of mass $m = 20\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless inclined plane tilted at $\theta = 30°$ above the horizontal. Construct the FBD and find the acceleration.

Identifying forces: Two external forces act on the crate — its weight $\vec{W} = m\vec{g}$ directed vertically downward, and the normal force $\vec{N}$ perpendicular to the inclined surface.

Coordinate system: Choose axes parallel and perpendicular to the incline. Decomposing weight:

$$ W_\parallel = mg\sin\theta, \qquad W_\perp = mg\cos\theta. $$

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ \text{Along incline: } mg\sin\theta = ma \implies a = g\sin\theta $$ $$ \text{Perpendicular: } N - mg\cos\theta = 0 \implies N = mg\cos\theta. $$

Substituting $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$: $a = 9.8\sin 30° = 4.9\ \text{m/s}^2$ down the slope, and $N = 20(9.8)\cos 30° \approx 170\ \text{N}$.

Key Theorem (Newton's Second Law in Component Form). For any object in an inertial reference frame, resolving all forces into orthogonal components yields independent scalar equations:

$$ \sum F_x = ma_x, \qquad \sum F_y = ma_y. $$

The FBD guarantees that every external force appears exactly once in this summation, preventing double-counting or omission.

🍃 Newtons First Law

🍃 Newton's First Law

Definition. Newton's First Law of Motion, often called the law of inertia, states that an object remains in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by a net external force. Formally, if the vector sum of all forces acting on an object satisfies $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, then the object's velocity $\vec{v}$ remains constant — meaning both its magnitude (speed) and direction are unchanged. This includes the special case $\vec{v} = \vec{0}$, in which the object remains at rest. The law applies strictly in inertial reference frames, which are frames that are themselves not accelerating.

Worked Example. A hockey puck slides across a frictionless ice surface with velocity $\vec{v} = 3.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}$. The only forces acting on it are gravity $\vec{F}_g = -mg\,\hat{y}$ and the normal force $\vec{N} = +mg\,\hat{y}$ from the ice. Computing the net force:

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{N} + \vec{F}_g = mg\,\hat{y} - mg\,\hat{y} = \vec{0} $$

Since the net force is zero, Newton's First Law predicts that the puck continues moving at $3.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}$ indefinitely. In practice, real ice is not perfectly frictionless, but the example illustrates the idealized principle: absent a net force, no change in motion occurs.

Key Theorem — Equivalence of Inertial Frames. Any reference frame moving at constant velocity relative to a known inertial frame is itself inertial. If Newton's First Law holds in frame $S$, it holds equally in frame $S'$ moving with constant velocity $\vec{V}$ relative to $S$. This follows directly from the Galilean transformation: $\vec{v}' = \vec{v} - \vec{V}$, which preserves the constancy of velocity when $\vec{V}$ is constant. Consequently, there is no preferred inertial frame — a foundational insight that later motivated special relativity.

🍃 Newtons Second Law

🍃 Newton's Second Law

Definition. Newton's Second Law of Motion states that the net external force acting on an object is equal to the product of the object's mass and its acceleration:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

where $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$ is the vector sum of all external forces acting on the object (in newtons, N), $m$ is the inertial mass (in kilograms, kg), and $\vec{a}$ is the resulting acceleration (in m/s²). Because force and acceleration are both vectors, this single equation encodes three independent scalar equations — one for each spatial component:

$$ F_{\text{net},x} = ma_x, \quad F_{\text{net},y} = ma_y, \quad F_{\text{net},z} = ma_z $$

The law implies a strictly proportional and directional relationship: doubling the net force doubles the acceleration, and the acceleration is always directed along the net force, not the velocity. The new primitive introduced here is acceleration — the rate of change of velocity, defined as $\vec{a} = d\vec{v}/dt$.

Worked Example. A 5.0 kg block rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\,\hat{x}$ N and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\,\hat{x}$ N. Find the acceleration.

First, compute the net force:

$$ F_{\text{net},x} = 12\,\text{N} + (-4\,\text{N}) = 8\,\text{N} $$

Apply Newton's Second Law:

$$ a_x = \frac{F_{\text{net},x}}{m} = \frac{8\,\text{N}}{5.0\,\text{kg}} = 1.6\,\text{m/s}^2 $$

The block accelerates at $1.6\,\text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction.

Key Theorem (Superposition of Forces). Because Newton's Second Law is linear in force, forces superpose: if forces $\vec{F}_1, \vec{F}_2, \ldots, \vec{F}_n$ act simultaneously on a mass $m$, the resulting acceleration is

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\vec{F}_i $$

This linearity underpins the construction of free-body diagrams — each force is identified independently, then summed to find the net effect.

🍃 Newtons Third Law

🍃 Newton's Third Law

Definition. Newton's Third Law states that whenever one object exerts a force on a second object, the second object exerts a force on the first that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. Formally, if object A exerts force $\vec{F}_{AB}$ on object B, then object B simultaneously exerts force $\vec{F}_{BA}$ on object A such that:

$$ \vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA} $$

These paired forces—called an action-reaction pair—always act on different objects, never on the same object. This distinction is critical: action-reaction pairs cannot cancel each other because cancellation requires forces acting on the same body.

Worked Example. A person of mass $m = 70\,\text{kg}$ stands stationary on the floor. Identify the action-reaction pairs and verify consistency with equilibrium.

The person exerts a downward gravitational-contact force (their weight reaction) on the floor: $\vec{F}_{\text{person on floor}} = -(70)(9.8)\,\hat{j} = -686\,\hat{j}\,\text{N}$. By Newton's Third Law, the floor exerts an equal and opposite normal force on the person: $\vec{N} = +686\,\hat{j}\,\text{N}$.

Separately, Earth's gravity pulls the person downward: $\vec{W} = -686\,\hat{j}\,\text{N}$. The reaction to this is the person pulling Earth upward with $+686\,\hat{j}\,\text{N}$—an imperceptibly small acceleration on Earth given its enormous mass.

Applying Newton's Second Law to the person alone: $\vec{N} + \vec{W} = 686\,\hat{j} - 686\,\hat{j} = \vec{0}$, confirming static equilibrium. Note that $\vec{N}$ and $\vec{W}$ are not an action-reaction pair; they happen to be equal because the person is in equilibrium, not because of Newton's Third Law.

Key Theorem. For any system of $n$ interacting particles, the internal action-reaction forces sum to zero:

$$ \sum_{i \neq j} \vec{F}_{ij} = \vec{0} $$

This result follows directly from Newton's Third Law: each internal force $\vec{F}_{ij}$ is paired with $\vec{F}_{ji} = -\vec{F}_{ij}$, so every internal contribution cancels. Consequently, only external forces can alter the net force on the system as a whole.

🌸 Newton Problem Solving Strategy

🌸 Newton Problem Solving Strategy

Definition. Newton's problem-solving strategy is a systematic procedure for applying Newton's second law, $\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$, to mechanical systems. The strategy transforms a physical scenario into a solvable mathematical system by enforcing a disciplined sequence: identify the system, draw a free-body diagram, establish a coordinate system, decompose all forces into components, apply Newton's second law, and solve algebraically before substituting numerical values.

Formally, for a particle of mass $m$ subject to $n$ forces $\vec{F}_1, \vec{F}_2, \ldots, \vec{F}_n$, the governing equations in two dimensions are

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} F_{ix} = ma_x, \qquad \sum_{i=1}^{n} F_{iy} = ma_y. $$

The strategy treats these as a coupled linear system in the unknowns of interest—acceleration components, normal forces, tension, and similar quantities.

Worked Example. A 5.0 kg block is pulled across a frictionless horizontal surface by a rope inclined at $\theta = 30°$ above the horizontal with tension $T = 20\,\text{N}$. Find the acceleration and normal force.

Step 1 — System: The block is the system of interest.

Step 2 — Free-body diagram: Forces acting on the block are gravity $\vec{w} = -mg\hat{j}$, normal force $N\hat{j}$, and tension with components $T\cos\theta\,\hat{i} + T\sin\theta\,\hat{j}$.

Step 3 — Coordinate system: $+x$ along the surface, $+y$ upward.

Step 4 — Apply Newton's second law:

$$ x{:}\ T\cos\theta = ma_x \implies a_x = \frac{(20)\cos30°}{5.0} \approx 3.46\,\text{m/s}^2 $$ $$ y{:}\ N + T\sin\theta - mg = 0 \implies N = mg - T\sin\theta = (5.0)(9.8) - (20)(0.5) = 39\,\text{N} $$

Key Theorem. Newton's second law holds only in inertial reference frames. In any such frame, the decomposition into orthogonal components is valid because vector addition is commutative and associative, and the resulting component equations are independently solvable. For a system of $N$ particles, Newton's third law guarantees that all internal forces cancel pairwise when summed over the entire system, so only external forces determine the motion. This yields the center-of-mass equation $\sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}} = M\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}$, where $M$ is the total mass and $\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}$ is the acceleration of the center of mass—the single new principle introduced here.

Payoff 🎯

Newton's Problem Solving Strategy is the methodological capstone of classical mechanics because it transforms the three laws of motion from abstract principles into a disciplined, reproducible procedure for predicting the behavior of any mechanical system. The strategy distills centuries of physical intuition into four precise steps: identify the system and its boundary, construct a complete free-body diagram, resolve all forces into a consistent coordinate system, and apply Newton's second law $\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$ to each independent direction. Mastery of this procedure is not merely a computational skill — it is the acquisition of a systematic physical reasoning framework that extends far beyond introductory mechanics.

Formal Statement. For a particle of mass $m$ in mechanical equilibrium or uniform acceleration, the vector sum of all external forces satisfies

$$ \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i = m\vec{a}, $$

where $\vec{a}$ is the acceleration of the center of mass. In component form, this decouples into independent scalar equations: $\sum F_x = ma_x$ and $\sum F_y = ma_y$, each solved separately.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 5.0\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless incline of angle $\theta = 30°$. Choosing axes parallel and perpendicular to the surface, the free-body diagram yields two components of the normal force $N$ and gravity $mg$. Applying Newton's second law: along the incline, $mg\sin\theta = ma$, giving $a = g\sin 30° = 4.9\,\text{m/s}^2$; perpendicular to the incline, $N - mg\cos\theta = 0$, so $N = mg\cos 30° \approx 42.4\,\text{N}$. The coordinate choice made an otherwise entangled vector problem trivially separable.

Key Theorem. The superposition principle for forces — that net force is the vector sum of all individual forces — guarantees that the free-body diagram captures the complete mechanical state of the system. No additional forces arise from the act of drawing the diagram; the procedure is exhaustive by construction.
