🍃 Free Body Diagram

🍃 Free Body Diagram

Definition. A free body diagram (FBD) is a schematic representation of a single object — isolated from its surroundings — in which every external force acting on that object is drawn as a vector arrow originating at the object's center of mass (or point of application). The diagram excludes internal forces and forces exerted by the object on other bodies. Formally, if an object of mass $m$ experiences $n$ external forces $\vec{F}_1, \vec{F}_2, \ldots, \vec{F}_n$, the net force is

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \vec{F}_i, $$

and Newton's second law requires $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}$. The FBD makes this summation explicit and systematic.

Worked Example. A crate of mass $m = 20\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless inclined plane tilted at $\theta = 30°$ above the horizontal. Construct the FBD and find the acceleration.

Identifying forces: Two external forces act on the crate — its weight $\vec{W} = m\vec{g}$ directed vertically downward, and the normal force $\vec{N}$ perpendicular to the inclined surface.

Coordinate system: Choose axes parallel and perpendicular to the incline. Decomposing weight:

$$ W_\parallel = mg\sin\theta, \qquad W_\perp = mg\cos\theta. $$

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ \text{Along incline: } mg\sin\theta = ma \implies a = g\sin\theta $$ $$ \text{Perpendicular: } N - mg\cos\theta = 0 \implies N = mg\cos\theta. $$

Substituting $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$: $a = 9.8\sin 30° = 4.9\ \text{m/s}^2$ down the slope, and $N = 20(9.8)\cos 30° \approx 170\ \text{N}$.

Key Theorem (Newton's Second Law in Component Form). For any object in an inertial reference frame, resolving all forces into orthogonal components yields independent scalar equations:

$$ \sum F_x = ma_x, \qquad \sum F_y = ma_y. $$

The FBD guarantees that every external force appears exactly once in this summation, preventing double-counting or omission.

Lab Exercise. Hang a block of known mass from a single string, then displace it sideways with a second horizontal string so that the vertical string makes a known angle $\theta$ with the ceiling. Draw the FBD showing the two string tensions and the weight, write the equilibrium conditions $\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$, and solve for each tension algebraically. Verify your predictions by measuring both tensions with spring scales and computing the percent error.
