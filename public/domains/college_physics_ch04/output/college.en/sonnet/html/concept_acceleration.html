🌱 Acceleration

🌱 Acceleration

Definition. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time. For a particle whose velocity $\vec{v}$ is a differentiable function of time $t$, the instantaneous acceleration is defined as

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = \frac{d^2\vec{r}}{dt^2}, $$

where $\vec{r}$ is the position vector. In one dimension, acceleration reduces to $a = dv/dt$. Because velocity is a vector, acceleration arises whenever the speed, the direction of motion, or both change. The SI unit of acceleration is metres per second squared ($\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$).

Worked Example. A car traveling along a straight highway increases its speed uniformly from $v_0 = 20\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ to $v_f = 35\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ over an interval $\Delta t = 5.0\ \mathrm{s}$. The average acceleration is

$$ \bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_f - v_0}{\Delta t} = \frac{35 - 20}{5.0} = 3.0\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}. $$

Because the acceleration is constant, $\bar{a}$ equals the instantaneous acceleration throughout the interval. The distance covered is obtained from the kinematic relation $\Delta x = v_0 \Delta t + \tfrac{1}{2}a(\Delta t)^2$:

$$ \Delta x = (20)(5.0) + \tfrac{1}{2}(3.0)(5.0)^2 = 100 + 37.5 = 137.5\ \mathrm{m}. $$

Key Theorem (Kinematic Equations for Constant Acceleration). When acceleration $a$ is constant, the equations of motion take the closed form

$$ v = v_0 + at, \qquad \Delta x = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2, \qquad v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x. $$

These three relations are not independent; any two can be derived from the definition $a = dv/dt$ together with $v = dx/dt$. They hold exactly under constant acceleration and serve as first-order approximations when acceleration varies slowly compared with the timescale of interest.

Lab Exercise. Mount a motion sensor directed along a low-friction air track. Release a glider from rest and record position $x(t)$ at $10\ \mathrm{ms}$ intervals. Fit a quadratic $x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$ to the data using least-squares regression, and extract $a$. Compare your result with the value predicted from $a = g\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the track inclination measured with a protractor. Quantify the fractional discrepancy and identify at least two systematic sources of error.
