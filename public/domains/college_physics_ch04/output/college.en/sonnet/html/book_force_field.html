🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. A force is a vector quantity representing an interaction between two objects that causes, or tends to cause, a change in the motion of either object. Formally, force $\vec{F}$ is defined through Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass of the object (in kilograms) and $\vec{a}$ is its acceleration (in $\text{m/s}^2$). The SI unit of force is the newton: $1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Because force is a vector, the net force is the vector sum of all individual forces acting on the object:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i $$

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 5.0\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\,\hat{x}\,\text{N} + 3\,\hat{y}\,\text{N}$. Find the net force and the resulting acceleration.

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (12 - 4)\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y} = 8\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y}\;\text{N} $$

The magnitude is $|\vec{F}_{\text{net}}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{73} \approx 8.54\,\text{N}$, directed at angle $\theta = \arctan(3/8) \approx 20.6°$ above the $x$-axis. The acceleration follows directly:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y}}{5.0} = 1.6\,\hat{x} + 0.6\,\hat{y}\;\text{m/s}^2 $$

Key Theorem (Newton's Third Law). For every force exerted by object A on object B, there exists an equal and opposite force exerted by B on A:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A} $$

These action-reaction pairs always act on different objects and therefore do not cancel in any single free-body diagram. This principle is essential for analyzing systems of interacting bodies.

Lab Exercise. Using a force sensor and a dynamics cart on a low-friction track, apply a series of measured horizontal forces and record the resulting accelerations. Plot $a$ versus $F_{\text{net}}$ and determine the slope. Verify that the slope equals $1/m$ and confirm Newton's second law within experimental uncertainty. Repeat with additional mass added to the cart and comment on how the slope changes.

🌸 Force Field

🌸 Force Field

Definition. A force field is a vector-valued function $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r})$ that assigns to every point $\mathbf{r}$ in space a force vector representing the force that would be exerted on a test particle placed at that location. Formally, for a gravitational field near a massive body $M$,

$$ \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{r}) = -\frac{GM}{r^2}\hat{r}, $$

where $G$ is the universal gravitational constant and $\hat{r}$ is the outward radial unit vector. The field exists independently of whether a test mass is present; it encodes the influence of the source throughout the surrounding region. Similarly, an electric field $\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r})$ due to a point charge $Q$ is

$$ \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r^2}\hat{r}. $$

The force on a test particle of mass $m$ or charge $q$ is then $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{g}$ or $\mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{E}$, respectively.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$) is placed $r = 0.50\ \text{m}$ from a fixed charge $Q = 2.0\ \mu\text{C} = 2.0 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{C}$. Find the magnitude of the electric field at the proton's location and the force on the proton.

$$ E = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(2.0\times10^{-6})}{(0.50)^2} = \frac{1.798\times10^4}{0.25} \approx 7.2\times10^4\ \text{N/C}. $$ $$ F = qE = (1.60\times10^{-19})(7.2\times10^4) \approx 1.15\times10^{-14}\ \text{N}. $$

The direction is radially outward from $Q$ (repulsive, like charges).

Key Theorem (Superposition Principle). For a system of $n$ source charges $Q_1, Q_2, \ldots, Q_n$, the total electric field at position $\mathbf{r}$ is the vector sum of the individual fields:

$$ \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{Q_i}{r_i^2}\hat{r}_i, $$

where $r_i = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_i|$. This linearity is a fundamental experimental fact, not derivable from more basic principles, and it underlies the entire framework of classical electromagnetism.

Payoff 🎯

What Force Fields Achieve

The concept of a force field represents the culmination of Chapter 4's treatment of forces by shifting the explanatory framework from direct contact interactions to a continuous spatial description of influence. Rather than asking "what force does object A exert on object B at this instant?", the force-field formalism asks "what force would a test object experience at every point in space?" Formally, a force field $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r})$ is a vector-valued function that assigns to each position vector $\mathbf{r}$ the force a standard test particle would experience there:

$$ \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}) = q\,\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) \quad \text{(electric)} \qquad \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}) = m\,\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{r}) \quad \text{(gravitational)} $$

This abstraction is natural because it decouples the source from the test object, allowing the field to exist and be characterized independently of any particular probe.

Worked Example

Consider a uniform gravitational field $\mathbf{g} = -g\,\hat{j}$ near Earth's surface. A projectile of mass $m = 2.0\,\text{kg}$ launched at $v_0 = 20\,\text{m/s}$ at $\theta = 30°$ experiences a constant force $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{g} = -19.6\,\hat{j}\,\text{N}$ at every point along its trajectory. The field description makes it immediate that horizontal motion is force-free while vertical motion undergoes constant acceleration — without re-deriving the force at each moment.

Key Theorem

By the superposition principle, the total field at any point is the vector sum of contributions from all sources: $\mathbf{F}_{\text{total}}(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_i \mathbf{F}_i(\mathbf{r})$. This linearity is what makes field theory computationally tractable and physically predictive at scale — from planetary orbits to semiconductor devices.

Invitation to Explore

The force-field framework is the conceptual foundation for electromagnetic theory, orbital mechanics, and quantum field theory alike. Each of these disciplines inherits the same core question: given a field configuration, what trajectories does it produce? If you wish to see this payoff realized most concretely, pursue the electric field and Coulomb's law next — there, the superposition principle, field visualization via field lines, and the first non-trivial field geometries (dipoles, continuous charge distributions) come together in a setting where every prediction is experimentally testable to high precision.
