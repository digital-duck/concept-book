🌱 Kinetic Energy

🌱 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is defined as

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

This scalar quantity represents the energy a body possesses by virtue of its motion. In SI units, mass is measured in kilograms and speed in meters per second, so kinetic energy carries units of joules ($1\,\text{J} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-2}$). Because $v^2$ appears in the expression, kinetic energy is always non-negative and depends only on the magnitude of the velocity, not its direction.

Worked Example. A 1200 kg automobile accelerates from rest to a highway speed of 27 m/s (approximately 97 km/h). Determine the kinetic energy acquired.

Applying the definition directly:

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(1200\,\text{kg})(27\,\text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1200)(729)\,\text{J} = 437{,}400\,\text{J} \approx 4.37 \times 10^5\,\text{J}. $$

This energy must be supplied by the engine and ultimately derives from the chemical energy of the fuel. The same quantity of energy must be dissipated—primarily as heat through the brakes—when the car returns to rest, illustrating the intimate connection between kinetic energy and the work required to change a body's state of motion.

Key Theorem: Work–Energy Theorem. For a particle subject to a net force $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$, the net work $W_{\text{net}}$ done on the particle as it moves from position $A$ to position $B$ equals the change in its kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K = K_B - K_A = \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_A^2. $$

The proof follows directly from Newton's second law: $F_{\text{net}} = ma$, combined with the kinematic relation $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x$ for constant acceleration, or more generally via the line integral $W = \int_A^B \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$. This theorem reduces dynamical problems to energy bookkeeping, avoiding detailed force analysis at every instant.

Compute $W_{\text{net}}$ from $F_{\text{net}}\cdot\Delta x$ and compare it to $\Delta K$ calculated from the photogate data. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify sources of systematic error, such as friction and rotational inertia of the wheels.

🍃 Temperature

🍃 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar intensive property that characterizes the thermal state of a system and determines the direction of spontaneous heat flow between bodies in thermal contact. Formally, two systems are in thermal equilibrium if and only if they share the same temperature — a statement codified by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system A is in thermal equilibrium with system C, and system B is in thermal equilibrium with system C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This transitive property makes temperature a well-defined, measurable quantity.

On the microscopic level, temperature is proportional to the mean translational kinetic energy of molecules in an ideal gas:

$$ \bar{K} = \frac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2} = \frac{3}{2}k_B T $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \mathrm{J\,K^{-1}}$ is the Boltzmann constant and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvins (K). The SI unit is the kelvin; the Celsius scale connects to it by $T(\mathrm{K}) = T(°\mathrm{C}) + 273.15$.

Worked Example. A sample of nitrogen gas ($m = 4.65 \times 10^{-26}\ \mathrm{kg}$ per molecule) is held at $T = 300\ \mathrm{K}$. Find the root-mean-square speed $v_{\mathrm{rms}}$.

Rearranging the kinetic-energy relation:

$$ v_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\overline{v^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_B T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3(1.381\times10^{-23})(300)}{4.65\times10^{-26}}} $$ $$ v_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{1.243\times10^{-20}}{4.65\times10^{-26}}} \approx \sqrt{2.674\times10^{5}} \approx 517\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}} $$

This result illustrates that temperature alone sets the characteristic molecular speed for a given species; heavier molecules at the same $T$ move more slowly.

Key Theorem. For an ideal gas, absolute temperature is directly proportional to mean molecular kinetic energy: $T = \frac{2\bar{K}}{3k_B}$. Because $\bar{K} \geq 0$, absolute zero ($T = 0\ \mathrm{K}$) represents the lower bound of all temperatures, a limit approached but never reached by any finite system.

Lab Exercise. Using a constant-volume gas thermometer containing a fixed mass of helium, record the pressure $P$ at three known temperatures (ice water, boiling water, and a third reference). Plot $P$ vs. $T$ (in kelvins) and verify linearity. Extrapolate to $P = 0$ and confirm the intercept approaches $T = 0\ \mathrm{K}$, empirically validating absolute zero and the ideal-gas temperature scale.

🌱 Volume

🌱 Volume

Definition. Volume is the measure of the three-dimensional extent of a region in space. For a continuous matter distribution, the volume $V$ of a body is obtained by integrating over all infinitesimal volume elements $dV$:

$$ V = \int_{\mathcal{R}} dV $$

In Cartesian coordinates, $dV = dx\, dy\, dz$; in cylindrical coordinates, $dV = r\, dr\, d\phi\, dz$; in spherical coordinates, $dV = r^2 \sin\theta\, dr\, d\theta\, d\phi$. The SI unit of volume is the cubic meter ($\text{m}^3$), though liters ($1\,\text{L} = 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3$) are commonly used in thermodynamic contexts.

Worked Example. A solid cylinder of radius $R = 0.050\,\text{m}$ and height $h = 0.20\,\text{m}$ is filled with an ideal gas. Compute the volume of the container.

Using cylindrical coordinates and integrating:

$$ V = \int_0^{2\pi} d\phi \int_0^R r\, dr \int_0^h dz = 2\pi \cdot \frac{R^2}{2} \cdot h = \pi R^2 h $$

Substituting:

$$ V = \pi (0.050)^2 (0.20) = \pi (2.5 \times 10^{-3})(0.20) \approx 1.57 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3 $$

This volume directly enters thermodynamic equations of state such as the ideal gas law $PV = nRT$.

Key Theorem — Cavalieri's Principle. If two solids have equal cross-sectional areas at every height $z \in [0, h]$, then they have equal volumes. Formally, if $A_1(z) = A_2(z)$ for all $z$, then:

$$ V_1 = \int_0^h A_1(z)\, dz = \int_0^h A_2(z)\, dz = V_2 $$

This principle justifies the formula $V = Ah$ for any prism or cylinder of uniform cross-section $A$, regardless of the solid's lateral profile, and underpins the derivation of volumes for oblique cylinders and Cavalieri-equivalent solids encountered throughout physics and engineering.

Lab Exercise. Fill a graduated cylinder with water to a known volume $V_0$. Submerge a small irregularly shaped object (e.g., a metal bolt) and record the new reading $V_1$. By Archimedes' displacement principle, the object's volume equals $V_1 - V_0$. Compare your measured value to a geometric estimate obtained by approximating the bolt as a cylinder. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify sources of systematic error, including surface tension effects at the meniscus and partial submersion.

🍃 Thermal Expansion

🍃 Thermal Expansion

Definition. When the temperature of a substance changes, the average kinetic energy of its constituent particles changes, altering the mean separation between particles. For most solids and liquids, an increase in temperature produces an increase in volume — a phenomenon called thermal expansion. For a solid rod of initial length $L_0$ subjected to a temperature change $\Delta T$, the change in length is

$$ \Delta L = \alpha L_0 \,\Delta T, $$

where $\alpha$ is the coefficient of linear thermal expansion (units: $\mathrm{K^{-1}}$ or $\mathrm{°C^{-1}}$), a material-dependent constant. Extending this to three dimensions, the volumetric (or cubical) expansion of a solid is

$$ \Delta V = \beta V_0 \,\Delta T, \qquad \beta \approx 3\alpha, $$

because volume scales as the cube of a linear dimension and cross terms are negligible for small $\Delta T$.

Worked Example. A steel railroad rail has length $L_0 = 12.0\ \mathrm{m}$ at $T_i = 0\ \mathrm{°C}$. On a summer day the temperature rises to $T_f = 35.0\ \mathrm{°C}$. Given $\alpha_\text{steel} = 1.20 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{°C^{-1}}$, find the change in length.

$$ \Delta T = 35.0 - 0 = 35.0\ \mathrm{°C} $$ $$ \Delta L = (1.20 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{°C^{-1}})(12.0\ \mathrm{m})(35.0\ \mathrm{°C}) = 5.04 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 5.0\ \mathrm{mm}. $$

This small but non-negligible gap motivates the expansion joints placed between rail sections to prevent buckling.

Key Theorem. For isotropic materials, linear and volumetric expansion coefficients satisfy $\beta = 3\alpha$ exactly in the limit $\Delta T \to 0$. This follows from differentiating $V = L^3$ with respect to $T$ and applying the chain rule: $\frac{dV}{dT} = 3L^2 \frac{dL}{dT} = 3L^2(\alpha L) = 3\alpha V$.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a metal rod of measured length $L_0$ at room temperature $T_0$. Submerge it in a water bath and raise the temperature to $T_f$ in $10\ \mathrm{°C}$ increments. Record $\Delta L$ at each step using a micrometer. Plot $\Delta L$ versus $\Delta T$ and extract $\alpha$ from the slope, then compare to the tabulated value for the material. Estimate the uncertainty in $\alpha$ arising from measurement precision and discuss sources of systematic error.

🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the magnitude of the normal force $F_\perp$ exerted on a surface divided by the area $A$ over which that force is distributed:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; unlike force, it has no directional component. In fluid contexts, pressure at a depth $h$ below the surface of a fluid of density $\rho$ under gravitational acceleration $g$ is given by the gauge pressure relation:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the fluid surface. This expression assumes an incompressible fluid in static equilibrium.

Worked Example. A hydraulic piston of circular cross-section has a radius of $0.050\,\text{m}$. A technician applies a force of $500\,\text{N}$ perpendicular to the piston face. Determine the pressure exerted on the fluid.

The area of the piston face is:

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.050)^2 \approx 7.85 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2 $$

The resulting pressure is:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} = \frac{500\,\text{N}}{7.85 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2} \approx 6.37 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa} $$

This pressure is transmitted undiminished throughout the enclosed fluid, a consequence of Pascal's Principle.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. Any change in pressure applied to an enclosed incompressible fluid is transmitted equally and undiminished to all points within the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if a pressure increment $\Delta P$ is applied at one point, then $\Delta P$ appears identically at every other point. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small area can generate a large force over a larger area, since $F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2$.

Lab Exercise. Fill a sealed syringe with water and attach a pressure gauge. Record the baseline pressure $P_0$ with the plunger at rest. Press the plunger inward with a measured force $F$ while noting the new gauge reading. Compute the theoretical pressure increase $\Delta P = F/A_{\text{plunger}}$ and compare it with the gauge reading. Investigate how immersing the syringe outlet to varying depths $h$ shifts the absolute pressure reading, and verify the linear relationship $P = P_0 + \rho g h$ by plotting measured pressure against depth.

🍃 Linear Thermal Expansion

🍃 Linear Thermal Expansion

Definition

When a solid object is subjected to a temperature change, its dimensions change in proportion to both its original size and the magnitude of the temperature variation. For changes confined to a single spatial dimension, this phenomenon is described by linear thermal expansion. If an object has initial length $L_0$ at temperature $T_0$, its new length $L$ at temperature $T$ is given by:

$$ L = L_0\left(1 + \alpha\,\Delta T\right) $$

where $\Delta T = T - T_0$ is the temperature change and $\alpha$ is the coefficient of linear thermal expansion, a material-dependent constant with SI units of $\mathrm{K^{-1}}$ (or equivalently ${}^\circ\mathrm{C}^{-1}$). The change in length is therefore:

$$ \Delta L = \alpha\, L_0\, \Delta T $$

This expression applies provided $\Delta T$ is small enough that $\alpha$ can be treated as constant over the temperature range.

Worked Example

A steel railroad rail has a length of $L_0 = 12.0\ \mathrm{m}$ at $T_0 = 20.0\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}$. The coefficient of linear expansion for steel is $\alpha = 1.2 \times 10^{-5}\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}^{-1}$. Find the change in length when the temperature rises to $T = 50.0\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}$.

$$ \Delta T = 50.0 - 20.0 = 30.0\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C} $$ $$ \Delta L = \alpha\, L_0\, \Delta T = (1.2 \times 10^{-5})(12.0)(30.0) = 4.32 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 4.3\ \mathrm{mm} $$

This small but non-negligible expansion explains why expansion gaps are incorporated into rail joints and bridge decks.

Key Theorem

For isotropic materials, linear thermal expansion in one dimension is related to volumetric (bulk) thermal expansion by $\beta \approx 3\alpha$, valid when $\alpha\,\Delta T \operatorname{ll} 1$. This follows directly from expanding $(1 + \alpha\,\Delta T)^3$ and retaining only first-order terms.

🍃 Thermal Stress

🍃 Thermal Stress

Definition. When a solid object is prevented from expanding or contracting freely in response to a temperature change, internal forces develop within the material. These internal forces per unit cross-sectional area constitute thermal stress. For a rod of Young's modulus $E$ and linear thermal expansion coefficient $\alpha$, constrained at both ends so that its length cannot change, a temperature shift $\Delta T$ produces a thermal strain $\varepsilon = \alpha \Delta T$. Because the constraint supplies the restoring force that would otherwise appear as physical elongation, the resulting thermal stress is

$$ \sigma = E \alpha \Delta T. $$

The stress is compressive when the temperature rises (the rod "wants" to expand but cannot) and tensile when the temperature falls.

Worked Example. A steel rail of length $L = 10\,\text{m}$ is rigidly fixed between two concrete abutments at $T_0 = 10\,^\circ\text{C}$. On a summer afternoon the rail heats to $T = 50\,^\circ\text{C}$, giving $\Delta T = 40\,\text{K}$. For steel, $E = 200\,\text{GPa}$ and $\alpha = 1.2 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{K}^{-1}$. The thermal stress is

$$ \sigma = (200 \times 10^9\,\text{Pa})(1.2 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{K}^{-1})(40\,\text{K}) = 9.6 \times 10^7\,\text{Pa} = 96\,\text{MPa}. $$

Since the compressive yield strength of structural steel is approximately $250\,\text{MPa}$, the rail remains elastic—but the stress is substantial. Railway engineers address this by pre-stressing rails or leaving calibrated expansion gaps.

Key Theorem. For an isotropic elastic solid under purely thermal loading with no external forces and full constraint in one dimension, the equilibrium condition requires the thermal strain to be exactly balanced by the mechanical strain: $\varepsilon_{\text{mech}} + \alpha \Delta T = 0$. It follows immediately that $\sigma = -E \alpha \Delta T$, with the sign indicating compression for positive $\Delta T$.

Lab Exercise. Clamp a 30 cm aluminum rod ($\alpha = 2.3 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{K}^{-1}$, $E = 69\,\text{GPa}$) between two force sensors. Heat the rod uniformly by $\Delta T = 25\,\text{K}$ using a heat gun. Record the compressive force $F$ reported by the sensors. Compute the cross-sectional area $A$ from caliper measurements, determine the experimental stress $\sigma_{\text{exp}} = F/A$, and compare it to the theoretical prediction $E \alpha \Delta T$. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including imperfect constraint stiffness and non-uniform heating.

🌸 Expansion Joints

🌸 Expansion Joints

Definition. An expansion joint is a structural gap or flexible connector deliberately incorporated into buildings, bridges, pipelines, and rail lines to accommodate thermally induced dimensional changes. When a solid of length $L_0$ undergoes a temperature change $\Delta T$, its length changes by

$$ \Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta T, $$

where $\alpha$ is the coefficient of linear thermal expansion (units: $\text{°C}^{-1}$ or $\text{K}^{-1}$). Without expansion joints, the constrained thermal strain $\varepsilon = \alpha \Delta T$ generates a compressive or tensile stress $\sigma = E \alpha \Delta T$, where $E$ is Young's modulus. For steel ($E \approx 200\ \text{GPa}$, $\alpha \approx 12 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{°C}^{-1}$), even a modest $\Delta T = 40\ \text{°C}$ produces $\sigma \approx 96\ \text{MPa}$—approaching the yield stress—making unconstrained expansion essential in engineering design.

Worked Example. A steel railway rail has length $L_0 = 12.0\ \text{m}$ at $0\ \text{°C}$. On a summer day the temperature rises to $40\ \text{°C}$. Find the expansion $\Delta L$.

$$ \Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta T = (12 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{°C}^{-1})(12.0\ \text{m})(40\ \text{°C}) = 5.76 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m} \approx 5.8\ \text{mm}. $$

Each rail must therefore have a gap of at least $5.8\ \text{mm}$ at its coldest expected temperature to prevent buckling at peak summer heat. In practice, engineers specify gaps accounting for the full seasonal temperature range $\Delta T_{\max}$.

Key Theorem (Thermal Stress). For a rod of length $L_0$, cross-sectional area $A$, and Young's modulus $E$, rigidly fixed at both ends, a temperature change $\Delta T$ induces no change in length but instead a thermal stress

$$ \sigma = -E \alpha \Delta T. $$

The negative sign indicates compression for $\Delta T > 0$. The corresponding force exerted on the supports is $F = EA\alpha\Delta T$, independent of $L_0$. Expansion joints eliminate this constraint, reducing $\sigma$ to zero.

Payoff 🎯

Expansion joints represent a culminating application of thermal physics, bridging the abstract principles of thermal expansion studied throughout this chapter into the realm of tangible structural engineering. After developing the quantitative framework for linear and volumetric expansion — governed by $\Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta T$ and $\Delta V = \beta V_0 \Delta T$ — the concept of expansion joints emerges as the natural endpoint: it is the answer to the practical question that thermal expansion itself raises. If every material expands and contracts with temperature change, how do engineers build structures that survive those changes without catastrophic failure?

The answer lies in deliberate design. An expansion joint is a gap or flexible interface intentionally incorporated into a structure to accommodate thermally induced dimensional changes. Without them, constrained expansion generates compressive stress $\sigma = Y \alpha \Delta T$, where $Y$ is Young's modulus. For steel with $Y \approx 2.0 \times 10^{11}\ \text{Pa}$ and $\alpha \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{°C}^{-1}$, a temperature rise of $\Delta T = 40\ \text{°C}$ produces $\sigma \approx 9.6 \times 10^7\ \text{Pa}$, approaching the yield strength of structural steel. Expansion joints prevent this stress from accumulating.

Worked Example. A steel bridge span of length $L_0 = 50\ \text{m}$ experiences a seasonal temperature variation of $\Delta T = 60\ \text{°C}$. The required joint gap is:

$$ \Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta T = (1.2 \times 10^{-5})(50)(60) = 3.6 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{m} = 3.6\ \text{cm.} $$

Engineers size the joint to at least this clearance, ensuring the span can expand freely without buckling.

Key Theorem. For a rod of length $L_0$ with both ends rigidly fixed, a temperature change $\Delta T$ induces a thermal stress $\sigma = -Y\alpha\Delta T$. The negative sign indicates compression upon heating; the stress is independent of the rod's length, depending only on material properties and temperature change.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a steel and an aluminum rod of equal length. Clamp one end of each, heat both uniformly by $\Delta T = 50\ \text{°C}$, and measure the free-end displacement. Compare your measurements to predictions from $\Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta T$ and compute the percent error for each material.

Among the applications that expansion joints unlock, structural engineering offers the richest terrain: skyscrapers, highway bridges, railway tracks, and concrete pavements all depend on precisely engineered joints.
