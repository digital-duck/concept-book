🍃 Thermal Stress

🍃 Thermal Stress

Definition. When a solid object is prevented from expanding or contracting freely in response to a temperature change, internal forces develop within the material. These internal forces per unit cross-sectional area constitute thermal stress. For a rod of Young's modulus $E$ and linear thermal expansion coefficient $\alpha$, constrained at both ends so that its length cannot change, a temperature shift $\Delta T$ produces a thermal strain $\varepsilon = \alpha \Delta T$. Because the constraint supplies the restoring force that would otherwise appear as physical elongation, the resulting thermal stress is

$$ \sigma = E \alpha \Delta T. $$

The stress is compressive when the temperature rises (the rod "wants" to expand but cannot) and tensile when the temperature falls.

Worked Example. A steel rail of length $L = 10\,\text{m}$ is rigidly fixed between two concrete abutments at $T_0 = 10\,^\circ\text{C}$. On a summer afternoon the rail heats to $T = 50\,^\circ\text{C}$, giving $\Delta T = 40\,\text{K}$. For steel, $E = 200\,\text{GPa}$ and $\alpha = 1.2 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{K}^{-1}$. The thermal stress is

$$ \sigma = (200 \times 10^9\,\text{Pa})(1.2 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{K}^{-1})(40\,\text{K}) = 9.6 \times 10^7\,\text{Pa} = 96\,\text{MPa}. $$

Since the compressive yield strength of structural steel is approximately $250\,\text{MPa}$, the rail remains elastic—but the stress is substantial. Railway engineers address this by pre-stressing rails or leaving calibrated expansion gaps.

Key Theorem. For an isotropic elastic solid under purely thermal loading with no external forces and full constraint in one dimension, the equilibrium condition requires the thermal strain to be exactly balanced by the mechanical strain: $\varepsilon_{\text{mech}} + \alpha \Delta T = 0$. It follows immediately that $\sigma = -E \alpha \Delta T$, with the sign indicating compression for positive $\Delta T$.

Lab Exercise. Clamp a 30 cm aluminum rod ($\alpha = 2.3 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{K}^{-1}$, $E = 69\,\text{GPa}$) between two force sensors. Heat the rod uniformly by $\Delta T = 25\,\text{K}$ using a heat gun. Record the compressive force $F$ reported by the sensors. Compute the cross-sectional area $A$ from caliper measurements, determine the experimental stress $\sigma_{\text{exp}} = F/A$, and compare it to the theoretical prediction $E \alpha \Delta T$. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including imperfect constraint stiffness and non-uniform heating.
