🍃 Phase Diagram

🍃 Phase Diagram

Definition. A phase diagram is a pressure–temperature ($P$–$T$) graph that delineates the regions of thermodynamic stability for the distinct phases of a pure substance—solid, liquid, and gas. Each region is bounded by coexistence curves along which two phases exist simultaneously in thermodynamic equilibrium. Three special features define every phase diagram: the triple point, where all three phases coexist at a unique pair $(T_t, P_t)$; the critical point $(T_c, P_c)$, beyond which the liquid–gas distinction vanishes; and the sublimation curve, the fusion curve, and the vaporization curve, which separate adjacent phase regions.

Along any coexistence curve the chemical potentials of the two phases are equal, and the slope of each curve is governed by the Clausius–Clapeyron equation:

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T\,\Delta v} $$

where $L$ is the latent heat per mole and $\Delta v = v_2 - v_1$ is the molar volume change between phases.

Worked Example. Consider water at an initial state of $P = 1\text{ atm}$, $T = 110\,^\circ\text{C}$ (superheated steam). Suppose the system is cooled isobarically. On the $P$–$T$ diagram, this corresponds to moving horizontally leftward at constant $P = 1\text{ atm}$. The path crosses the vaporization curve at $T = 100\,^\circ\text{C}$, where condensation to liquid begins. Continued cooling keeps the system in the liquid region until, at $T = 0\,^\circ\text{C}$, the path intersects the fusion curve and freezing commences. The phase diagram immediately reveals which phase transformations occur and at what temperatures, without solving any equations of state.

Key Theorem (Gibbs Phase Rule). For a single-component system, the number of thermodynamic degrees of freedom $F$ satisfies

$$ F = C - P + 2 = 3 - P $$

where $C = 1$ is the number of components and $P$ is the number of coexisting phases. A single-phase region has $F = 2$ (both $T$ and $P$ free), a coexistence curve has $F = 1$ (one variable fixes the other), and the triple point has $F = 0$ (fully determined).

Lab Exercise. Obtain a sealed syringe containing a small sample of a volatile liquid (e.g., ethanol). Record the temperature and note the initial two-phase (liquid–vapor) meniscus. Slowly compress the syringe while monitoring pressure with an attached gauge. Plot your $(T, P)$ measurements on a schematic $P$–$T$ diagram and identify the coexistence curve you are traversing. Estimate the slope $dP/dT$ at two temperature points and use the Clausius–Clapeyron equation to infer the latent heat of vaporization.
