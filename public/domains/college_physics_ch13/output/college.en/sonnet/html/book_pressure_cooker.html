🌱 Kinetic Energy

🌱 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is defined as

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

This scalar quantity represents the energy a body possesses by virtue of its motion. In SI units, mass is measured in kilograms and speed in meters per second, so kinetic energy carries units of joules ($1\,\text{J} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-2}$). Because $v^2$ appears in the expression, kinetic energy is always non-negative and depends only on the magnitude of the velocity, not its direction.

Worked Example. A 1200 kg automobile accelerates from rest to a highway speed of 27 m/s (approximately 97 km/h). Determine the kinetic energy acquired.

Applying the definition directly:

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(1200\,\text{kg})(27\,\text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1200)(729)\,\text{J} = 437{,}400\,\text{J} \approx 4.37 \times 10^5\,\text{J}. $$

This energy must be supplied by the engine and ultimately derives from the chemical energy of the fuel. The same quantity of energy must be dissipated—primarily as heat through the brakes—when the car returns to rest, illustrating the intimate connection between kinetic energy and the work required to change a body's state of motion.

Key Theorem: Work–Energy Theorem. For a particle subject to a net force $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$, the net work $W_{\text{net}}$ done on the particle as it moves from position $A$ to position $B$ equals the change in its kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K = K_B - K_A = \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_A^2. $$

The proof follows directly from Newton's second law: $F_{\text{net}} = ma$, combined with the kinematic relation $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x$ for constant acceleration, or more generally via the line integral $W = \int_A^B \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$. This theorem reduces dynamical problems to energy bookkeeping, avoiding detailed force analysis at every instant.

🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the magnitude of the normal force $F_\perp$ exerted on a surface divided by the area $A$ over which that force is distributed:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; unlike force, it has no directional component. In fluid contexts, pressure at a depth $h$ below the surface of a fluid of density $\rho$ under gravitational acceleration $g$ is given by the gauge pressure relation:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the fluid surface. This expression assumes an incompressible fluid in static equilibrium.

Worked Example. A hydraulic piston of circular cross-section has a radius of $0.050\,\text{m}$. A technician applies a force of $500\,\text{N}$ perpendicular to the piston face. Determine the pressure exerted on the fluid.

The area of the piston face is:

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.050)^2 \approx 7.85 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2 $$

The resulting pressure is:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} = \frac{500\,\text{N}}{7.85 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2} \approx 6.37 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa} $$

This pressure is transmitted undiminished throughout the enclosed fluid, a consequence of Pascal's Principle.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. Any change in pressure applied to an enclosed incompressible fluid is transmitted equally and undiminished to all points within the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if a pressure increment $\Delta P$ is applied at one point, then $\Delta P$ appears identically at every other point. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small area can generate a large force over a larger area, since $F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2$.

🍃 Temperature

🍃 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar intensive property that characterizes the thermal state of a system and determines the direction of spontaneous heat flow between bodies in thermal contact. Formally, two systems are in thermal equilibrium if and only if they share the same temperature — a statement codified by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system A is in thermal equilibrium with system C, and system B is in thermal equilibrium with system C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This transitive property makes temperature a well-defined, measurable quantity.

On the microscopic level, temperature is proportional to the mean translational kinetic energy of molecules in an ideal gas:

$$ \bar{K} = \frac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2} = \frac{3}{2}k_B T $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \mathrm{J\,K^{-1}}$ is the Boltzmann constant and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvins (K). The SI unit is the kelvin; the Celsius scale connects to it by $T(\mathrm{K}) = T(°\mathrm{C}) + 273.15$.

Worked Example. A sample of nitrogen gas ($m = 4.65 \times 10^{-26}\ \mathrm{kg}$ per molecule) is held at $T = 300\ \mathrm{K}$. Find the root-mean-square speed $v_{\mathrm{rms}}$.

Rearranging the kinetic-energy relation:

$$ v_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\overline{v^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_B T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3(1.381\times10^{-23})(300)}{4.65\times10^{-26}}} $$ $$ v_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{1.243\times10^{-20}}{4.65\times10^{-26}}} \approx \sqrt{2.674\times10^{5}} \approx 517\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}} $$

This result illustrates that temperature alone sets the characteristic molecular speed for a given species; heavier molecules at the same $T$ move more slowly.

Key Theorem. For an ideal gas, absolute temperature is directly proportional to mean molecular kinetic energy: $T = \frac{2\bar{K}}{3k_B}$. Because $\bar{K} \geq 0$, absolute zero ($T = 0\ \mathrm{K}$) represents the lower bound of all temperatures, a limit approached but never reached by any finite system.

Lab Exercise. Using a constant-volume gas thermometer containing a fixed mass of helium, record the pressure $P$ at three known temperatures (ice water, boiling water, and a third reference). Plot $P$ vs. $T$ (in kelvins) and verify linearity. Extrapolate to $P = 0$ and confirm the intercept approaches $T = 0\ \mathrm{K}$, empirically validating absolute zero and the ideal-gas temperature scale.

🍃 Phase Change

🍃 Phase Change

A phase change is a thermodynamic process in which a substance transitions between distinct states of matter—solid, liquid, or gas—at constant temperature and pressure. During a phase change, energy supplied to or removed from the system alters the intermolecular potential energy rather than the kinetic energy of the molecules, which is why temperature remains fixed throughout the transition.

The energy required to complete a phase change is characterized by the latent heat $L$, defined by the relation

$$ Q = mL $$

where $Q$ is the heat transferred (in joules), $m$ is the mass of the substance (in kilograms), and $L$ is the specific latent heat (in $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$). For the solid–liquid transition, $L$ takes the value of the latent heat of fusion $L_f$; for the liquid–gas transition, it takes the value of the latent heat of vaporization $L_v$.

Worked Example. Determine the total heat required to convert $0.500\,\mathrm{kg}$ of ice at $0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ entirely into liquid water at $0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. For water, $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$.

$$ Q = mL_f = (0.500\,\mathrm{kg})(3.34 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}) = 1.67 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J} $$

No temperature change occurs; the $1.67 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J}$ of heat breaks the hydrogen bonds holding the crystalline lattice together, increasing potential energy at fixed kinetic energy.

Key Theorem (Clausius–Clapeyron Relation). The dependence of the phase-transition temperature on pressure is governed by

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T\,\Delta v} $$

where $\Delta v$ is the specific volume change between phases. For vaporization, $\Delta v > 0$, so increasing pressure raises the boiling point—a principle exploited in pressure cookers.

Record temperature every $30\,\mathrm{s}$ and plot a heating curve. Identify the plateau region corresponding to the melting phase change, measure its duration $\Delta t$, and compute the experimental latent heat via $L_f = P\Delta t / m$. Compare your result with the accepted value $3.34 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$ and discuss sources of heat loss.

🍃 Vapor

🍃 Vapor

Definition. A vapor is the gaseous phase of a substance that exists below the substance's critical temperature, in thermodynamic equilibrium or coexistence with its liquid or solid phase. Unlike a gas that exists above the critical temperature and cannot be liquefied by pressure alone, a vapor can be condensed into a liquid by isothermal compression. The partial pressure exerted by a vapor in equilibrium with its liquid is called the equilibrium vapor pressure (or saturation pressure), $P_{\text{vap}}$, which depends solely on temperature and is described by the Clausius–Clapeyron equation:

$$ \frac{dP_{\text{vap}}}{dT} = \frac{L \, P_{\text{vap}}}{R T^2} $$

where $L$ is the molar latent heat of vaporization and $R = 8.314 \ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the ideal gas constant. Integrating between temperatures $T_1$ and $T_2$ yields:

$$ \ln\!\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = -\frac{L}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right) $$

Worked Example. The vapor pressure of water is $P_1 = 3.17 \ \text{kPa}$ at $T_1 = 298 \ \text{K}$. Find the vapor pressure at $T_2 = 373 \ \text{K}$, given $L = 40{,}700 \ \text{J mol}^{-1}$.

$$ \ln\!\left(\frac{P_2}{3.17}\right) = -\frac{40700}{8.314}\left(\frac{1}{373} - \frac{1}{298}\right) = 4892 \times 6.75 \times 10^{-4} \approx 3.30 $$ $$ P_2 = 3.17 \times e^{3.30} \approx 3.17 \times 27.1 \approx 85.9 \ \text{kPa} $$

The accepted value at $373 \ \text{K}$ is $101.3 \ \text{kPa}$; the discrepancy arises because $L$ varies with temperature—this calculation assumes constant $L$.

Key Theorem. At thermodynamic equilibrium, the chemical potential of the vapor phase equals that of the liquid phase: $\mu_{\text{liq}}(T, P) = \mu_{\text{vap}}(T, P)$. This equality enforces the uniqueness of $P_{\text{vap}}(T)$ and ensures that, at fixed temperature, any additional vapor above saturation will spontaneously condense.

🍃 Vapor Pressure

🍃 Vapor Pressure

Definition. Vapor pressure is the equilibrium pressure exerted by the vapor phase of a substance above its liquid (or solid) surface in a closed system at a given temperature. At equilibrium, the rate at which molecules escape the liquid surface equals the rate at which vapor molecules return to it. The vapor pressure $P_v$ depends solely on temperature and the identity of the substance; it is independent of the amount of liquid or vapor present, provided both phases coexist.

The relationship between vapor pressure and temperature is governed by the Clausius–Clapeyron equation:

$$ \frac{dP_v}{dT} = \frac{L\, P_v}{R T^2} $$

where $L$ is the molar latent heat of vaporization and $R = 8.314\ \mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$ is the universal gas constant. Integrating between two temperatures $T_1$ and $T_2$ yields the integrated form:

$$ \ln\!\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = -\frac{L}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right). $$

Worked Example. The vapor pressure of water at $T_1 = 373\ \mathrm{K}$ (normal boiling point) is $P_1 = 1.013 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}$. Given $L = 4.07 \times 10^4\ \mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}$, find the vapor pressure at $T_2 = 353\ \mathrm{K}$.

$$ \ln\!\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = -\frac{4.07\times10^4}{8.314}\left(\frac{1}{353} - \frac{1}{373}\right) = -4895\times(2.834\times10^{-4} - 2.681\times10^{-4}) $$ $$ = -4895 \times 1.527\times10^{-4} \approx -0.747 $$ $$ P_2 = 1.013\times10^5 \times e^{-0.747} \approx 4.74\times10^4\ \mathrm{Pa}. $$

This result confirms that lowering temperature significantly reduces vapor pressure, consistent with everyday observation.

Key Theorem. A liquid boils when its vapor pressure equals the ambient pressure. At standard atmospheric pressure ($1.013\times10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}$), water boils at exactly $373\ \mathrm{K}$. At reduced pressure — for instance, at high altitude — boiling occurs below $373\ \mathrm{K}$, since the vapor pressure reaches the lower ambient value at a lower temperature.

Plot $\ln P_v$ versus $1/T$ and determine the molar latent heat $L$ from the slope. 🍃 Boiling

🍃 Boiling

Definition. Boiling is the phase transition from liquid to vapor that occurs throughout the bulk of a liquid when its vapor pressure equals the external pressure acting on its surface. This distinguishes boiling from evaporation, which occurs only at the liquid–vapor interface and at temperatures below the boiling point. The temperature at which this equality is achieved is called the boiling point, $T_b$, and depends on the external pressure $P_\text{ext}$. At standard atmospheric pressure ($P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$), water boils at $T_b = 100\ ^\circ\text{C} = 373.15\ \text{K}$.

The energy required to convert a mass $m$ of liquid to vapor at constant temperature and pressure is given by

$$ Q = mL_v, $$

where $L_v$ is the latent heat of vaporization (J/kg). For water at $100\ ^\circ\text{C}$, $L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6\ \text{J/kg}$.

Worked Example. A kettle contains $0.500\ \text{kg}$ of water already at $100\ ^\circ\text{C}$. How much energy must be supplied to vaporize it completely?

$$ Q = mL_v = (0.500\ \text{kg})(2.26 \times 10^6\ \text{J/kg}) = 1.13 \times 10^6\ \text{J}. $$

This is roughly five times the energy needed to heat the same mass of water from $0\ ^\circ\text{C}$ to $100\ ^\circ\text{C}$ (which requires only $Q = mc\Delta T = (0.500)(4186)(100) \approx 2.09 \times 10^5\ \text{J}$), illustrating that breaking intermolecular bonds dominates the energy budget.

Key Theorem (Clausius–Clapeyron Equation). The dependence of boiling point on pressure is governed by

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L_v}{T\,\Delta v}, $$

where $\Delta v = v_\text{vapor} - v_\text{liquid}$ is the specific volume change between phases. Since $\Delta v > 0$, increasing $P_\text{ext}$ raises $T_b$—the principle underlying pressure cookers—while decreasing pressure lowers $T_b$, explaining why water boils below $100\ ^\circ\text{C}$ at high altitude.

🌸 Pressure Cooker

🌸 Pressure Cooker

Definition. A pressure cooker is a sealed vessel that exploits the relationship between pressure and the boiling point of water to cook food at temperatures exceeding 100 °C. The governing principle is the Clausius-Clapeyron relation, which describes how the equilibrium vapor pressure $P$ of a liquid varies with temperature $T$:

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T\,\Delta v} $$

where $L$ is the latent heat of vaporization per unit mass and $\Delta v = v_g - v_l$ is the specific volume difference between the vapor and liquid phases. Because the cooker's rigid lid prevents steam from escaping, the vapor pressure rises above atmospheric as temperature increases, raising the boiling point correspondingly. At a gauge pressure of approximately $\Delta P = 101\text{ kPa}$ above atmospheric ($P_\text{abs} \approx 202\text{ kPa}$), water boils near $120\text{ °C}$, roughly $20\text{ °C}$ above its sea-level boiling point.

Worked Example. A pressure cooker maintains an absolute internal pressure of $2.0 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$. Estimate the boiling point of water inside, given $L = 2.26 \times 10^6\text{ J/kg}$ and the normal boiling point $T_0 = 373\text{ K}$ at $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$.

Integrating the Clausius-Clapeyron equation with the ideal-gas approximation for the vapor ($\Delta v \approx RT/MP$, where $M = 0.018\text{ kg/mol}$):

$$ \ln\!\frac{P}{P_0} = -\frac{ML}{R}\!\left(\frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{T_0}\right) $$

Substituting $P = 2.0\times10^5\text{ Pa}$:

$$ \ln\!\left(\frac{2.0}{1.013}\right) = 0.681 = \frac{(0.018)(2.26\times10^6)}{8.314}\!\left(\frac{1}{373} - \frac{1}{T}\right) $$

Solving gives $T \approx 394\text{ K}\ (121\text{ °C})$, consistent with experimental observations.

Key Theorem. The elevated boiling point follows directly from the Clausius-Clapeyron relation itself: since $dP/dT > 0$ for water, any increase in the equilibrium vapor pressure $P$ above atmospheric requires a correspondingly higher temperature $T$ for the liquid and vapor phases to coexist. Constraining the vapor inside a sealed vessel therefore monotonically raises the boiling point with applied pressure.

Payoff 🎯

The pressure cooker stands as the natural capstone of thermodynamics and fluid statics because it is precisely the device in which abstract principles—Boyle's law, Gay-Lussac's law, the Clausius-Clapeyron relation, and Pascal's principle—converge into a single, controllable system. Far from being merely a kitchen appliance, the pressure cooker is a closed thermodynamic vessel that exploits the dependence of a liquid's boiling point on external pressure to achieve temperatures inaccessible at atmospheric conditions.

What the pressure cooker achieves. At standard atmospheric pressure $P_0 = 101.325\ \text{kPa}$, water boils at $T = 100\ ^\circ\text{C}$. A sealed pressure cooker elevates the internal pressure to roughly $P = 2P_0$, and by the Clausius-Clapeyron equation,

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T\,\Delta v}, $$

where $L$ is the latent heat of vaporization and $\Delta v$ is the specific volume change, the boiling point rises to approximately $120\ ^\circ\text{C}$. This $20\ ^\circ\text{C}$ elevation dramatically accelerates chemical reaction rates inside the vessel, reducing cooking times by a factor of two to three while preserving moisture and nutrients.

Worked example. Suppose a pressure cooker's relief valve opens at gauge pressure $P_g = 103\ \text{kPa}$. The absolute internal pressure is $P = P_0 + P_g = 204\ \text{kPa}$. Using the approximation from the Clausius-Clapeyron relation with $L \approx 2.26 \times 10^6\ \text{J kg}^{-1}$, one estimates the new boiling point to be $T \approx 121\ ^\circ\text{C}$, consistent with empirical steam tables.

Key theorem — Phase equilibrium condition. At equilibrium, the chemical potential of liquid and vapor phases must be equal: $\mu_\ell(T,P) = \mu_v(T,P)$. This condition, combined with the ideal-gas approximation for the vapor phase, directly yields the Clausius-Clapeyron equation as the governing relation for pressure-temperature coexistence curves.
