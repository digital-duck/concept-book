🍃 Linear Thermal Expansion

🍃 Linear Thermal Expansion

Definition

When a solid object is subjected to a temperature change, its dimensions change in proportion to both its original size and the magnitude of the temperature variation. For changes confined to a single spatial dimension, this phenomenon is described by linear thermal expansion. If an object has initial length $L_0$ at temperature $T_0$, its new length $L$ at temperature $T$ is given by:

$$ L = L_0\left(1 + \alpha\,\Delta T\right) $$

where $\Delta T = T - T_0$ is the temperature change and $\alpha$ is the coefficient of linear thermal expansion, a material-dependent constant with SI units of $\mathrm{K^{-1}}$ (or equivalently ${}^\circ\mathrm{C}^{-1}$). The change in length is therefore:

$$ \Delta L = \alpha\, L_0\, \Delta T $$

This expression applies provided $\Delta T$ is small enough that $\alpha$ can be treated as constant over the temperature range.

Worked Example

A steel railroad rail has a length of $L_0 = 12.0\ \mathrm{m}$ at $T_0 = 20.0\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}$. The coefficient of linear expansion for steel is $\alpha = 1.2 \times 10^{-5}\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}^{-1}$. Find the change in length when the temperature rises to $T = 50.0\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}$.

$$ \Delta T = 50.0 - 20.0 = 30.0\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C} $$ $$ \Delta L = \alpha\, L_0\, \Delta T = (1.2 \times 10^{-5})(12.0)(30.0) = 4.32 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 4.3\ \mathrm{mm} $$

This small but non-negligible expansion explains why expansion gaps are incorporated into rail joints and bridge decks.

Key Theorem

For isotropic materials, linear thermal expansion in one dimension is related to volumetric (bulk) thermal expansion by $\beta \approx 3\alpha$, valid when $\alpha\,\Delta T \operatorname{ll} 1$. This follows directly from expanding $(1 + \alpha\,\Delta T)^3$ and retaining only first-order terms.

Lab Exercise

Obtain metal rods of equal initial length made from aluminum ($\alpha \approx 2.3 \times 10^{-5}\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}^{-1}$) and copper ($\alpha \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-5}\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}^{-1}$). Using a steam jacket to heat each rod from $20\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}$ to $100\ {}^\circ\mathrm{C}$, measure $\Delta L$ with a dial gauge. Compare your experimental $\alpha$ values to the tabulated constants and compute the percent error, identifying the dominant sources of uncertainty in your measurement.
