🌱 Kinetic Energy

🌱 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is defined as

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

This scalar quantity represents the energy a body possesses by virtue of its motion. In SI units, mass is measured in kilograms and speed in meters per second, so kinetic energy carries units of joules ($1\,\text{J} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-2}$). Because $v^2$ appears in the expression, kinetic energy is always non-negative and depends only on the magnitude of the velocity, not its direction.

Worked Example. A 1200 kg automobile accelerates from rest to a highway speed of 27 m/s (approximately 97 km/h). Determine the kinetic energy acquired.

Applying the definition directly:

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(1200\,\text{kg})(27\,\text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1200)(729)\,\text{J} = 437{,}400\,\text{J} \approx 4.37 \times 10^5\,\text{J}. $$

This energy must be supplied by the engine and ultimately derives from the chemical energy of the fuel. The same quantity of energy must be dissipated—primarily as heat through the brakes—when the car returns to rest, illustrating the intimate connection between kinetic energy and the work required to change a body's state of motion.

Key Theorem: Work–Energy Theorem. For a particle subject to a net force $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$, the net work $W_{\text{net}}$ done on the particle as it moves from position $A$ to position $B$ equals the change in its kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K = K_B - K_A = \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_A^2. $$

The proof follows directly from Newton's second law: $F_{\text{net}} = ma$, combined with the kinematic relation $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x$ for constant acceleration, or more generally via the line integral $W = \int_A^B \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$. This theorem reduces dynamical problems to energy bookkeeping, avoiding detailed force analysis at every instant.

🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the magnitude of the normal force $F_\perp$ exerted on a surface divided by the area $A$ over which that force is distributed:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; unlike force, it has no directional component. In fluid contexts, pressure at a depth $h$ below the surface of a fluid of density $\rho$ under gravitational acceleration $g$ is given by the gauge pressure relation:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the fluid surface. This expression assumes an incompressible fluid in static equilibrium.

Worked Example. A hydraulic piston of circular cross-section has a radius of $0.050\,\text{m}$. A technician applies a force of $500\,\text{N}$ perpendicular to the piston face. Determine the pressure exerted on the fluid.

The area of the piston face is:

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.050)^2 \approx 7.85 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2 $$

The resulting pressure is:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} = \frac{500\,\text{N}}{7.85 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2} \approx 6.37 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa} $$

This pressure is transmitted undiminished throughout the enclosed fluid, a consequence of Pascal's Principle.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. Any change in pressure applied to an enclosed incompressible fluid is transmitted equally and undiminished to all points within the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if a pressure increment $\Delta P$ is applied at one point, then $\Delta P$ appears identically at every other point. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small area can generate a large force over a larger area, since $F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2$.

Compute the theoretical pressure increase $\Delta P = F/A_{\text{plunger}}$ and compare it with the gauge reading. Investigate how immersing the syringe outlet to varying depths $h$ shifts the absolute pressure reading, and verify the linear relationship $P = P_0 + \rho g h$ by plotting measured pressure against depth.

🍃 Temperature

🍃 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar intensive property that characterizes the thermal state of a system and determines the direction of spontaneous heat flow between bodies in thermal contact. Formally, two systems are in thermal equilibrium if and only if they share the same temperature — a statement codified by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system A is in thermal equilibrium with system C, and system B is in thermal equilibrium with system C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This transitive property makes temperature a well-defined, measurable quantity.

On the microscopic level, temperature is proportional to the mean translational kinetic energy of molecules in an ideal gas:

$$ \bar{K} = \frac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2} = \frac{3}{2}k_B T $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \mathrm{J\,K^{-1}}$ is the Boltzmann constant and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvins (K). The SI unit is the kelvin; the Celsius scale connects to it by $T(\mathrm{K}) = T(°\mathrm{C}) + 273.15$.

Worked Example. A sample of nitrogen gas ($m = 4.65 \times 10^{-26}\ \mathrm{kg}$ per molecule) is held at $T = 300\ \mathrm{K}$. Find the root-mean-square speed $v_{\mathrm{rms}}$.

Rearranging the kinetic-energy relation:

$$ v_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\overline{v^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_B T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3(1.381\times10^{-23})(300)}{4.65\times10^{-26}}} $$ $$ v_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{1.243\times10^{-20}}{4.65\times10^{-26}}} \approx \sqrt{2.674\times10^{5}} \approx 517\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}} $$

This result illustrates that temperature alone sets the characteristic molecular speed for a given species; heavier molecules at the same $T$ move more slowly.

Key Theorem. For an ideal gas, absolute temperature is directly proportional to mean molecular kinetic energy: $T = \frac{2\bar{K}}{3k_B}$. Because $\bar{K} \geq 0$, absolute zero ($T = 0\ \mathrm{K}$) represents the lower bound of all temperatures, a limit approached but never reached by any finite system.

Lab Exercise. Using a constant-volume gas thermometer containing a fixed mass of helium, record the pressure $P$ at three known temperatures (ice water, boiling water, and a third reference). Plot $P$ vs. $T$ (in kelvins) and verify linearity. Extrapolate to $P = 0$ and confirm the intercept approaches $T = 0\ \mathrm{K}$, empirically validating absolute zero and the ideal-gas temperature scale.

🍃 Phase Change

🍃 Phase Change

A phase change is a thermodynamic process in which a substance transitions between distinct states of matter—solid, liquid, or gas—at constant temperature and pressure. During a phase change, energy supplied to or removed from the system alters the intermolecular potential energy rather than the kinetic energy of the molecules, which is why temperature remains fixed throughout the transition.

The energy required to complete a phase change is characterized by the latent heat $L$, defined by the relation

$$ Q = mL $$

where $Q$ is the heat transferred (in joules), $m$ is the mass of the substance (in kilograms), and $L$ is the specific latent heat (in $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$). For the solid–liquid transition, $L$ takes the value of the latent heat of fusion $L_f$; for the liquid–gas transition, it takes the value of the latent heat of vaporization $L_v$.

Worked Example. Determine the total heat required to convert $0.500\,\mathrm{kg}$ of ice at $0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ entirely into liquid water at $0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. For water, $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$.

$$ Q = mL_f = (0.500\,\mathrm{kg})(3.34 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}) = 1.67 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J} $$

No temperature change occurs; the $1.67 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J}$ of heat breaks the hydrogen bonds holding the crystalline lattice together, increasing potential energy at fixed kinetic energy.

Key Theorem (Clausius–Clapeyron Relation). The dependence of the phase-transition temperature on pressure is governed by

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T\,\Delta v} $$

where $\Delta v$ is the specific volume change between phases. For vaporization, $\Delta v > 0$, so increasing pressure raises the boiling point—a principle exploited in pressure cookers.

Lab Exercise. Place $200\,\mathrm{g}$ of crushed ice in a calorimeter and supply heat at a constant rate using an immersion heater rated at $50\,\mathrm{W}$. Record temperature every $30\,\mathrm{s}$ and plot a heating curve. Identify the plateau region corresponding to the melting phase change, measure its duration $\Delta t$, and compute the experimental latent heat via $L_f = P\Delta t / m$. Compare your result with the accepted value $3.34 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$ and discuss sources of heat loss.

🍃 Phase Diagram

🍃 Phase Diagram

Definition. A phase diagram is a pressure–temperature ($P$–$T$) graph that delineates the regions of thermodynamic stability for the distinct phases of a pure substance—solid, liquid, and gas. Each region is bounded by coexistence curves along which two phases exist simultaneously in thermodynamic equilibrium. Three special features define every phase diagram: the triple point, where all three phases coexist at a unique pair $(T_t, P_t)$; the critical point $(T_c, P_c)$, beyond which the liquid–gas distinction vanishes; and the sublimation curve, the fusion curve, and the vaporization curve, which separate adjacent phase regions.

Along any coexistence curve the chemical potentials of the two phases are equal, and the slope of each curve is governed by the Clausius–Clapeyron equation:

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T\,\Delta v} $$

where $L$ is the latent heat per mole and $\Delta v = v_2 - v_1$ is the molar volume change between phases.

Worked Example. Consider water at an initial state of $P = 1\text{ atm}$, $T = 110\,^\circ\text{C}$ (superheated steam). Suppose the system is cooled isobarically. On the $P$–$T$ diagram, this corresponds to moving horizontally leftward at constant $P = 1\text{ atm}$. The path crosses the vaporization curve at $T = 100\,^\circ\text{C}$, where condensation to liquid begins. Continued cooling keeps the system in the liquid region until, at $T = 0\,^\circ\text{C}$, the path intersects the fusion curve and freezing commences. The phase diagram immediately reveals which phase transformations occur and at what temperatures, without solving any equations of state.

Key Theorem (Gibbs Phase Rule). For a single-component system, the number of thermodynamic degrees of freedom $F$ satisfies

$$ F = C - P + 2 = 3 - P $$

where $C = 1$ is the number of components and $P$ is the number of coexisting phases. A single-phase region has $F = 2$ (both $T$ and $P$ free), a coexistence curve has $F = 1$ (one variable fixes the other), and the triple point has $F = 0$ (fully determined).

Lab Exercise. Obtain a sealed syringe containing a small sample of a volatile liquid (e.g., ethanol). Record the temperature and note the initial two-phase (liquid–vapor) meniscus. Slowly compress the syringe while monitoring pressure with an attached gauge. Plot your $(T, P)$ measurements on a schematic $P$–$T$ diagram and identify the coexistence curve you are traversing. Estimate the slope $dP/dT$ at two temperature points and use the Clausius–Clapeyron equation to infer the latent heat of vaporization.

🍃 Triple Point

🍃 Triple Point

Definition. The triple point of a pure substance is the unique thermodynamic state—defined by a specific temperature $T_{tp}$ and pressure $P_{tp}$—at which all three phases (solid, liquid, and vapor) coexist in thermodynamic equilibrium simultaneously. At this state, the chemical potential of each phase is equal:

$$ \mu_{\text{solid}}(T_{tp}, P_{tp}) = \mu_{\text{liquid}}(T_{tp}, P_{tp}) = \mu_{\text{vapor}}(T_{tp}, P_{tp}) $$

On a phase diagram, the triple point appears as the single intersection of the sublimation curve, the fusion curve, and the vaporization curve. Unlike boiling or melting points, which vary with applied pressure, the triple point is an intrinsic, invariant property of the substance. For water, the triple point occurs at $T_{tp} = 273.16\,\text{K}$ ($0.01^\circ\text{C}$) and $P_{tp} = 611.73\,\text{Pa}$ ($\approx 0.006\,\text{atm}$), values precise enough that the kelvin was historically defined in terms of this state.

Worked Example. Suppose a sealed container holds water at $T = 274\,\text{K}$ and $P = 700\,\text{Pa}$. Locate this state on the water phase diagram. Since $T > T_{tp}$ and $P > P_{tp}$, the point lies above and to the right of the triple point. Consulting the fusion and vaporization curves for water, this state falls within the liquid region: the water exists entirely as liquid. Now reduce the pressure isothermally to $P = 500\,\text{Pa} < P_{tp}$ while keeping $T = 274\,\text{K}$. The system crosses the vaporization curve and transitions directly into the vapor phase—no liquid persists—because the pressure is now below the triple-point pressure.

Key Theorem (Gibbs Phase Rule). For a single-component system, the number of degrees of freedom $F$ is given by:

$$ F = C - P + 2 = 1 - P + 2 = 3 - P $$

where $P$ is the number of coexisting phases. At the triple point, $P = 3$, so $F = 0$: the system is fully determined with no free variables. This confirms that the triple point is a fixed, unique state.

Lab Exercise. Place a small sample of water in a vacuum chamber equipped with a pressure gauge and thermocouple. Slowly evacuate the chamber while monitoring temperature. Record the pressure and temperature at which you simultaneously observe ice, liquid water, and water vapor coexisting. Compare your measured values to the accepted triple-point values $T_{tp} = 273.16\,\text{K}$ and $P_{tp} = 611.73\,\text{Pa}$, and discuss sources of experimental uncertainty.

🍃 Sublimation

🍃 Sublimation

Definition. Sublimation is the direct phase transition from the solid state to the gaseous state without passing through an intermediate liquid phase. It occurs when the vapor pressure of the solid equals the partial pressure of the surrounding gas, provided the ambient pressure lies below the substance's triple point pressure. Thermodynamically, sublimation requires the system to absorb the enthalpy of sublimation, $\Delta H_\text{sub}$, which equals the sum of the enthalpy of fusion and the enthalpy of vaporization:

$$ \Delta H_\text{sub} = \Delta H_\text{fus} + \Delta H_\text{vap} $$

Because both phase changes are endothermic, $\Delta H_\text{sub}$ is always positive, meaning energy must be supplied from the surroundings. The reverse process—deposition—releases this same energy.

Worked Example. Dry ice (solid $\text{CO}_2$) sublimes at atmospheric pressure ($101.3\ \text{kPa}$) because the triple point of carbon dioxide occurs at $5.11\ \text{atm}$ and $-56.6\ ^\circ\text{C}$. At $1\ \text{atm}$, the solid-gas equilibrium temperature is approximately $-78.5\ ^\circ\text{C}$. Suppose $50\ \text{g}$ of dry ice sublimes completely. Given $\Delta H_\text{sub}(\text{CO}_2) = 571\ \text{kJ/mol}$ and molar mass $M = 44.0\ \text{g/mol}$:

$$ n = \frac{50.0\ \text{g}}{44.0\ \text{g/mol}} = 1.136\ \text{mol} $$ $$ Q = n\,\Delta H_\text{sub} = (1.136\ \text{mol})(571\ \text{kJ/mol}) \approx 649\ \text{kJ} $$

This energy is absorbed from the surroundings, producing a strong cooling effect.

Key Theorem (Clausius–Clapeyron for Sublimation). The temperature dependence of the sublimation vapor pressure $P$ follows:

$$ \frac{d\ln P}{dT} = \frac{\Delta H_\text{sub}}{RT^2} $$

Integrating between temperatures $T_1$ and $T_2$ yields:

$$ \ln\!\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = -\frac{\Delta H_\text{sub}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right) $$

This relation allows determination of $\Delta H_\text{sub}$ from experimental vapor pressure data.

Lab Exercise. Place a small sample of dry ice on a balance inside an insulated enclosure and record mass versus time at room temperature. Plot $\ln P_\text{vap}$ against $1/T$ using tabulated vapor pressure data for $\text{CO}_2$ near $-78\ ^\circ\text{C}$, and extract $\Delta H_\text{sub}$ from the slope. Compare your result with the accepted value and discuss sources of systematic error, including convective heat transfer and balance drift.

🌸 Freeze Drying

🌸 Freeze Drying

Definition. Freeze drying, or lyophilization, is a dehydration process that exploits the phase behavior of water to remove moisture from a substance without passing through the liquid phase. The material is first frozen, reducing water to ice, and then placed in a low-pressure environment where the ice undergoes sublimation — a direct solid-to-vapor transition — driven by the thermodynamic condition that the ambient partial pressure of water vapor is held below the triple-point pressure of water, $P_\text{tp} \approx 611\,\text{Pa}$ at $T_\text{tp} = 273.16\,\text{K}$. Energy for sublimation is supplied as heat, governed by the latent heat of sublimation $L_s \approx 2.83 \times 10^6\,\text{J kg}^{-1}$, which is the sum of the latent heats of fusion and vaporization.

Worked Example. A pharmaceutical vial contains $m = 2.0\,\text{g} = 2.0 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{kg}$ of frozen water at $T = -20\,^\circ\text{C}$. The chamber pressure is maintained at $P = 100\,\text{Pa}$, well below $P_\text{tp}$. Estimate the minimum thermal energy required to completely sublime the ice.

The required energy is

$$ Q = m L_s = (2.0 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{kg})(2.83 \times 10^6\,\text{J kg}^{-1}) = 5.66 \times 10^3\,\text{J} \approx 5.7\,\text{kJ}. $$

In practice, additional energy is needed to first warm the ice from $-20\,^\circ\text{C}$ to $0\,^\circ\text{C}$: $Q_\text{warm} = m c_\text{ice} \Delta T = (2.0 \times 10^{-3})(2090)(20) \approx 84\,\text{J}$, which is negligible compared to $Q$, confirming that sublimation dominates the energy budget.

Key Theorem (Clausius–Clapeyron Relation). The slope of the sublimation curve on the phase diagram is

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L_s P}{R T^2}, $$

where $R = 8.314\,\text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the molar gas constant. This relation governs how the equilibrium sublimation pressure varies with temperature, dictating the precise chamber conditions required at each stage of the drying cycle.

Lab Exercise. Using a vacuum pump and pressure gauge, set the chamber containing an ice sample to three pressures: $800\,\text{Pa}$, $400\,\text{Pa}$, and $100\,\text{Pa}$. Record the sample mass every ten minutes. Plot mass loss rate versus chamber pressure and compare your measured sublimation onset with the Clausius–Clapeyron prediction for the triple-point boundary. Discuss sources of systematic error, including thermal gradients within the sample.

Payoff 🎯

Freeze drying, or lyophilization, represents the convergence of thermodynamic principles, phase equilibria, and heat and mass transfer into a single, elegantly controlled process. At its core, freeze drying achieves what no other drying technique can: the removal of water from a material entirely through sublimation, bypassing the liquid phase and preserving the structural, chemical, and biological integrity of the sample. This outcome is not incidental — it is the direct consequence of operating below the triple point of water, where the phase diagram mandates that ice transitions to vapor without ever becoming liquid.

The triple point of water occurs at a temperature of approximately $T_\text{tp} = 273.16\ \text{K}$ and a pressure of $P_\text{tp} = 611.7\ \text{Pa}$. By reducing the chamber pressure below this value and maintaining the sample in a frozen state, the system is constrained to a region of the $P$-$T$ diagram where only solid and vapor coexist. The driving force for sublimation is the vapor pressure differential between the ice surface and the surrounding chamber, described by the Clausius–Clapeyron relation:

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L_s P}{R T^2} $$

where $L_s$ is the latent heat of sublimation and $R$ is the molar gas constant. Integrating this expression allows engineers to predict sublimation rates and design efficient drying cycles.

Worked Example. Suppose a pharmaceutical sample is frozen to $-40\ ^\circ\text{C}$ and the chamber is evacuated to $10\ \text{Pa}$. Since $10\ \text{Pa} \operatorname{ll} 611.7\ \text{Pa}$, the system lies entirely within the solid–vapor coexistence region. Water molecules at the ice surface acquire sufficient thermal energy to escape into the vapor phase, which is continuously evacuated by the vacuum pump, sustaining the sublimation front.

Key Theorem (Phase Rule). Gibbs's phase rule, $F = C - P + 2$, confirms that for a single-component ($C = 1$) two-phase ($P = 2$) system, one degree of freedom remains. Temperature alone determines the equilibrium vapor pressure, giving precise experimental control.
