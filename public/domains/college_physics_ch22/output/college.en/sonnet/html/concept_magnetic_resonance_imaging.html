🌸 Magnetic Resonance Imaging

🌸 Magnetic Resonance Imaging

Definition. Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a non-invasive diagnostic technique that exploits the quantum mechanical property of nuclear spin to produce detailed images of biological tissue. At its core, MRI relies on nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR): atomic nuclei with nonzero spin possess a magnetic dipole moment $\boldsymbol{\mu} = \gamma \mathbf{J}$, where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio and $\mathbf{J}$ is the nuclear angular momentum. When placed in a static external magnetic field $\mathbf{B}_0$, these moments precess about the field direction at the Larmor frequency:

$$ f_0 = \frac{\gamma B_0}{2\pi} $$

For hydrogen nuclei (protons), $\gamma/2\pi \approx 42.58\ \text{MHz/T}$, making protons the primary target in clinical MRI because of their abundance in water and fat.

Worked Example. A clinical MRI scanner operates at $B_0 = 1.5\ \text{T}$. Determine the Larmor frequency of protons in this field.

$$ f_0 = \frac{\gamma}{2\pi} B_0 = (42.58\ \text{MHz/T})(1.5\ \text{T}) = 63.87\ \text{MHz} $$

This frequency lies in the radio-frequency (RF) band. A transverse RF pulse tuned to $f_0$ tips the net magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ away from $\mathbf{B}_0$. After the pulse is removed, $\mathbf{M}$ relaxes back to equilibrium, emitting a detectable RF signal. Spatial encoding is achieved by superimposing linearly varying gradient fields $\mathbf{G}$, so that the local resonance frequency $f(\mathbf{r}) = (\gamma/2\pi)(B_0 + \mathbf{G}\cdot\mathbf{r})$ encodes position.

Key Theorem — Bloch Equations. The macroscopic magnetization obeys:

$$ \frac{d\mathbf{M}}{dt} = \gamma\,\mathbf{M}\times\mathbf{B} - \frac{M_x\,\hat{x} + M_y\,\hat{y}}{T_2} - \frac{(M_z - M_0)\hat{z}}{T_1} $$

where $T_1$ is the longitudinal (spin-lattice) relaxation time and $T_2$ is the transverse (spin-spin) relaxation time. Tissue contrast in MRI images arises precisely because different tissues have characteristic $(T_1, T_2)$ pairs.

Lab Exercise. Using a benchtop NMR spectrometer set to $B_0 = 0.5\ \text{T}$, acquire free-induction decay (FID) signals from water samples doped with varying concentrations of $\text{MnCl}_2$. Measure $T_2$ for each sample via a Carr-Purcell-Meiboom-Gill (CPMG) pulse sequence, and plot $1/T_2$ versus $[\text{Mn}^{2+}]$ to verify the linear relaxivity relationship $1/T_2 = 1/T_{2,0} + r_2 C$.
