🍃 Magnetic Field Straight Wire

🍃 Magnetic Field Straight Wire

Definition. A long, straight conductor carrying a steady current $I$ produces a magnetic field that encircles the wire in concentric rings. By Ampère's Law, the magnitude of this field at a perpendicular distance $r$ from the wire is

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}, $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space. The field lines are closed loops centered on the wire; the direction of circulation is given by the right-hand rule: wrap the right hand around the wire with the thumb pointing in the direction of conventional current, and the fingers curl in the direction of $\mathbf{B}$.

Proof Sketch. Apply Ampère's circuital law,

$$ \oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}, $$

to a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$ coaxial with the wire. By the cylindrical symmetry of an infinite straight wire, $|\mathbf{B}|$ is constant on $C$ and $\mathbf{B}$ is everywhere tangent to $C$. The line integral reduces to $B(2\pi r)$, and $I_{\text{enc}} = I$, yielding $B = \mu_0 I / (2\pi r)$ directly.

Worked Example. A household extension cord carries $I = 15\ \text{A}$. Find the magnetic field at a point $r = 3.0\ \text{cm} = 0.030\ \text{m}$ from the center of the wire.

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(15)}{2\pi (0.030)} = \frac{4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 15}{2\pi \times 0.030}. $$

Simplifying:

$$ B = \frac{2 \times 10^{-7} \times 15}{0.030} = \frac{3.0 \times 10^{-6}}{0.030} = 1.0 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{T} = 100\ \mu\text{T}. $$

This is roughly twice Earth's surface field ($\approx 50\ \mu\text{T}$), illustrating that ordinary currents generate fields of measurable magnitude at close range.

Key Theorem (Superposition). For a system of $n$ parallel wires carrying currents $I_1, I_2, \ldots, I_n$, the net magnetic field at any point is the vector sum $\mathbf{B}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{k=1}^n \mathbf{B}_k$, where each $\mathbf{B}_k$ obeys the single-wire formula above.

Lab Exercise. Set up a vertical wire carrying a DC current between $5\ \text{A}$ and $20\ \text{A}$. Using a calibrated Hall-effect sensor, measure $B$ at distances $r = 1, 2, 3, 4,$ and $5\ \text{cm}$ from the wire's axis. Plot $B$ versus $1/r$ and determine the slope; compare it to the theoretical value $\mu_0 I / (2\pi)$ and calculate the percent deviation for each data point.
