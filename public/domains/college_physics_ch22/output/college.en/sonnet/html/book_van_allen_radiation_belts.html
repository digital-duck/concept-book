🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: every observable charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e \approx 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb (SI unit). Matter carries two polarities—positive and negative—and the algebraic sum of charge in an isolated system is conserved absolutely. The proton carries charge $+e$, the electron $-e$, and the neutron zero net charge. Macroscopic objects become charged through transfer of electrons (or, in ionic solutions, ions), never through creation or destruction of charge.

Worked Example. A glass rod is rubbed with silk. Suppose $3.0 \times 10^{10}$ electrons are transferred from the glass to the silk. Find the charge acquired by each object.

The charge transferred equals:

$$ q = n \cdot e = (3.0 \times 10^{10})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}) \approx 4.8\ \mu\text{C} $$

The silk gains $-4.8\ \mu\text{C}$ (excess electrons), and the glass rod acquires $+4.8\ \mu\text{C}$ (electron deficit). Note that the total charge of the glass-silk system is zero before and after rubbing, confirming charge conservation.

Key Theorem — Coulomb's Law. The electrostatic force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is:

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\,\hat{r}_{12}, \qquad k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \approx 8.988 \times 10^{9}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2 $$

Like charges ($q_1 q_2 > 0$) repel; unlike charges ($q_1 q_2 < 0$) attract. The force obeys Newton's third law: $\vec{F}_{12} = -\vec{F}_{21}$.

Lab Exercise. Hang two small conducting spheres of equal mass $m = 0.50\ \text{g}$ on insulating threads of length $L = 30\ \text{cm}$ from the same point. After charging both spheres identically, they deflect to an equilibrium half-angle $\theta$ from vertical. Measure $\theta$ and show that the charge on each sphere is:

$$ q = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi\epsilon_0 m g \tan\theta \cdot (2L\sin\theta)^2}{1}} $$

Evaluate $q$ for $\theta = 5.0°$ and discuss sources of experimental uncertainty.

🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the time rate of change of displacement. For a particle whose position vector is $\mathbf{r}(t)$, the instantaneous velocity is defined as the limit of the average velocity as the time interval shrinks to zero:

$$ \mathbf{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \mathbf{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} $$

Velocity is a vector quantity possessing both magnitude and direction. The magnitude $|\mathbf{v}|$ is called the speed, measured in SI units of meters per second (m/s). Unlike displacement, velocity encodes the instantaneous state of motion—not merely where a particle has been, but how it is moving at each moment.

Worked Example. A charged particle moves along the $x$-axis with position given by $x(t) = 3t^2 - 2t + 1$ (in meters, with $t$ in seconds). Find the velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$.

Differentiating with respect to time:

$$ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 2 $$

Evaluating at $t = 2\,\text{s}$:

$$ v(2) = 6(2) - 2 = 10\,\text{m/s} $$

The positive sign confirms motion in the positive $x$-direction at that instant. Note that setting $v(t) = 0$ gives $t = 1/3\,\text{s}$, identifying the turning point where the particle momentarily reverses direction.

Key Theorem — Kinematic Velocity Relation. For an object undergoing constant acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ from initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$, integration of $\mathbf{a} = d\mathbf{v}/dt$ directly yields:

$$ \mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{v}_0 + \mathbf{a}t $$

This result, derived rigorously from the definition of velocity as a derivative, is the foundation of kinematic analysis in mechanics and electromagnetism. It remains valid component-wise in two and three dimensions, where $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{v}_0$, and $\mathbf{a}$ are treated as independent vector equations along each coordinate axis.

Lab Exercise. A steel ball is released from rest on an inclined air track (inclination angle $\theta = 5°$). Compute the average velocity over each interval, then extrapolate to estimate the instantaneous velocity at $t = 0.3\,\text{s}$. 🍃 Magnetic Field

🍃 Magnetic Field

Definition. A magnetic field is a vector field $\mathbf{B}$ that permeates space in the vicinity of magnetic sources—moving charges, electric currents, or magnetized materials—and exerts forces on other moving charges. The SI unit of magnetic field strength is the tesla (T), where $1\,\text{T} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{s}^{-2}\cdot\text{A}^{-1}$. The magnetic force on a charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a field $\mathbf{B}$ is given by the Lorentz force law:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} $$

This force is always perpendicular to both $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, doing no work on the particle and therefore changing its direction but not its speed. Magnetic field lines are closed loops (divergence-free: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$), emerging from north poles and entering south poles by convention.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, $m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$) moves with velocity $\mathbf{v} = 3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}\,\text{m/s}$ into a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}\,\text{T}$.

The magnetic force is:

$$ \mathbf{F} = qv B\,(\hat{x} \times \hat{z}) = (1.602 \times 10^{-19})(3.0 \times 10^6)(0.50)\,(-\hat{y}) $$ $$ \mathbf{F} \approx -2.4 \times 10^{-13}\,\hat{y}\,\text{N} $$

The proton curves in the $xy$-plane with radius:

$$ r = \frac{mv}{|q|B} = \frac{(1.673 \times 10^{-27})(3.0 \times 10^6)}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})(0.50)} \approx 0.063\,\text{m} = 6.3\,\text{cm} $$

Key Theorem (Biot–Savart Law). The magnetic field produced by a steady current $I$ flowing through an infinitesimal element $d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ at position $\mathbf{r}'$ is:

$$ d\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{I\,d\boldsymbol{\ell} \times \hat{r}}{r^2} $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\text{T}\cdot\text{m/A}$ is the permeability of free space and $\hat{r}$ points from the source element to the field point. Integrating over a complete circuit yields the total field $\mathbf{B}$.

Plot $B$ versus axial position, compare with the theoretical prediction $B = \mu_0 n I$ at the center (where $n$ is turn density), and compute the percentage deviation. 🌱 Centripetal Force

🌱 Centripetal Force

Definition. When an object moves along a circular path, its velocity vector continuously changes direction, which constitutes acceleration even if the speed remains constant. This acceleration—called centripetal acceleration—always points radially inward, toward the center of the circular path. By Newton's second law, a net force must produce this acceleration. Centripetal force is defined as the net inward force required to maintain circular motion:

$$ F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = m\omega^2 r $$

where $m$ is the mass of the object, $v$ is its tangential speed, $r$ is the radius of the circular path, and $\omega$ is the angular velocity. Centripetal force is not a new fundamental force; rather, it is the name given to whatever real force (or vector sum of forces) acts centripetally in a given situation—tension, gravity, normal force, friction, or some combination thereof.

Worked Example. A car of mass $1200\ \text{kg}$ rounds a flat, unbanked curve of radius $r = 80\ \text{m}$ at a constant speed of $v = 20\ \text{m/s}$. Determine the minimum coefficient of static friction $\mu_s$ required to prevent skidding.

On a flat road, static friction is the sole source of centripetal force. Setting the frictional force equal to the centripetal requirement:

$$ \mu_s mg = \frac{mv^2}{r} $$ $$ \mu_s = \frac{v^2}{rg} = \frac{(20)^2}{(80)(9.8)} = \frac{400}{784} \approx 0.51 $$

A coefficient below this value would cause the car to slide outward.

Key Theorem. For uniform circular motion, the centripetal acceleration magnitude is

$$ a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} $$

This result follows directly from the geometric fact that, for a small arc subtended by angle $d\theta$, the change in velocity vector has magnitude $|d\mathbf{v}| = v\,d\theta$, giving $a_c = v\,d\theta/dt = v\omega = v^2/r$.

Lab Exercise. Attach a known mass $m$ to a string of length $r$ and swing it in a horizontal circle, measuring the period $T$ with a stopwatch. Compute the experimental centripetal force as $F_c = 4\pi^2 mr/T^2$ and compare it to the tension measured with an in-line spring scale. Investigate how $F_c$ scales with $r$ and $T$ by repeating the trial at three different radii and speeds.

🍃 Lorentz Force

🍃 Lorentz Force

Definition. When a charged particle moves through a region containing both electric and magnetic fields, it experiences a combined electromagnetic force known as the Lorentz force. For a particle with charge $q$, velocity $\mathbf{v}$, electric field $\mathbf{E}$, and magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, the Lorentz force is given by:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) $$

The electric component $q\mathbf{E}$ acts parallel to the field and does work on the particle, altering its kinetic energy. The magnetic component $q(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})$ always acts perpendicular to the velocity; consequently, it does no work and cannot change the particle's speed — only its direction. The magnitude of the magnetic force alone is $F = |q|vB\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C, $m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg) moves with velocity $\mathbf{v} = 3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}$ m/s into a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}$ T, with no electric field present. The magnetic force is:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} = (1.60 \times 10^{-19})(3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}) \times (0.50\,\hat{z}) $$

Using $\hat{x} \times \hat{z} = -\hat{y}$:

$$ \mathbf{F} = (1.60 \times 10^{-19})(1.5 \times 10^6)(-\hat{y}) = -2.4 \times 10^{-13}\,\hat{y}\text{ N} $$

The proton deflects in the $-y$ direction. Because $\mathbf{F} \perp \mathbf{v}$, the proton undergoes uniform circular motion with radius $r = mv/(|q|B) = (1.67 \times 10^{-27})(3.0 \times 10^6)/[(1.60 \times 10^{-19})(0.50)] \approx 0.063$ m.

Key Theorem. A particle of charge $q$ and mass $m$ moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ undergoes uniform circular motion with cyclotron radius $r = mv/(|q|B)$ and cyclotron frequency $\omega_c = |q|B/m$, independent of the particle's speed.

Lab Exercise. An electron enters a region with $\mathbf{E} = 4.0 \times 10^4\,\hat{y}$ V/m and $\mathbf{B} = 0.20\,\hat{z}$ T. 🍃 Circular Motion In Magnetic Field

🍃 Circular Motion In Magnetic Field

Definition. When a charged particle moves through a uniform magnetic field with its velocity perpendicular to the field, the magnetic force acts as a centripetal force, constraining the particle to circular motion. For a particle of charge $q$, mass $m$, and speed $v$ moving in a field $\mathbf{B}$, the magnetic force is

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}. $$

Since this force is always perpendicular to $\mathbf{v}$, it does no work and cannot change the particle's speed—only its direction. Setting the magnitude of the magnetic force equal to the centripetal force required for circular motion:

$$ |q|vB = \frac{mv^2}{r}, $$

which yields the cyclotron radius (or Larmor radius):

$$ r = \frac{mv}{|q|B}. $$

The period of revolution is independent of speed:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi m}{|q|B}, $$

and the corresponding cyclotron frequency is $f = 1/T = |q|B/(2\pi m)$.

Worked Example. A proton ($m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$, $q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$) enters a uniform magnetic field $B = 0.500\ \text{T}$ perpendicular to the field lines with speed $v = 2.00 \times 10^6\ \text{m/s}$. Find the radius of its circular path and the cyclotron frequency.

$$ r = \frac{mv}{|q|B} = \frac{(1.67\times10^{-27})(2.00\times10^6)}{(1.60\times10^{-19})(0.500)} = 4.18 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{m} \approx 4.18\ \text{cm}. $$ $$ f = \frac{|q|B}{2\pi m} = \frac{(1.60\times10^{-19})(0.500)}{2\pi(1.67\times10^{-27})} \approx 7.64\ \text{MHz}. $$

Key Theorem. The cyclotron frequency $f = |q|B/(2\pi m)$ depends only on the charge-to-mass ratio $q/m$ and the field strength $B$, not on the particle's speed. This isochronous property is the operating principle of the cyclotron accelerator: a fixed-frequency alternating voltage can continuously accelerate particles because their orbital period remains constant even as their energy—and hence orbital radius—increases.

Lab Exercise. Using an electron beam tube in a uniform magnetic field, measure the deflection radius of electrons accelerated through a known potential difference $V$. From $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = eV$ and $r = mv/(eB)$, determine the electron charge-to-mass ratio $e/m$ and compare your result with the accepted value $1.76 \times 10^{11}\ \text{C/kg}$.

🍃 Magnetic Mirror

🍃 Magnetic Mirror

Definition. A magnetic mirror is a configuration of non-uniform magnetic field in which the field strength increases toward both ends of a region, creating a potential barrier that can reflect charged particles back toward weaker-field zones. The underlying mechanism is the conservation of two adiabatic invariants: the magnetic moment $\mu = \frac{mv_\perp^2}{2B}$ and the total kinetic energy $K = \frac{1}{2}m(v_\perp^2 + v_\parallel^2)$, where $v_\perp$ and $v_\parallel$ are the speed components perpendicular and parallel to the field, respectively. Because $\mu$ is conserved as the particle drifts into a region of increasing $B$, the perpendicular energy $\frac{1}{2}mv_\perp^2 = \mu B$ must grow, and since total energy is fixed, $v_\parallel$ must decrease. If the field is strong enough, $v_\parallel$ reaches zero and the particle is reflected.

Reflection condition. A particle with pitch angle $\theta_0$ (the angle between $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ at the midplane where $B = B_0$) is reflected before reaching a mirror point where $B = B_m$ if and only if

$$ \sin^2\theta_0 \geq \frac{B_0}{B_m}. $$

Particles with $\theta_0$ smaller than the critical angle $\theta_c = \arcsin\!\sqrt{B_0/B_m}$ fall within the loss cone and escape.

Worked example. A proton gyrates in a magnetic bottle with $B_0 = 0.10\ \text{T}$ at the midplane and mirror-point field $B_m = 0.80\ \text{T}$. At the midplane the proton has $v_\perp = 2.0\times10^6\ \text{m/s}$ and $v_\parallel = 1.0\times10^6\ \text{m/s}$, giving $\sin^2\theta_0 = v_\perp^2/(v_\perp^2+v_\parallel^2) = 4/5 = 0.80$. Since $B_0/B_m = 0.10/0.80 = 0.125 < 0.80$, the condition is satisfied and the proton is reflected. The mirror-point field required to just reflect a particle with $\sin^2\theta_0 = 0.80$ is $B_m = B_0/0.80 = 0.125\ \text{T}$, well below the actual mirror field.

Key theorem (adiabatic invariance). For a slowly varying field ($|\nabla B|/B \operatorname{ll} 1/r_g$, where $r_g$ is the gyroradius), the magnetic moment $\mu = mv_\perp^2/(2B)$ is an adiabatic invariant conserved to all orders in the expansion parameter, establishing the rigorous foundation for the mirror reflection argument.

🌸 Van Allen Radiation Belts

🌸 Van Allen Radiation Belts

Definition. The Van Allen radiation belts are two concentric, donut-shaped regions of trapped energetic charged particles surrounding Earth, held in place by the planet's magnetic field. Discovered by James Van Allen in 1958 using data from Explorer 1, the inner belt extends from approximately $1.2$ to $3$ Earth radii ($R_E$) and contains primarily protons with energies exceeding $100\,\text{MeV}$, while the outer belt spans roughly $3$ to $9\,R_E$ and consists mainly of electrons with energies up to several $\text{MeV}$. Particle trapping occurs because a charged particle moving through a magnetic field experiences the Lorentz force

$$ \vec{F} = q\vec{v} \times \vec{B}, $$

which causes the particle to spiral along magnetic field lines in helical paths. The radius of this helical motion, called the cyclotron radius or Larmor radius, is

$$ r_L = \frac{mv_\perp}{|q|B}, $$

where $v_\perp$ is the component of velocity perpendicular to $\vec{B}$.

Worked Example. Consider a proton ($m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$, $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$) moving with $v_\perp = 1.0 \times 10^7\,\text{m/s}$ perpendicular to Earth's magnetic field at the inner belt, where $B \approx 1.0 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{T}$. The Larmor radius is

$$ r_L = \frac{(1.67 \times 10^{-27})(1.0 \times 10^7)}{(1.6 \times 10^{-19})(1.0 \times 10^{-5})} = \frac{1.67 \times 10^{-20}}{1.6 \times 10^{-24}} \approx 1.04 \times 10^4\,\text{m} = 10.4\,\text{km}. $$

This compact spiral confines the proton tightly to a field line, illustrating how particles remain trapped over long timescales.

Key Theorem (Magnetic Mirror Effect). As a trapped particle drifts toward Earth's magnetic poles, the field lines converge and $B$ increases. By conservation of the magnetic moment $\mu = \frac{mv_\perp^2}{2B} = \text{const}$, the perpendicular kinetic energy must increase, so the parallel component decreases. If $B$ increases sufficiently, the particle reflects back — a phenomenon called magnetic mirroring — causing particles to bounce repeatedly between the northern and southern hemispheres.

Payoff 🎯

The Van Allen Radiation Belts represent the natural culmination of Chapter 22's treatment of magnetism and charged-particle dynamics. Every concept developed in the preceding sections — the Lorentz force law, circular motion of charges in uniform fields, magnetic mirrors, and the behavior of plasmas — converges here in a single, physically real structure that encircles Earth. Understanding the belts is not merely an exercise in applied magnetism; it is the proof that the abstract machinery of electromagnetism governs phenomena at planetary scale.

What the Van Allen Belts achieve. Earth's dipole magnetic field, approximated near the equatorial plane by $B \approx B_0 (R_E/r)^3$, creates a topology in which charged particles undergo three simultaneous periodic motions: gyration about a field line with cyclotron radius $r_c = mv_\perp / qB$, bounce between magnetic mirror points where $B$ increases toward the poles, and slow azimuthal drift around Earth due to gradient and curvature forces. The combined invariant structure traps protons (inner belt, $\sim 1$–$2\, R_E$) and electrons (outer belt, $\sim 3$–$7\, R_E$) for timescales ranging from minutes to years. The mirror condition $\sin^2\alpha_0 = B_0/B_m$ ties the loss cone directly to the particle's pitch angle $\alpha_0$ at the equator, giving a precise, quantitative criterion for confinement.

Connections to application domains. The belts are the primary hazard environment for spacecraft electronics; radiation-hardening specifications derive directly from particle flux models ($J \sim 10^4$–$10^7\ \text{particles cm}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1}$) computed from the same drift-shell theory developed here. Space weather forecasting relies on monitoring belt dynamics during geomagnetic storms, when energetic electrons are injected from the magnetotail and the outer belt can intensify by orders of magnitude in hours. In nuclear fusion research, the magnetic mirror concept — born from belt physics — informs the design of tandem mirror confinement devices. Auroral physics, too, is a direct descendant: particles lost through the loss cone precipitate along field lines into the ionosphere, producing the characteristic optical emissions.
