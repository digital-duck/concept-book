🍃 Magnetic Field Circular Loop

🍃 Magnetic Field Circular Loop

Definition. A circular current loop is one of the fundamental configurations for generating a magnetic field. When a steady current $I$ flows through a circular loop of radius $R$, the magnetic field at the center of the loop is given by the Biot–Savart law integrated over the full circumference. Each infinitesimal element $d\vec{\ell}$ of the loop contributes a field element

$$ d\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \frac{d\vec{\ell} \times \hat{r}}{r^2}, $$

where $\hat{r}$ is the unit vector from the current element to the field point and $r = R$ for all elements at the center. Because every element is tangential and the displacement to the center is radial, the cross product $|d\vec{\ell} \times \hat{r}| = d\ell$ everywhere, and all contributions point in the same direction (along the loop axis). Integrating over the full circumference $2\pi R$ yields

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R}. $$

The direction is determined by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of current flow, and the thumb points in the direction of $\vec{B}$ at the center.

Worked Example. A circular loop of radius $R = 0.05\,\text{m}$ carries a current $I = 3.0\,\text{A}$. Find the magnetic field at the center.

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\text{T·m/A})(3.0\,\text{A})}{2(0.05\,\text{m})} = \frac{1.257 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{T·m}}{0.10\,\text{m}} \approx 3.77 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{T}. $$

The field points perpendicular to the plane of the loop, in the direction given by the right-hand rule.

Key Theorem. For a flat coil of $N$ turns, the total field at the center scales linearly with turn count:

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 N I}{2R}. $$

This superposition principle follows directly from the linearity of the Biot–Savart law and forms the operating basis of Helmholtz coils used to create uniform magnetic field regions in laboratory settings.

Lab Exercise. Using a 50-turn coil of radius $R = 0.10\,\text{m}$, determine the current $I$ required to produce a central field of $B = 1.0\,\text{mT}$. Verify your result by connecting the coil to a calibrated power supply and measuring the field with a Hall probe at the geometric center. Compare your measured value to the theoretical prediction and calculate the percent error.
