🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: every observable charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e \approx 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb (SI unit). Matter carries two polarities—positive and negative—and the algebraic sum of charge in an isolated system is conserved absolutely. The proton carries charge $+e$, the electron $-e$, and the neutron zero net charge. Macroscopic objects become charged through transfer of electrons (or, in ionic solutions, ions), never through creation or destruction of charge.

Worked Example. A glass rod is rubbed with silk. Suppose $3.0 \times 10^{10}$ electrons are transferred from the glass to the silk. Find the charge acquired by each object.

The charge transferred equals:

$$ q = n \cdot e = (3.0 \times 10^{10})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}) \approx 4.8\ \mu\text{C} $$

The silk gains $-4.8\ \mu\text{C}$ (excess electrons), and the glass rod acquires $+4.8\ \mu\text{C}$ (electron deficit). Note that the total charge of the glass-silk system is zero before and after rubbing, confirming charge conservation.

Key Theorem — Coulomb's Law. The electrostatic force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is:

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\,\hat{r}_{12}, \qquad k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \approx 8.988 \times 10^{9}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2 $$

Like charges ($q_1 q_2 > 0$) repel; unlike charges ($q_1 q_2 < 0$) attract. The force obeys Newton's third law: $\vec{F}_{12} = -\vec{F}_{21}$.

Lab Exercise. Hang two small conducting spheres of equal mass $m = 0.50\ \text{g}$ on insulating threads of length $L = 30\ \text{cm}$ from the same point. After charging both spheres identically, they deflect to an equilibrium half-angle $\theta$ from vertical. Measure $\theta$ and show that the charge on each sphere is:

$$ q = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi\epsilon_0 m g \tan\theta \cdot (2L\sin\theta)^2}{1}} $$

Evaluate $q$ for $\theta = 5.0°$ and discuss sources of experimental uncertainty.

🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the time rate of change of displacement. For a particle whose position vector is $\mathbf{r}(t)$, the instantaneous velocity is defined as the limit of the average velocity as the time interval shrinks to zero:

$$ \mathbf{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \mathbf{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} $$

Velocity is a vector quantity possessing both magnitude and direction. The magnitude $|\mathbf{v}|$ is called the speed, measured in SI units of meters per second (m/s). Unlike displacement, velocity encodes the instantaneous state of motion—not merely where a particle has been, but how it is moving at each moment.

Worked Example. A charged particle moves along the $x$-axis with position given by $x(t) = 3t^2 - 2t + 1$ (in meters, with $t$ in seconds). Find the velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$.

Differentiating with respect to time:

$$ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 2 $$

Evaluating at $t = 2\,\text{s}$:

$$ v(2) = 6(2) - 2 = 10\,\text{m/s} $$

The positive sign confirms motion in the positive $x$-direction at that instant. Note that setting $v(t) = 0$ gives $t = 1/3\,\text{s}$, identifying the turning point where the particle momentarily reverses direction.

Key Theorem — Kinematic Velocity Relation. For an object undergoing constant acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ from initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$, integration of $\mathbf{a} = d\mathbf{v}/dt$ directly yields:

$$ \mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{v}_0 + \mathbf{a}t $$

This result, derived rigorously from the definition of velocity as a derivative, is the foundation of kinematic analysis in mechanics and electromagnetism. It remains valid component-wise in two and three dimensions, where $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{v}_0$, and $\mathbf{a}$ are treated as independent vector equations along each coordinate axis.

Compute the average velocity over each interval, then extrapolate to estimate the instantaneous velocity at $t = 0.3\,\text{s}$. Compare your result to the theoretical prediction $v = g\sin\theta \cdot t$ and quantify the percent deviation. Discuss sources of systematic error, including friction and timing resolution.

🌱 Electric Current

🌱 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional surface. If a charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface during a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limit

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of current is taken as the direction of positive charge flow, even when the actual charge carriers are negatively charged electrons moving in the opposite direction.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries a steady current such that $3.6 \times 10^{20}$ electrons pass through a cross-section every $30\,\text{s}$. Find the magnitude of the current.

The total charge transferred is

$$ \Delta Q = N \cdot e = (3.6 \times 10^{20})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}) = 57.67\,\text{C} $$

Therefore,

$$ I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} = \frac{57.67\,\text{C}}{30\,\text{s}} \approx 1.92\,\text{A} $$

Since electrons are the carriers, they move opposite to the conventional current direction, but the magnitude of the current is $1.92\,\text{A}$.

Key Theorem: Current Continuity. For a steady-state (DC) circuit with no charge accumulation at any node, the algebraic sum of currents entering any junction equals zero:

$$ \sum_k I_k = 0 $$

This is Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL), a direct consequence of conservation of charge. It implies that in a simple series circuit, the current is identical at every cross-section, regardless of variations in wire geometry or material—a result that can be derived formally from the continuity equation $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = -\partial\rho/\partial t = 0$ under steady-state conditions, where $\mathbf{J}$ is the current density.

🍃 Magnetic Field

🍃 Magnetic Field

Definition. A magnetic field is a vector field $\mathbf{B}$ that permeates space in the vicinity of magnetic sources—moving charges, electric currents, or magnetized materials—and exerts forces on other moving charges. The SI unit of magnetic field strength is the tesla (T), where $1\,\text{T} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{s}^{-2}\cdot\text{A}^{-1}$. The magnetic force on a charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a field $\mathbf{B}$ is given by the Lorentz force law:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} $$

This force is always perpendicular to both $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, doing no work on the particle and therefore changing its direction but not its speed. Magnetic field lines are closed loops (divergence-free: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$), emerging from north poles and entering south poles by convention.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, $m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$) moves with velocity $\mathbf{v} = 3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}\,\text{m/s}$ into a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}\,\text{T}$.

The magnetic force is:

$$ \mathbf{F} = qv B\,(\hat{x} \times \hat{z}) = (1.602 \times 10^{-19})(3.0 \times 10^6)(0.50)\,(-\hat{y}) $$ $$ \mathbf{F} \approx -2.4 \times 10^{-13}\,\hat{y}\,\text{N} $$

The proton curves in the $xy$-plane with radius:

$$ r = \frac{mv}{|q|B} = \frac{(1.673 \times 10^{-27})(3.0 \times 10^6)}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})(0.50)} \approx 0.063\,\text{m} = 6.3\,\text{cm} $$

Key Theorem (Biot–Savart Law). The magnetic field produced by a steady current $I$ flowing through an infinitesimal element $d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ at position $\mathbf{r}'$ is:

$$ d\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{I\,d\boldsymbol{\ell} \times \hat{r}}{r^2} $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\text{T}\cdot\text{m/A}$ is the permeability of free space and $\hat{r}$ points from the source element to the field point. Integrating over a complete circuit yields the total field $\mathbf{B}$.

Plot $B$ versus axial position, compare with the theoretical prediction $B = \mu_0 n I$ at the center (where $n$ is turn density), and compute the percentage deviation. Identify and explain the field fall-off near the solenoid ends.

🍃 Permeability Of Free Space

🍃 Permeability Of Free Space

Definition. The permeability of free space, denoted $\mu_0$, is a fundamental physical constant that quantifies the ability of a vacuum to support the formation of a magnetic field. It appears in Ampère's law and the Biot–Savart law as the proportionality constant relating electric currents to the magnetic fields they produce. Its defined value in SI units is

$$ \mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A} \approx 1.2566 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{T·m/A}. $$

Physically, $\mu_0$ sets the scale of magnetic interactions in free space, playing a role analogous to the permittivity of free space $\varepsilon_0$ in electrostatics. Together they are related to the speed of light by $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}$.

Worked Example. A long, straight wire carries a steady current $I = 5.0 \, \text{A}$. Find the magnetic field strength at a perpendicular distance $r = 0.10 \, \text{m}$ from the wire.

Applying Ampère's law to a circular path of radius $r$ centered on the wire:

$$ \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}. $$

By symmetry, $B$ is constant along the loop and parallel to $d\boldsymbol{\ell}$, so

$$ B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I \implies B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}. $$

Substituting values:

$$ B = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(5.0)}{2\pi (0.10)} = \frac{2.0 \times 10^{-6}}{0.20} = 1.0 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{T}. $$

Key Theorem. The Biot–Savart law states that the infinitesimal magnetic field $d\mathbf{B}$ produced by a current element $I \, d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ at displacement $\mathbf{r}$ is

$$ d\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{I \, d\boldsymbol{\ell} \times \hat{r}}{r^2}. $$

This result, combined with the superposition principle, enables calculation of magnetic fields for arbitrary current distributions. The constant $\mu_0/4\pi = 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A}$ is its rationalized form.

Lab Exercise. Using a calibrated Hall probe, measure the magnetic field at several radial distances from a long wire carrying a known current. Plot $B$ versus $1/r$ and determine the slope. From the slope and the known current, extract an experimental value of $\mu_0$ and compare it to the defined constant, computing the percent error.

🍃 Right Hand Rule 2

🍃 Right Hand Rule 2

Definition

Right Hand Rule 2 (RHR-2) is a mnemonic procedure for determining the direction of the magnetic field produced by a current-carrying conductor. Given a long, straight wire carrying conventional current $I$ in a specified direction, the magnetic field lines form concentric circles around the wire. To apply RHR-2: point the right thumb in the direction of the conventional current; the curled fingers then indicate the direction in which the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ circulates around the wire. This rule is a direct consequence of the Biot–Savart Law and Ampère's Law, both of which establish that the magnetic field of a straight wire is azimuthal and obeys

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}, $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space and $r$ is the perpendicular distance from the wire.

Worked Example

A vertical wire carries a current of $5.0\ \text{A}$ directed upward. Determine the direction and magnitude of $\vec{B}$ at a point $P$ located $0.10\ \text{m}$ directly to the east of the wire.

Direction: Point the right thumb upward (direction of $I$). The curled fingers at point $P$ (to the east) curl from north, through east, toward south. At the eastern point specifically, the fingers point south. Therefore, $\vec{B}$ points southward at $P$.

Magnitude:

$$ B = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(5.0)}{2\pi (0.10)} = \frac{2.0 \times 10^{-6}}{0.628} \approx 1.0 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{T}. $$

Key Theorem (Ampère's Law)

For any closed Amperian loop encircling a net current $I_{\text{enc}}$:

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}. $$

For a circular path of radius $r$ centered on a long straight wire, symmetry demands $B$ is constant along the path, yielding $B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I$, which recovers the formula above and confirms that RHR-2 correctly identifies the circulation sense.

Lab Exercise

Set up a long vertical wire connected to a DC power supply. Using a compass placed at several positions around the wire (north, south, east, west at $r = 5\ \text{cm}$), record the compass needle orientation at each location. Predict each orientation using RHR-2 before observing, then compare. Reverse the current direction and repeat. Quantify $B$ at each position using the formula and compare with a Hall probe measurement.

🍃 Magnetic Field Straight Wire

🍃 Magnetic Field Straight Wire

Definition. A long, straight conductor carrying a steady current $I$ produces a magnetic field that encircles the wire in concentric rings. By Ampère's Law, the magnitude of this field at a perpendicular distance $r$ from the wire is

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}, $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space. The field lines are closed loops centered on the wire; the direction of circulation is given by the right-hand rule: wrap the right hand around the wire with the thumb pointing in the direction of conventional current, and the fingers curl in the direction of $\mathbf{B}$.

Proof Sketch. Apply Ampère's circuital law,

$$ \oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}, $$

to a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$ coaxial with the wire. By the cylindrical symmetry of an infinite straight wire, $|\mathbf{B}|$ is constant on $C$ and $\mathbf{B}$ is everywhere tangent to $C$. The line integral reduces to $B(2\pi r)$, and $I_{\text{enc}} = I$, yielding $B = \mu_0 I / (2\pi r)$ directly.

Worked Example. A household extension cord carries $I = 15\ \text{A}$. Find the magnetic field at a point $r = 3.0\ \text{cm} = 0.030\ \text{m}$ from the center of the wire.

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(15)}{2\pi (0.030)} = \frac{4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 15}{2\pi \times 0.030}. $$

Simplifying:

$$ B = \frac{2 \times 10^{-7} \times 15}{0.030} = \frac{3.0 \times 10^{-6}}{0.030} = 1.0 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{T} = 100\ \mu\text{T}. $$

This is roughly twice Earth's surface field ($\approx 50\ \mu\text{T}$), illustrating that ordinary currents generate fields of measurable magnitude at close range.

Key Theorem (Superposition). For a system of $n$ parallel wires carrying currents $I_1, I_2, \ldots, I_n$, the net magnetic field at any point is the vector sum $\mathbf{B}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{k=1}^n \mathbf{B}_k$, where each $\mathbf{B}_k$ obeys the single-wire formula above.

🍃 Amperes Law

🍃 Amperes Law

Definition. Ampère's Law relates the magnetic field circulating around a closed loop to the net electric current passing through any surface bounded by that loop. In its integral form, the law states:

$$ \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = \mu_0 \, I_{\text{enc}} $$

where $\oint_{\mathcal{C}} \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell}$ is the line integral of the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ around a closed Amperian path $\mathcal{C}$, $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space, and $I_{\text{enc}}$ is the total current enclosed by (passing through the surface bounded by) $\mathcal{C}$. The direction convention follows the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of integration; the thumb points in the positive current direction.

Worked Example. Find the magnetic field at a perpendicular distance $r$ from an infinitely long straight wire carrying current $I$.

Choose a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$ centered on the wire and lying in a plane perpendicular to it. By the symmetry of the configuration, $\vec{B}$ is tangential and constant in magnitude everywhere on this circle. Therefore:

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = B \oint d\ell = B (2\pi r) $$

The enclosed current is simply $I$, so Ampère's Law gives:

$$ B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I \implies B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$

This result confirms the $1/r$ field dependence for a long straight conductor.

Key Theorem. Ampère's Law holds for any closed path and any current distribution, provided all currents are steady (magnetostatics). For a toroid with $N$ turns, total current $I$, and mean radius $r$, the field inside the toroid is:

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 N I}{2\pi r} $$

Outside the toroid, perfect cancellation yields $B = 0$, a result that follows directly from choosing an exterior Amperian loop enclosing zero net current.

🍃 Magnetic Field Circular Loop

🍃 Magnetic Field Circular Loop

Definition. A circular current loop is one of the fundamental configurations for generating a magnetic field. When a steady current $I$ flows through a circular loop of radius $R$, the magnetic field at the center of the loop is given by the Biot–Savart law integrated over the full circumference. Each infinitesimal element $d\vec{\ell}$ of the loop contributes a field element

$$ d\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \frac{d\vec{\ell} \times \hat{r}}{r^2}, $$

where $\hat{r}$ is the unit vector from the current element to the field point and $r = R$ for all elements at the center. Because every element is tangential and the displacement to the center is radial, the cross product $|d\vec{\ell} \times \hat{r}| = d\ell$ everywhere, and all contributions point in the same direction (along the loop axis). Integrating over the full circumference $2\pi R$ yields

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R}. $$

The direction is determined by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of current flow, and the thumb points in the direction of $\vec{B}$ at the center.

Worked Example. A circular loop of radius $R = 0.05\,\text{m}$ carries a current $I = 3.0\,\text{A}$. Find the magnetic field at the center.

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\text{T·m/A})(3.0\,\text{A})}{2(0.05\,\text{m})} = \frac{1.257 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{T·m}}{0.10\,\text{m}} \approx 3.77 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{T}. $$

The field points perpendicular to the plane of the loop, in the direction given by the right-hand rule.

Key Theorem. For a flat coil of $N$ turns, the total field at the center scales linearly with turn count:

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 N I}{2R}. $$

This superposition principle follows directly from the linearity of the Biot–Savart law and forms the operating basis of Helmholtz coils used to create uniform magnetic field regions in laboratory settings.

🍃 Solenoid

🍃 Solenoid

Definition. A solenoid is a helical coil of wire wound uniformly around a cylindrical form, designed so that when carrying a steady current $I$ it produces a nearly uniform magnetic field confined within its interior. For an ideal solenoid of length $L$ much greater than its radius, with $N$ total turns (linear turn density $n = N/L$ turns per meter), Ampère's law yields the interior field

$$ B = \mu_0 n I, $$

directed along the solenoid's axis, where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space. The exterior field of an ideal infinite solenoid is identically zero; in practice it is negligibly small.

Derivation sketch. Apply Ampère's law $\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}$ to a rectangular Ampèrian loop with one side of length $\ell$ inside the solenoid (parallel to the axis) and the opposing side outside. Only the interior segment contributes, giving $B\ell = \mu_0 (n\ell) I$, hence $B = \mu_0 n I$.

Worked Example. A solenoid has $500$ turns wound over a length of $0.25\ \text{m}$ and carries a current of $3.0\ \text{A}$. Find the magnetic field strength inside.

The linear turn density is

$$ n = \frac{500}{0.25\ \text{m}} = 2000\ \text{turns/m}. $$

Applying the solenoid formula:

$$ B = \mu_0 n I = (4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A})(2000\ \text{m}^{-1})(3.0\ \text{A}). $$ $$ B = (4\pi \times 10^{-7})(6000)\ \text{T} \approx 7.54 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{T} = 7.54\ \text{mT}. $$

Key Theorem (Magnetic Flux and Inductance). The self-inductance of a solenoid is $L_{\text{coil}} = \mu_0 n^2 V$, where $V = A \cdot \ell$ is the interior volume and $A$ is the cross-sectional area. This follows from $\Phi_B = B A = \mu_0 n I A$ per turn, giving total flux linkage $N\Phi_B = \mu_0 n^2 A \ell \cdot I$.

Plot $B$ versus $I$ and determine the experimental value of $\mu_0 n$ from the slope. Compare your result with the theoretical prediction and discuss sources of discrepancy, including end effects and non-uniform winding.

🌸 Magnetic Resonance Imaging

🌸 Magnetic Resonance Imaging

Definition. Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a non-invasive diagnostic technique that exploits the quantum mechanical property of nuclear spin to produce detailed images of biological tissue. At its core, MRI relies on nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR): atomic nuclei with nonzero spin possess a magnetic dipole moment $\boldsymbol{\mu} = \gamma \mathbf{J}$, where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio and $\mathbf{J}$ is the nuclear angular momentum. When placed in a static external magnetic field $\mathbf{B}_0$, these moments precess about the field direction at the Larmor frequency:

$$ f_0 = \frac{\gamma B_0}{2\pi} $$

For hydrogen nuclei (protons), $\gamma/2\pi \approx 42.58\ \text{MHz/T}$, making protons the primary target in clinical MRI because of their abundance in water and fat.

Worked Example. A clinical MRI scanner operates at $B_0 = 1.5\ \text{T}$. Determine the Larmor frequency of protons in this field.

$$ f_0 = \frac{\gamma}{2\pi} B_0 = (42.58\ \text{MHz/T})(1.5\ \text{T}) = 63.87\ \text{MHz} $$

This frequency lies in the radio-frequency (RF) band. A transverse RF pulse tuned to $f_0$ tips the net magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ away from $\mathbf{B}_0$. After the pulse is removed, $\mathbf{M}$ relaxes back to equilibrium, emitting a detectable RF signal. Spatial encoding is achieved by superimposing linearly varying gradient fields $\mathbf{G}$, so that the local resonance frequency $f(\mathbf{r}) = (\gamma/2\pi)(B_0 + \mathbf{G}\cdot\mathbf{r})$ encodes position.

Key Theorem — Bloch Equations. The macroscopic magnetization obeys:

$$ \frac{d\mathbf{M}}{dt} = \gamma\,\mathbf{M}\times\mathbf{B} - \frac{M_x\,\hat{x} + M_y\,\hat{y}}{T_2} - \frac{(M_z - M_0)\hat{z}}{T_1} $$

where $T_1$ is the longitudinal (spin-lattice) relaxation time and $T_2$ is the transverse (spin-spin) relaxation time. Tissue contrast in MRI images arises precisely because different tissues have characteristic $(T_1, T_2)$ pairs.

Lab Exercise. Using a benchtop NMR spectrometer set to $B_0 = 0.5\ \text{T}$, acquire free-induction decay (FID) signals from water samples doped with varying concentrations of $\text{MnCl}_2$. Measure $T_2$ for each sample via a Carr-Purcell-Meiboom-Gill (CPMG) pulse sequence, and plot $1/T_2$ versus $[\text{Mn}^{2+}]$ to verify the linear relaxivity relationship $1/T_2 = 1/T_{2,0} + r_2 C$.

Payoff 🎯

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) represents the culmination of electromagnetic theory as applied to biological systems, synthesizing nuclear spin physics, radio-frequency electromagnetic fields, and precisely controlled gradient magnetic fields into a non-invasive diagnostic tool of extraordinary power. At its core, MRI exploits the quantum mechanical property of nuclear spin—particularly that of the hydrogen nucleus (proton)—to generate spatially resolved maps of tissue composition and structure without ionizing radiation.

The physical foundation rests on the Larmor precession relation,

$$ \omega_0 = \gamma B_0, $$

where $\omega_0$ is the resonant angular frequency of a spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ nucleus, $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio ($\gamma/2\pi \approx 42.58\ \text{MHz/T}$ for protons), and $B_0$ is the applied static field strength. Protons aligned with a strong external field $\mathbf{B}_0$ are tipped by a transverse radio-frequency pulse oscillating at exactly $\omega_0$; upon removal of the pulse, the spins precess and relax, inducing a detectable electromotive force in a receiver coil. Spatial encoding is achieved by superimposing linear gradient fields $\mathbf{G} = G_x\hat{x} + G_y\hat{y} + G_z\hat{z}$, which cause $\omega_0$ to vary with position, allowing Fourier reconstruction of a full three-dimensional image.

Worked example. A clinical scanner operates at $B_0 = 3.0\ \text{T}$. The Larmor frequency for protons is $f_0 = \gamma B_0 / 2\pi = (42.58\ \text{MHz/T})(3.0\ \text{T}) \approx 127.7\ \text{MHz}$, placing the excitation pulse firmly in the RF band—far below the ionizing threshold of X-rays.

Key theorem (Bloch equations). The time evolution of the net magnetization vector $\mathbf{M}$ is governed by

$$ \frac{d\mathbf{M}}{dt} = \gamma\,\mathbf{M} \times \mathbf{B} - \frac{M_x\hat{x} + M_y\hat{y}}{T_2} - \frac{(M_z - M_0)\hat{z}}{T_1}, $$

where $T_1$ (longitudinal relaxation) and $T_2$ (transverse relaxation) are tissue-specific constants that furnish the contrast distinguishing white matter, gray matter, tumors, and edema.

MRI is the natural endpoint of this chapter because it demands mastery of every preceding concept: static magnetic fields, electromagnetic induction, AC circuits, and electromagnetic wave propagation. Each application domain opened by this understanding—functional neuroimaging, cardiac motion tracking, diffusion tensor tractography—draws on a distinct facet of the same underlying physics.
