🍃 Amperes Law

🍃 Amperes Law

Definition. Ampère's Law relates the magnetic field circulating around a closed loop to the net electric current passing through any surface bounded by that loop. In its integral form, the law states:

$$ \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = \mu_0 \, I_{\text{enc}} $$

where $\oint_{\mathcal{C}} \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell}$ is the line integral of the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ around a closed Amperian path $\mathcal{C}$, $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space, and $I_{\text{enc}}$ is the total current enclosed by (passing through the surface bounded by) $\mathcal{C}$. The direction convention follows the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of integration; the thumb points in the positive current direction.

Worked Example. Find the magnetic field at a perpendicular distance $r$ from an infinitely long straight wire carrying current $I$.

Choose a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$ centered on the wire and lying in a plane perpendicular to it. By the symmetry of the configuration, $\vec{B}$ is tangential and constant in magnitude everywhere on this circle. Therefore:

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = B \oint d\ell = B (2\pi r) $$

The enclosed current is simply $I$, so Ampère's Law gives:

$$ B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I \implies B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$

This result confirms the $1/r$ field dependence for a long straight conductor.

Key Theorem. Ampère's Law holds for any closed path and any current distribution, provided all currents are steady (magnetostatics). For a toroid with $N$ turns, total current $I$, and mean radius $r$, the field inside the toroid is:

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 N I}{2\pi r} $$

Outside the toroid, perfect cancellation yields $B = 0$, a result that follows directly from choosing an exterior Amperian loop enclosing zero net current.

Lab Exercise. Wind a solenoid of $n = 800$ turns/m carrying $I = 2.0\ \text{A}$. Using Ampère's Law applied to a rectangular Amperian loop straddling the solenoid wall, derive the interior field $B = \mu_0 n I$. Calculate the expected value, then measure the axial field at the solenoid center with a Hall probe and compute the percent deviation from the theoretical prediction. Identify at least two sources of systematic error.
