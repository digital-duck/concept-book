🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: every observable charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e \approx 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb (SI unit). Matter carries two polarities—positive and negative—and the algebraic sum of charge in an isolated system is conserved absolutely. The proton carries charge $+e$, the electron $-e$, and the neutron zero net charge. Macroscopic objects become charged through transfer of electrons (or, in ionic solutions, ions), never through creation or destruction of charge.

Worked Example. A glass rod is rubbed with silk. Suppose $3.0 \times 10^{10}$ electrons are transferred from the glass to the silk. Find the charge acquired by each object.

The charge transferred equals:

$$ q = n \cdot e = (3.0 \times 10^{10})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}) \approx 4.8\ \mu\text{C} $$

The silk gains $-4.8\ \mu\text{C}$ (excess electrons), and the glass rod acquires $+4.8\ \mu\text{C}$ (electron deficit). Note that the total charge of the glass-silk system is zero before and after rubbing, confirming charge conservation.

Key Theorem — Coulomb's Law. The electrostatic force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is:

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\,\hat{r}_{12}, \qquad k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \approx 8.988 \times 10^{9}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2 $$

Like charges ($q_1 q_2 > 0$) repel; unlike charges ($q_1 q_2 < 0$) attract. The force obeys Newton's third law: $\vec{F}_{12} = -\vec{F}_{21}$.

Lab Exercise. Hang two small conducting spheres of equal mass $m = 0.50\ \text{g}$ on insulating threads of length $L = 30\ \text{cm}$ from the same point. After charging both spheres identically, they deflect to an equilibrium half-angle $\theta$ from vertical. Measure $\theta$ and show that the charge on each sphere is:

$$ q = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi\epsilon_0 m g \tan\theta \cdot (2L\sin\theta)^2}{1}} $$

Evaluate $q$ for $\theta = 5.0°$ and discuss sources of experimental uncertainty.

🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the time rate of change of displacement. For a particle whose position vector is $\mathbf{r}(t)$, the instantaneous velocity is defined as the limit of the average velocity as the time interval shrinks to zero:

$$ \mathbf{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \mathbf{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} $$

Velocity is a vector quantity possessing both magnitude and direction. The magnitude $|\mathbf{v}|$ is called the speed, measured in SI units of meters per second (m/s). Unlike displacement, velocity encodes the instantaneous state of motion—not merely where a particle has been, but how it is moving at each moment.

Worked Example. A charged particle moves along the $x$-axis with position given by $x(t) = 3t^2 - 2t + 1$ (in meters, with $t$ in seconds). Find the velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$.

Differentiating with respect to time:

$$ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 2 $$

Evaluating at $t = 2\,\text{s}$:

$$ v(2) = 6(2) - 2 = 10\,\text{m/s} $$

The positive sign confirms motion in the positive $x$-direction at that instant. Note that setting $v(t) = 0$ gives $t = 1/3\,\text{s}$, identifying the turning point where the particle momentarily reverses direction.

Key Theorem — Kinematic Velocity Relation. For an object undergoing constant acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ from initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$, integration of $\mathbf{a} = d\mathbf{v}/dt$ directly yields:

$$ \mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{v}_0 + \mathbf{a}t $$

This result, derived rigorously from the definition of velocity as a derivative, is the foundation of kinematic analysis in mechanics and electromagnetism. It remains valid component-wise in two and three dimensions, where $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{v}_0$, and $\mathbf{a}$ are treated as independent vector equations along each coordinate axis.

🍃 Magnetic Field

🍃 Magnetic Field

Definition. A magnetic field is a vector field $\mathbf{B}$ that permeates space in the vicinity of magnetic sources—moving charges, electric currents, or magnetized materials—and exerts forces on other moving charges. The SI unit of magnetic field strength is the tesla (T), where $1\,\text{T} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{s}^{-2}\cdot\text{A}^{-1}$. The magnetic force on a charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a field $\mathbf{B}$ is given by the Lorentz force law:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} $$

This force is always perpendicular to both $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, doing no work on the particle and therefore changing its direction but not its speed. Magnetic field lines are closed loops (divergence-free: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$), emerging from north poles and entering south poles by convention.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, $m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$) moves with velocity $\mathbf{v} = 3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}\,\text{m/s}$ into a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}\,\text{T}$.

The magnetic force is:

$$ \mathbf{F} = qv B\,(\hat{x} \times \hat{z}) = (1.602 \times 10^{-19})(3.0 \times 10^6)(0.50)\,(-\hat{y}) $$ $$ \mathbf{F} \approx -2.4 \times 10^{-13}\,\hat{y}\,\text{N} $$

The proton curves in the $xy$-plane with radius:

$$ r = \frac{mv}{|q|B} = \frac{(1.673 \times 10^{-27})(3.0 \times 10^6)}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})(0.50)} \approx 0.063\,\text{m} = 6.3\,\text{cm} $$

Key Theorem (Biot–Savart Law). The magnetic field produced by a steady current $I$ flowing through an infinitesimal element $d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ at position $\mathbf{r}'$ is:

$$ d\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{I\,d\boldsymbol{\ell} \times \hat{r}}{r^2} $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\text{T}\cdot\text{m/A}$ is the permeability of free space and $\hat{r}$ points from the source element to the field point. Integrating over a complete circuit yields the total field $\mathbf{B}$.

Plot $B$ versus axial position, compare with the theoretical prediction $B = \mu_0 n I$ at the center (where $n$ is turn density), and compute the percentage deviation. Identify and explain the field fall-off near the solenoid ends.

🌱 Electric Current

🌱 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional surface. If a charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface during a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limit

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of current is taken as the direction of positive charge flow, even when the actual charge carriers are negatively charged electrons moving in the opposite direction.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries a steady current such that $3.6 \times 10^{20}$ electrons pass through a cross-section every $30\,\text{s}$. Find the magnitude of the current.

The total charge transferred is

$$ \Delta Q = N \cdot e = (3.6 \times 10^{20})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}) = 57.67\,\text{C} $$

Therefore,

$$ I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} = \frac{57.67\,\text{C}}{30\,\text{s}} \approx 1.92\,\text{A} $$

Since electrons are the carriers, they move opposite to the conventional current direction, but the magnitude of the current is $1.92\,\text{A}$.

Key Theorem: Current Continuity. For a steady-state (DC) circuit with no charge accumulation at any node, the algebraic sum of currents entering any junction equals zero:

$$ \sum_k I_k = 0 $$

This is Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL), a direct consequence of conservation of charge. It implies that in a simple series circuit, the current is identical at every cross-section, regardless of variations in wire geometry or material—a result that can be derived formally from the continuity equation $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = -\partial\rho/\partial t = 0$ under steady-state conditions, where $\mathbf{J}$ is the current density.

Lab Exercise. Connect three resistors ($R_1 = 10\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 20\,\Omega$, $R_3 = 30\,\Omega$) in parallel across a $12\,\text{V}$ battery. Use an ammeter to measure the current through each branch and the total current from the battery. Calculate the theoretical branch currents using $I_k = V/R_k$, compute their sum, and verify that it equals the measured total current. Confirm that KCL holds at the junction to within experimental uncertainty.

🌱 Electric Field

🌱 Electric Field

Definition. The electric field $\mathbf{E}$ at a point in space is defined as the electrostatic force $\mathbf{F}$ experienced by a small positive test charge $q_0$ placed at that point, divided by the magnitude of that test charge:

$$ \mathbf{E} = \lim_{q_0 \to 0} \frac{\mathbf{F}}{q_0} $$

The limit is taken so that the test charge does not disturb the source distribution. The SI unit of electric field is newtons per coulomb ($\mathrm{N/C}$), equivalently volts per meter ($\mathrm{V/m}$). For a point source charge $Q$, Coulomb's law yields the field at displacement $\mathbf{r}$ from $Q$:

$$ \mathbf{E} = \frac{kQ}{r^2}\hat{r} = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\hat{r} $$

where $k \approx 8.99 \times 10^9\ \mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C^2}$ and $\hat{r}$ points from the source to the field point. The field is radially outward for positive $Q$ and inward for negative $Q$.

Worked Example. Two point charges $Q_1 = +3.0\ \mu\mathrm{C}$ and $Q_2 = -3.0\ \mu\mathrm{C}$ are separated by $d = 0.10\ \mathrm{m}$. Find the electric field at the midpoint $P$ between them.

At $P$, each charge is $r = 0.05\ \mathrm{m}$ away. The magnitude of the field from each charge is:

$$ E = \frac{kQ}{r^2} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(3.0\times10^{-6})}{(0.05)^2} \approx 1.08\times10^7\ \mathrm{N/C} $$

$Q_1$ produces a field directed from $Q_1$ toward $Q_2$ (rightward), and $Q_2$ produces a field also directed toward $Q_2$ (rightward), since $P$ lies to the right of $Q_2$'s attractive direction. Both contributions are parallel, so:

$$ E_\mathrm{total} = 2E \approx 2.16\times10^7\ \mathrm{N/C} $$

directed from $Q_1$ toward $Q_2$.

Key Theorem — Superposition. The total electric field due to a collection of $N$ point charges is the vector sum of individual contributions:

$$ \mathbf{E}_\mathrm{total} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{kq_i}{r_i^2}\hat{r}_i $$

This principle, exact in classical electrostatics, underlies all field calculations for continuous charge distributions via integration.

Using superposition, calculate the magnitude and direction of $\mathbf{E}$ at the centroid of the triangle. Verify by symmetry that the net field must be zero, then confirm analytically.

🍃 Lorentz Force

🍃 Lorentz Force

Definition. When a charged particle moves through a region containing both electric and magnetic fields, it experiences a combined electromagnetic force known as the Lorentz force. For a particle with charge $q$, velocity $\mathbf{v}$, electric field $\mathbf{E}$, and magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, the Lorentz force is given by:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) $$

The electric component $q\mathbf{E}$ acts parallel to the field and does work on the particle, altering its kinetic energy. The magnetic component $q(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})$ always acts perpendicular to the velocity; consequently, it does no work and cannot change the particle's speed — only its direction. The magnitude of the magnetic force alone is $F = |q|vB\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C, $m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg) moves with velocity $\mathbf{v} = 3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}$ m/s into a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}$ T, with no electric field present. The magnetic force is:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} = (1.60 \times 10^{-19})(3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}) \times (0.50\,\hat{z}) $$

Using $\hat{x} \times \hat{z} = -\hat{y}$:

$$ \mathbf{F} = (1.60 \times 10^{-19})(1.5 \times 10^6)(-\hat{y}) = -2.4 \times 10^{-13}\,\hat{y}\text{ N} $$

The proton deflects in the $-y$ direction. Because $\mathbf{F} \perp \mathbf{v}$, the proton undergoes uniform circular motion with radius $r = mv/(|q|B) = (1.67 \times 10^{-27})(3.0 \times 10^6)/[(1.60 \times 10^{-19})(0.50)] \approx 0.063$ m.

Key Theorem. A particle of charge $q$ and mass $m$ moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ undergoes uniform circular motion with cyclotron radius $r = mv/(|q|B)$ and cyclotron frequency $\omega_c = |q|B/m$, independent of the particle's speed.

Lab Exercise. An electron enters a region with $\mathbf{E} = 4.0 \times 10^4\,\hat{y}$ V/m and $\mathbf{B} = 0.20\,\hat{z}$ T. Determine the velocity $v_x$ for which the net Lorentz force on the electron is zero (the velocity selector condition), then calculate the radius of curvature if the electric field is subsequently removed.

🍃 Hall Effect

🍃 Hall Effect

Definition. The Hall effect is the production of a transverse voltage — the Hall voltage $V_H$ — across a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field perpendicular to the current flow. When charge carriers moving with drift velocity $v_d$ along a conductor experience a magnetic force $\vec{F} = q\vec{v}_d \times \vec{B}$, they are deflected toward one face of the conductor. Charge accumulates on that face until the resulting electric field $E_H$ exerts an opposing force that exactly balances the magnetic force:

$$ qE_H = qv_dB \implies E_H = v_dB. $$

Since $E_H = V_H / w$ for a conductor of width $w$, the Hall voltage is

$$ V_H = v_d B w. $$

The sign of $V_H$ reveals the sign of the charge carriers, making the Hall effect the definitive experimental test distinguishing electron conduction from hole conduction.

Worked Example. A rectangular copper strip of width $w = 2.0\,\text{cm}$ and thickness $t = 1.0\,\text{mm}$ carries a current $I = 50\,\text{A}$ in the $+x$-direction through a uniform field $B = 1.5\,\text{T}$ directed in the $+z$-direction. Find the Hall voltage.

First, express the drift velocity in terms of measurable quantities. The current density is $J = I/(wt)$, and $J = nqv_d$, so

$$ v_d = \frac{I}{nqwt}. $$

For copper, the free-electron density is $n \approx 8.5 \times 10^{28}\,\text{m}^{-3}$. Substituting:

$$ v_d = \frac{50}{(8.5\times10^{28})(1.6\times10^{-19})(0.02)(0.001)} \approx 1.84\times10^{-4}\,\text{m/s}. $$

Therefore,

$$ V_H = v_d B w = (1.84\times10^{-4})(1.5)(0.02) \approx 5.5\,\mu\text{V}. $$

Key Theorem. The Hall coefficient $R_H$ is defined by $R_H = E_H / (JB)$. Combining $E_H = v_dB$ and $J = nqv_d$ gives

$$ R_H = \frac{1}{nq}. $$

A measurement of $R_H$ therefore yields the carrier density $n$ directly, independent of any microscopic model of the metal.

Plot $V_H$ versus $B$ for each $I$, extract the slope $v_d w$ from each line, and verify that $v_d \propto I$ as predicted. 🍃 Hall Emf

🍃 Hall Emf

Definition. When a current-carrying conductor is placed in an external magnetic field perpendicular to the current, charge carriers experience a Lorentz force that deflects them transversely. As carriers accumulate on one face of the conductor, an electric field builds in opposition until equilibrium is reached. The resulting transverse potential difference is called the Hall emf (or Hall voltage), given by

$$ \mathcal{E}_H = v_d B \ell $$

where $v_d$ is the drift speed of charge carriers, $B$ is the magnetic flux density, and $\ell$ is the width of the conductor perpendicular to both the current and the field. At equilibrium, the electric force $qE_H$ exactly balances the magnetic force $qv_d B$, so $E_H = v_d B$ and $\mathcal{E}_H = E_H \ell$.

Worked Example. A copper strip of width $\ell = 2.0\,\text{cm}$ carries a current $I = 5.0\,\text{A}$ in a magnetic field $B = 0.80\,\text{T}$ directed perpendicularly to the strip's face. The strip has a cross-sectional area $A = 4.0 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{m}^2$ and copper's number density is $n = 8.5 \times 10^{28}\,\text{m}^{-3}$.

First, find the drift speed:

$$ v_d = \frac{I}{nqA} = \frac{5.0}{(8.5\times10^{28})(1.6\times10^{-19})(4.0\times10^{-6})} \approx 9.2\times10^{-4}\,\text{m/s} $$

Then the Hall emf:

$$ \mathcal{E}_H = v_d B \ell = (9.2\times10^{-4})(0.80)(0.020) \approx 1.5\times10^{-5}\,\text{V} = 15\,\mu\text{V} $$

This small voltage is nonetheless measurable with a sensitive voltmeter and reveals both the sign and density of charge carriers.

Key Theorem. Combining $\mathcal{E}_H = v_d B \ell$ with $J = nqv_d$ and $J = I/A$ yields the Hall coefficient:

$$ R_H = \frac{\mathcal{E}_H}{IB/\ell} = \frac{1}{nq} $$

This result shows that $R_H$ depends only on the carrier density $n$ and charge sign $q$, making the Hall effect a direct probe of a material's electronic structure. A negative $R_H$ indicates electron conduction; a positive value indicates hole conduction.

🌸 Hall Probe

🌸 Hall Probe

Definition. A Hall probe is a thin semiconductor device that exploits the Hall effect to measure magnetic field strength directly. When a current-carrying conductor of width $w$ and thickness $d$ is placed in a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ perpendicular to the current direction, the Lorentz force deflects charge carriers laterally until an opposing electric field builds up. At equilibrium, the transverse (Hall) voltage is

$$ V_H = \frac{IB}{nqd} $$

where $I$ is the driving current, $n$ is the charge-carrier number density, $q$ is the elementary charge, and $d$ is the dimension of the conductor parallel to $\mathbf{B}$. Because $V_H$ is directly proportional to $B$, a calibrated measurement of $V_H$ yields the field magnitude. The probe is oriented so that the plane of its sensing element is perpendicular to the field lines, maximizing the deflection signal.

Worked Example. A Hall probe made of indium antimonide (InSb) carries a current $I = 50\,\text{mA}$ through a slab of thickness $d = 1.0\,\text{mm}$. The carrier density is $n = 2.0\times10^{22}\,\text{m}^{-3}$. When inserted into an unknown field, it registers $V_H = 25\,\text{mV}$. Solving for $B$:

$$ B = \frac{V_H \, n \, q \, d}{I} = \frac{(0.025)(2.0\times10^{22})(1.6\times10^{-19})(1.0\times10^{-3})}{0.050} $$ $$ B = \frac{8.0\times10^{-2}}{0.050} = 1.6\,\text{T} $$

The field strength is $1.6\,\text{T}$, consistent with a strong laboratory electromagnet.

Key Theorem. The Hall coefficient $R_H = 1/(nq)$ is a material constant that relates the geometry-independent ratio $V_H d / (IB) = R_H$ to carrier density and charge sign. A positive $R_H$ indicates hole-dominated conduction (p-type material), while a negative $R_H$ indicates electron-dominated conduction (n-type), allowing the probe to simultaneously determine carrier type and field magnitude.

Lab Exercise. Connect a Hall probe in series with a precision current source set to $I = 100\,\text{mA}$. Insert the probe into the gap of a solenoid whose field you calculate from $B = \mu_0 n I_s$. Record $V_H$ at five solenoid currents $I_s$ between $1\,\text{A}$ and $5\,\text{A}$, plot $V_H$ versus $B$, and extract the slope to determine the Hall coefficient $R_H$ of your probe material. Compare with the manufacturer's tabulated value.

Payoff 🎯

The Hall probe stands as the natural culmination of Chapter 22's treatment of magnetism because it transforms the abstract Lorentz force into a precise, quantitative instrument. Throughout the chapter, students encounter magnetic fields as invisible agents that deflect charges and torque current loops. The Hall probe closes this arc by making the field directly measurable: when a current-carrying conductor is placed in an external magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, charge carriers accumulate on opposite faces until the resulting electric field $\mathbf{E}_H$ exactly balances the magnetic force. The equilibrium condition

$$ qE_H = qv_d B \implies V_H = \frac{IB}{nqt} $$

where $n$ is the carrier density, $q$ the carrier charge, and $t$ the conductor thickness, delivers the field strength $B$ directly from a voltage reading. No moving parts, no calibration against a reference coil — just Ohm-scale electronics and the fundamental balance of forces.

Worked Example. A semiconductor Hall probe carries $I = 2.00\ \text{mA}$ through a slab of thickness $t = 1.00\ \text{mm}$. In an unknown field, the Hall voltage reads $V_H = 4.50\ \text{mV}$. Given $n = 8.00 \times 10^{22}\ \text{m}^{-3}$ and $q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$,

$$ B = \frac{nqtV_H}{I} = \frac{(8.00\times10^{22})(1.60\times10^{-19})(1.00\times10^{-3})(4.50\times10^{-3})}{2.00\times10^{-3}} \approx 2.88\ \text{T}. $$

This single algebraic step, derived from first principles, is the operational heart of every commercial Hall sensor.

Key Theorem. The sign of $V_H$ identifies the dominant carrier species. If holes carry current, they accumulate on the same face as electrons would in an equivalent electron current, but the conventional current direction is reversed, inverting $V_H$. Measurement of this sign was historically decisive in confirming p-type conduction in semiconductors.

Lab Exercise. Connect a calibrated Hall probe to a Gaussmeter, suspend it inside a solenoid carrying variable current, and record $V_H$ versus $I_{\text{solenoid}}$. Extract $B$ at each current setting, plot $B$ against $I_{\text{solenoid}}$, and verify the linear relationship $B = \mu_0 n_{\text{sol}} I_{\text{solenoid}}$. Compare the measured slope to the theoretical value using the solenoid's known turn density $n_{\text{sol}}$.

From this foundation, every application domain opens naturally. In materials science, the probe maps carrier density across semiconductor wafers. In medical imaging, Hall-effect current sensors monitor gradient coil currents in MRI systems. In automotive engineering, brushless motor controllers rely on Hall sensors for rotor-position feedback. Each domain is a chapter unto itself. Choose the one that compels you most — the quantum Hall effect, where $V_H/I$ is quantized to extraordinary precision, awaits as a particularly rewarding frontier at the boundary of condensed-matter physics and metrology.
