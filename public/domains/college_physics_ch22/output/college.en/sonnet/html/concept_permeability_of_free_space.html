🍃 Permeability Of Free Space

🍃 Permeability Of Free Space

Definition. The permeability of free space, denoted $\mu_0$, is a fundamental physical constant that quantifies the ability of a vacuum to support the formation of a magnetic field. It appears in Ampère's law and the Biot–Savart law as the proportionality constant relating electric currents to the magnetic fields they produce. Its defined value in SI units is

$$ \mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A} \approx 1.2566 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{T·m/A}. $$

Physically, $\mu_0$ sets the scale of magnetic interactions in free space, playing a role analogous to the permittivity of free space $\varepsilon_0$ in electrostatics. Together they are related to the speed of light by $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}$.

Worked Example. A long, straight wire carries a steady current $I = 5.0 \, \text{A}$. Find the magnetic field strength at a perpendicular distance $r = 0.10 \, \text{m}$ from the wire.

Applying Ampère's law to a circular path of radius $r$ centered on the wire:

$$ \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}. $$

By symmetry, $B$ is constant along the loop and parallel to $d\boldsymbol{\ell}$, so

$$ B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I \implies B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}. $$

Substituting values:

$$ B = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(5.0)}{2\pi (0.10)} = \frac{2.0 \times 10^{-6}}{0.20} = 1.0 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{T}. $$

Key Theorem. The Biot–Savart law states that the infinitesimal magnetic field $d\mathbf{B}$ produced by a current element $I \, d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ at displacement $\mathbf{r}$ is

$$ d\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{I \, d\boldsymbol{\ell} \times \hat{r}}{r^2}. $$

This result, combined with the superposition principle, enables calculation of magnetic fields for arbitrary current distributions. The constant $\mu_0/4\pi = 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A}$ is its rationalized form.

Lab Exercise. Using a calibrated Hall probe, measure the magnetic field at several radial distances from a long wire carrying a known current. Plot $B$ versus $1/r$ and determine the slope. From the slope and the known current, extract an experimental value of $\mu_0$ and compare it to the defined constant, computing the percent error.
