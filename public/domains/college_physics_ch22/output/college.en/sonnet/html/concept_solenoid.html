🍃 Solenoid

🍃 Solenoid

Definition. A solenoid is a helical coil of wire wound uniformly around a cylindrical form, designed so that when carrying a steady current $I$ it produces a nearly uniform magnetic field confined within its interior. For an ideal solenoid of length $L$ much greater than its radius, with $N$ total turns (linear turn density $n = N/L$ turns per meter), Ampère's law yields the interior field

$$ B = \mu_0 n I, $$

directed along the solenoid's axis, where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space. The exterior field of an ideal infinite solenoid is identically zero; in practice it is negligibly small.

Derivation sketch. Apply Ampère's law $\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}$ to a rectangular Ampèrian loop with one side of length $\ell$ inside the solenoid (parallel to the axis) and the opposing side outside. Only the interior segment contributes, giving $B\ell = \mu_0 (n\ell) I$, hence $B = \mu_0 n I$.

Worked Example. A solenoid has $500$ turns wound over a length of $0.25\ \text{m}$ and carries a current of $3.0\ \text{A}$. Find the magnetic field strength inside.

The linear turn density is

$$ n = \frac{500}{0.25\ \text{m}} = 2000\ \text{turns/m}. $$

Applying the solenoid formula:

$$ B = \mu_0 n I = (4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A})(2000\ \text{m}^{-1})(3.0\ \text{A}). $$ $$ B = (4\pi \times 10^{-7})(6000)\ \text{T} \approx 7.54 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{T} = 7.54\ \text{mT}. $$

Key Theorem (Magnetic Flux and Inductance). The self-inductance of a solenoid is $L_{\text{coil}} = \mu_0 n^2 V$, where $V = A \cdot \ell$ is the interior volume and $A$ is the cross-sectional area. This follows from $\Phi_B = B A = \mu_0 n I A$ per turn, giving total flux linkage $N\Phi_B = \mu_0 n^2 A \ell \cdot I$.

Lab Exercise. Wind a solenoid of approximately $300$ turns on a cardboard tube of length $15\ \text{cm}$ and radius $2\ \text{cm}$. Connect it to a variable DC supply and measure the axial field at the center using a Hall probe for currents of $0.5$, $1.0$, and $1.5\ \text{A}$. Plot $B$ versus $I$ and determine the experimental value of $\mu_0 n$ from the slope. Compare your result with the theoretical prediction and discuss sources of discrepancy, including end effects and non-uniform winding.
