🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: every observable charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e \approx 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb (SI unit). Matter carries two polarities—positive and negative—and the algebraic sum of charge in an isolated system is conserved absolutely. The proton carries charge $+e$, the electron $-e$, and the neutron zero net charge. Macroscopic objects become charged through transfer of electrons (or, in ionic solutions, ions), never through creation or destruction of charge.

Worked Example. A glass rod is rubbed with silk. Suppose $3.0 \times 10^{10}$ electrons are transferred from the glass to the silk. Find the charge acquired by each object.

The charge transferred equals:

$$ q = n \cdot e = (3.0 \times 10^{10})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}) \approx 4.8\ \mu\text{C} $$

The silk gains $-4.8\ \mu\text{C}$ (excess electrons), and the glass rod acquires $+4.8\ \mu\text{C}$ (electron deficit). Note that the total charge of the glass-silk system is zero before and after rubbing, confirming charge conservation.

Key Theorem — Coulomb's Law. The electrostatic force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is:

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\,\hat{r}_{12}, \qquad k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \approx 8.988 \times 10^{9}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2 $$

Like charges ($q_1 q_2 > 0$) repel; unlike charges ($q_1 q_2 < 0$) attract. The force obeys Newton's third law: $\vec{F}_{12} = -\vec{F}_{21}$.

Lab Exercise. Hang two small conducting spheres of equal mass $m = 0.50\ \text{g}$ on insulating threads of length $L = 30\ \text{cm}$ from the same point. After charging both spheres identically, they deflect to an equilibrium half-angle $\theta$ from vertical. Measure $\theta$ and show that the charge on each sphere is:

$$ q = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi\epsilon_0 m g \tan\theta \cdot (2L\sin\theta)^2}{1}} $$

Evaluate $q$ for $\theta = 5.0°$ and discuss sources of experimental uncertainty.

🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the time rate of change of displacement. For a particle whose position vector is $\mathbf{r}(t)$, the instantaneous velocity is defined as the limit of the average velocity as the time interval shrinks to zero:

$$ \mathbf{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \mathbf{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} $$

Velocity is a vector quantity possessing both magnitude and direction. The magnitude $|\mathbf{v}|$ is called the speed, measured in SI units of meters per second (m/s). Unlike displacement, velocity encodes the instantaneous state of motion—not merely where a particle has been, but how it is moving at each moment.

Worked Example. A charged particle moves along the $x$-axis with position given by $x(t) = 3t^2 - 2t + 1$ (in meters, with $t$ in seconds). Find the velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$.

Differentiating with respect to time:

$$ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 2 $$

Evaluating at $t = 2\,\text{s}$:

$$ v(2) = 6(2) - 2 = 10\,\text{m/s} $$

The positive sign confirms motion in the positive $x$-direction at that instant. Note that setting $v(t) = 0$ gives $t = 1/3\,\text{s}$, identifying the turning point where the particle momentarily reverses direction.

Key Theorem — Kinematic Velocity Relation. For an object undergoing constant acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ from initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$, integration of $\mathbf{a} = d\mathbf{v}/dt$ directly yields:

$$ \mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{v}_0 + \mathbf{a}t $$

This result, derived rigorously from the definition of velocity as a derivative, is the foundation of kinematic analysis in mechanics and electromagnetism. It remains valid component-wise in two and three dimensions, where $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{v}_0$, and $\mathbf{a}$ are treated as independent vector equations along each coordinate axis.

Lab Exercise. A steel ball is released from rest on an inclined air track (inclination angle $\theta = 5°$). Using a photogate timer, record the ball's position at $t = 0.0,\,0.2,\,0.4,\,0.6\,\text{s}$. Compute the average velocity over each interval, then extrapolate to estimate the instantaneous velocity at $t = 0.3\,\text{s}$. Compare your result to the theoretical prediction $v = g\sin\theta \cdot t$ and quantify the percent deviation. Discuss sources of systematic error, including friction and timing resolution.

🍃 Magnetic Field

🍃 Magnetic Field

Definition. A magnetic field is a vector field $\mathbf{B}$ that permeates space in the vicinity of magnetic sources—moving charges, electric currents, or magnetized materials—and exerts forces on other moving charges. The SI unit of magnetic field strength is the tesla (T), where $1\,\text{T} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{s}^{-2}\cdot\text{A}^{-1}$. The magnetic force on a charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a field $\mathbf{B}$ is given by the Lorentz force law:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} $$

This force is always perpendicular to both $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, doing no work on the particle and therefore changing its direction but not its speed. Magnetic field lines are closed loops (divergence-free: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$), emerging from north poles and entering south poles by convention.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, $m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$) moves with velocity $\mathbf{v} = 3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}\,\text{m/s}$ into a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}\,\text{T}$.

The magnetic force is:

$$ \mathbf{F} = qv B\,(\hat{x} \times \hat{z}) = (1.602 \times 10^{-19})(3.0 \times 10^6)(0.50)\,(-\hat{y}) $$ $$ \mathbf{F} \approx -2.4 \times 10^{-13}\,\hat{y}\,\text{N} $$

The proton curves in the $xy$-plane with radius:

$$ r = \frac{mv}{|q|B} = \frac{(1.673 \times 10^{-27})(3.0 \times 10^6)}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})(0.50)} \approx 0.063\,\text{m} = 6.3\,\text{cm} $$

Key Theorem (Biot–Savart Law). The magnetic field produced by a steady current $I$ flowing through an infinitesimal element $d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ at position $\mathbf{r}'$ is:

$$ d\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{I\,d\boldsymbol{\ell} \times \hat{r}}{r^2} $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\text{T}\cdot\text{m/A}$ is the permeability of free space and $\hat{r}$ points from the source element to the field point. Integrating over a complete circuit yields the total field $\mathbf{B}$.

Lab Exercise. Using a Hall probe, map the magnetic field along the central axis of a current-carrying solenoid at three current settings (0.5 A, 1.0 A, 1.5 A). Plot $B$ versus axial position, compare with the theoretical prediction $B = \mu_0 n I$ at the center (where $n$ is turn density), and compute the percentage deviation. Identify and explain the field fall-off near the solenoid ends.

🌱 Electric Current

🌱 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional surface. If a charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface during a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limit

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of current is taken as the direction of positive charge flow, even when the actual charge carriers are negatively charged electrons moving in the opposite direction.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries a steady current such that $3.6 \times 10^{20}$ electrons pass through a cross-section every $30\,\text{s}$. Find the magnitude of the current.

The total charge transferred is

$$ \Delta Q = N \cdot e = (3.6 \times 10^{20})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}) = 57.67\,\text{C} $$

Therefore,

$$ I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} = \frac{57.67\,\text{C}}{30\,\text{s}} \approx 1.92\,\text{A} $$

Since electrons are the carriers, they move opposite to the conventional current direction, but the magnitude of the current is $1.92\,\text{A}$.

Key Theorem: Current Continuity. For a steady-state (DC) circuit with no charge accumulation at any node, the algebraic sum of currents entering any junction equals zero:

$$ \sum_k I_k = 0 $$

This is Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL), a direct consequence of conservation of charge. It implies that in a simple series circuit, the current is identical at every cross-section, regardless of variations in wire geometry or material—a result that can be derived formally from the continuity equation $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = -\partial\rho/\partial t = 0$ under steady-state conditions, where $\mathbf{J}$ is the current density.

Lab Exercise. Connect three resistors ($R_1 = 10\,\Omega$, $R_2 = 20\,\Omega$, $R_3 = 30\,\Omega$) in parallel across a $12\,\text{V}$ battery. Use an ammeter to measure the current through each branch and the total current from the battery. Calculate the theoretical branch currents using $I_k = V/R_k$, compute their sum, and verify that it equals the measured total current. Confirm that KCL holds at the junction to within experimental uncertainty.

🍃 Lorentz Force

🍃 Lorentz Force

Definition. When a charged particle moves through a region containing both electric and magnetic fields, it experiences a combined electromagnetic force known as the Lorentz force. For a particle with charge $q$, velocity $\mathbf{v}$, electric field $\mathbf{E}$, and magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, the Lorentz force is given by:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) $$

The electric component $q\mathbf{E}$ acts parallel to the field and does work on the particle, altering its kinetic energy. The magnetic component $q(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})$ always acts perpendicular to the velocity; consequently, it does no work and cannot change the particle's speed — only its direction. The magnitude of the magnetic force alone is $F = |q|vB\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C, $m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg) moves with velocity $\mathbf{v} = 3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}$ m/s into a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}$ T, with no electric field present. The magnetic force is:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} = (1.60 \times 10^{-19})(3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}) \times (0.50\,\hat{z}) $$

Using $\hat{x} \times \hat{z} = -\hat{y}$:

$$ \mathbf{F} = (1.60 \times 10^{-19})(1.5 \times 10^6)(-\hat{y}) = -2.4 \times 10^{-13}\,\hat{y}\text{ N} $$

The proton deflects in the $-y$ direction. Because $\mathbf{F} \perp \mathbf{v}$, the proton undergoes uniform circular motion with radius $r = mv/(|q|B) = (1.67 \times 10^{-27})(3.0 \times 10^6)/[(1.60 \times 10^{-19})(0.50)] \approx 0.063$ m.

Key Theorem. A particle of charge $q$ and mass $m$ moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ undergoes uniform circular motion with cyclotron radius $r = mv/(|q|B)$ and cyclotron frequency $\omega_c = |q|B/m$, independent of the particle's speed.

Lab Exercise. An electron enters a region with $\mathbf{E} = 4.0 \times 10^4\,\hat{y}$ V/m and $\mathbf{B} = 0.20\,\hat{z}$ T. Determine the velocity $v_x$ for which the net Lorentz force on the electron is zero (the velocity selector condition), then calculate the radius of curvature if the electric field is subsequently removed.

🍃 Magnetic Force On Current

🍃 Magnetic Force On Current

Definition. A current-carrying conductor placed in an external magnetic field experiences a net force because the field exerts individual forces on the moving charges that constitute the current. For a straight wire segment of length $L$ carrying current $I$ in a uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$, the magnetic force is

$$ \vec{F} = I\vec{L} \times \vec{B} $$

where $\vec{L}$ is a vector of magnitude $L$ pointing in the direction of conventional current flow. The magnitude of this force is

$$ F = BIL\sin\theta $$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{L}$ and $\vec{B}$. The direction follows the right-hand rule applied to the cross product: orient the fingers of the right hand along $\vec{L}$, curl them toward $\vec{B}$, and the thumb points in the direction of $\vec{F}$. Maximum force occurs when the wire is perpendicular to the field ($\theta = 90°$); zero force occurs when they are parallel.

Worked Example. A horizontal wire of length $L = 0.25\ \text{m}$ carries a current $I = 4.0\ \text{A}$ directed eastward. A uniform magnetic field $B = 0.30\ \text{T}$ points vertically upward. Find the magnetic force on the wire segment.

Since $\vec{L}$ points east $(\hat{x})$ and $\vec{B}$ points up $(\hat{z})$:

$$ \vec{F} = I L(\hat{x} \times \hat{z}) = (4.0)(0.25)(0.30)(\hat{x} \times \hat{z}) $$

Recalling $\hat{x} \times \hat{z} = -\hat{y}$ (southward):

$$ \vec{F} = 0.30\ \text{N},\ \text{directed south} $$

Because $\theta = 90°$, this is the maximum possible force for these parameters.

Key Theorem. For a curved wire of arbitrary shape in a uniform field, the net magnetic force equals $I\vec{L}_{\text{eff}} \times \vec{B}$, where $\vec{L}_{\text{eff}}$ is the straight-line vector from the wire's start point to its end point. This follows directly from the linearity of the cross product and the superposition of infinitesimal force elements $d\vec{F} = I\,d\vec{\ell} \times \vec{B}$. A consequence is that a closed current loop in a uniform field experiences zero net translational force, though it may experience a net torque.

Lab Exercise. Suspend a rigid horizontal wire (length $0.15\ \text{m}$, current $3.0\ \text{A}$) between the poles of a horseshoe magnet so that the wire is perpendicular to the field lines. Measure the deflection using a force sensor, then vary the current in steps of $0.5\ \text{A}$ from $1.0\ \text{A}$ to $4.0\ \text{A}$. Plot $F$ versus $I$ and verify that the slope equals $BL$; use this slope to determine $B$ experimentally and compare against a Hall-probe measurement of the field.

🌸 Magnetohydrodynamic Drive

🌸 Magnetohydrodynamic Drive

Definition. A magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) drive is a propulsion system that accelerates an electrically conducting fluid—typically seawater—by exploiting the Lorentz force. When a current density $\mathbf{J}$ is established through the fluid in the presence of an external magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, the resulting body force per unit volume acting on the fluid is

$$ \mathbf{f} = \mathbf{J} \times \mathbf{B}. $$

By orienting $\mathbf{J}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ perpendicular to each other and to the desired thrust direction, a net force is produced along the propulsion axis without any moving mechanical parts.

Worked Example. Consider a rectangular MHD channel of cross-sectional area $A = 0.20\ \text{m}^2$ and length $L = 1.0\ \text{m}$ filled with seawater of electrical conductivity $\sigma = 4.0\ \text{S m}^{-1}$. An applied voltage $V = 500\ \text{V}$ drives current across the channel width $d = 0.10\ \text{m}$, and a magnetic field $B = 5.0\ \text{T}$ is directed perpendicular to both the current and the channel axis.

The current density is

$$ J = \frac{\sigma V}{d} = \frac{4.0 \times 500}{0.10} = 2.0 \times 10^4\ \text{A m}^{-2}. $$

The body force per unit volume is $f = JB = (2.0 \times 10^4)(5.0) = 1.0 \times 10^5\ \text{N m}^{-3}$. Integrating over the channel volume $V_{\text{ch}} = AL = 0.20\ \text{m}^3$ gives a total thrust

$$ F = f \cdot V_{\text{ch}} = 1.0 \times 10^5 \times 0.20 = 2.0 \times 10^4\ \text{N}. $$

Key Theorem (Hartmann Flow). For laminar MHD flow between parallel electrodes, the dimensionless Hartmann number $\text{Ha} = BL\sqrt{\sigma/\eta}$ (where $\eta$ is dynamic viscosity) governs the velocity profile. At large $\text{Ha}$, viscous boundary layers flatten and the bulk flow approaches a uniform plug profile. Thrust efficiency improves as $\text{Ha} \operatorname{gg} 1$, making strong magnetic fields and high conductivity essential design parameters.

Lab Exercise. Set up a shallow trough of brine connected to a low-voltage DC supply so that current flows horizontally between two submerged electrodes. Place the trough between the poles of a strong permanent magnet with $\mathbf{B}$ directed vertically. Vary the applied voltage in steps of 1 V from 3 V to 9 V. Measure the surface flow velocity at the channel midpoint using a floating tracer particle and record how thrust scales with $V$. Compare your measured velocity profile with the prediction $v \propto JB \propto V$ and identify the onset of turbulence.

Payoff 🎯

Magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) drive represents the conceptual apex of classical electromagnetism applied to fluid systems. Defined formally, an MHD drive is a propulsion mechanism that accelerates an electrically conducting fluid by subjecting it to crossed electric and magnetic fields, producing a body force — the Lorentz force — directly on the fluid without any moving mechanical parts. If a conducting fluid carries current density $\mathbf{J}$ in the presence of an external magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, the volumetric body force is given by:

$$ \mathbf{f} = \mathbf{J} \times \mathbf{B} $$

This force, integrated over the fluid volume, yields net thrust. For seawater with conductivity $\sigma \approx 4\ \mathrm{S\,m^{-1}}$, an applied electric field $\mathbf{E}$ drives $\mathbf{J} = \sigma \mathbf{E}$, and a transverse magnetic field $B \sim 5\ \mathrm{T}$ produces a thrust density on the order of $f = \sigma E B \sim 200\ \mathrm{N\,m^{-3}}$ — sufficient to propel a vessel at tactically relevant speeds.

Worked example. Consider a rectangular MHD channel of cross-sectional area $A = 0.1\ \mathrm{m^2}$ and length $L = 2\ \mathrm{m}$, filled with seawater. With $E = 50\ \mathrm{V\,m^{-1}}$ and $B = 5\ \mathrm{T}$, the current density is $J = \sigma E = 200\ \mathrm{A\,m^{-2}}$. The total thrust force is:

$$ F = J B \cdot (A \cdot L) = 200 \times 5 \times 0.2 = 200\ \mathrm{N} $$

This thrust is generated with zero rotating machinery — eliminating cavitation, mechanical vibration, and acoustic signature.

Key theorem. The Hartmann number $\mathrm{Ha} = BL\sqrt{\sigma/\mu}$, where $\mu$ is fluid dynamic viscosity, governs the regime of MHD flow. For $\mathrm{Ha} \operatorname{gg} 1$, electromagnetic forces dominate viscous forces, and the velocity profile flattens dramatically (Hartmann layers form at the walls), maximizing bulk momentum transfer efficiency.

Lab exercise. Using a shallow tray of saltwater, two graphite electrodes, and a strong neodymium magnet oriented perpendicular to the current flow, construct a benchtop MHD pump. Measure fluid velocity as a function of applied voltage (5–20 V) and magnet field strength. Plot $v$ versus $JB$ and verify the linear relationship predicted by the Lorentz force law. Estimate the effective channel conductivity from your slope.

The MHD drive is the natural endpoint of this chapter because it synthesizes every prior concept — Ampère's law, Faraday induction, Ohm's law in conducting media, and the Lorentz force — into a single, elegant, mechanically silent propulsion system. It stands at the intersection of plasma physics, naval engineering, and energy technology. For those drawn to the frontier where electromagnetic theory meets real fluid dynamics, the study of magnetohydrodynamic power generation — where the same crossed-field geometry extracts electrical energy from a flowing plasma — offers an immediate and intellectually rich avenue for deeper exploration.
