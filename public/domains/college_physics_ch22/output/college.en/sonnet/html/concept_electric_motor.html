🌸 Electric Motor

🌸 Electric Motor

Definition. An electric motor is a device that converts electrical energy into mechanical (rotational) kinetic energy through the interaction of a current-carrying conductor with an external magnetic field. At its core, a motor exploits the magnetic force on a current-carrying loop: when a planar coil of $N$ turns, each enclosing area $A$, carries current $I$ in an external magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, it experiences a net torque

$$ \tau = NIAB\sin\theta, $$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the magnetic dipole moment $\boldsymbol{\mu} = NIA\hat{n}$ and $\mathbf{B}$. This torque rotates the coil toward the alignment position ($\theta = 0$). A commutator reverses the current direction each half-revolution, sustaining unidirectional rotation by ensuring the torque always acts in the same rotational sense.

Worked Example. A rectangular coil has $N = 200$ turns, dimensions $\ell = 0.12\ \text{m}$ by $w = 0.08\ \text{m}$, and carries $I = 3.0\ \text{A}$. It sits in a uniform field $B = 0.25\ \text{T}$. Find the maximum torque.

The area is $A = \ell w = (0.12)(0.08) = 9.6\times10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2$. Maximum torque occurs at $\theta = 90°$, so $\sin\theta = 1$:

$$ \tau_{\max} = NIAB = (200)(3.0)(9.6\times10^{-3})(0.25) = 1.44\ \text{N\cdot m}. $$

This is the peak mechanical output before the commutator switches current direction.

Key Theorem (Back-EMF Principle). A rotating motor coil also acts as a generator. By Faraday's law, the changing flux through the spinning coil induces an opposing electromotive force called the back-EMF $\varepsilon_b = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t)$, where $\omega$ is the angular velocity. Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law to the motor circuit,

$$ I = \frac{\mathcal{E} - \varepsilon_b}{R}, $$

where $\mathcal{E}$ is the supply voltage and $R$ is coil resistance. As $\omega$ increases, $\varepsilon_b$ increases, reducing $I$ and hence torque—establishing the natural self-regulation of motor speed under load.

Lab Exercise. Wind a 150-turn coil ($A = 50\ \text{cm}^2$) and place it between the poles of a permanent magnet ($B = 0.30\ \text{T}$). Connect it to a $9\ \text{V}$ supply through a known resistance $R = 6\ \Omega$. (a) Calculate $\tau_{\max}$. (b) Measure the no-load current $I_0$ and deduce the back-EMF $\varepsilon_b = \mathcal{E} - I_0 R$. (c) Estimate $\omega$ from $\varepsilon_b = NBA\omega$ and compare with a tachometer reading.
