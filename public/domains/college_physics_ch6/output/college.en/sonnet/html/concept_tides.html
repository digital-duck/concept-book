🌸 Tides

🌸 Tides

Definition. Tides are the periodic rise and fall of sea levels caused by the differential gravitational forces exerted by the Moon and, to a lesser extent, the Sun on the rotating Earth. Newton's law of universal gravitation states that the gravitational force between two masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ separated by distance $r$ is

$$ F = \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2} $$

Because the Moon's gravitational field is nonuniform across Earth's diameter, the side of Earth nearest the Moon experiences a stronger pull than the center, which in turn is pulled more strongly than the far side. This differential, or tidal force, stretches Earth's oceans into a prolate (elongated) shape, producing two tidal bulges simultaneously — one facing the Moon and one on the opposite side.

Worked Example. Estimate the tidal acceleration difference between Earth's near and far sides due to the Moon. Let $M_M = 7.35 \times 10^{22}$ kg be the lunar mass, $d = 3.84 \times 10^8$ m the Earth–Moon center-to-center distance, and $R_E = 6.37 \times 10^6$ m Earth's radius. The gravitational acceleration toward the Moon at Earth's center is

$$ g_c = \frac{GM_M}{d^2} $$

At the near side, the distance is $d - R_E$, so the differential tidal acceleration is

$$ \Delta g = \frac{GM_M}{(d-R_E)^2} - \frac{GM_M}{d^2} \approx \frac{2GM_M R_E}{d^3} $$

Substituting values: $\Delta g \approx \frac{2(6.674\times10^{-11})(7.35\times10^{22})(6.37\times10^6)}{(3.84\times10^8)^3} \approx 1.1 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{m/s}^2$, roughly one ten-millionth of surface gravity.

Key Theorem. The tidal force scales as $r^{-3}$ rather than $r^{-2}$, because it arises from the gradient of the gravitational field:

$$ F_{\text{tidal}} \approx \frac{2GM_{\text{source}} m R}{r^3} $$

where $R$ is the size of the body being deformed. This cubic dependence explains why the Moon dominates over the more massive but far more distant Sun: the Sun produces only about 46% of the Moon's tidal effect.

Lab Exercise. Using tide-table data for a coastal station, plot water height versus time over one lunar month. Identify the spring tides (near new and full Moon) and neap tides (near quarter Moon). Compute the ratio of spring-to-neap tidal amplitudes and compare your result to the theoretical prediction that spring tides occur when solar and lunar tidal forces add constructively.
