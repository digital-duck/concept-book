🌱 Units

When a pharmacist reads a prescription that says "500" without specifying milligrams or micrograms, the ambiguity is not just inconvenient — it can be life-threatening. Units are what transform a bare number into a meaningful quantity. Mathematics and science depend on them to make statements that are true regardless of who reads them.

Definition. A *unit* is a fixed, agreed-upon reference quantity used to express measurements. A *measurement* is a pair $(n, u)$ where $n$ is a dimensionless number (the *magnitude*) and $u$ is the unit. Two measurements are equal when their magnitudes agree after conversion to a common unit.

Units organize into *dimensions* — the fundamental physical categories. The SI system recognizes seven base dimensions; every derived unit is a product of powers of these:

$$[u] = \mathsf{L}^{a}\,\mathsf{M}^{b}\,\mathsf{T}^{c}\cdots$$

Worked example. Speed has dimension $\mathsf{L}\,\mathsf{T}^{-1}$. Suppose a cyclist travels $36\,\mathrm{km}$ in $1\,\mathrm{h}$. Converting:

$$36\,\mathrm{km/h} \times \frac{1000\,\mathrm{m}}{1\,\mathrm{km}} \times \frac{1\,\mathrm{h}}{3600\,\mathrm{s}} = 10\,\mathrm{m/s}$$

The conversion factors are *dimensionless ratios* equal to $1$, so multiplying by them changes the representation without changing the quantity.

Why it's true. The key invariant is *dimensional homogeneity*: in any valid equation, every term must share the same dimension. This is not a convention — it follows from the fact that physical laws must hold independently of the measurement system chosen. Adding a length to a time is undefined because no choice of units can make them commensurable.

Practice problem. A recipe calls for $2.5\,\mathrm{cups}$ of flour. Given that $1\,\mathrm{cup} = 236.6\,\mathrm{mL}$ and $1\,\mathrm{L} = 1000\,\mathrm{mL}$, express the amount in liters. What is the dimension of "volume of flour," and why does it not depend on whether you use cups or liters?
