🍃 Precision

A master watchmaker and a child with a ruler both measure the same table as "about one meter long." The child's result varies wildly between attempts; the watchmaker's repeated measurements cluster tightly around 1.003 m every single time. Both may be wrong about the true length — but only the watchmaker is precise.

Precision describes the degree of repeatability in a set of measurements: how closely multiple measurements of the same quantity agree with one another. It is a statement about the spread of results, not their accuracy. A measurement tool with fine graduations and a stable procedure yields high precision; inconsistent technique or coarse markings produces low precision.

Precision is often quantified by the uncertainty $\delta x$ associated with a measured value $x$, expressed as $x \pm \delta x$. The percent uncertainty makes comparison easier:

$$ \text{Percent uncertainty} = \frac{\delta x}{x} \times 100\% $$

Worked example. A student measures the diameter of a coin five times with a digital caliper:

$$ d = 24.26,\ 24.25,\ 24.26,\ 24.27,\ 24.26 \text{ mm} $$

The spread is only $0.02\ \text{mm}$, so we report $d = 24.26 \pm 0.01\ \text{mm}$. The percent uncertainty is:

$$ \frac{0.01}{24.26} \times 100\% \approx 0.04\% $$

That is a highly precise measurement — the readings are tightly grouped regardless of whether 24.26 mm matches the coin's "true" diameter.

Why does this matter? Precision is limited by the smallest division readable on an instrument and by random fluctuations in the measurement process. Reducing random error — by averaging many trials or using a finer instrument — improves precision. Systematic error, by contrast, shifts every reading the same way and cannot be averaged out; that is an accuracy problem, not a precision problem.

Practice problem. A student records a spring constant as $k = 45\ \text{N/m}$ with an uncertainty of $\pm 2\ \text{N/m}$. What is the percent uncertainty, and should the result be reported to the nearest whole number or to one decimal place?
