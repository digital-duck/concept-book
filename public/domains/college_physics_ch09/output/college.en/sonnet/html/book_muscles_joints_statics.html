🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. In classical mechanics, a force is a vector quantity that describes an interaction capable of changing the momentum of an object. Formally, Newton's second law defines net force as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} = m\vec{a} $$

where $\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$ is the linear momentum, $m$ is the inertial mass (assumed constant in non-relativistic contexts), and $\vec{a}$ is the acceleration of the object. The SI unit of force is the newton: $1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Force is a free vector applied at a point of contact or acting at a distance (e.g., gravity, electrostatic force), and multiple forces superpose by vector addition: $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_i \vec{F}_i$.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 5.0\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\,\hat{x} + 6\,\hat{y}\,\text{N}$. Find the acceleration.

Solution. Compute the net force component-wise:

$$ F_x = 12 + (-4) = 8\,\text{N}, \qquad F_y = 0 + 6 = 6\,\text{N} $$ $$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = 8\,\hat{x} + 6\,\hat{y}\,\text{N}, \qquad |\vec{F}_{\text{net}}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 6^2} = 10\,\text{N} $$

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8\,\hat{x} + 6\,\hat{y}}{5.0} = 1.6\,\hat{x} + 1.2\,\hat{y}\;\text{m/s}^2 $$

The magnitude of acceleration is $|\vec{a}| = 2.0\,\text{m/s}^2$ directed at $\theta = \arctan(6/8) \approx 36.9°$ above the $x$-axis.

Key Theorem (Newton's Third Law). For every force $\vec{F}_{A \to B}$ exerted by object $A$ on object $B$, there exists a reaction force $\vec{F}_{B \to A} = -\vec{F}_{A \to B}$. These forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects, so they do not cancel when analyzing a single body's free-body diagram.

🍃 Pivot Point

🍃 Pivot Point

Definition. A pivot point (also called a fulcrum or axis of rotation) is the fixed point about which a rigid body rotates when subjected to a net torque. For a body in static equilibrium or undergoing pure rotation, the pivot point serves as the reference location from which all torques are calculated. The torque $\tau$ produced by a force $\vec{F}$ applied at position $\vec{r}$ relative to the pivot is defined as

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F} $$

with magnitude $\tau = rF\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$. The choice of pivot point is mathematically arbitrary for a system in complete static equilibrium, but a judicious choice eliminates unknown reaction forces from the torque equation, greatly simplifying analysis.

Worked Example. Consider a uniform horizontal beam of length $L = 4.0\ \text{m}$ and mass $m = 20\ \text{kg}$, supported at its left end by a hinge and held horizontal by a cable attached at the right end at angle $\theta = 30°$ above the beam. A box of mass $M = 50\ \text{kg}$ rests at $x = 3.0\ \text{m}$ from the hinge. Find the tension $T$ in the cable.

Choose the hinge as the pivot point. The hinge exerts an unknown reaction force, but by placing the pivot there, that force contributes zero torque (moment arm $= 0$). Applying $\sum \tau = 0$:

$$ T\sin(30°)\cdot L - Mg\cdot x - mg\cdot\frac{L}{2} = 0 $$ $$ T(0.500)(4.0) = (50)(9.8)(3.0) + (20)(9.8)(2.0) $$ $$ 2T = 1470 + 392 = 1862\ \text{N·m} $$ $$ T = 931\ \text{N} $$

Key Theorem. For a rigid body in static equilibrium, the sum of all torques about any pivot point must vanish:

$$ \sum_i \tau_i = 0 $$

This follows from Newton's second law for rotation: $\sum \tau = I\alpha$, which equals zero when $\alpha = 0$. The theorem guarantees that equilibrium is pivot-independent — a powerful consistency check.

🍃 Perpendicular Lever Arm

🍃 Perpendicular Lever Arm

Definition. The perpendicular lever arm (also called the moment arm) of a force $\vec{F}$ with respect to a pivot point $O$ is the perpendicular distance $r_\perp$ from $O$ to the line of action of $\vec{F}$. Formally, if $\vec{r}$ is the position vector from $O$ to any point on the line of action, and $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$, then

$$ r_\perp = r\sin\theta, $$

where $r = |\vec{r}|$. The torque produced by the force about $O$ is then

$$ \tau = F\, r_\perp = rF\sin\theta, $$

which is the magnitude of the cross product $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$. The sign of $\tau$ is positive for counterclockwise rotation and negative for clockwise rotation (using the right-hand rule in three dimensions).

Worked Example. A mechanic applies a force $F = 80\,\text{N}$ to a wrench handle at a point $r = 0.30\,\text{m}$ from the bolt center. The force is directed at $\theta = 60°$ from the handle axis. Find the torque about the bolt.

$$ r_\perp = r\sin\theta = (0.30\,\text{m})\sin 60° = (0.30)(0.866) \approx 0.260\,\text{m}. $$ $$ \tau = F\,r_\perp = (80\,\text{N})(0.260\,\text{m}) \approx 20.8\,\text{N·m}. $$

Equivalently, $\tau = rF\sin\theta = (0.30)(80)(0.866) \approx 20.8\,\text{N·m}$. Note that applying the force perpendicular to the handle ($\theta = 90°$) would maximize the torque to $\tau_{\max} = rF = 24\,\text{N·m}$, confirming that $r_\perp = r$ only when the force is perpendicular to the position vector.

Key Theorem. The torque of a force about a point depends only on its perpendicular lever arm, not on where along the line of action the force is applied. Any two forces sharing the same line of action produce identical torques about a given pivot — a consequence of the geometric definition of $r_\perp$ as a property of the line, not the specific point of application.

🌱 Net External Force

🌱 Net External Force

Definition. The net external force acting on a system is the vector sum of all forces exerted on that system by agents outside its boundaries. For a system of particles with total mass $M$, Newton's second law states

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_{i}^{\text{ext}} = M\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}, $$

where $\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}$ is the acceleration of the system's center of mass. Internal forces—those between particles within the system—appear in equal and opposite pairs by Newton's third law and therefore cancel identically in the sum, contributing nothing to $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$. This cancellation reflects the physical principle that a system cannot accelerate itself through purely internal interactions.

Worked Example. Two blocks, $m_1 = 3\,\text{kg}$ and $m_2 = 5\,\text{kg}$, rest on a frictionless horizontal surface and are connected by a light string. A horizontal force $\vec{F} = 16\,\text{N}$ is applied to $m_2$. Treating both blocks together as the system (total mass $M = 8\,\text{kg}$), the tension $T$ in the string is an internal force and drops out of the net-force calculation. The only external horizontal force is $\vec{F}$, so

$$ a_{\text{cm}} = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{M} = \frac{16\,\text{N}}{8\,\text{kg}} = 2\,\text{m/s}^2. $$

To find $T$, isolate $m_1$ as a sub-system: the sole external horizontal force on it is $T$, giving $T = m_1 a = (3\,\text{kg})(2\,\text{m/s}^2) = 6\,\text{N}$.

Key Theorem — Momentum–Force Relation. The net external force equals the time rate of change of the system's total linear momentum $\vec{p} = M\vec{v}_{\text{cm}}$:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}. $$

This formulation is more general than $\vec{F} = M\vec{a}$ because it remains valid even when the system's mass changes with time, as in rocket propulsion. When $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{0}$, it follows immediately that $\vec{p}$ is constant—the conservation of linear momentum.

🍃 Torque

🍃 Torque

Definition. Torque is the rotational analog of force, quantifying the tendency of an applied force to produce angular acceleration about a specified pivot point. Given a force $\vec{F}$ applied at a position vector $\vec{r}$ measured from the pivot, the torque is defined as the cross product

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}. $$

The magnitude is $\tau = rF\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$. The SI unit of torque is the newton-meter ($\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$). The perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force is called the moment arm $\ell = r\sin\theta$, so equivalently $\tau = F\ell$. The direction of $\vec{\tau}$ is given by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand from $\vec{r}$ toward $\vec{F}$; the extended thumb points in the direction of $\vec{\tau}$.

Worked Example. A mechanic applies a force of $F = 80\,\text{N}$ perpendicular to the end of a wrench of length $r = 0.35\,\text{m}$. Since $\theta = 90°$, the torque about the bolt is

$$ \tau = rF\sin 90° = (0.35\,\text{m})(80\,\text{N})(1) = 28\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}. $$

If the same force were applied at $\theta = 30°$ instead, the torque would be $\tau = (0.35)(80)\sin 30° = 14\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$, half the previous value, illustrating that only the component of force perpendicular to $\vec{r}$ contributes to rotation.

Key Theorem — Newton's Second Law for Rotation. For a rigid body, the net torque about a fixed axis equals the product of the moment of inertia $I$ and the angular acceleration $\alpha$:

$$ \sum \tau = I\alpha. $$

This relation is the rotational counterpart of $\sum F = ma$. Moment of inertia $I$ encodes how mass is distributed about the rotation axis and is the single new primitive introduced here; once $I$ is known, this equation governs how quickly an object's angular velocity changes under an applied net torque.

Verify your prediction experimentally and compute the percentage error between the theoretical and measured positions.

🍃 Equilibrium

🍃 Equilibrium

Definition. A rigid body is in mechanical equilibrium when both its translational and rotational motion remain constant—equivalently, when the net force and net torque acting on it simultaneously vanish:

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}, \qquad \sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}. $$

The first condition ensures zero linear acceleration ($\vec{a} = \vec{0}$), while the second ensures zero angular acceleration ($\alpha = 0$). An object satisfying both conditions may be at rest or moving at constant velocity and constant angular velocity; in most introductory contexts, the body is at rest and the analysis focuses on the static case.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ and length $L = 4\,\text{m}$ is pinned to a wall at its left end and supported by a cable attached at its right end, making an angle $\theta = 30°$ above horizontal. Find the tension $T$ in the cable and the reaction force at the pin.

Choose the pivot at the pin to eliminate the pin reaction from the torque equation. The beam's weight $W = mg = (20)(9.8) = 196\,\text{N}$ acts downward at the midpoint $L/2$. Setting net torque about the pin to zero:

$$ T \sin\theta \cdot L - W \cdot \frac{L}{2} = 0 \implies T = \frac{W}{2\sin\theta} = \frac{196}{2\sin 30°} = 196\,\text{N}. $$

Applying the force conditions: horizontal, $R_x = T\cos 30° \approx 169.7\,\text{N}$; vertical, $R_y = W - T\sin 30° = 196 - 98 = 98\,\text{N}$.

Key Theorem. The torque balance is pivot-independent for a body in complete equilibrium: if $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, then $\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$ computed about any point $O$ implies $\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$ about every other point $O'$. This follows because the additional torque from shifting the pivot equals $\vec{r}_{OO'} \times \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$. Strategically placing the pivot at an unknown force's point of application eliminates that unknown from the torque equation, simplifying algebraic work.

🍃 Mechanical Advantage

🍃 Mechanical Advantage

Definition. The mechanical advantage (MA) of a simple machine is the ratio of the output force it exerts on a load to the input force applied by an agent:

$$ MA = \frac{F_{\text{out}}}{F_{\text{in}}} $$

For an ideal (frictionless, massless) machine, energy conservation requires that the work input equal the work output. Since work equals force times displacement, $W = Fd$, this constraint links force and displacement:

$$ F_{\text{in}}\, d_{\text{in}} = F_{\text{out}}\, d_{\text{out}} $$

It follows immediately that for an ideal machine:

$$ MA_{\text{ideal}} = \frac{F_{\text{out}}}{F_{\text{in}}} = \frac{d_{\text{in}}}{d_{\text{out}}} $$

A machine with $MA > 1$ amplifies force at the cost of displacement; one with $MA < 1$ amplifies displacement at the cost of requiring a larger input force.

Worked Example. A worker uses a lever of total length $3.0\ \text{m}$ to lift a crate weighing $900\ \text{N}$. The fulcrum is placed $0.50\ \text{m}$ from the load end. Find the minimum input force required and the mechanical advantage.

The effort arm is $d_{\text{in}} = 3.0 - 0.50 = 2.5\ \text{m}$, and the load arm is $d_{\text{out}} = 0.50\ \text{m}$. Applying torque balance about the fulcrum:

$$ F_{\text{in}}\,(2.5\ \text{m}) = (900\ \text{N})(0.50\ \text{m}) $$ $$ F_{\text{in}} = \frac{450}{2.5} = 180\ \text{N} $$

The mechanical advantage is:

$$ MA = \frac{F_{\text{out}}}{F_{\text{in}}} = \frac{900\ \text{N}}{180\ \text{N}} = 5.0 $$

Equivalently, $MA = d_{\text{in}}/d_{\text{out}} = 2.5/0.50 = 5.0$, confirming energy conservation.

Key Theorem. For any real machine subject to friction, the actual mechanical advantage satisfies $MA_{\text{actual}} < MA_{\text{ideal}}$. The efficiency $\eta$ of the machine is defined as:

$$ \eta = \frac{W_{\text{out}}}{W_{\text{in}}} = \frac{MA_{\text{actual}}}{MA_{\text{ideal}}} < 1 $$

Measuring both $MA_{\text{actual}}$ and $MA_{\text{ideal}}$ therefore fully characterizes a machine's performance, capturing both its force-amplification capability and its energy losses.

🍃 First Condition Equilibrium

🍃 First Condition Equilibrium

Definition. A rigid body is said to be in translational equilibrium when the vector sum of all external forces acting on it vanishes. This requirement is called the first condition of equilibrium and is expressed mathematically as

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}, $$

or, resolving into Cartesian components,

$$ \sum F_x = 0, \qquad \sum F_y = 0, \qquad \sum F_z = 0. $$

This condition is a direct consequence of Newton's second law: if $\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$ and the body is not accelerating ($\vec{a} = \vec{0}$), then the net force must be zero. Note that translational equilibrium does not preclude rotation; a body may simultaneously satisfy the first condition while still possessing a net torque, which is why a second condition (zero net torque) must be independently imposed for complete static equilibrium.

Worked Example. A traffic light of mass $m = 15\,\text{kg}$ hangs from two cables making angles $\theta_1 = 30°$ and $\theta_2 = 45°$ with the horizontal. Find the tension in each cable.

Let $T_1$ and $T_2$ denote the respective tensions. Applying the first condition:

$$ \sum F_x = 0: \quad -T_1\cos 30° + T_2\cos 45° = 0 $$ $$ \sum F_y = 0: \quad T_1\sin 30° + T_2\sin 45° - mg = 0. $$

From the $x$-equation, $T_1 = T_2\dfrac{\cos 45°}{\cos 30°}$. Substituting into the $y$-equation with $mg = (15)(9.8) = 147\,\text{N}$ yields $T_2 \approx 108\,\text{N}$ and $T_1 \approx 88\,\text{N}$.

Key Theorem. For a particle or a body modeled as a point mass, satisfying $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$ is both necessary and sufficient for equilibrium. For an extended rigid body, it is necessary but not sufficient; the second condition $\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$ must also hold.

🍃 Second Condition Equilibrium

🍃 Second Condition of Equilibrium

Definition. An object is in rotational equilibrium when the net torque acting on it about any chosen pivot point is zero. This requirement is the second condition of equilibrium:

$$ \sum \tau = 0 $$

Recall that each torque $\tau = rF\sin\theta$ has already been established; here, counterclockwise torques are taken as positive and clockwise torques as negative. Together with the first condition ($\sum \mathbf{F} = 0$), this condition guarantees complete static equilibrium.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ and length $L = 4.0\,\text{m}$ is attached to a wall at its left end by a hinge and supported by a cable at angle $\theta = 30°$ attached at the right end. A sign of mass $M = 10\,\text{kg}$ hangs from the beam's midpoint. Find the tension $T$ in the cable.

Choose the hinge as the pivot to eliminate the unknown hinge reaction forces. The torques acting are:

  • Weight of beam: $\tau_1 = -mg(L/2) = -(20)(9.8)(2.0) = -392\,\text{N·m}$
  • Weight of sign: $\tau_2 = -Mg(L/2) = -(10)(9.8)(2.0) = -196\,\text{N·m}$
  • Cable tension (vertical component acts at $x = L$): $\tau_3 = +T\sin 30°\cdot L = +T(0.5)(4.0) = +2T$

Applying $\sum \tau = 0$:

$$ 2T - 392 - 196 = 0 \implies T = \frac{588}{2} = 294\,\text{N} $$

Key Theorem. The second condition of equilibrium is pivot-independent for any object satisfying the first condition. If $\sum \mathbf{F} = 0$ and $\sum \tau = 0$ about one point, then $\sum \tau = 0$ about every point. This follows because shifting the pivot by vector $\mathbf{d}$ adds a term $\mathbf{d} \times \sum \mathbf{F}$, which vanishes identically when net force is zero.

Predict the net torque, then add a third mass at the 90 cm mark to restore equilibrium. Verify your prediction by observing whether the stick remains horizontal, and compare the calculated mass to the actual mass used.

🍃 Fulcrum

🍃 Fulcrum

Definition. A fulcrum is the fixed pivot point about which a lever rotates when a torque is applied. In the context of static equilibrium and simple machines, the fulcrum defines the axis of rotation for the system. Its position relative to the applied force and the load determines the mechanical advantage of the lever. For a rigid beam resting on a fulcrum, the condition for rotational equilibrium requires that the net torque about the fulcrum vanishes:

$$ \sum \tau = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad F_1 d_1 = F_2 d_2 $$

where $F_1$ and $F_2$ are the magnitudes of forces applied at perpendicular distances $d_1$ and $d_2$ from the fulcrum, respectively.

Worked Example. A uniform beam of negligible mass and length $L = 3.0\ \text{m}$ is balanced on a fulcrum placed $1.0\ \text{m}$ from the left end. A child of weight $W_1 = 400\ \text{N}$ sits at the left end. Determine the weight $W_2$ that must be placed at the right end to maintain equilibrium.

Taking the fulcrum as the pivot, $d_1 = 1.0\ \text{m}$ (left arm) and $d_2 = 2.0\ \text{m}$ (right arm). Applying the torque balance:

$$ W_1 d_1 = W_2 d_2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad (400\ \text{N})(1.0\ \text{m}) = W_2 (2.0\ \text{m}) $$ $$ W_2 = \frac{400}{2.0} = 200\ \text{N} $$

The mechanical advantage is $MA = d_2 / d_1 = 2.0$, meaning the load on the longer arm is half the effort force.

Key Theorem. For any lever in static equilibrium, the net torque about the fulcrum is zero. This follows directly from Newton's second law for rotation, $\sum \tau = I\alpha$: when the system is in equilibrium, the angular acceleration $\alpha = 0$, so $\sum \tau = 0$. Repositioning the fulcrum alters both moment arms simultaneously, scaling mechanical advantage as $MA = d_{\text{load}} / d_{\text{effort}}$. A fulcrum closer to the load increases mechanical advantage, allowing a smaller effort force to balance a larger load.

🍃 Simple Machine

🍃 Simple Machine

A simple machine is a mechanical device that redirects or amplifies an applied force to perform useful work, doing so without any internal energy source. Formally, a simple machine is characterized by its mechanical advantage (MA), defined as the ratio of the output force $F_{\text{out}}$ to the input force $F_{\text{in}}$:

$$ \text{MA} = \frac{F_{\text{out}}}{F_{\text{in}}} $$

The six classical simple machines—lever, wheel and axle, pulley, inclined plane, wedge, and screw—all operate on the same energy conservation principle: in the ideal (frictionless) case, the work input equals the work output,

$$ W_{\text{in}} = W_{\text{out}} \implies F_{\text{in}} \, d_{\text{in}} = F_{\text{out}} \, d_{\text{out}} $$

where $d_{\text{in}}$ and $d_{\text{out}}$ are the respective displacement distances. A larger mechanical advantage is therefore achieved at the cost of a proportionally greater input displacement.

Worked Example. Consider an ideal lever with a fulcrum placed such that the effort arm has length $\ell_1 = 2.0\,\text{m}$ and the load arm has length $\ell_2 = 0.5\,\text{m}$. A load of $F_{\text{out}} = 400\,\text{N}$ rests on the short end. The torque balance about the fulcrum gives:

$$ F_{\text{in}} \, \ell_1 = F_{\text{out}} \, \ell_2 \implies F_{\text{in}} = \frac{400 \times 0.5}{2.0} = 100\,\text{N} $$

The mechanical advantage is $\text{MA} = 400/100 = 4$. To lift the load by $\Delta y = 0.10\,\text{m}$, the effort end must move $d_{\text{in}} = 4 \times 0.10 = 0.40\,\text{m}$, confirming $W_{\text{in}} = W_{\text{out}} = 40\,\text{J}$.

Key Theorem — Conservation of Energy Constraint. For any real simple machine, friction reduces efficiency below unity: $\eta = W_{\text{out}}/W_{\text{in}} < 1$. Consequently, the actual mechanical advantage $\text{MA}_{\text{actual}} = \eta \cdot \text{MA}_{\text{ideal}}$. No arrangement of simple machines can create energy; they can only transform the manner in which a force is applied.

Calculate $\text{MA}_{\text{actual}}$, compare with the theoretical value of $\text{MA}_{\text{ideal}} = 2$, and determine the pulley system's efficiency $\eta$.

🍃 Lever

🍃 Lever

Definition. A lever is a rigid bar constrained to rotate about a fixed point called the fulcrum. Forces applied at different points along the bar produce torques about the fulcrum; in static equilibrium, the net torque vanishes. If a force $F_1$ is applied at perpendicular distance $r_1$ from the fulcrum and a load $F_2$ acts at distance $r_2$, the equilibrium condition is

$$ \tau_{\text{net}} = 0 \implies F_1 r_1 = F_2 r_2. $$

The ratio $r_1 / r_2$ defines the mechanical advantage (MA):

$$ \text{MA} = \frac{F_2}{F_1} = \frac{r_1}{r_2}. $$

A lever is classified by the relative positions of the fulcrum, effort, and load: Class 1 places the fulcrum between effort and load (e.g., a seesaw); Class 2 places the load between fulcrum and effort (e.g., a wheelbarrow); Class 3 places the effort between fulcrum and load (e.g., a forearm lifting a weight).

Worked Example. A uniform lever of negligible mass has its fulcrum $0.30\ \text{m}$ from one end. A child weighing $F_2 = 400\ \text{N}$ sits at that end. Determine the effort force $F_1$ required at the opposite end, which is $1.20\ \text{m}$ from the fulcrum.

Applying the torque equilibrium condition:

$$ F_1 \cdot 1.20\ \text{m} = 400\ \text{N} \cdot 0.30\ \text{m} $$ $$ F_1 = \frac{400 \times 0.30}{1.20} = 100\ \text{N}. $$

The mechanical advantage is $\text{MA} = 400/100 = 4$, meaning the effort is four times smaller than the load.

Key Theorem. The principle of the lever is a direct consequence of the condition for rotational equilibrium, $\sum \tau = 0$, applied to a rigid body with one degree of rotational freedom. Because work equals force times displacement, and the arc displacement of each point is proportional to its distance from the fulcrum, the work done by the effort equals the work done against the load: $F_1 d_1 = F_2 d_2$. A lever therefore conserves energy while redistributing force and displacement.

🌸 Statics

🌸 Statics

Definition. Statics is the branch of classical mechanics concerned with bodies that are in static equilibrium — that is, bodies that remain at rest or move with constant velocity (typically rest) under the action of external forces and torques. For a rigid body to be in static equilibrium, two independent conditions must be satisfied simultaneously:

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0} \qquad \text{(translational equilibrium)} $$ $$ \sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0} \qquad \text{(rotational equilibrium)} $$

where the torques may be computed about any convenient pivot point. These conditions follow directly from Newton's second law applied in the limit $\vec{a} = \vec{0}$ and $\vec{\alpha} = \vec{0}$.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ and length $L = 4.0\,\text{m}$ is pinned to a wall at its left end and supported by a cable attached at the right end, making an angle $\theta = 30°$ above horizontal. Find the tension $T$ in the cable and the reaction force at the pin.

Taking torques about the pin eliminates the unknown pin forces:

$$ \sum \tau_{\text{pin}} = T \sin\theta \cdot L - mg \cdot \frac{L}{2} = 0 $$ $$ T = \frac{mg}{2\sin\theta} = \frac{(20)(9.8)}{2\sin 30°} = \frac{196}{1.0} = 196\,\text{N} $$

Applying translational equilibrium:

$$ R_x = T\cos\theta = 196\cos 30° \approx 170\,\text{N}, \qquad R_y = mg - T\sin\theta = 196 - 98 = 98\,\text{N} $$

The pin exerts a resultant force $R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2} \approx 197\,\text{N}$.

Key Theorem — Principle of Moments. For a body in rotational equilibrium, the sum of all clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of all counterclockwise moments about that same point. Crucially, the choice of pivot is arbitrary; selecting it at an unknown force's line of action eliminates that unknown from the torque equation, simplifying the algebra.

🌸 Muscles Joints Statics

🌸 Muscles Joints Statics

Definition. Muscles, joints, and the skeletal framework constitute a system of levers governed by the principles of static equilibrium. A body segment in static equilibrium satisfies two vector conditions simultaneously: the net force vanishes, $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, and the net torque about any chosen pivot vanishes, $\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$. Because muscles typically attach close to the joint (short moment arm $r_m$), they must exert forces far larger than the external load to maintain balance. The joint reaction force $\vec{F}_J$ then absorbs the difference, often exceeding body weight by a substantial multiple.

The forearm held horizontal while supporting a weight $W_L$ in the hand illustrates this lever geometry. Let $d_m$ be the perpendicular distance from the elbow joint to the bicep insertion point, and $d_L$ the distance from the joint to the load. Taking torques about the elbow joint eliminates the unknown joint force from the equation:

$$ F_m \, d_m = W_L \, d_L + W_a \, d_a $$

where $W_a$ is the forearm's weight acting at its center of mass, distance $d_a$ from the elbow.

Worked Example. A person holds a $W_L = 50\,\text{N}$ dumbbell with the forearm horizontal. Given $d_m = 0.04\,\text{m}$, $d_L = 0.35\,\text{m}$, $W_a = 16\,\text{N}$, and $d_a = 0.15\,\text{m}$, find the bicep force and the joint reaction force.

Applying torque equilibrium about the elbow:

$$ F_m = \frac{W_L d_L + W_a d_a}{d_m} = \frac{(50)(0.35) + (16)(0.15)}{0.04} = \frac{17.5 + 2.4}{0.04} = 498\,\text{N} $$

Force equilibrium along the vertical axis gives:

$$ F_J = F_m - W_L - W_a = 498 - 50 - 16 = 432\,\text{N} $$

The joint must withstand more than eight times the load weight.

Key Theorem. For a musculoskeletal lever with $d_m \operatorname{ll} d_L$, torque equilibrium requires $F_m \approx W_L(d_L/d_m)$, so the muscle force scales linearly with load distance and inversely with insertion distance. This geometric constraint explains why small changes in tendon attachment position have large consequences for the forces experienced by both muscle and joint.

Payoff 🎯

The study of static equilibrium reaches its most compelling expression in the mechanics of the human musculoskeletal system. Muscles, joints, and the principles of statics converge to explain how biological structures support loads, transmit forces, and maintain postural stability — all without violating Newton's laws. This capstone concept is the natural endpoint of Chapter 9 because it demands the full toolkit developed throughout: free-body diagrams, torque balance, and the simultaneous satisfaction of $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$ and $\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$. Unlike idealized rigid rods, the human body presents lever systems with short moment arms, meaning muscles must generate forces that are large multiples of the external load they resist.

Definition and structure. A joint is the fulcrum; the muscle insertion point is the effort arm; the center of mass of the limb (plus any held object) defines the load arm. For the forearm held horizontal, with the bicep inserting at distance $d_m$ from the elbow and a load of weight $W_L$ held at distance $d_L$, torque balance about the elbow gives

$$ F_m \, d_m = W_L \, d_L + W_{\text{arm}} \, d_{\text{cm}} $$

where $d_{\text{cm}}$ is the distance from the elbow to the forearm's center of mass. Because $d_m \operatorname{ll} d_L$, the required muscle force $F_m$ far exceeds $W_L$, and the joint reaction force $F_j = F_m - W_L - W_{\text{arm}}$ can be several times body weight even for modest loads.

Worked example. Suppose $d_m = 4\,\text{cm}$, $d_L = 35\,\text{cm}$, $W_L = 50\,\text{N}$, $W_{\text{arm}} = 16\,\text{N}$, and $d_{\text{cm}} = 15\,\text{cm}$. Then $F_m = (50 \times 0.35 + 16 \times 0.15)/0.04 = 497.5\,\text{N}$, and the compressive load on the elbow joint is approximately $431\,\text{N}$ — nearly nine times the external weight.

Key theorem. The mechanical disadvantage inherent in biological levers is not a design flaw; it is traded for speed and range of motion at the end effector, a principle formalized by the torque–velocity relationship of muscle physiology.
