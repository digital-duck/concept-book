🍃 Fulcrum

🍃 Fulcrum

Definition. A fulcrum is the fixed pivot point about which a lever rotates when a torque is applied. In the context of static equilibrium and simple machines, the fulcrum defines the axis of rotation for the system. Its position relative to the applied force and the load determines the mechanical advantage of the lever. For a rigid beam resting on a fulcrum, the condition for rotational equilibrium requires that the net torque about the fulcrum vanishes:

$$ \sum \tau = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad F_1 d_1 = F_2 d_2 $$

where $F_1$ and $F_2$ are the magnitudes of forces applied at perpendicular distances $d_1$ and $d_2$ from the fulcrum, respectively.

Worked Example. A uniform beam of negligible mass and length $L = 3.0\ \text{m}$ is balanced on a fulcrum placed $1.0\ \text{m}$ from the left end. A child of weight $W_1 = 400\ \text{N}$ sits at the left end. Determine the weight $W_2$ that must be placed at the right end to maintain equilibrium.

Taking the fulcrum as the pivot, $d_1 = 1.0\ \text{m}$ (left arm) and $d_2 = 2.0\ \text{m}$ (right arm). Applying the torque balance:

$$ W_1 d_1 = W_2 d_2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad (400\ \text{N})(1.0\ \text{m}) = W_2 (2.0\ \text{m}) $$ $$ W_2 = \frac{400}{2.0} = 200\ \text{N} $$

The mechanical advantage is $MA = d_2 / d_1 = 2.0$, meaning the load on the longer arm is half the effort force.

Key Theorem. For any lever in static equilibrium, the net torque about the fulcrum is zero. This follows directly from Newton's second law for rotation, $\sum \tau = I\alpha$: when the system is in equilibrium, the angular acceleration $\alpha = 0$, so $\sum \tau = 0$. Repositioning the fulcrum alters both moment arms simultaneously, scaling mechanical advantage as $MA = d_{\text{load}} / d_{\text{effort}}$. A fulcrum closer to the load increases mechanical advantage, allowing a smaller effort force to balance a larger load.

Lab Exercise. Using a meter stick, a triangular pivot block, and calibrated masses, locate the fulcrum position that balances a 200 g mass at the 10 cm mark against a 100 g mass. Predict the required fulcrum position theoretically using $m_1 d_1 = m_2 d_2$, then verify experimentally. Record the percentage error between prediction and measurement, and identify sources of systematic deviation such as nonuniform mass distribution along the beam.
