🍃 Torque

🍃 Torque

Definition. Torque is the rotational analog of force, quantifying the tendency of an applied force to produce angular acceleration about a specified pivot point. Given a force $\vec{F}$ applied at a position vector $\vec{r}$ measured from the pivot, the torque is defined as the cross product

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}. $$

The magnitude is $\tau = rF\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$. The SI unit of torque is the newton-meter ($\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$). The perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force is called the moment arm $\ell = r\sin\theta$, so equivalently $\tau = F\ell$. The direction of $\vec{\tau}$ is given by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand from $\vec{r}$ toward $\vec{F}$; the extended thumb points in the direction of $\vec{\tau}$.

Worked Example. A mechanic applies a force of $F = 80\,\text{N}$ perpendicular to the end of a wrench of length $r = 0.35\,\text{m}$. Since $\theta = 90°$, the torque about the bolt is

$$ \tau = rF\sin 90° = (0.35\,\text{m})(80\,\text{N})(1) = 28\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}. $$

If the same force were applied at $\theta = 30°$ instead, the torque would be $\tau = (0.35)(80)\sin 30° = 14\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$, half the previous value, illustrating that only the component of force perpendicular to $\vec{r}$ contributes to rotation.

Key Theorem — Newton's Second Law for Rotation. For a rigid body, the net torque about a fixed axis equals the product of the moment of inertia $I$ and the angular acceleration $\alpha$:

$$ \sum \tau = I\alpha. $$

This relation is the rotational counterpart of $\sum F = ma$. Moment of inertia $I$ encodes how mass is distributed about the rotation axis and is the single new primitive introduced here; once $I$ is known, this equation governs how quickly an object's angular velocity changes under an applied net torque.

Lab Exercise. Mount a uniform meter stick horizontally on a pivot at its center. Hang a mass $m_1 = 200\,\text{g}$ at the $20\,\text{cm}$ mark and determine the position $x$ at which a second mass $m_2 = 150\,\text{g}$ must be placed on the opposite side to achieve rotational equilibrium (net torque = 0). Verify your prediction experimentally and compute the percentage error between the theoretical and measured positions.
