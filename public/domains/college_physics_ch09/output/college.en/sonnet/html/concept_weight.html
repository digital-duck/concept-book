🌱 Weight

🌱 Weight

Definition. Weight is the gravitational force exerted on an object by a massive body, typically Earth. For an object of mass $m$ near Earth's surface, where the local gravitational field strength is $g \approx 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$, weight is defined as

$$ \vec{w} = m\vec{g} $$

where $\vec{g}$ points toward the center of the Earth. Weight is a force, measured in newtons (N), and must be carefully distinguished from mass, which is a scalar measure of inertia measured in kilograms (kg). Mass is an intrinsic property of an object, whereas weight depends on the local gravitational environment — a distinction that is the single most important conceptual point in this section.

Worked Example. A student of mass $m = 65.0\ \text{kg}$ stands on the surface of the Earth. Determine their weight.

Applying the definition directly:

$$ w = mg = (65.0\ \text{kg})(9.80\ \text{m/s}^2) = 637\ \text{N} $$

Now suppose the same student stands on the Moon, where $g_{\text{Moon}} \approx 1.62\ \text{m/s}^2$. Their mass remains $65.0\ \text{kg}$, but their weight becomes

$$ w_{\text{Moon}} = (65.0\ \text{kg})(1.62\ \text{m/s}^2) = 105\ \text{N} $$

This illustrates that weight is location-dependent while mass is not.

Key Theorem (Newton's Second Law Applied to Free Fall). When weight is the only force acting on an object — that is, when air resistance and all contact forces are absent — Newton's second law gives

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} \implies m\vec{g} = m\vec{a} \implies \vec{a} = \vec{g} $$

All objects, regardless of mass, fall with the same acceleration $\vec{g}$ under pure gravitational influence. This result, experimentally confirmed by Galileo, follows directly from the equivalence of gravitational and inertial mass.

Lab Exercise. Using a spring scale calibrated in newtons, measure the weight of five objects of different materials (e.g., aluminum block, rubber stopper, steel washer). For each object, independently determine its mass using a balance. Compute the ratio $w/m$ for each and compare to the accepted value of $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$. Quantify your percent error and propose two sources of systematic uncertainty in the spring-scale measurement.
