🍃 Mechanical Advantage

🍃 Mechanical Advantage

Definition. The mechanical advantage (MA) of a simple machine is the ratio of the output force it exerts on a load to the input force applied by an agent:

$$ MA = \frac{F_{\text{out}}}{F_{\text{in}}} $$

For an ideal (frictionless, massless) machine, energy conservation requires that the work input equal the work output. Since work equals force times displacement, $W = Fd$, this constraint links force and displacement:

$$ F_{\text{in}}\, d_{\text{in}} = F_{\text{out}}\, d_{\text{out}} $$

It follows immediately that for an ideal machine:

$$ MA_{\text{ideal}} = \frac{F_{\text{out}}}{F_{\text{in}}} = \frac{d_{\text{in}}}{d_{\text{out}}} $$

A machine with $MA > 1$ amplifies force at the cost of displacement; one with $MA < 1$ amplifies displacement at the cost of requiring a larger input force.

Worked Example. A worker uses a lever of total length $3.0\ \text{m}$ to lift a crate weighing $900\ \text{N}$. The fulcrum is placed $0.50\ \text{m}$ from the load end. Find the minimum input force required and the mechanical advantage.

The effort arm is $d_{\text{in}} = 3.0 - 0.50 = 2.5\ \text{m}$, and the load arm is $d_{\text{out}} = 0.50\ \text{m}$. Applying torque balance about the fulcrum:

$$ F_{\text{in}}\,(2.5\ \text{m}) = (900\ \text{N})(0.50\ \text{m}) $$ $$ F_{\text{in}} = \frac{450}{2.5} = 180\ \text{N} $$

The mechanical advantage is:

$$ MA = \frac{F_{\text{out}}}{F_{\text{in}}} = \frac{900\ \text{N}}{180\ \text{N}} = 5.0 $$

Equivalently, $MA = d_{\text{in}}/d_{\text{out}} = 2.5/0.50 = 5.0$, confirming energy conservation.

Key Theorem. For any real machine subject to friction, the actual mechanical advantage satisfies $MA_{\text{actual}} < MA_{\text{ideal}}$. The efficiency $\eta$ of the machine is defined as:

$$ \eta = \frac{W_{\text{out}}}{W_{\text{in}}} = \frac{MA_{\text{actual}}}{MA_{\text{ideal}}} < 1 $$

Measuring both $MA_{\text{actual}}$ and $MA_{\text{ideal}}$ therefore fully characterizes a machine's performance, capturing both its force-amplification capability and its energy losses.

Lab Exercise. Assemble a single movable pulley system. Hang a load of known weight $F_{\text{out}}$ and measure the force $F_{\text{in}}$ needed to raise it at constant velocity using a spring scale. Record the rope lengths moved on each side to compute $MA_{\text{ideal}} = d_{\text{in}}/d_{\text{out}}$. Compare with the measured $MA_{\text{actual}} = F_{\text{out}}/F_{\text{in}}$ and calculate the system efficiency $\eta$. Repeat with an additional fixed pulley and discuss how routing the rope changes $MA_{\text{ideal}}$ without altering the load.
