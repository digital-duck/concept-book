🍃 Perpendicular Lever Arm

🍃 Perpendicular Lever Arm

Definition. The perpendicular lever arm (also called the moment arm) of a force $\vec{F}$ with respect to a pivot point $O$ is the perpendicular distance $r_\perp$ from $O$ to the line of action of $\vec{F}$. Formally, if $\vec{r}$ is the position vector from $O$ to any point on the line of action, and $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$, then

$$ r_\perp = r\sin\theta, $$

where $r = |\vec{r}|$. The torque produced by the force about $O$ is then

$$ \tau = F\, r_\perp = rF\sin\theta, $$

which is the magnitude of the cross product $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$. The sign of $\tau$ is positive for counterclockwise rotation and negative for clockwise rotation (using the right-hand rule in three dimensions).

Worked Example. A mechanic applies a force $F = 80\,\text{N}$ to a wrench handle at a point $r = 0.30\,\text{m}$ from the bolt center. The force is directed at $\theta = 60°$ from the handle axis. Find the torque about the bolt.

$$ r_\perp = r\sin\theta = (0.30\,\text{m})\sin 60° = (0.30)(0.866) \approx 0.260\,\text{m}. $$ $$ \tau = F\,r_\perp = (80\,\text{N})(0.260\,\text{m}) \approx 20.8\,\text{N·m}. $$

Equivalently, $\tau = rF\sin\theta = (0.30)(80)(0.866) \approx 20.8\,\text{N·m}$. Note that applying the force perpendicular to the handle ($\theta = 90°$) would maximize the torque to $\tau_{\max} = rF = 24\,\text{N·m}$, confirming that $r_\perp = r$ only when the force is perpendicular to the position vector.

Key Theorem. The torque of a force about a point depends only on its perpendicular lever arm, not on where along the line of action the force is applied. Any two forces sharing the same line of action produce identical torques about a given pivot — a consequence of the geometric definition of $r_\perp$ as a property of the line, not the specific point of application.

Lab Exercise. Mount a uniform meter stick horizontally on a pivot at the 50 cm mark. Hang a 200 g mass at the 20 cm mark. At what position must a 150 g mass be placed on the opposite side of the pivot to achieve rotational equilibrium? Apply $\sum \tau = 0$, computing each torque using $\tau = mg\,r_\perp$, and verify your predicted position experimentally by observing whether the stick remains level.
