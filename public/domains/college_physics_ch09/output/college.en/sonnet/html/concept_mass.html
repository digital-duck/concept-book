🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. Mass is a fundamental scalar quantity that measures the amount of matter contained in an object and quantifies its resistance to changes in translational motion. In classical mechanics, mass appears in two distinct but experimentally equivalent roles: inertial mass $m_i$, defined through Newton's second law as the proportionality constant between net force and acceleration, and gravitational mass $m_g$, defined through the gravitational force law. The equivalence principle asserts $m_i = m_g$, a cornerstone result confirmed experimentally to better than one part in $10^{12}$. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg).

Formally, inertial mass is defined by the relation

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\,\vec{a}, $$

so that for a given net force, a larger mass produces a smaller acceleration. Mass is an intrinsic property of an object and is invariant under changes of inertial reference frame in Newtonian mechanics.

Worked Example. A 5.00 kg block rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12.0\,\hat{i}$ N and $\vec{F}_2 = -4.0\,\hat{i}$ N. Find the acceleration.

The net force is

$$ F_{\text{net}} = 12.0 - 4.0 = 8.0\;\text{N}. $$

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ a = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8.0\;\text{N}}{5.00\;\text{kg}} = 1.6\;\text{m/s}^2. $$

The block accelerates at $1.6\;\text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction.

Key Theorem (Additivity of Mass). For a system of $N$ non-relativistic particles, the total inertial mass is

$$ M = \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i, $$

and the system's center of mass obeys $\vec{F}_{\text{net,ext}} = M\,\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}$. This additivity underlies the treatment of extended bodies as point masses located at their centers of mass, and it is the property that makes mass a conserved, extensive quantity in classical mechanics.

Lab Exercise. Using an air track and a calibrated set of hanging masses, measure the acceleration of a glider under a constant tension force for at least five different glider masses. Plot $a$ versus $1/m$ and verify the linear relationship predicted by Newton's second law. Determine the slope and compare it to the applied force measured by the hanging-mass weight $F = m_{\text{hang}}\,g$.
