🌸 Statics

🌸 Statics

Definition. Statics is the branch of classical mechanics concerned with bodies that are in static equilibrium — that is, bodies that remain at rest or move with constant velocity (typically rest) under the action of external forces and torques. For a rigid body to be in static equilibrium, two independent conditions must be satisfied simultaneously:

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0} \qquad \text{(translational equilibrium)} $$ $$ \sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0} \qquad \text{(rotational equilibrium)} $$

where the torques may be computed about any convenient pivot point. These conditions follow directly from Newton's second law applied in the limit $\vec{a} = \vec{0}$ and $\vec{\alpha} = \vec{0}$.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ and length $L = 4.0\,\text{m}$ is pinned to a wall at its left end and supported by a cable attached at the right end, making an angle $\theta = 30°$ above horizontal. Find the tension $T$ in the cable and the reaction force at the pin.

Taking torques about the pin eliminates the unknown pin forces:

$$ \sum \tau_{\text{pin}} = T \sin\theta \cdot L - mg \cdot \frac{L}{2} = 0 $$ $$ T = \frac{mg}{2\sin\theta} = \frac{(20)(9.8)}{2\sin 30°} = \frac{196}{1.0} = 196\,\text{N} $$

Applying translational equilibrium:

$$ R_x = T\cos\theta = 196\cos 30° \approx 170\,\text{N}, \qquad R_y = mg - T\sin\theta = 196 - 98 = 98\,\text{N} $$

The pin exerts a resultant force $R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2} \approx 197\,\text{N}$.

Key Theorem — Principle of Moments. For a body in rotational equilibrium, the sum of all clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of all counterclockwise moments about that same point. Crucially, the choice of pivot is arbitrary; selecting it at an unknown force's line of action eliminates that unknown from the torque equation, simplifying the algebra.

Lab Exercise. Set up a meter stick balanced on a fulcrum at the 40 cm mark. Hang a 200 g mass at the 10 cm mark. Using the principle of moments, predict the mass $M$ needed at the 70 cm mark to restore equilibrium. Verify experimentally and compute the percent error between prediction and measurement, identifying systematic sources of deviation such as the non-negligible mass of the meter stick itself.
