🍃 Second Condition Equilibrium

🍃 Second Condition of Equilibrium

Definition. An object is in rotational equilibrium when the net torque acting on it about any chosen pivot point is zero. This requirement is the second condition of equilibrium:

$$ \sum \tau = 0 $$

Recall that each torque $\tau = rF\sin\theta$ has already been established; here, counterclockwise torques are taken as positive and clockwise torques as negative. Together with the first condition ($\sum \mathbf{F} = 0$), this condition guarantees complete static equilibrium.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ and length $L = 4.0\,\text{m}$ is attached to a wall at its left end by a hinge and supported by a cable at angle $\theta = 30°$ attached at the right end. A sign of mass $M = 10\,\text{kg}$ hangs from the beam's midpoint. Find the tension $T$ in the cable.

Choose the hinge as the pivot to eliminate the unknown hinge reaction forces. The torques acting are:

  • Weight of beam: $\tau_1 = -mg(L/2) = -(20)(9.8)(2.0) = -392\,\text{N·m}$
  • Weight of sign: $\tau_2 = -Mg(L/2) = -(10)(9.8)(2.0) = -196\,\text{N·m}$
  • Cable tension (vertical component acts at $x = L$): $\tau_3 = +T\sin 30°\cdot L = +T(0.5)(4.0) = +2T$

Applying $\sum \tau = 0$:

$$ 2T - 392 - 196 = 0 \implies T = \frac{588}{2} = 294\,\text{N} $$

Key Theorem. The second condition of equilibrium is pivot-independent for any object satisfying the first condition. If $\sum \mathbf{F} = 0$ and $\sum \tau = 0$ about one point, then $\sum \tau = 0$ about every point. This follows because shifting the pivot by vector $\mathbf{d}$ adds a term $\mathbf{d} \times \sum \mathbf{F}$, which vanishes identically when net force is zero.

Lab Exercise. Place a meter stick on a fulcrum at the 40 cm mark. Hang a 200 g mass at the 10 cm mark and a 100 g mass at the 70 cm mark. Predict the net torque, then add a third mass at the 90 cm mark to restore equilibrium. Verify your prediction by observing whether the stick remains horizontal, and compare the calculated mass to the actual mass used.
