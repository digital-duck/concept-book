🍃 Center Of Gravity

🍃 Center Of Gravity

Definition. The center of gravity of a body (or system of bodies) is the single point at which the entire gravitational force on the system may be considered to act for the purpose of computing torques. For a collection of $N$ discrete particles with masses $m_i$ located at positions $\vec{r}_i$, the center of gravity is defined by

$$ \vec{r}_{cg} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \vec{g}_i \cdot \vec{r}_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \vec{g}_i}, $$

where $\vec{g}_i$ is the local gravitational field at particle $i$. In the uniform-field approximation valid near Earth's surface ($\vec{g}_i = \vec{g}$ for all $i$), this simplifies exactly to the center of mass $\vec{r}_{cm}$:

$$ \vec{r}_{cg} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \vec{r}_i}{M}, \quad M = \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i. $$

The distinction between center of gravity and center of mass is therefore negligible for objects small compared to Earth's radius; for extended bodies in non-uniform fields (e.g., spacecraft in orbit), the two points diverge.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $M = 12\ \text{kg}$ and length $L = 4.0\ \text{m}$ is supported at its left end. A block of mass $m = 6\ \text{kg}$ sits $3.0\ \text{m}$ from the left end. Locate the system's center of gravity measured from the left end.

Taking the left end as the origin, the beam's center of gravity lies at $x_1 = L/2 = 2.0\ \text{m}$ and the block's at $x_2 = 3.0\ \text{m}$. Then

$$ x_{cg} = \frac{M x_1 + m x_2}{M + m} = \frac{(12)(2.0) + (6)(3.0)}{18} = \frac{24 + 18}{18} = \frac{42}{18} \approx 2.33\ \text{m}. $$

Key Theorem. A rigid body in static equilibrium under gravity behaves as though its entire weight $W = Mg$ acts downward through the center of gravity. Because the net torque about any pivot must vanish for equilibrium ($\sum \tau = 0$), a freely suspended object will orient itself so that its center of gravity lies directly below the pivot point — a result used to locate the center of gravity experimentally by successive suspension.

Lab Exercise. Cut an irregular flat lamina from cardboard. Suspend it from three different points in turn, dropping a plumb line from each pivot. Mark each vertical line on the lamina. The intersection of any two lines identifies the center of gravity. Verify that the third line passes through the same intersection, confirming the uniqueness of $\vec{r}_{cg}$.
