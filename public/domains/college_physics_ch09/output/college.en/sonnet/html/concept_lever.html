🍃 Lever

🍃 Lever

Definition. A lever is a rigid bar constrained to rotate about a fixed point called the fulcrum. Forces applied at different points along the bar produce torques about the fulcrum; in static equilibrium, the net torque vanishes. If a force $F_1$ is applied at perpendicular distance $r_1$ from the fulcrum and a load $F_2$ acts at distance $r_2$, the equilibrium condition is

$$ \tau_{\text{net}} = 0 \implies F_1 r_1 = F_2 r_2. $$

The ratio $r_1 / r_2$ defines the mechanical advantage (MA):

$$ \text{MA} = \frac{F_2}{F_1} = \frac{r_1}{r_2}. $$

A lever is classified by the relative positions of the fulcrum, effort, and load: Class 1 places the fulcrum between effort and load (e.g., a seesaw); Class 2 places the load between fulcrum and effort (e.g., a wheelbarrow); Class 3 places the effort between fulcrum and load (e.g., a forearm lifting a weight).

Worked Example. A uniform lever of negligible mass has its fulcrum $0.30\ \text{m}$ from one end. A child weighing $F_2 = 400\ \text{N}$ sits at that end. Determine the effort force $F_1$ required at the opposite end, which is $1.20\ \text{m}$ from the fulcrum.

Applying the torque equilibrium condition:

$$ F_1 \cdot 1.20\ \text{m} = 400\ \text{N} \cdot 0.30\ \text{m} $$ $$ F_1 = \frac{400 \times 0.30}{1.20} = 100\ \text{N}. $$

The mechanical advantage is $\text{MA} = 400/100 = 4$, meaning the effort is four times smaller than the load.

Key Theorem. The principle of the lever is a direct consequence of the condition for rotational equilibrium, $\sum \tau = 0$, applied to a rigid body with one degree of rotational freedom. Because work equals force times displacement, and the arc displacement of each point is proportional to its distance from the fulcrum, the work done by the effort equals the work done against the load: $F_1 d_1 = F_2 d_2$. A lever therefore conserves energy while redistributing force and displacement.

Lab Exercise. Set up a meter stick as a Class 1 lever balanced on a knife-edge fulcrum at the 50 cm mark. Hang a 200 g mass at the 20 cm mark and determine experimentally the position at which a 150 g mass must be placed to restore equilibrium. Compare the measured position with the theoretical prediction from $F_1 r_1 = F_2 r_2$, and compute the percent error.
