🍃 Pivot Point

🍃 Pivot Point

Definition. A pivot point (also called a fulcrum or axis of rotation) is the fixed point about which a rigid body rotates when subjected to a net torque. For a body in static equilibrium or undergoing pure rotation, the pivot point serves as the reference location from which all torques are calculated. The torque $\tau$ produced by a force $\vec{F}$ applied at position $\vec{r}$ relative to the pivot is defined as

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F} $$

with magnitude $\tau = rF\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$. The choice of pivot point is mathematically arbitrary for a system in complete static equilibrium, but a judicious choice eliminates unknown reaction forces from the torque equation, greatly simplifying analysis.

Worked Example. Consider a uniform horizontal beam of length $L = 4.0\ \text{m}$ and mass $m = 20\ \text{kg}$, supported at its left end by a hinge and held horizontal by a cable attached at the right end at angle $\theta = 30°$ above the beam. A box of mass $M = 50\ \text{kg}$ rests at $x = 3.0\ \text{m}$ from the hinge. Find the tension $T$ in the cable.

Choose the hinge as the pivot point. The hinge exerts an unknown reaction force, but by placing the pivot there, that force contributes zero torque (moment arm $= 0$). Applying $\sum \tau = 0$:

$$ T\sin(30°)\cdot L - Mg\cdot x - mg\cdot\frac{L}{2} = 0 $$ $$ T(0.500)(4.0) = (50)(9.8)(3.0) + (20)(9.8)(2.0) $$ $$ 2T = 1470 + 392 = 1862\ \text{N·m} $$ $$ T = 931\ \text{N} $$

Key Theorem. For a rigid body in static equilibrium, the sum of all torques about any pivot point must vanish:

$$ \sum_i \tau_i = 0 $$

This follows from Newton's second law for rotation: $\sum \tau = I\alpha$, which equals zero when $\alpha = 0$. The theorem guarantees that equilibrium is pivot-independent — a powerful consistency check.

Lab Exercise. Place a meter stick on a knife-edge fulcrum at the 50 cm mark. Hang a 200 g mass at the 20 cm mark. Determine experimentally where a 100 g mass must be placed to restore balance. Verify your result using the torque-balance equation with the fulcrum as pivot, and calculate the percent discrepancy between predicted and measured positions.
