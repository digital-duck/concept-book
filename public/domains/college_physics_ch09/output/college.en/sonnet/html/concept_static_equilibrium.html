🍃 Static Equilibrium

🍃 Static Equilibrium

Definition. A rigid body is in static equilibrium when it is both translationally and rotationally at rest, and remains so. Two independent conditions must hold simultaneously:

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0} \qquad \text{(first condition)} $$ $$ \sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0} \qquad \text{(second condition)} $$

where torques are computed about a chosen pivot. Because the net torque must vanish for any valid pivot, a strategically placed pivot can eliminate unknown forces from the torque equation, simplifying algebra considerably.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $m = 20\ \text{kg}$ and length $L = 4.0\ \text{m}$ is hinged to a wall at its left end and supported by a cable attached at the right end, making an angle $\theta = 30°$ above horizontal. Find the cable tension $T$ and the hinge reaction force.

Choose the hinge as pivot to eliminate its unknown reaction. The beam's weight $W = mg = 196\ \text{N}$ acts downward at the center of mass, $L/2$ from the pivot. The cable's vertical component $T\sin\theta$ acts upward at distance $L$. Setting $\sum \tau = 0$:

$$ T\sin\theta \cdot L - W \cdot \frac{L}{2} = 0 \implies T = \frac{W}{2\sin\theta} = \frac{196}{2(0.50)} = 196\ \text{N} $$

Applying $\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$ then yields the hinge components $H_x = T\cos\theta \approx 170\ \text{N}$ (inward) and $H_y = W - T\sin\theta = 98\ \text{N}$ (upward).

Key Theorem. For a body in static equilibrium, the torque condition $\sum \tau = 0$ is independent of the chosen pivot provided the force condition $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$ is also satisfied. Proof sketch: if $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, shifting the pivot by displacement $\vec{d}$ changes each torque by $\vec{d} \times \vec{F}_i$, and $\vec{d} \times \sum \vec{F}_i = \vec{d} \times \vec{0} = \vec{0}$, leaving $\sum \tau$ unchanged.

Lab Exercise. Suspend a meter stick horizontally from a single spring scale placed at an unknown position. Hang two known masses at specified positions. Using only the spring scale reading and the masses, determine the stick's center of mass experimentally. Verify your result by solving the two equilibrium conditions algebraically and comparing with a direct balance-point measurement.
