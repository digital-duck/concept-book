🍃 Black Hole

Definition. A black hole is a region of spacetime in which the gravitational field is so intense that no matter or electromagnetic radiation—including light—can escape to spatial infinity. The boundary of this region is called the event horizon, located at the Schwarzschild radius $r_s$ given by

$$ r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2} $$

where $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/kg}^2$ is Newton's gravitational constant, $M$ is the mass of the collapsed object, and $c$ is the speed of light. Any object or signal originating at $r \leq r_s$ cannot reach $r > r_s$; the escape velocity equals $c$ precisely at $r = r_s$.

Worked Example. Compute the Schwarzschild radius of a black hole whose mass equals ten solar masses ($M = 10\, M_\odot$, where $M_\odot = 1.989 \times 10^{30}\ \text{kg}$).

$$ r_s = \frac{2(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(10 \times 1.989 \times 10^{30})}{(3.00 \times 10^8)^2} $$ $$ r_s = \frac{2(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(1.989 \times 10^{31})}{9.00 \times 10^{16}} $$ $$ r_s = \frac{2.655 \times 10^{21}}{9.00 \times 10^{16}} \approx 2.95 \times 10^4\ \text{m} \approx 29.5\ \text{km} $$

A stellar-mass black hole of $10\, M_\odot$ would thus have an event horizon roughly 30 km in radius—smaller than a typical city.

Key Theorem (Birkhoff's Theorem). The exterior gravitational field of any spherically symmetric mass distribution is identical to that of a point mass $M$ located at the origin. As a corollary, the Schwarzschild metric

$$ ds^2 = -\!\left(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\right)c^2\,dt^2 + \left(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\right)^{-1}dr^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2 $$

uniquely describes the vacuum spacetime outside any non-rotating, uncharged black hole. At $r \to \infty$ this reduces to flat Minkowski spacetime, while at $r = r_s$ the metric component $g_{tt}$ vanishes, signaling the event horizon.

Lab Exercise. The supermassive black hole at the center of galaxy M87 has mass $M \approx 6.5 \times 10^9\, M_\odot$.
(a) Calculate its Schwarzschild radius in meters, then convert to astronomical units (1 AU $= 1.496 \times 10^{11}$ m).
(b) Determine the average density $\bar{\rho} = M / \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r_s^3$ of the region enclosed by the event horizon, and comment on whether this density is surprising compared to ordinary matter.
