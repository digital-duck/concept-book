🌱 Escape Velocity

🌱 Escape Velocity

Definition. The escape velocity of a body is the minimum speed required for a projectile to escape the gravitational influence of a massive object—reaching infinity with zero remaining kinetic energy. Derived from energy conservation, it represents the threshold at which the total mechanical energy of the projectile equals zero. For a planet of mass $M$ and radius $R$, the escape velocity is:

$$ v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}} $$

where $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/kg}^2$ is the universal gravitational constant. This result is independent of the mass of the escaping object and independent of the direction of launch (in the absence of atmosphere).

Derivation sketch. Setting the sum of kinetic and gravitational potential energy to zero:

$$ \frac{1}{2}mv_e^2 - \frac{GMm}{R} = 0 $$

Solving for $v_e$ yields the expression above. The choice of zero total energy corresponds to the object arriving at $r \to \infty$ with $v \to 0$—the boundary condition for marginal escape.

Worked Example. Calculate the escape velocity from Earth's surface, given $M_\oplus = 5.972 \times 10^{24}\ \text{kg}$ and $R_\oplus = 6.371 \times 10^6\ \text{m}$.

$$ v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.972 \times 10^{24})}{6.371 \times 10^6}} $$ $$ v_e = \sqrt{\frac{7.972 \times 10^{14}}{6.371 \times 10^6}} = \sqrt{1.251 \times 10^8} \approx 1.118 \times 10^4\ \text{m/s} $$

Thus $v_e \approx 11.2\ \text{km/s}$, a well-established benchmark in astrodynamics.

Key Theorem (Schwarzschild Limit). When $v_e = c$ (the speed of light), the corresponding radius $R_s = 2GM/c^2$ defines the Schwarzschild radius. Any object compressed within $R_s$ becomes a black hole—light itself cannot escape. This connects Newtonian escape velocity to general relativistic event horizons, illustrating the concept's reach across classical and modern physics.

Lab Exercise. Using the formula $v_e = \sqrt{2GM/R}$, compute the escape velocity from (a) the Moon ($M = 7.34 \times 10^{22}\ \text{kg}$, $R = 1.737 \times 10^6\ \text{m}$) and (b) a neutron star of mass $2M_\odot$ and radius $10\ \text{km}$. Compare the neutron star result to $c$ and comment on its physical significance.
