🌱 Shear Modulus

🌱 Shear Modulus

Definition. The shear modulus $S$ (also denoted $G$) characterizes a material's resistance to deformation produced by shear stress — a stress that acts tangentially to a surface rather than perpendicular to it. Formally, it is defined as the ratio of shear stress $\tau$ to shear strain $\gamma$:

$$ S = \frac{\tau}{\gamma} = \frac{F/A}{\Delta x / L} $$

where $F$ is the tangential force applied over area $A$, $\Delta x$ is the lateral displacement of the top surface, and $L$ is the height of the object. The SI unit of shear modulus is the pascal (Pa). Like Young's modulus, $S$ is an intrinsic material property valid only within the elastic (linear) regime.

Worked Example. A steel plate of height $L = 0.050\ \text{m}$ and cross-sectional area $A = 2.0 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2$ is subjected to a tangential force $F = 3.0 \times 10^{4}\ \text{N}$. The shear modulus of steel is $S = 8.0 \times 10^{10}\ \text{Pa}$. Find the lateral displacement $\Delta x$.

First compute the shear stress:

$$ \tau = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{3.0 \times 10^4}{2.0 \times 10^{-4}} = 1.5 \times 10^8\ \text{Pa} $$

Then solve for shear strain:

$$ \gamma = \frac{\tau}{S} = \frac{1.5 \times 10^8}{8.0 \times 10^{10}} = 1.875 \times 10^{-3} $$

Finally, recover the displacement:

$$ \Delta x = \gamma \cdot L = (1.875 \times 10^{-3})(0.050) = 9.4 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{m} $$

Key Theorem. For an isotropic, linearly elastic solid, the shear modulus is related to Young's modulus $E$ and Poisson's ratio $\nu$ by:

$$ S = \frac{E}{2(1 + \nu)} $$

This relation confirms that shear modulus is not independent — it is determined by two other elastic constants. Because $0 < \nu < 0.5$ for most materials, $S$ always falls in the range $E/3 < S < E/2$.

Lab Exercise. Clamp a rubber block ($L = 0.10\ \text{m}$, $A = 5.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2$) to a bench and apply measured horizontal forces using calibrated weights via a pulley. Record $\Delta x$ with a micrometer at each load. Plot $\tau$ versus $\gamma$ and extract $S$ from the slope of the linear region. Compare your result to the accepted value $S \approx 0.003\ \text{GPa}$ for natural rubber.
