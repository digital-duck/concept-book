🌱 Bulk Modulus

🌱 Bulk Modulus

Definition. The bulk modulus $B$ of a material quantifies its resistance to uniform compression. When a body is subjected to a compressive pressure $\Delta p$ applied equally from all directions, it responds with a fractional change in volume $\Delta V / V_0$, where $V_0$ is the original volume. The bulk modulus is defined as:

$$ B = -\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta V / V_0} $$

The negative sign ensures $B$ is positive, since an increase in pressure ($\Delta p > 0$) produces a decrease in volume ($\Delta V < 0$). The SI unit of $B$ is the pascal (Pa). Incompressible materials have very large bulk moduli; gases have relatively small ones.

Worked Example. A steel sphere of volume $V_0 = 0.500\ \text{m}^3$ is lowered to a depth where the gauge pressure is $\Delta p = 2.00 \times 10^7\ \text{Pa}$. Given that the bulk modulus of steel is $B = 1.60 \times 10^{11}\ \text{Pa}$, find the change in volume.

Rearranging the definition:

$$ \Delta V = -\frac{\Delta p \cdot V_0}{B} = -\frac{(2.00 \times 10^7)(0.500)}{1.60 \times 10^{11}} $$ $$ \Delta V = -6.25 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{m}^3 $$

The sphere decreases in volume by $6.25 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{m}^3$, or about $0.0125\%$ of its original volume, illustrating the near-incompressibility of steel.

Key Theorem. For an ideal gas undergoing an isothermal process, the bulk modulus equals the equilibrium pressure: $B_{\text{isothermal}} = p$. For an adiabatic process, $B_{\text{adiabatic}} = \gamma p$, where $\gamma = C_p / C_v$ is the ratio of specific heats. This result, derivable from the ideal gas law and the first law of thermodynamics, explains why the speed of sound in air—which propagates adiabatically—is given by $v = \sqrt{\gamma p / \rho}$, with $\rho$ the air density.

Lab Exercise. Fill a syringe (volume $V_0 \approx 20\ \text{mL}$) with water, seal the tip, and apply a measured force $F$ over the plunger area $A$. Record the resulting pressure $\Delta p = F/A$ and the observed volume decrease $|\Delta V|$. Calculate $B$ from the definition and compare your result with the accepted value for water, $B \approx 2.2 \times 10^9\ \text{Pa}$. Discuss sources of systematic error, including air bubbles trapped in the syringe.
