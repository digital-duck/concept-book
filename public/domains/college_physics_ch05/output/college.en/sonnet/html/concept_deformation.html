🍃 Deformation

🍃 Deformation

Definition. Deformation refers to the change in shape or size of an object resulting from an applied force. When an external force acts on a solid body, the relative positions of its constituent particles shift, producing either a change in length (linear deformation), a change in volume (volumetric deformation), or a change in shape without volume change (shear deformation). For elastic materials, deformation is reversible: upon removal of the applied force, the object returns to its original configuration. This behavior is governed by Hooke's Law, which states that, within the elastic limit, the deformation is directly proportional to the applied force:

$$ F = k \Delta x $$

where $F$ is the applied force (N), $k$ is the spring constant or stiffness coefficient (N/m), and $\Delta x$ is the displacement from equilibrium (m). The stress $\sigma$ and strain $\varepsilon$ formulation generalizes this to continuous media:

$$ \sigma = E \varepsilon $$

where $E$ is Young's modulus (Pa), $\sigma = F/A$ is the normal stress, and $\varepsilon = \Delta L / L_0$ is the fractional change in length.

Worked Example. A steel rod of cross-sectional area $A = 2.0 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2$ and initial length $L_0 = 1.50\ \text{m}$ is subjected to a tensile force $F = 6.0 \times 10^4\ \text{N}$. Given Young's modulus for steel $E = 2.0 \times 10^{11}\ \text{Pa}$, find the elongation $\Delta L$.

$$ \sigma = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{6.0 \times 10^4}{2.0 \times 10^{-4}} = 3.0 \times 10^8\ \text{Pa} $$ $$ \varepsilon = \frac{\sigma}{E} = \frac{3.0 \times 10^8}{2.0 \times 10^{11}} = 1.5 \times 10^{-3} $$ $$ \Delta L = \varepsilon \cdot L_0 = (1.5 \times 10^{-3})(1.50) = 2.25 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m} \approx 2.25\ \text{mm} $$

Key Theorem. Within the proportional limit, elastic deformation satisfies energy conservation: the work done by the applied force is stored entirely as elastic potential energy $U = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta x)^2$, recoverable upon unloading. Beyond the elastic limit, permanent (plastic) deformation occurs and energy is dissipated.

Lab Exercise. Suspend known masses from a rubber band and measure its length after each addition. Plot $F$ versus $\Delta x$, identify the linear (elastic) region, determine $k$ from the slope, and note the load at which the curve deviates from linearity, marking the onset of plastic deformation.
