🍃 Bulk Compression

🍃 Bulk Compression

Definition. Bulk compression describes the uniform reduction in volume experienced by a solid or fluid when subjected to an isotropic (equal in all directions) increase in pressure. The governing material property is the bulk modulus $B$, defined as

$$ B = -\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta V / V_0} $$

where $\Delta p$ is the applied pressure change, $\Delta V = V - V_0$ is the change in volume, and $V_0$ is the original volume. The negative sign ensures $B > 0$, since an increase in pressure ($\Delta p > 0$) produces a decrease in volume ($\Delta V < 0$). The SI unit of $B$ is the pascal (Pa). Larger values of $B$ indicate a stiffer material that resists compression more strongly; steel ($B \approx 160\,\text{GPa}$) is far less compressible than water ($B \approx 2.2\,\text{GPa}$).

Worked Example. A hydraulic press subjects a steel sphere of volume $V_0 = 0.500\,\text{m}^3$ to an additional pressure of $\Delta p = 1.20 \times 10^8\,\text{Pa}$. Find the change in volume.

Rearranging the bulk modulus definition:

$$ \Delta V = -\frac{\Delta p \, V_0}{B} = -\frac{(1.20 \times 10^8\,\text{Pa})(0.500\,\text{m}^3)}{1.60 \times 10^{11}\,\text{Pa}} $$ $$ \Delta V = -3.75 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}^3 $$

The sphere decreases in volume by $3.75 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}^3$, or roughly $0.075\%$ of its original volume — illustrating the extreme stiffness of steel under compression.

Key Theorem. For a linearly elastic, isotropic material, the bulk modulus relates to Young's modulus $E$ and Poisson's ratio $\nu$ through

$$ B = \frac{E}{3(1 - 2\nu)} $$

This identity arises from the generalized three-dimensional Hooke's law applied to uniform triaxial stress, and it shows that a material with $\nu \to 0.5$ (incompressible limit) yields $B \to \infty$, consistent with the definition.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a syringe filled with water and another filled with air, sealing both ends. Apply the same measured force to each plunger and record the displacement. Calculate the effective bulk modulus for each fluid using the relation above, and explain quantitatively why air compresses far more readily than water at room temperature.
