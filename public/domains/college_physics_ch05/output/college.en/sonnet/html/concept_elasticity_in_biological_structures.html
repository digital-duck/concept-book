🌸 Elasticity In Biological Structures

🌸 Elasticity In Biological Structures

Definition. Biological materials exhibit elasticity — the ability to deform under stress and return to their original shape upon removal of that stress — but their mechanical behavior is far more complex than that of idealized Hookean solids. The elastic modulus $E$ (Young's modulus) characterizes the stiffness of a material according to

$$ E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon} = \frac{F/A}{\Delta L / L_0} $$

where $\sigma$ is tensile stress, $\varepsilon$ is tensile strain, $F$ is the applied force, $A$ is the cross-sectional area, $\Delta L$ is the elongation, and $L_0$ is the original length. Biological tissues such as bone, tendon, cartilage, and arterial walls are composites — hierarchically organized at the molecular, fibrillar, and tissue levels — so their effective moduli span many orders of magnitude (cartilage $\sim 1\ \text{MPa}$; compact bone $\sim 17\ \text{GPa}$).

Worked Example. A segment of Achilles tendon has a resting length $L_0 = 25\ \text{cm}$, a circular cross-section of diameter $d = 1.0\ \text{cm}$, and Young's modulus $E = 1.5\ \text{GPa}$. Determine the elongation when a runner exerts a tensile force $F = 4500\ \text{N}$ on the tendon.

$$ A = \pi \left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi (0.005)^2 \approx 7.85 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{m}^2 $$ $$ \Delta L = \frac{F L_0}{E A} = \frac{(4500)(0.25)}{(1.5 \times 10^9)(7.85 \times 10^{-5})} \approx 9.6\ \text{mm} $$

This $\sim 4\%$ strain falls within the tendon's elastic (reversible) regime, which typically extends to about $4\text{–}8\%$ before micro-damage accumulates.

Key Theorem. For anisotropic biological materials, the full elastic response requires a fourth-rank stiffness tensor $C_{ijkl}$ relating the stress tensor $\sigma_{ij}$ to the strain tensor $\varepsilon_{kl}$: $\sigma_{ij} = C_{ijkl}\,\varepsilon_{kl}$. Bone, for example, is transversely isotropic, reducing the 81 tensor components to 5 independent elastic constants. The nonlinear, viscoelastic "J-curve" stress–strain response common to soft tissues (ligaments, arteries) reflects progressive recruitment of collagen fibrils aligned along the loading direction.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a cylindrical specimen of porcine cortical bone (approximate dimensions: length $40\ \text{mm}$, diameter $6\ \text{mm}$). Mount it in a tensile testing machine and record the force–displacement curve at a strain rate of $10^{-3}\ \text{s}^{-1}$. Compute $E$ from the linear region of the curve, identify the proportional limit, and compare your result to published values for human cortical bone ($E \approx 15\text{–}20\ \text{GPa}$). Discuss sources of discrepancy, including anisotropy, specimen hydration, and age-related changes in mineral content.
