🍃 Strain

🍃 Strain

Definition. When an external force is applied to a deformable body, the body changes shape or size. Strain is the dimensionless measure of this deformation, defined as the fractional change in a relevant geometric quantity. For a rod of original length $L_0$ subjected to a tensile or compressive load along its axis, the linear strain (or longitudinal strain) is

$$ \varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L_0} = \frac{L - L_0}{L_0}, $$

where $\Delta L = L - L_0$ is the change in length. Because both numerator and denominator carry units of length, $\varepsilon$ is dimensionless (often expressed as m/m or reported as a percentage). Elongation yields positive strain; compression yields negative strain. Analogous definitions apply in two and three dimensions: shear strain $\gamma$ measures angular deformation, and volumetric strain $\theta$ measures the fractional change in volume.

Worked Example. A steel wire of original length $L_0 = 2.00\,\text{m}$ and cross-sectional area $A = 1.00 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{m}^2$ is suspended vertically and supports a mass of $m = 10.0\,\text{kg}$. The elastic modulus of steel is $Y = 2.00 \times 10^{11}\,\text{Pa}$. Find the linear strain and the elongation.

The tensile stress is

$$ \sigma = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{mg}{A} = \frac{(10.0)(9.80)}{1.00 \times 10^{-6}} = 9.80 \times 10^{7}\,\text{Pa}. $$

From the definition of Young's modulus, $Y = \sigma/\varepsilon$, so

$$ \varepsilon = \frac{\sigma}{Y} = \frac{9.80 \times 10^{7}}{2.00 \times 10^{11}} = 4.90 \times 10^{-4}. $$

The elongation is therefore $\Delta L = \varepsilon \, L_0 = (4.90 \times 10^{-4})(2.00) = 9.80 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{m} \approx 0.98\,\text{mm}$.

Key Theorem (Hooke's Law in terms of strain). Within the elastic limit, stress and strain are linearly proportional:

$$ \sigma = Y\varepsilon, $$

where $Y$ is the Young's modulus of the material. This relation holds up to the proportional limit; beyond it, strain increases nonlinearly and permanent (plastic) deformation may occur.

Lab Exercise. Suspend known masses from a copper wire of measured length and diameter. Record the elongation for each load using a traveling microscope. Plot $\sigma$ versus $\varepsilon$ and determine $Y$ from the slope of the linear region. Compare your result with the accepted value $Y_{\text{Cu}} \approx 1.1 \times 10^{11}\,\text{Pa}$ and estimate the proportional limit from the graph.
