🌱 Youngs Modulus

🌱 Youngs Modulus

Definition. Young's modulus (also called the elastic modulus) quantifies the stiffness of a solid material under uniaxial tensile or compressive stress. For a specimen of cross-sectional area $A$ and original length $L_0$, when an axial force $F$ is applied, the material develops a stress $\sigma = F/A$ and a longitudinal strain $\varepsilon = \Delta L / L_0$. Within the elastic (linear) regime, these quantities are proportional:

$$ Y = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon} = \frac{F/A}{\Delta L/L_0} = \frac{F L_0}{A\,\Delta L}. $$

Young's modulus $Y$ has SI units of pascals (Pa = N/m²) and is an intrinsic material property — independent of specimen geometry. Typical values range from $\sim 1\ \text{GPa}$ for rubber-like polymers to $\sim 200\ \text{GPa}$ for structural steel.

Worked Example. A steel cable of diameter $d = 4.00\ \text{mm}$ and length $L_0 = 3.00\ \text{m}$ supports a load of $F = 500\ \text{N}$. Given $Y_{\text{steel}} = 2.00 \times 10^{11}\ \text{Pa}$, find the elongation $\Delta L$.

The cross-sectional area is

$$ A = \pi\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi(2.00\times10^{-3})^2 = 1.257\times10^{-5}\ \text{m}^2. $$

Rearranging the definition of $Y$:

$$ \Delta L = \frac{F L_0}{Y A} = \frac{(500)(3.00)}{(2.00\times10^{11})(1.257\times10^{-5})} = \frac{1500}{2.513\times10^{6}} \approx 5.97\times10^{-4}\ \text{m} \approx 0.597\ \text{mm}. $$

The cable stretches by roughly $0.6\ \text{mm}$, a small but measurable deformation consistent with elastic behavior.

Key Theorem (Hooke's Law in continuum form). For an isotropic linear elastic solid, the stress–strain relationship $\sigma = Y\varepsilon$ holds for all $|\varepsilon| \operatorname{ll} 1$, up to the proportional limit. Beyond this limit, the relationship becomes nonlinear and eventually plastic deformation occurs; $Y$ is defined only in the linear regime.

Lab Exercise. Using a Searle's apparatus (or a slotted-mass hanger with a meter stick and micrometer), measure $\Delta L$ for at least five values of $F$ applied to a copper wire of known $L_0$ and $d$. Plot $\sigma$ versus $\varepsilon$, fit a line through the origin, and extract $Y_{\text{copper}}$. Compare your result to the accepted value of $1.20 \times 10^{11}\ \text{Pa}$ and calculate the percent error. Identify potential sources of systematic uncertainty (wire kinking, non-uniform cross-section, zero-error in the micrometer).
