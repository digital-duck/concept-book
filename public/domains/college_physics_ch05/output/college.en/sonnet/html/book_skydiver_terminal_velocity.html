🌱 Drag Coefficient

🌱 Drag Coefficient

Definition. When an object moves through a fluid (liquid or gas), the fluid exerts a resistive force opposite to the direction of motion called drag. For objects traveling at moderate to high speeds, this drag force is modeled as

$$ F_D = \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v^2, $$

where $\rho$ is the fluid density (kg/m³), $A$ is the cross-sectional area of the object perpendicular to flow (m²), $v$ is the speed of the object relative to the fluid (m/s), and $C_D$ is the drag coefficient — a dimensionless scalar that encapsulates the object's geometry, surface texture, and the nature of the flow around it. Lower values of $C_D$ indicate a more streamlined shape; a smooth sphere has $C_D \approx 0.47$, while a modern sports car may achieve $C_D \approx 0.30$.

Worked Example. A cyclist of mass $m = 70\ \text{kg}$ rides at a constant velocity $v = 10\ \text{m/s}$ on a level road. The frontal area is $A = 0.40\ \text{m}^2$, air density is $\rho = 1.20\ \text{kg/m}^3$, and the drag coefficient for the cyclist-bicycle system is $C_D = 0.88$. Find the drag force.

$$ F_D = \frac{1}{2}(0.88)(1.20\ \text{kg/m}^3)(0.40\ \text{m}^2)(10\ \text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.88)(1.20)(0.40)(100)\ \text{N} = 21.1\ \text{N}. $$

Since the cyclist moves at constant velocity, the net force is zero; therefore, the cyclist must supply a propulsive force of $21.1\ \text{N}$ to overcome air resistance alone.

Key Theorem (Terminal Velocity). A falling object reaches terminal velocity $v_t$ when drag exactly balances gravitational force. Setting $F_D = mg$:

$$ \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v_t^2 = mg \implies v_t = \sqrt{\frac{2mg}{C_D \rho A}}. $$

This result shows that $v_t$ depends inversely on $\sqrt{C_D}$: more streamlined objects fall faster.

🌱 Fluid Density

🌱 Fluid Density

Definition. The density of a fluid is defined as the mass per unit volume of the substance:

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} $$

where $\rho$ (Greek rho) is density in $\text{kg/m}^3$, $m$ is mass in kilograms, and $V$ is volume in cubic meters. Density is an intrinsic property — it characterizes the material itself rather than any particular sample. For an ideal homogeneous fluid in static equilibrium, $\rho$ is uniform throughout the volume. For real fluids, density may vary with pressure and temperature; for liquids, these variations are generally small, whereas for gases the dependence on pressure is significant.

Standard reference values include: freshwater at $4^\circ\text{C}$, $\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3$; seawater, $\rho \approx 1025\ \text{kg/m}^3$; air at STP, $\rho \approx 1.29\ \text{kg/m}^3$.

Worked Example. A cylindrical container has an inner radius of $r = 0.10\ \text{m}$ and is filled with ethanol ($\rho_{\text{eth}} = 789\ \text{kg/m}^3$) to a height of $h = 0.25\ \text{m}$. Find the mass of ethanol.

First, compute the volume:

$$ V = \pi r^2 h = \pi (0.10)^2 (0.25) = 7.854 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3 $$

Then apply the density definition:

$$ m = \rho V = (789)(7.854 \times 10^{-3}) \approx 6.20\ \text{kg} $$

Key Theorem — Archimedes' Principle. When an object is wholly or partially submerged in a fluid of density $\rho_f$, the fluid exerts an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the displaced fluid:

$$ F_b = \rho_f V_{\text{disp}}\, g $$

This result follows directly from integrating the hydrostatic pressure $P = P_0 + \rho_f g h$ over the submerged surface. An object floats when $F_b \geq mg$, which requires $\rho_{\text{obj}} \leq \rho_f$.

🍃 Drag Force

🍃 Drag Force

Definition. When an object moves through a fluid (liquid or gas), the fluid exerts a resistive force opposing the motion called the drag force. For objects moving at sufficiently high speeds through air, the drag force is well described by:

$$ F_D = \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v^2 $$

where $C_D$ is the dimensionless drag coefficient (dependent on object geometry and surface texture), $\rho$ is the fluid mass density (kg/m³), $A$ is the cross-sectional area of the object perpendicular to the velocity (m²), and $v$ is the object's speed relative to the fluid (m/s). The drag force acts antiparallel to the velocity vector at all times.

Worked Example. A skydiver of mass $m = 80\ \text{kg}$ and cross-sectional area $A = 0.70\ \text{m}^2$ falls vertically through air ($\rho = 1.21\ \text{kg/m}^3$) with $C_D = 1.0$. Determine the terminal speed $v_t$.

At terminal speed, the net force is zero, so drag exactly balances gravity:

$$ F_D = mg $$ $$ \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v_t^2 = mg $$ $$ v_t = \sqrt{\frac{2mg}{C_D \rho A}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(80)(9.8)}{(1.0)(1.21)(0.70)}} $$ $$ v_t = \sqrt{\frac{1568}{0.847}} \approx \sqrt{1851} \approx 43\ \text{m/s} \approx 155\ \text{km/h} $$

This result is consistent with observed free-fall speeds before parachute deployment.

Key Theorem — Terminal Velocity. For a body of mass $m$ subject to gravity and quadratic drag, the equation of motion is:

$$ m\frac{dv}{dt} = mg - \frac{1}{2}C_D \rho A v^2 $$

Setting $dv/dt = 0$ yields the terminal speed $v_t = \sqrt{2mg/(C_D \rho A)}$. The approach to terminal velocity is asymptotic: speed increases monotonically and approaches $v_t$ exponentially in time, never exceeding it.

🍃 Terminal Velocity

🍃 Terminal Velocity

Definition. Terminal velocity is the constant speed attained by a falling object when the net force acting upon it becomes zero. For an object of mass $m$ descending through a fluid, two forces act vertically: gravitational force $F_g = mg$ directed downward and drag force $F_D$ directed upward. The drag force on a macroscopic object at moderate-to-high speeds is well modeled by

$$ F_D = \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v^2, $$

where $C_D$ is the dimensionless drag coefficient, $\rho$ is the fluid density, $A$ is the object's cross-sectional area, and $v$ is its instantaneous speed. Terminal velocity $v_t$ is reached when $F_D = F_g$:

$$ \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v_t^2 = mg \implies v_t = \sqrt{\frac{2mg}{C_D \rho A}}. $$

Worked Example. A skydiver of mass $m = 80\,\text{kg}$ falls in the spread-eagle position, for which $C_D \approx 1.0$ and $A \approx 0.70\,\text{m}^2$. Taking $\rho_{\text{air}} = 1.21\,\text{kg/m}^3$ and $g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$:

$$ v_t = \sqrt{\frac{2(80)(9.8)}{(1.0)(1.21)(0.70)}} = \sqrt{\frac{1568}{0.847}} \approx \sqrt{1851} \approx 43\,\text{m/s}\,(155\,\text{km/h}). $$

This matches observed freefall speeds for typical skydivers, confirming the model's validity. If the skydiver tucks into a streamlined position, $A$ decreases and $C_D$ decreases, raising $v_t$ substantially.

Key Theorem. During descent before terminal velocity is reached, Newton's second law gives $ma = mg - \frac{1}{2}C_D\rho A v^2$. As $v$ increases, the net force—and therefore the acceleration—decreases monotonically. One can show by separation of variables that

$$ v(t) = v_t \tanh\!\left(\frac{g\,t}{v_t}\right), $$

confirming that speed approaches $v_t$ asymptotically; strictly speaking, terminal velocity is a limit, not a value attained in finite time.

🌸 Skydiver Terminal Velocity

🌸 Skydiver Terminal Velocity

Definition. Terminal velocity is the constant speed attained by a falling object when the net force acting on it is zero — that is, when the upward drag force exactly balances the downward gravitational force. For a skydiver of mass $m$ falling through air, two forces act along the vertical axis: gravity $F_g = mg$ (downward) and aerodynamic drag $F_D$ (upward). The drag force on a body moving at speed $v$ through a fluid of density $\rho$ is given by

$$ F_D = \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v^2, $$

where $C_D$ is the dimensionless drag coefficient and $A$ is the skydiver's effective cross-sectional area. Terminal velocity $v_t$ is reached when $F_D = F_g$:

$$ \frac{1}{2} C_D \rho A v_t^2 = mg \implies v_t = \sqrt{\frac{2mg}{C_D \rho A}}. $$

Worked Example. Consider a skydiver of mass $m = 80\ \text{kg}$ in a belly-to-earth position with $A = 0.70\ \text{m}^2$ and $C_D = 1.0$. Taking air density at sea level as $\rho = 1.21\ \text{kg/m}^3$ and $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$:

$$ v_t = \sqrt{\frac{2(80)(9.8)}{(1.0)(1.21)(0.70)}} = \sqrt{\frac{1568}{0.847}} \approx \sqrt{1851} \approx 43\ \text{m/s}\ (\approx 155\ \text{km/h}). $$

If the skydiver tucks into a head-down dive, $A$ decreases to roughly $0.18\ \text{m}^2$, yielding $v_t \approx 85\ \text{m/s}$ — consistent with observed speeds exceeding $300\ \text{km/h}$ in competitive freefall.

Key Theorem (Newton's Second Law at Terminal Velocity). The equation of motion along the fall axis is $ma = mg - \frac{1}{2}C_D \rho A v^2$. As $v \to v_t$, the net force $F_{\text{net}} \to 0$ and therefore $a \to 0$. The skydiver's velocity asymptotically approaches $v_t$; it is never instantaneously achieved but is reached exponentially quickly once drag becomes dominant.

Payoff 🎯

Terminal velocity represents the conceptual culmination of Chapter 5's treatment of forces, because it is the first physical scenario in which a student must hold Newton's second law and Newton's third law simultaneously in mind while accounting for a velocity-dependent resistive force. Every earlier topic — free-body diagrams, net external force, inertia, mass — was preparatory scaffolding. Terminal velocity is where that scaffolding bears real weight.

What terminal velocity achieves. A skydiver of mass $m$ falling through air experiences gravitational force $F_g = mg$ downward and aerodynamic drag $F_d = \tfrac{1}{2}C_D \rho A v^2$ upward, where $C_D$ is the drag coefficient, $\rho$ is air density, and $A$ is the skydiver's projected cross-sectional area. Newton's second law gives

$$ ma = mg - \tfrac{1}{2}C_D \rho A v^2. $$

Terminal velocity $v_T$ is reached when $a = 0$, so

$$ v_T = \sqrt{\frac{2mg}{C_D \rho A}}. $$

This is not merely a formula — it encodes a dynamic equilibrium. The system self-regulates: if the skydiver's speed exceeds $v_T$, drag dominates and decelerates them; if speed falls below $v_T$, gravity dominates and accelerates them. The attractor is $v_T$.

Worked example. A skydiver has mass $m = 80\,\text{kg}$, $C_D = 1.0$, $A = 0.70\,\text{m}^2$, and $\rho = 1.2\,\text{kg/m}^3$. Setting $mg = \tfrac{1}{2}C_D \rho A v_T^2$:

$$ v_T = \sqrt{\frac{2(80)(9.8)}{(1.0)(1.2)(0.70)}} \approx \sqrt{\frac{1568}{0.84}} \approx 43.2\,\text{m/s} \approx 155\,\text{km/h}. $$

A skydiver who deploys a parachute increases $A$ dramatically, reducing $v_T$ to a survivable landing speed near $5\text{–}7\,\text{m/s}$.

Key theorem. For any object falling under constant $F_g$ with quadratic drag, the velocity approaches $v_T$ asymptotically according to $v(t) = v_T \tanh\!\left(\frac{gt}{v_T}\right)$, confirming that $v_T$ is a stable fixed point of the dynamical system.

Lab exercise. Drop coffee filters of varying stack sizes from a fixed height and measure fall time with a photogate. Plot $v_T$ versus stacked mass and verify that $v_T \propto \sqrt{m}$, as the formula predicts.

The story of terminal velocity does not end in freefall. The same balance of driving force and velocity-dependent drag governs sedimentation of particles in a centrifuge, drag reduction in competitive cycling, and Reynolds-number scaling in aeronautical design. If you wish to push further, consider exploring aerodynamic drag reduction — the engineering discipline that asks not merely what $v_T$ is, but how $C_D$ and $A$ can be systematically minimized to reshape it.
