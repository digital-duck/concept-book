🍃 Tension Compression Deformation

🍃 Tension Compression Deformation

Definition

When an external force acts along the axis of a solid object, it produces either tension or compression, both of which result in measurable deformation. Tension occurs when equal and opposite forces pull the object apart along its length, while compression arises when such forces push inward. The resulting deformation is quantified by strain, defined as the fractional change in length:

$$ \varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L_0} $$

where $\Delta L$ is the change in length and $L_0$ is the original length. The internal restoring stress $\sigma$ (force per unit cross-sectional area) is related to strain through Young's modulus $E$:

$$ \sigma = E\varepsilon \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{F}{A} = E\frac{\Delta L}{L_0} $$

This linear relationship, known as Hooke's Law for continuous media, holds within the elastic limit of the material.

Worked Example

A steel rod of length $L_0 = 2.00\ \text{m}$, cross-sectional area $A = 1.00 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2$, and Young's modulus $E = 2.00 \times 10^{11}\ \text{Pa}$ is subjected to a tensile force of $F = 5.00 \times 10^4\ \text{N}$. Determine the elongation.

Rearranging the stress-strain relation:

$$ \Delta L = \frac{F L_0}{A E} = \frac{(5.00 \times 10^4)(2.00)}{(1.00 \times 10^{-4})(2.00 \times 10^{11})} = 5.00 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m} $$

The rod elongates by $5.00\ \text{mm}$, a deformation readily measurable with a micrometer.

Key Theorem: Elastic Potential Energy Density

The energy stored per unit volume during elastic deformation equals the area under the stress-strain curve:

$$ u = \frac{1}{2}\sigma\varepsilon = \frac{\sigma^2}{2E} = \frac{E\varepsilon^2}{2} $$

This result follows directly from integrating $\sigma\,d\varepsilon$ from zero to the applied strain, treating $E$ as constant within the elastic regime. The total elastic potential energy stored in the deformed rod is therefore $U = uV = u A L_0$.

Laboratory Exercise

Suspend masses of known weight from a thin wire of measured diameter and initial length. Record the elongation for each load using a traveling microscope. Plot $F$ versus $\Delta L$, extract the slope, and calculate Young's modulus for the wire material. Compare your result to the accepted tabulated value and quantify the percent discrepancy. Identify the load at which the data deviates from linearity to estimate the elastic limit.
