🍃 Stress

🍃 Stress

Definition. When an external force acts on a deformable object, the material develops an internal restoring force distributed across its cross-section. Stress is defined as the internal force per unit area acting on that cross-section. For a force $F$ applied perpendicular (normal) to a surface of cross-sectional area $A$, the tensile or compressive stress is

$$ \sigma = \frac{F}{A}, $$

measured in pascals ($\text{Pa} = \text{N/m}^2$). When the applied force is parallel to the surface, the quantity is called shear stress and is denoted $\tau = F_\parallel / A$. Stress is a second-rank tensor in general, but introductory treatments restrict attention to these two scalar components acting on a single plane.

Worked Example. A steel cable of circular cross-section with diameter $d = 2.0\,\text{cm}$ supports a suspended load of $m = 5000\,\text{kg}$. Find the tensile stress in the cable.

The cross-sectional area is

$$ A = \pi\!\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^{\!2} = \pi(0.010\,\text{m})^2 = 3.14 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}^2. $$

Taking $g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$, the tension equals the weight:

$$ F = mg = (5000)(9.8) = 4.9 \times 10^4\,\text{N}. $$

Therefore,

$$ \sigma = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{4.9 \times 10^4}{3.14 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 1.56 \times 10^8\,\text{Pa} = 156\,\text{MPa}. $$

Since the ultimate tensile strength of structural steel is roughly $400\,\text{MPa}$, this cable operates well within its safe range.

Key Theorem (Hooke's Law in terms of stress and strain). Within the elastic limit, stress is proportional to strain $\varepsilon$:

$$ \sigma = E\,\varepsilon, $$

where $E$ is Young's modulus, a material constant. This relationship is linear and reversible: the material returns to its original geometry once the load is removed. Beyond the elastic limit, permanent (plastic) deformation occurs, and the linear relation no longer holds.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a rubber band of measured width $w$ and thickness $t$. Hang known masses from it and measure elongation $\Delta L$ relative to the natural length $L_0$. Compute $\sigma = mg/(wt)$ and $\varepsilon = \Delta L/L_0$ for each mass. Plot $\sigma$ versus $\varepsilon$ and determine the slope to extract an effective Young's modulus. Identify the point, if observable, where the response becomes nonlinear.
