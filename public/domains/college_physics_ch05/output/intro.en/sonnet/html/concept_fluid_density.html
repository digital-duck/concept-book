🌱 Fluid Density

Imagine you have two cups of the same size — one filled with water and one filled with cooking oil. Even though both cups hold the same amount of liquid, the water feels noticeably heavier when you pick it up. Why? The answer lies in a property called density.

Density tells us how much matter is packed into a given space. A fluid (any liquid or gas that flows) with high density has a lot of mass squeezed into each tiny portion of its volume. A fluid with low density has less mass spread across that same space.

We write density using the Greek letter $\rho$ (rho), and the rule is simple:

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} $$

where $m$ is the mass of the fluid (in kilograms) and $V$ is the volume it occupies (in cubic meters). The unit of density is therefore $\text{kg/m}^3$.

For example, fresh water has a density of about $1{,}000 \ \text{kg/m}^3$, while cooking oil sits around $900 \ \text{kg/m}^3$ — less mass per cubic meter, which is why oil floats on water.

Try it yourself: A sample of seawater has a mass of $1.025 \ \text{kg}$ and occupies exactly $0.001 \ \text{m}^3$ (one liter). Use $\rho = m/V$ to find its density. Does your answer make sense compared to fresh water?
