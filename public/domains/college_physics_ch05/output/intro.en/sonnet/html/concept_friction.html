🍃 Friction

🍃 Friction

When you slide a book across a table and let go, it slows down and stops. Nothing visible is pushing back on it — yet something clearly is. That invisible resistance is called friction, a force that acts between two surfaces in contact and opposes the motion (or tendency to move) between them.

Picture the surfaces under a microscope. Even a "smooth" tabletop looks like a mountain range at tiny scales. The bumps on the book and the bumps on the table interlock and grip each other. The harder the surfaces press together, the more the bumps engage, and the stronger friction becomes.

Here is the simple rule. The magnitude of kinetic friction (friction while surfaces are already sliding) is:

$$ f_k = \mu_k N $$

where $N$ is the normal force — the force the surface pushes back on the object perpendicular to itself — and $\mu_k$ (pronounced "myu-sub-k") is a dimensionless number called the coefficient of kinetic friction. It depends only on what the two materials are, not on speed or surface area.

There is also static friction, which prevents sliding before motion begins. Its maximum value is $f_{s,\text{max}} = \mu_s N$, with $\mu_s$ slightly larger than $\mu_k$.

Try it yourself. Stack a heavy book on a lighter one and try to slide the top one. Then remove the heavy book and try again. Notice how much easier it is — because $N$ is smaller, and so is $f_k$.
