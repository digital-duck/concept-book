🍃 Derivative

The derivative is the central concept of differential calculus, providing a rigorous method for quantifying the instantaneous rate of change of a function. Formally, given a real-valued function $f(x)$, its derivative, denoted by $f'(x)$ or $\frac{d}{dx} f(x)$, measures how rapidly the corresponding output value changes with respect to its input variable at any specific point $x$. Conceptually, we approach this instantaneous rate by considering the slope of a secant line connecting two points $(x, f(x))$ and $(x+h, f(x+h))$. As the distance between these two points, $h$, approaches zero, the secant line converges to the tangent line, and its slope defines the derivative. The formal definition is given by the limit:

$$ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} $$

Worked Example: Consider the function $f(t) = t^2 + 3$. To find the rate of change at an arbitrary point $t$, we apply the definition. First, calculate $f(t+h)$:

$$ f(t+h) = (t+h)^2 + 3 = t^2 + 2th + h^2 + 3 $$

Now, substitute this into the difference quotient:

$$ \frac{d}{dt} f(t) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(t^2 + 2th + h^2 + 3) - (t^2 + 3)}{h} $$
