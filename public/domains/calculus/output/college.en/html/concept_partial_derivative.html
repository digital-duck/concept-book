Partial Derivative

Definition. Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function of $n$ variables $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$. The partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x_i$ at a point $\mathbf{a} = (a_1, \ldots, a_n)$ is defined as the limit

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a_1, \ldots, a_i + h, \ldots, a_n) - f(a_1, \ldots, a_n)}{h},$$

provided the limit exists. Geometrically, $\partial f / \partial x_i$ measures the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ as only $x_i$ varies while all other variables are held fixed. This is equivalent to restricting $f$ to the line parallel to the $x_i$-axis through $\mathbf{a}$ and computing the ordinary derivative along that line.

Worked Example. Let $f(x, y) = x^3 y^2 + \sin(xy)$. To compute $\partial f / \partial x$, treat $y$ as a constant and differentiate with respect to $x$:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 3x^2 y^2 + y\cos(xy).$$

Similarly, treating $x$ as a constant:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2x^3 y + x\cos(xy).$$

Evaluating at the point $(1, \pi)$ gives $\partial f / \partial x \big|_{(1,\pi)} = 3\pi^2 + \pi\cos(\pi) = 3\pi^2 - \pi$.

Key Theorem (Clairaut–Schwarz). If $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ has second-order partial derivatives $\partial^2 f / \partial x \, \partial y$ and $\partial^2 f / \partial y \, \partial x$ that are both continuous on an open set $U$, then they are equal on $U$:

$$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \, \partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \, \partial x}.$$

This symmetry of mixed partials is foundational for the study of the Hessian matrix and second-order optimality conditions.

Lab Exercise. Let $g(x, y, z) = e^{x^2 + y} \ln(z + 1)$.

  • Compute all three first-order partial derivatives $\partial g / \partial x$, $\partial g / \partial y$, $\partial g / \partial z$.
  • Compute the mixed partial $\partial^2 g / \partial x \, \partial y$ and verify Clairaut's theorem by computing $\partial^2 g / \partial y \, \partial x$ and confirming equality.
  • Evaluate $\nabla g$ at the point $(0, 0, 0)$ and interpret the result geometrically.
