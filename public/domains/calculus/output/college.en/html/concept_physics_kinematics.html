Physics Kinematics

Definition. Kinematics is the branch of classical mechanics that describes the motion of particles and rigid bodies without reference to the forces causing that motion. The fundamental kinematic quantities are position $\vec{r}(t)$, velocity $\vec{v}(t) = \dfrac{d\vec{r}}{dt}$, and acceleration $\vec{a}(t) = \dfrac{d\vec{v}}{dt}$. For one-dimensional motion under constant acceleration $a$, the kinematic equations are:

$$v = v_0 + at, \qquad x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2, \qquad v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0).$$

These three equations are not independent — any two imply the third — and they apply exclusively when $a$ is constant over the interval of interest.

Worked Example. A stone is dropped from rest at the edge of a cliff $h = 80\,\text{m}$ high. Taking downward as positive and $g = 10\,\text{m/s}^2$:

  • Time to impact: With $x_0 = 0$, $v_0 = 0$, $a = g$,
    • $$h = \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(80)}{10}} = 4\,\text{s}.$$
  • Speed at impact:
    • $$v = v_0 + gt = 0 + (10)(4) = 40\,\text{m/s}.$$

    Equivalently, $v^2 = 2gh = 2(10)(80) = 1600\,\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2 \Rightarrow v = 40\,\text{m/s}$, confirming consistency between equations.

    Key Theorem (Kinematic Decomposition). For projectile motion in two dimensions with constant gravitational acceleration $\vec{g} = -g\hat{j}$, the horizontal and vertical components of motion are kinematically independent:

    $$x(t) = x_0 + v_{0x}\,t, \qquad y(t) = y_0 + v_{0y}\,t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2.$$

    Proof sketch: Because $\vec{a} = -g\hat{j}$ has no $x$-component, integrating $\ddot{x} = 0$ yields $\dot{x} = v_{0x}$ (constant), and integrating again gives the linear $x(t)$ relation. The $y$-equation follows from $\ddot{y} = -g$ by two successive integrations with boundary conditions $y(0) = y_0$, $\dot{y}(0) = v_{0y}$. $\square$

    Lab Exercise. Launch a marble horizontally from a table of measured height $H$ with an initial speed $v_0$ (measured via photogate). Predict the horizontal range $R = v_0\sqrt{2H/g}$. Repeat for three launch speeds, record observed range, and compute the percent error. Discuss sources of systematic deviation, including air resistance and launch-angle uncertainty.
