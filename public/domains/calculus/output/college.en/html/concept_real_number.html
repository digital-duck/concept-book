Real Number

Definition. A real number is any element of the unique complete ordered field $(\mathbb{R}, +, \cdot, \leq)$. Formally, $\mathbb{R}$ is characterized by three axiom families: the field axioms (closure, associativity, commutativity, distributivity, identity, and inverse elements for $+$ and $\cdot$), the order axioms (totality, transitivity, and compatibility with field operations), and the completeness axiom — every non-empty subset of $\mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above has a least upper bound (supremum) in $\mathbb{R}$. This last axiom distinguishes $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$ and is the foundation of all limit-based analysis.

The real numbers encompass the integers $\mathbb{Z}$, the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$, and the irrationals. Irrationals such as $\sqrt{2}$ and $\pi$ are precisely those reals that cannot be expressed as $p/q$ with $p, q \in \mathbb{Z},\, q \neq 0$.

Worked Example. We show $\sqrt{2} \in \mathbb{R}$ by constructing its supremum. Let

$$S = \{ x \in \mathbb{Q} \mid x \geq 0 \text{ and } x^2 < 2 \}.$$

$S$ is non-empty ($1 \in S$) and bounded above ($2 \notin S$ implies $x < 2$ for all $x \in S$). By completeness, $\alpha = \sup S$ exists in $\mathbb{R}$. A standard trichotomy argument shows $\alpha^2 < 2$ and $\alpha^2 > 2$ both lead to contradictions, so $\alpha^2 = 2$, i.e., $\alpha = \sqrt{2}$.

Key Theorem (Archimedean Property). For every $x \in \mathbb{R}$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $n > x$.

Proof sketch. Suppose not; then $\mathbb{N}$ is bounded above by $x$. By completeness, $\sup \mathbb{N} = M$ exists. Since $M - 1$ is not an upper bound, there is $m \in \mathbb{N}$ with $m > M - 1$, giving $m + 1 > M$ — contradicting $M = \sup \mathbb{N}$. $\square$

This property implies $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$: between any two reals lies a rational.

Lab Exercise. Let $T = \{ r \in \mathbb{Q} \mid r^3 < 5 \}$. (a) Prove $T$ is bounded above. (b) Use the completeness axiom to argue $\sup T$ exists and equals $5^{1/3}$. (c) Verify that $5^{1/3} \notin \mathbb{Q}$ by a parity argument analogous to the classical proof for $\sqrt{2}$.
