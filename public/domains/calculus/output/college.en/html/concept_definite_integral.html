Definite Integral

Definition. Let $f$ be a bounded function on a closed interval $[a, b]$. Partition $[a, b]$ into $n$ subintervals by choosing points $a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = b$. For each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ of width $\Delta x_i = x_i - x_{i-1}$, select a sample point $x_i^* \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$. The definite integral of $f$ from $a$ to $b$ is defined as the limit of Riemann sums:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = \lim_{\|\mathcal{P}\| \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\, \Delta x_i,$$

where $\|\mathcal{P}\| = \max_i \Delta x_i$ is the mesh of the partition. When this limit exists and is finite, $f$ is said to be Riemann integrable on $[a, b]$. Geometrically, the definite integral represents the signed area between the graph of $f$ and the $x$-axis: regions where $f > 0$ contribute positively, and regions where $f < 0$ contribute negatively.

Worked Example. Compute $\displaystyle\int_0^2 (3x^2)\, dx$ using the definition with a uniform partition of $n$ subintervals and right endpoints $x_i^* = \frac{2i}{n}$.

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} 3\!\left(\frac{2i}{n}\right)^2 \cdot \frac{2}{n} = \frac{24}{n^3}\sum_{i=1}^{n} i^2 = \frac{24}{n^3} \cdot \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}.$$

Taking $n \to \infty$:

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{4(n+1)(2n+1)}{n^2} = \lim_{n\to\infty} 4\!\left(1+\tfrac{1}{n}\right)\!\left(2+\tfrac{1}{n}\right) = 8.$$

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part 2). If $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$—that is, $F'(x) = f(x)$—then:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = F(b) - F(a).$$

This theorem bridges the apparently distinct operations of differentiation and integration. Applying it to the example above: $F(x) = x^3$, so $F(2) - F(0) = 8 - 0 = 8$, confirming the Riemann sum calculation.

Then verify your answer numerically by computing a right-endpoint Riemann sum with $n = 1000$ subdivisions in a programming environment of your choice.
