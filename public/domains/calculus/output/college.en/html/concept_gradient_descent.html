Gradient Descent

Definition. Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a differentiable function. Gradient descent is an iterative first-order optimization algorithm that seeks a local minimum of $f$ by repeatedly moving in the direction of the negative gradient. Starting from an initial point $\mathbf{x}^{(0)} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the update rule at iteration $t$ is

$$\mathbf{x}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{x}^{(t)} - \eta \, \nabla f\!\left(\mathbf{x}^{(t)}\right),$$

where $\eta > 0$ is the learning rate (step size) and $\nabla f(\mathbf{x})$ denotes the gradient vector whose $i$-th component is $\partial f / \partial x_i$.

The negative gradient $-\nabla f(\mathbf{x})$ is the direction of steepest descent at $\mathbf{x}$: any directional derivative $D_{\mathbf{v}} f(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{x})^\top \mathbf{v}$ is minimized over unit vectors $\mathbf{v}$ precisely when $\mathbf{v} = -\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) / \|\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\|$, a consequence of the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality.

Worked Example. Consider $f(x) = x^2 + 4x + 5$ with learning rate $\eta = 0.3$ and starting point $x^{(0)} = 4$. The gradient is $f'(x) = 2x + 4$. The first three iterations proceed as follows:

Key Theorem (Convergence for Smooth Convex Functions). If $f$ is convex and $L$-smooth — meaning $\|\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) - \nabla f(\mathbf{y})\| \le L \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\|$ for all $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$ — then gradient descent with step size $\eta = 1/L$ satisfies

$$f\!\left(\mathbf{x}^{(T)}\right) - f(\mathbf{x}^*) \;\le\; \frac{L \,\|\mathbf{x}^{(0)} - \mathbf{x}^*\|^2}{2T},$$

giving an $O(1/T)$ convergence rate. Under the additional assumption of $\mu$-strong convexity, the rate improves to $O\!\left((1 - \mu/L)^T\right)$, i.e., linear (exponential) convergence.
