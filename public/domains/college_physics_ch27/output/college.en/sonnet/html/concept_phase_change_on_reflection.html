🍃 Phase Change On Reflection

🍃 Phase Change On Reflection

Definition. When a wave traveling through one medium reflects off a boundary with a second medium of higher wave speed (equivalently, lower index of refraction for light), the reflected wave undergoes no phase change. Conversely, when the wave reflects off a boundary where the transmitted medium has a lower wave speed (higher index of refraction), the reflected wave acquires a phase shift of $\pi$ radians, equivalent to a path-length shift of $\lambda/2$. This principle governs thin-film interference and has a direct analogy in mechanical waves: a transverse pulse on a string reflects inverted at a fixed (denser) boundary and upright at a free (less dense) boundary.

Mathematically, if the incident electric field is $E_i = E_0 \cos(kx - \omega t)$, then at a high-$n$ boundary the reflected field is

$$ E_r = -E_0 \cos(kx + \omega t) = E_0 \cos(kx + \omega t + \pi), $$

representing the $\pi$ phase shift explicitly.

Worked Example. A thin soap film ($n = 1.35$) of thickness $t = 120\,\text{nm}$ is surrounded by air ($n = 1.00$). Determine whether 648 nm light interferes constructively or destructively upon normal incidence.

Step 1. At the air–film interface, light goes from $n = 1.00$ to $n = 1.35$: reflected ray 1 acquires a $\pi$ phase shift. At the film–air interface, light goes from $n = 1.35$ to $n = 1.00$: reflected ray 2 acquires no phase shift. Thus the two reflections introduce a net relative phase of $\pi$.

Step 2. The optical path difference from the film thickness is $2nt = 2(1.35)(120\,\text{nm}) = 324\,\text{nm}$.

Step 3. Accounting for the $\pi$ shift (equivalent to $\lambda/2 = 324\,\text{nm}$), the effective path difference becomes $324\,\text{nm} + 324\,\text{nm} = 648\,\text{nm} = \lambda$. This satisfies the constructive-interference condition, so the film appears bright in reflected 648 nm (red) light.

Key Theorem. For a thin film of thickness $t$ and refractive index $n_f$ bounded by media with indices $n_1 < n_f$ and $n_f > n_2$, constructive interference in reflection requires

$$ 2n_f t = m\lambda, \quad m = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

because only one reflected ray undergoes a phase shift.
