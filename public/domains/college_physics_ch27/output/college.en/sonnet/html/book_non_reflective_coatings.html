🌱 Wavelength

🌱 Wavelength

Definition. The wavelength of a periodic wave is the spatial period of the wave pattern — the minimum distance over which the wave repeats itself. For a sinusoidal wave propagating through a medium, the wavelength $\lambda$ is the distance between two successive points that are in phase (e.g., crest to crest or trough to trough). Because the wave speed $v$ and frequency $f$ are related by $v = f\lambda$, wavelength is the spatial complement of the temporal period $T = 1/f$: in one period the wave front advances exactly one wavelength. In optics, the free-space wavelength is $\lambda_0 = c/f$, where $c \approx 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$; inside a medium with index of refraction $n$, the wave slows to $v = c/n$ while frequency is conserved, so the wavelength shortens to

$$ \lambda = \frac{\lambda_0}{n}. $$

Worked Example. A helium–neon laser emits red light with a free-space wavelength $\lambda_0 = 632.8\ \text{nm}$. Determine (a) the frequency of the light and (b) its wavelength inside a glass lens with $n = 1.52$.

(a) From $c = f\lambda_0$:

$$ f = \frac{c}{\lambda_0} = \frac{2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}{632.8 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{m}} = 4.738 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}. $$

(b) Inside the glass:

$$ \lambda = \frac{\lambda_0}{n} = \frac{632.8\ \text{nm}}{1.52} = 416.3\ \text{nm}. $$

Frequency is invariant at the interface; only wavelength and wave speed change.

Key Theorem — Diffraction Grating Condition. A grating with line spacing $d$ produces constructive interference in order $m$ at angle $\theta$ satisfying

$$ d\sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

This result follows directly from requiring that path-length differences between adjacent slits equal integer multiples of $\lambda$, making wavelength the measurable quantity that links interference geometry to source color.

🌱 Electromagnetic Wave

🌱 Electromagnetic Wave

Definition. An electromagnetic wave is a self-propagating disturbance in the electromagnetic field, consisting of mutually perpendicular oscillating electric field $\vec{E}$ and magnetic field $\vec{B}$ vectors, both transverse to the direction of propagation. Starting from Maxwell's equations—which govern $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ as previously established—one can derive that these fields satisfy coupled wave equations in vacuum:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial x^2} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial t^2}, \qquad \frac{\partial^2 B}{\partial x^2} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 B}{\partial t^2} $$

Comparing with the standard wave equation $\partial^2 f/\partial x^2 = (1/v^2)\,\partial^2 f/\partial t^2$ identifies the propagation speed as:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}} \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s} $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ T·m/A and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}$ C²/(N·m²). At every point and instant the field amplitudes satisfy $E = cB$.

Worked Example. A plane electromagnetic wave propagates in the $+x$ direction with electric field amplitude $E_0 = 150$ V/m and wavelength $\lambda = 500$ nm. Find the magnetic field amplitude $B_0$ and the wave frequency $f$.

Solution. From $E = cB$:

$$ B_0 = \frac{E_0}{c} = \frac{150 \text{ V/m}}{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}} = 5.00 \times 10^{-7} \text{ T} $$

From the universal wave relation $c = f\lambda$:

$$ f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{500 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}} = 6.00 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz} $$

This frequency places the wave in the visible green portion of the electromagnetic spectrum.

Key Theorem — Energy Transport. Because $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ store energy, an electromagnetic wave transports energy through space. The power per unit area is quantified by the Poynting vector $\vec{S} = (\vec{E} \times \vec{B})/\mu_0$, whose time average over one cycle gives the intensity:

$$ I = \frac{E_0^2}{2\mu_0 c} = \frac{c\varepsilon_0 E_0^2}{2} $$

This result connects the observable wave amplitude directly to radiated power, and underpins antenna design, optical power budgets, and radiometric calibration.

🌱 Index Of Refraction

🌱 Index Of Refraction

Definition

The index of refraction of a medium, denoted $n$, quantifies how much the speed of light is reduced as it propagates through that medium relative to its speed in vacuum. Formally,

$$ n = \frac{c}{v} $$

where $c = 2.998 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}$ is the speed of light in vacuum and $v$ is the phase speed of light in the medium. Since $v \leq c$ for all physical media, it follows that $n \geq 1$, with $n = 1$ exactly for vacuum. The index is dimensionless and, in general, depends weakly on wavelength. For visible light, glass typically has $n \approx 1.5$, water $n \approx 1.33$, and air $n \approx 1.0003$.

Worked Example

A ray of yellow light ($\lambda_0 = 589 \, \text{nm}$ in vacuum) enters a glass slab with $n = 1.52$. Determine the speed and wavelength of the light inside the glass.

Speed inside the glass:

$$ v = \frac{c}{n} = \frac{2.998 \times 10^8}{1.52} \approx 1.97 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s} $$

Wavelength inside the glass: since frequency is invariant across media,

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{c/n}{c/\lambda_0} = \frac{\lambda_0}{n} = \frac{589}{1.52} \approx 387 \, \text{nm} $$

The light slows and its wavelength shortens, but its frequency — and hence its color — remains unchanged.

Key Theorem (Snell's Law)

The index of refraction governs refraction at an interface between two media. If a ray crosses from medium 1 into medium 2, Snell's Law states:

$$ n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2 $$

where $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are the angles of incidence and refraction measured from the surface normal. This relation follows directly from requiring that the tangential component of the wave's phase velocity be continuous across the interface, so the wavefronts remain matched in phase along the boundary.

🍃 Interference

🍃 Interference

Definition. Interference is the superposition of two or more coherent waves occupying the same region of space, producing a resultant amplitude that differs from the simple sum of the individual amplitudes at each point. For two monochromatic waves of the same frequency and a fixed phase relationship, the principle of superposition states that the net electric field is $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{E}_1 + \mathbf{E}_2$. The resultant intensity depends critically on the path length difference $\Delta\ell$ between the two sources and the observation point. When $\Delta\ell = m\lambda$ (where $m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots$), the waves arrive in phase and constructive interference occurs, yielding maximum intensity $I_{\max} = (\sqrt{I_1}+\sqrt{I_2})^2$. When $\Delta\ell = (m + \tfrac{1}{2})\lambda$, the waves arrive $\pi$ radians out of phase and destructive interference occurs, yielding minimum intensity $I_{\min} = (\sqrt{I_1}-\sqrt{I_2})^2$. For equal-amplitude sources, $I_{\min} = 0$.

Worked Example. Two narrow slits separated by $d = 0.250\,\text{mm}$ are illuminated by a helium–neon laser ($\lambda = 632.8\,\text{nm}$). A screen is placed $L = 1.20\,\text{m}$ away. Find the positions of the first three bright fringes above the central maximum.

Using the small-angle approximation ($\theta \approx \sin\theta$), the condition for a bright fringe is $d\sin\theta = m\lambda$, so the vertical position on the screen is

$$ y_m = \frac{m\lambda L}{d}, \quad m = 1, 2, 3. $$

Substituting values:

$$ y_1 = \frac{(1)(632.8\times10^{-9})(1.20)}{2.50\times10^{-4}} = 3.04\,\text{mm}, $$ $$ y_2 = 6.07\,\text{mm}, \qquad y_3 = 9.11\,\text{mm}. $$

The fringe spacing is constant: $\Delta y = \lambda L/d = 3.04\,\text{mm}$.

Key Theorem. For two point sources of equal intensity $I_0$, the resultant intensity at any observation point is

$$ I = 4I_0\cos^2\!\left(\frac{\delta}{2}\right), $$

where $\delta = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\Delta\ell$ is the phase difference. This expression encapsulates both constructive ($\delta = 2m\pi$) and destructive ($\delta = (2m+1)\pi$) limits in a single closed form.

Compare your measured wavelength to the manufacturer specification and calculate the percent error.

🍃 Constructive Interference

🍃 Constructive Interference

Definition. When two or more waves occupy the same region of space simultaneously, the principle of superposition states that the resultant displacement at any point equals the vector sum of the individual displacements. Constructive interference occurs when superimposing waves combine to produce an amplitude greater than that of either constituent wave. For two sinusoidal waves of equal amplitude $A$ and wavelength $\lambda$, constructive interference arises when their phase difference $\delta$ satisfies

$$ \delta = 2\pi m, \quad m = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots $$

Equivalently, in terms of path-length difference $\Delta r$ between the two sources and the observation point,

$$ \Delta r = m\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots $$

Under perfect constructive interference the resultant amplitude is $2A$ and the resultant intensity is $I = 4I_0$, where $I_0 = \frac{1}{2}\rho v A^2$ is the intensity of each individual wave.

Worked Example. Two coherent point sources $S_1$ and $S_2$ emit sound at $f = 680\,\text{Hz}$ in air ($v = 340\,\text{m/s}$), separated by $d = 4.0\,\text{m}$. A detector lies along the perpendicular bisector. Determine the first off-center position of constructive interference along a line parallel to $S_1 S_2$ at perpendicular distance $L = 3.0\,\text{m}$.

The wavelength is $\lambda = v/f = 0.50\,\text{m}$. For $m = 1$, the required path difference is $\Delta r = \lambda = 0.50\,\text{m}$. Writing the geometry with the detector displaced by $y$ from center:

$$ \Delta r = \sqrt{L^2 + (y + d/2)^2} - \sqrt{L^2 + (y - d/2)^2} = 0.50\,\text{m} $$

Solving numerically (or via the small-angle approximation $y \approx m\lambda L/d$ when $y \operatorname{ll} d$) gives $y \approx 0.375\,\text{m}$.

Key Theorem (Young's Condition). For a two-slit experiment with slit separation $d$ and screen distance $L \operatorname{gg} d$, bright fringes form at lateral positions

$$ y_m = \frac{m\lambda L}{d} $$

This follows directly from the path-difference condition $\Delta r \approx d\sin\theta \approx d\,(y/L)$ in the small-angle regime, confirming that fringe spacing $\Delta y = \lambda L/d$ grows with wavelength and screen distance.

🍃 Destructive Interference

🍃 Destructive Interference

Definition. Destructive interference occurs when two or more coherent waves superpose such that their displacements partially or completely cancel. For two sinusoidal waves of equal amplitude $A$ and wavelength $\lambda$ traveling along the same path, complete destructive interference results when the waves are exactly out of phase by $\pi$ radians (or an odd multiple thereof). The net displacement at any point satisfies the superposition principle:

$$ y_{\text{net}}(x, t) = y_1 + y_2 = A\sin(kx - \omega t) + A\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

Using the sum-to-product identity, this simplifies to:

$$ y_{\text{net}} = 2A\cos\!\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right)\sin\!\left(kx - \omega t + \frac{\phi}{2}\right) $$

Complete destructive interference occurs when $\cos(\phi/2) = 0$, i.e., when the phase difference satisfies:

$$ \phi = (2m+1)\pi, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

Equivalently, in terms of path length difference $\Delta d$, destructive interference requires:

$$ \Delta d = \left(m + \frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

Worked Example. Two coherent speakers separated by $d = 4.00\ \text{m}$ emit sound at $f = 686\ \text{Hz}$. The speed of sound is $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$, giving $\lambda = v/f = 0.500\ \text{m}$. A listener stands at a point where the path difference from the two speakers is $\Delta d = 0.75\ \text{m}$. Determine whether this is a point of constructive or destructive interference.

Dividing by $\lambda$: $\Delta d / \lambda = 0.75/0.50 = 1.5 = 3/2$. Since $3/2 = m + 1/2$ with $m = 1$, the condition for destructive interference is satisfied. The listener hears a minimum in intensity — effectively silence if both speakers have equal amplitude.

Key Theorem (Condition for Destructive Interference in Double-Slit Geometry). In Young's double-slit experiment with slit separation $d$ and screen distance $L \operatorname{gg} d$, destructive interference minima occur at transverse positions:

$$ y_m = \left(m + \frac{1}{2}\right)\frac{\lambda L}{d}, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

This result follows directly from the small-angle approximation $\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta \approx y/L$.

🍃 Phase Change On Reflection

🍃 Phase Change On Reflection

Definition. When a wave traveling through one medium reflects off a boundary with a second medium of higher wave speed (equivalently, lower index of refraction for light), the reflected wave undergoes no phase change. Conversely, when the wave reflects off a boundary where the transmitted medium has a lower wave speed (higher index of refraction), the reflected wave acquires a phase shift of $\pi$ radians, equivalent to a path-length shift of $\lambda/2$. This principle governs thin-film interference and has a direct analogy in mechanical waves: a transverse pulse on a string reflects inverted at a fixed (denser) boundary and upright at a free (less dense) boundary.

Mathematically, if the incident electric field is $E_i = E_0 \cos(kx - \omega t)$, then at a high-$n$ boundary the reflected field is

$$ E_r = -E_0 \cos(kx + \omega t) = E_0 \cos(kx + \omega t + \pi), $$

representing the $\pi$ phase shift explicitly.

Worked Example. A thin soap film ($n = 1.35$) of thickness $t = 120\,\text{nm}$ is surrounded by air ($n = 1.00$). Determine whether 648 nm light interferes constructively or destructively upon normal incidence.

Step 1. At the air–film interface, light goes from $n = 1.00$ to $n = 1.35$: reflected ray 1 acquires a $\pi$ phase shift. At the film–air interface, light goes from $n = 1.35$ to $n = 1.00$: reflected ray 2 acquires no phase shift. Thus the two reflections introduce a net relative phase of $\pi$.

Step 2. The optical path difference from the film thickness is $2nt = 2(1.35)(120\,\text{nm}) = 324\,\text{nm}$.

Step 3. Accounting for the $\pi$ shift (equivalent to $\lambda/2 = 324\,\text{nm}$), the effective path difference becomes $324\,\text{nm} + 324\,\text{nm} = 648\,\text{nm} = \lambda$. This satisfies the constructive-interference condition, so the film appears bright in reflected 648 nm (red) light.

Key Theorem. For a thin film of thickness $t$ and refractive index $n_f$ bounded by media with indices $n_1 < n_f$ and $n_f > n_2$, constructive interference in reflection requires

$$ 2n_f t = m\lambda, \quad m = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

because only one reflected ray undergoes a phase shift.

🍃 Wavelength In Medium

🍃 Wavelength In Medium

Definition. When a monochromatic electromagnetic wave propagates through a transparent medium of index of refraction $n$, its frequency $f$ remains unchanged (determined by the source), but its speed decreases from $c$ to $v = c/n$. Since the wave relation $v = f\lambda$ must hold in any medium, the wavelength shortens correspondingly:

$$ \lambda_n = \frac{\lambda}{n} $$

where $\lambda$ is the free-space (vacuum) wavelength and $\lambda_n$ is the wavelength inside the medium. Equivalently, $\lambda_n = v/f = c/(nf)$. This reduction is physically significant: the spatial phase accumulated over a given path length $L$ is $\phi = 2\pi L / \lambda_n = 2\pi n L / \lambda$, so a medium of higher refractive index packs more wave cycles into the same geometric distance.

Worked Example. Sodium yellow light has a vacuum wavelength $\lambda = 589\,\text{nm}$. Find its wavelength inside crown glass, where $n = 1.52$.

$$ \lambda_n = \frac{589\,\text{nm}}{1.52} = 387\,\text{nm} $$

The frequency is unchanged: $f = c/\lambda = (3.00 \times 10^8\,\text{m/s})/(589 \times 10^{-9}\,\text{m}) = 5.09 \times 10^{14}\,\text{Hz}$, and indeed $f = v/\lambda_n = (c/n)/\lambda_n = c/\lambda$, confirming consistency. Note that 387 nm falls in the near-ultraviolet, illustrating that the color experienced by an observer is governed by frequency, not by the wavelength inside the medium.

Key Theorem (Phase Accumulation). Because $\lambda_n = \lambda/n$, a wave traveling a geometric distance $L$ through a medium of index $n$ accumulates phase

$$ \phi = \frac{2\pi L}{\lambda_n} = \frac{2\pi n L}{\lambda} $$

This result follows directly from the definition above with no additional primitives. It shows that phase depends on the product $nL$, not on $L$ alone—the foundation of optical path length accounting in all wave-optics calculations.

🍃 Thin Film Interference

🍃 Thin Film Interference

Definition. Thin film interference arises when light reflects from the two surfaces of a thin transparent layer (such as a soap film, oil slick, or anti-reflection coating) and the reflected rays superpose. Because the two reflections travel different optical path lengths, they acquire a phase difference that produces constructive or destructive interference. The optical path difference (OPD) for a film of thickness $t$ and refractive index $n$ viewed at near-normal incidence is

$$ \text{OPD} = 2nt, $$

with an additional phase shift of $\lambda/2$ (equivalent to a path difference of $\lambda/2$) occurring whenever light reflects from an interface where it passes into a medium of higher refractive index. This shift is analogous to the inversion of a reflected wave on a string fixed at one end.

Worked Example. A soap film ($n = 1.33$) in air has uniform thickness $t = 380\ \text{nm}$. For which visible wavelengths does the reflected light show constructive interference?

Both reflections are relevant: at the air–soap boundary, the wave undergoes a phase shift of $\lambda/2$ (low-to-high $n$); at the soap–air boundary it does not (high-to-low $n$). The net phase difference from the two boundary shifts is $\lambda/2$, so the condition for constructive interference becomes

$$ 2nt = \left(m + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\lambda, \quad m = 0, 1, 2, \ldots $$

Solving for $\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{2nt}{m + \tfrac{1}{2}} = \frac{2(1.33)(380\ \text{nm})}{m + 0.5}. $$

For $m = 0$: $\lambda = 2025\ \text{nm}$ (infrared, invisible).
For $m = 1$: $\lambda = 675\ \text{nm}$ (red, visible).
For $m = 2$: $\lambda = 405\ \text{nm}$ (violet, visible).

The film appears reddish-violet in reflected light.

Key Theorem. The general conditions for a film bounded by media of refractive indices $n_1 < n_2 > n_1$ (one phase shift) are:

$$ \text{Constructive: } 2n_2 t = \left(m + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\lambda, \qquad \text{Destructive: } 2n_2 t = m\lambda. $$

For a film bounded by $n_1 < n_2 < n_3$ (two phase shifts cancel), the conditions interchange: constructive requires $2n_2 t = m\lambda$.

🌸 Non Reflective Coatings

🌸 Non Reflective Coatings

Definition. A non-reflective coating (antireflection coating) is a thin film of transparent material deposited on an optical surface to minimize reflected light through destructive interference. When light travels from a medium of index $n_1$ into a film of index $n_f$ and then into a substrate of index $n_s$, reflections occur at both interfaces. Destructive interference between these two reflected beams eliminates reflection when two conditions are simultaneously satisfied: (1) the film thickness produces a path-length difference of half a wavelength, and (2) the phase shifts at the two interfaces are accounted for correctly.

At each interface where light travels from a lower to a higher index medium, the reflected wave undergoes a phase shift of $\pi$ (equivalent to $\lambda/2$). For a coating with $n_1 < n_f < n_s$ (e.g., air–MgF₂–glass), both reflections suffer the same phase shift, so they cancel. The condition for destructive interference reduces to a path difference of one full wavelength in the film, giving the minimum thickness:

$$ 2 n_f t = \lambda, \quad \Rightarrow \quad t = \frac{\lambda}{2 n_f} $$

For the reflection to vanish completely, the amplitudes must also match, requiring $n_f = \sqrt{n_1 n_s}$.

Worked Example. A camera lens ($n_s = 1.52$) is coated with magnesium fluoride ($n_f = 1.38$) in air ($n_1 = 1.00$). Find the minimum coating thickness for $\lambda = 550\,\text{nm}$ (green light).

Both interfaces involve a lower-to-higher index transition, so both reflected waves acquire a $\pi$ phase shift. The net phase difference from reflection alone is zero; the film must contribute the full half-wavelength path difference:

$$ t = \frac{\lambda}{2 n_f} = \frac{550\,\text{nm}}{2 \times 1.38} \approx 199\,\text{nm}. $$

Key Theorem. For a single thin film between two semi-infinite media with $n_1 < n_f < n_s$, complete destructive interference of the first-order reflected beams occurs when $n_f = \sqrt{n_1 n_s}$ and $t = \lambda/(2n_f)$. This follows from the transfer-matrix formalism applied to Maxwell's boundary conditions at each interface.

Payoff 🎯

Non-reflective coatings represent the culminating synthesis of wave optics as developed throughout this chapter. To understand their function precisely, consider a thin dielectric film of thickness $d$ and refractive index $n_f$ deposited on a substrate of refractive index $n_s$, where $n_1 < n_f < n_s$ (with $n_1$ denoting the incident medium, typically air). When monochromatic light of wavelength $\lambda$ strikes the film at normal incidence, reflections occur at both the air–film and film–substrate interfaces. Because $n_f > n_1$ and $n_s > n_f$, both reflections involve a transition from a lower to a higher index medium, so each reflected ray undergoes a phase shift of $\pi$. These two phase shifts cancel, leaving only the geometric path difference $2n_f d$ to determine the interference condition. Destructive interference in the reflected beam—and hence zero net reflection—occurs when

$$ 2n_f d = \left(m + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\lambda, \quad m = 0, 1, 2, \ldots $$

For the minimum thickness design ($m = 0$), the coating must satisfy $d = \lambda / (4n_f)$, the quarter-wave condition.

Worked example. Magnesium fluoride ($\text{MgF}_2$, $n_f = 1.38$) is a standard anti-reflection coating for glass lenses ($n_s \approx 1.52$). To suppress 550 nm light (peak solar irradiance), the required film thickness is

$$ d = \frac{550\,\text{nm}}{4 \times 1.38} \approx 99.6\,\text{nm}. $$

A layer less than a hundred nanometres thick eliminates the dominant reflection entirely—a striking consequence of coherent wave cancellation.

Key theorem. For a single-layer coating at normal incidence, perfect destructive interference requires not only the quarter-wave thickness but also that $n_f = \sqrt{n_1 n_s}$. When $n_1 = 1$ (air), this gives $n_f = \sqrt{n_s}$; for glass with $n_s = 1.52$, the ideal index is $n_f \approx 1.23$, slightly below that of $\text{MgF}_2$. This explains why practical single-layer coatings achieve strong but not perfect suppression, motivating the multi-layer designs used in precision optics.

Non-reflective coatings stand as the natural endpoint of this chapter because they draw together every preceding concept: the ray model establishes the interfaces, the wave model supplies the phase analysis, and thin-film interference provides the quantitative design rule. Mastery of this concept opens immediate pathways into optical instrument design, solar cell efficiency engineering, and display technology.
