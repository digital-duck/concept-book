🍃 Interference Order

🍃 Interference Order

Definition. In a double-slit interference experiment, the interference order (also called the order number) is the integer $m$ that labels each bright fringe according to the path-length difference between the two interfering waves. Constructive interference occurs whenever the path difference $\Delta L$ equals a whole number of wavelengths:

$$ \Delta L = d\sin\theta = m\lambda, \qquad m = 0,\,\pm 1,\,\pm 2,\,\ldots $$

where $d$ is the slit separation, $\theta$ is the angle from the central axis to the fringe, and $\lambda$ is the wavelength of light in the medium. The fringe at $m = 0$ is the central maximum (zeroth order); those at $m = \pm 1$ are the first-order maxima, and so on. Destructive interference (dark fringes) obeys

$$ d\sin\theta = \left(m + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\lambda, \qquad m = 0,\,\pm 1,\,\pm 2,\,\ldots $$

Worked Example. Coherent light of wavelength $\lambda = 589\,\text{nm}$ illuminates a double slit with separation $d = 0.250\,\text{mm}$. A screen is placed $L = 1.20\,\text{m}$ away. Find the position of the second-order bright fringe.

From $d\sin\theta = m\lambda$ with $m = 2$:

$$ \sin\theta = \frac{m\lambda}{d} = \frac{2 \times 589 \times 10^{-9}}{0.250 \times 10^{-3}} = 4.71 \times 10^{-3} $$

Since $\theta$ is small, $\tan\theta \approx \sin\theta$, giving the fringe position on the screen:

$$ y_m = L\tan\theta \approx L\sin\theta = 1.20 \times 4.71 \times 10^{-3} \approx 5.65\,\text{mm} $$

Key Theorem. For small angles ($\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta \approx \theta$), the spacing between consecutive bright fringes is uniform:

$$ \Delta y = \frac{\lambda L}{d} $$

This result confirms that the fringe pattern is linear in order number $m$, with fringe spacing determined solely by the ratio $\lambda/d$ and the screen distance $L$. Higher-order fringes become dimmer due to the single-slit diffraction envelope, which imposes an amplitude modulation on the interference pattern.

Lab Exercise. Illuminate a double slit ($d = 0.400\,\text{mm}$) with a laser and project the pattern onto a screen at $L = 2.00\,\text{m}$. Measure the positions of at least five bright fringes on each side of the central maximum, calculate the average fringe spacing $\Delta y$, and use $\lambda = \Delta y \cdot d / L$ to determine the laser wavelength. Compare your result against the manufacturer's specification and compute the percentage error.
