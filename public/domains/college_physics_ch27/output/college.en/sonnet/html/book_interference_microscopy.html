🌱 Wavelength

🌱 Wavelength

Definition. The wavelength of a periodic wave is the spatial period of the wave pattern — the minimum distance over which the wave repeats itself. For a sinusoidal wave propagating through a medium, the wavelength $\lambda$ is the distance between two successive points that are in phase (e.g., crest to crest or trough to trough). Because the wave speed $v$ and frequency $f$ are related by $v = f\lambda$, wavelength is the spatial complement of the temporal period $T = 1/f$: in one period the wave front advances exactly one wavelength. In optics, the free-space wavelength is $\lambda_0 = c/f$, where $c \approx 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$; inside a medium with index of refraction $n$, the wave slows to $v = c/n$ while frequency is conserved, so the wavelength shortens to

$$ \lambda = \frac{\lambda_0}{n}. $$

Worked Example. A helium–neon laser emits red light with a free-space wavelength $\lambda_0 = 632.8\ \text{nm}$. Determine (a) the frequency of the light and (b) its wavelength inside a glass lens with $n = 1.52$.

(a) From $c = f\lambda_0$:

$$ f = \frac{c}{\lambda_0} = \frac{2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}{632.8 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{m}} = 4.738 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}. $$

(b) Inside the glass:

$$ \lambda = \frac{\lambda_0}{n} = \frac{632.8\ \text{nm}}{1.52} = 416.3\ \text{nm}. $$

Frequency is invariant at the interface; only wavelength and wave speed change.

Key Theorem — Diffraction Grating Condition. A grating with line spacing $d$ produces constructive interference in order $m$ at angle $\theta$ satisfying

$$ d\sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

This result follows directly from requiring that path-length differences between adjacent slits equal integer multiples of $\lambda$, making wavelength the measurable quantity that links interference geometry to source color.

Calculate $\lambda$ from $d\sin\theta = m\lambda$, identify which region of the visible spectrum the source occupies, and estimate the uncertainty $\delta\lambda$ arising from a $\pm 0.2°$ uncertainty in $\theta$ by differentiating the grating equation with respect to $\theta$.

🌱 Electromagnetic Wave

🌱 Electromagnetic Wave

Definition. An electromagnetic wave is a self-propagating disturbance in the electromagnetic field, consisting of mutually perpendicular oscillating electric field $\vec{E}$ and magnetic field $\vec{B}$ vectors, both transverse to the direction of propagation. Starting from Maxwell's equations—which govern $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ as previously established—one can derive that these fields satisfy coupled wave equations in vacuum:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial x^2} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial t^2}, \qquad \frac{\partial^2 B}{\partial x^2} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 B}{\partial t^2} $$

Comparing with the standard wave equation $\partial^2 f/\partial x^2 = (1/v^2)\,\partial^2 f/\partial t^2$ identifies the propagation speed as:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}} \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s} $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ T·m/A and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}$ C²/(N·m²). At every point and instant the field amplitudes satisfy $E = cB$.

Worked Example. A plane electromagnetic wave propagates in the $+x$ direction with electric field amplitude $E_0 = 150$ V/m and wavelength $\lambda = 500$ nm. Find the magnetic field amplitude $B_0$ and the wave frequency $f$.

Solution. From $E = cB$:

$$ B_0 = \frac{E_0}{c} = \frac{150 \text{ V/m}}{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}} = 5.00 \times 10^{-7} \text{ T} $$

From the universal wave relation $c = f\lambda$:

$$ f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{500 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}} = 6.00 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz} $$

This frequency places the wave in the visible green portion of the electromagnetic spectrum.

Key Theorem — Energy Transport. Because $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ store energy, an electromagnetic wave transports energy through space. The power per unit area is quantified by the Poynting vector $\vec{S} = (\vec{E} \times \vec{B})/\mu_0$, whose time average over one cycle gives the intensity:

$$ I = \frac{E_0^2}{2\mu_0 c} = \frac{c\varepsilon_0 E_0^2}{2} $$

This result connects the observable wave amplitude directly to radiated power, and underpins antenna design, optical power budgets, and radiometric calibration.

(b) If the peak electric field inside is $E_0 = 1.20 \times 10^4$ V/m, determine the time-averaged intensity $I$. (c) Explain why standing waves in the cavity can produce uneven heating and describe one engineering solution used in consumer ovens to mitigate this effect.

🍃 Interference

🍃 Interference

Definition. Interference is the superposition of two or more coherent waves occupying the same region of space, producing a resultant amplitude that differs from the simple sum of the individual amplitudes at each point. For two monochromatic waves of the same frequency and a fixed phase relationship, the principle of superposition states that the net electric field is $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{E}_1 + \mathbf{E}_2$. The resultant intensity depends critically on the path length difference $\Delta\ell$ between the two sources and the observation point. When $\Delta\ell = m\lambda$ (where $m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots$), the waves arrive in phase and constructive interference occurs, yielding maximum intensity $I_{\max} = (\sqrt{I_1}+\sqrt{I_2})^2$. When $\Delta\ell = (m + \tfrac{1}{2})\lambda$, the waves arrive $\pi$ radians out of phase and destructive interference occurs, yielding minimum intensity $I_{\min} = (\sqrt{I_1}-\sqrt{I_2})^2$. For equal-amplitude sources, $I_{\min} = 0$.

Worked Example. Two narrow slits separated by $d = 0.250\,\text{mm}$ are illuminated by a helium–neon laser ($\lambda = 632.8\,\text{nm}$). A screen is placed $L = 1.20\,\text{m}$ away. Find the positions of the first three bright fringes above the central maximum.

Using the small-angle approximation ($\theta \approx \sin\theta$), the condition for a bright fringe is $d\sin\theta = m\lambda$, so the vertical position on the screen is

$$ y_m = \frac{m\lambda L}{d}, \quad m = 1, 2, 3. $$

Substituting values:

$$ y_1 = \frac{(1)(632.8\times10^{-9})(1.20)}{2.50\times10^{-4}} = 3.04\,\text{mm}, $$ $$ y_2 = 6.07\,\text{mm}, \qquad y_3 = 9.11\,\text{mm}. $$

The fringe spacing is constant: $\Delta y = \lambda L/d = 3.04\,\text{mm}$.

Key Theorem. For two point sources of equal intensity $I_0$, the resultant intensity at any observation point is

$$ I = 4I_0\cos^2\!\left(\frac{\delta}{2}\right), $$

where $\delta = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\Delta\ell$ is the phase difference. This expression encapsulates both constructive ($\delta = 2m\pi$) and destructive ($\delta = (2m+1)\pi$) limits in a single closed form.

Compare your measured wavelength to the manufacturer specification and calculate the percent error.

🌱 Wavefront

🌱 Wavefront

Definition. A wavefront is a surface of constant phase in a propagating wave field. More precisely, if a wave is described by a scalar field $\psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = A(\mathbf{r})\cos(\phi(\mathbf{r}) - \omega t)$, then a wavefront at time $t_0$ is the locus of all points $\mathbf{r}$ satisfying $\phi(\mathbf{r}) = \omega t_0 + C$ for some constant $C$. Every point on a wavefront oscillates in phase with every other point on that same surface, and the direction of wave propagation is perpendicular to the local wavefront at every point. For a point source radiating isotropically in a homogeneous medium, the wavefronts are concentric spheres; at large distances from the source, these spheres become locally indistinguishable from planes — the idealization underlying most optical calculations.

Worked Example. A small LED is placed at the origin in air. At $t = 0$, the $500\,\text{nm}$ light it emits has traveled a distance $r = 1.0\,\text{m}$. Find the radius of the wavefront and describe its geometry.

Since the source is a point and the medium is isotropic, the wavefront is a sphere of radius $r = ct$. With $c = 3.0 \times 10^8\,\text{m/s}$, the time elapsed is:

$$ t = \frac{r}{c} = \frac{1.0}{3.0 \times 10^8} \approx 3.3 \times 10^{-9}\,\text{s} $$

The wavefront is a sphere of radius $1.0\,\text{m}$, centered on the LED. The local radius of curvature of $1.0\,\text{m}$ is large compared to the wavelength ($500\,\text{nm}$), so within any small aperture the wavefront is effectively planar — the plane-wave approximation is valid.

Key Theorem (Huygens' Principle). Every point on a wavefront acts as a secondary source of spherical wavelets. The new wavefront at a later time is the envelope of all these secondary wavelets:

$$ r_{\text{new}} = r_{\text{old}} + c\,\Delta t $$

This principle underlies the derivation of Snell's law and predicts diffraction when a wavefront encounters an aperture, linking wavefront geometry directly to interference phenomena.

Measure the radii of three successive crests and verify that consecutive radii differ by one wavelength $\lambda = v/f$, where $v$ is the wave speed and $f$ is the driving frequency. Repeat with a linear vibrating bar to generate planar wavefronts and confirm that the crests appear as straight parallel lines.

🍃 Huygens Principle

🍃 Huygens Principle

Definition. Huygens' Principle states that every point on a propagating wavefront acts as a secondary source of spherical wavelets. The new wavefront at any later time is the envelope — the common tangent surface — of all these secondary wavelets. Mathematically, if a wavefront occupies surface $\Sigma$ at time $t$, then at time $t + \Delta t$ each point $P \in \Sigma$ emits a spherical wavelet of radius $c\,\Delta t$, where $c$ is the wave speed in the medium. The reconstructed wavefront $\Sigma'$ is the surface tangent to the family of spheres

$$ |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_P| = c\,\Delta t, \quad P \in \Sigma. $$

This geometric construction predicts straight-line propagation in a uniform medium, reflection, refraction, and diffraction from a single unified principle.

Worked Example. Consider a plane wave in air ($c_1 = 3.00 \times 10^8\ \mathrm{m/s}$) striking a glass interface ($c_2 = 2.00 \times 10^8\ \mathrm{m/s}$) at an angle of incidence $\theta_1 = 30°$. Using Huygens' construction, the time for one end of the wavefront to travel to the interface is $t = d/c_1$, where $d$ is the wavefront width. During this same interval, the refracted wavelet in glass travels a distance $c_2 t = d\,c_2/c_1$. The geometry yields

$$ \sin\theta_2 = \frac{c_2}{c_1}\sin\theta_1 = \frac{2.00}{3.00}\sin 30° = 0.333, $$

giving $\theta_2 = 19.5°$, in exact agreement with Snell's Law. This derivation reveals that Snell's Law is a direct consequence of the differing wave speeds across a boundary.

Key Theorem. The equivalence of Huygens' construction with the Fresnel–Kirchhoff diffraction integral shows that Huygens' Principle is not merely a geometric mnemonic but an exact result of the wave equation in the far-field limit. Specifically, for a monochromatic wave of wavelength $\lambda$, the amplitude at a field point is

$$ U(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{-i}{\lambda} \iint_\Sigma U(\mathbf{r}') \frac{e^{ikr'}}{r'}\cos\theta\, dS', $$

where $r' = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|$ and $\theta$ is the obliquity angle.

Lab Exercise. Fill a ripple tank with water and generate a straight wavefront using a linear oscillator at $f = 10\ \mathrm{Hz}$. Place a barrier with a single slit of width $a = 1\ \mathrm{cm}$. Observe the transmitted pattern and verify that when $a \approx \lambda$, the wavefront behind the slit is semicircular, confirming that the aperture acts as a single Huygens point source. Record the angular spread $\theta \approx \sin^{-1}(\lambda/a)$ and compare it to the measured half-power angle in the diffracted pattern.

🍃 Constructive Interference

🍃 Constructive Interference

Definition. When two or more waves occupy the same region of space simultaneously, the principle of superposition states that the resultant displacement at any point equals the vector sum of the individual displacements. Constructive interference occurs when superimposing waves combine to produce an amplitude greater than that of either constituent wave. For two sinusoidal waves of equal amplitude $A$ and wavelength $\lambda$, constructive interference arises when their phase difference $\delta$ satisfies

$$ \delta = 2\pi m, \quad m = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots $$

Equivalently, in terms of path-length difference $\Delta r$ between the two sources and the observation point,

$$ \Delta r = m\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots $$

Under perfect constructive interference the resultant amplitude is $2A$ and the resultant intensity is $I = 4I_0$, where $I_0 = \frac{1}{2}\rho v A^2$ is the intensity of each individual wave.

Worked Example. Two coherent point sources $S_1$ and $S_2$ emit sound at $f = 680\,\text{Hz}$ in air ($v = 340\,\text{m/s}$), separated by $d = 4.0\,\text{m}$. A detector lies along the perpendicular bisector. Determine the first off-center position of constructive interference along a line parallel to $S_1 S_2$ at perpendicular distance $L = 3.0\,\text{m}$.

The wavelength is $\lambda = v/f = 0.50\,\text{m}$. For $m = 1$, the required path difference is $\Delta r = \lambda = 0.50\,\text{m}$. Writing the geometry with the detector displaced by $y$ from center:

$$ \Delta r = \sqrt{L^2 + (y + d/2)^2} - \sqrt{L^2 + (y - d/2)^2} = 0.50\,\text{m} $$

Solving numerically (or via the small-angle approximation $y \approx m\lambda L/d$ when $y \operatorname{ll} d$) gives $y \approx 0.375\,\text{m}$.

Key Theorem (Young's Condition). For a two-slit experiment with slit separation $d$ and screen distance $L \operatorname{gg} d$, bright fringes form at lateral positions

$$ y_m = \frac{m\lambda L}{d} $$

This follows directly from the path-difference condition $\Delta r \approx d\sin\theta \approx d\,(y/L)$ in the small-angle regime, confirming that fringe spacing $\Delta y = \lambda L/d$ grows with wavelength and screen distance.

Lab Exercise. Set up a ripple tank with two coherent point sources separated by $d = 3\,\text{cm}$, oscillating at $f = 10\,\text{Hz}$. Measure the perpendicular distance to the first constructive nodal line from the central maximum. Compare your result to the theoretical prediction $y_1 = \lambda L/d$ and compute the percent discrepancy, noting how deviations grow as $y$ becomes comparable to $d$.

🍃 Destructive Interference

🍃 Destructive Interference

Definition. Destructive interference occurs when two or more coherent waves superpose such that their displacements partially or completely cancel. For two sinusoidal waves of equal amplitude $A$ and wavelength $\lambda$ traveling along the same path, complete destructive interference results when the waves are exactly out of phase by $\pi$ radians (or an odd multiple thereof). The net displacement at any point satisfies the superposition principle:

$$ y_{\text{net}}(x, t) = y_1 + y_2 = A\sin(kx - \omega t) + A\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

Using the sum-to-product identity, this simplifies to:

$$ y_{\text{net}} = 2A\cos\!\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right)\sin\!\left(kx - \omega t + \frac{\phi}{2}\right) $$

Complete destructive interference occurs when $\cos(\phi/2) = 0$, i.e., when the phase difference satisfies:

$$ \phi = (2m+1)\pi, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

Equivalently, in terms of path length difference $\Delta d$, destructive interference requires:

$$ \Delta d = \left(m + \frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

Worked Example. Two coherent speakers separated by $d = 4.00\ \text{m}$ emit sound at $f = 686\ \text{Hz}$. The speed of sound is $v = 343\ \text{m/s}$, giving $\lambda = v/f = 0.500\ \text{m}$. A listener stands at a point where the path difference from the two speakers is $\Delta d = 0.75\ \text{m}$. Determine whether this is a point of constructive or destructive interference.

Dividing by $\lambda$: $\Delta d / \lambda = 0.75/0.50 = 1.5 = 3/2$. Since $3/2 = m + 1/2$ with $m = 1$, the condition for destructive interference is satisfied. The listener hears a minimum in intensity — effectively silence if both speakers have equal amplitude.

Key Theorem (Condition for Destructive Interference in Double-Slit Geometry). In Young's double-slit experiment with slit separation $d$ and screen distance $L \operatorname{gg} d$, destructive interference minima occur at transverse positions:

$$ y_m = \left(m + \frac{1}{2}\right)\frac{\lambda L}{d}, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

This result follows directly from the small-angle approximation $\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta \approx y/L$.

Lab Exercise. Set up two coherent wave sources in a ripple tank separated by $3\lambda$. Map the nodal lines (destructive interference) experimentally, then predict their angular positions using $\sin\theta_m = (m + 1/2)\lambda/d$. Verify that the number of nodal lines between the central maximum and the first-order maximum equals the number predicted theoretically. Quantify discrepancies and attribute them to finite source size or boundary reflections.

🍃 Diffraction

🍃 Diffraction

Definition. Diffraction is the bending and spreading of waves around obstacles or through apertures whose dimensions are comparable to the wavelength of the wave. When a wavefront encounters an edge or a slit of width $a$, each point on the wavefront acts as a secondary source of spherical wavelets (Huygens's principle). The superposition of these wavelets produces a characteristic intensity pattern in which energy is redistributed into regions that would be geometrically shadowed. For light of wavelength $\lambda$ passing through a single slit of width $a$, destructive interference minima occur at angles $\theta$ satisfying

$$ a \sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \ldots $$

The central maximum spans the angular range between $m = -1$ and $m = +1$, making it twice as wide as any secondary maximum.

Worked Example. Monochromatic light of wavelength $\lambda = 589\,\text{nm}$ (sodium D-line) passes through a single slit of width $a = 0.250\,\text{mm}$. Find the angular position of the first diffraction minimum and the width of the central bright fringe on a screen $L = 1.20\,\text{m}$ away.

For $m = 1$:

$$ \sin\theta_1 = \frac{m\lambda}{a} = \frac{(1)(589 \times 10^{-9}\,\text{m})}{0.250 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}} = 2.356 \times 10^{-3} $$ $$ \theta_1 = \arcsin(2.356 \times 10^{-3}) \approx 0.1350° $$

The half-width of the central maximum on the screen is $y_1 = L\tan\theta_1 \approx L\sin\theta_1 = (1.20)(2.356 \times 10^{-3}) = 2.83\,\text{mm}$. The full central fringe width is therefore $2y_1 = 5.65\,\text{mm}$.

Key Theorem. The single-slit intensity distribution is given exactly by

$$ I(\theta) = I_0 \left(\frac{\sin\beta}{\beta}\right)^2, \quad \beta = \frac{\pi a \sin\theta}{\lambda} $$

where $I_0$ is the central maximum intensity. This $\text{sinc}^2$ envelope predicts that secondary maxima occur approximately at $\beta = (m + \tfrac{1}{2})\pi$ and that their peak intensities fall off as $1/m^2$.

Lab Exercise. Illuminate a variable single slit with a laser pointer ($\lambda \approx 650\,\text{nm}$). Project the diffraction pattern onto a wall $2.00\,\text{m}$ distant. Measure the distance between the first minima on either side of the central maximum for three slit widths in the range $0.10$–$0.50\,\text{mm}$. 🍃 Path Length Difference

🍃 Path Length Difference

Definition. When two coherent waves travel from their respective sources to a common observation point, the path length difference $\Delta L$ is the absolute difference between the distances each wave travels:

$$ \Delta L = |r_2 - r_1| $$

where $r_1$ and $r_2$ are the distances from source 1 and source 2 to the observation point, respectively. This scalar quantity governs whether the waves arrive in phase (constructive interference) or out of phase (destructive interference). Specifically, if $\lambda$ denotes the common wavelength, constructive interference occurs when

$$ \Delta L = m\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

and destructive interference occurs when

$$ \Delta L = \left(m + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

Worked Example. Two in-phase speakers separated by $d = 4.00\ \text{m}$ emit sound at $\lambda = 0.800\ \text{m}$. A listener stands $r_1 = 3.00\ \text{m}$ from speaker 1 and $r_2 = 5.40\ \text{m}$ from speaker 2. Compute $\Delta L$ and determine the interference condition.

$$ \Delta L = |5.40 - 3.00| = 2.40\ \text{m} $$

Dividing by the wavelength:

$$ \frac{\Delta L}{\lambda} = \frac{2.40}{0.800} = 3.00 $$

Since $\Delta L = 3\lambda$ (an integer multiple), the waves arrive perfectly in phase and the listener hears a maximum of sound intensity.

Key Theorem. For a double-slit geometry with slit separation $d$ and screen distance $L \operatorname{gg} d$, the path length difference to the $m$-th bright fringe at lateral position $y_m$ satisfies the small-angle approximation:

$$ \Delta L = d\sin\theta \approx \frac{d\, y_m}{L} $$

Setting $\Delta L = m\lambda$ yields the standard bright-fringe positions $y_m = m\lambda L / d$. This relation is foundational to Young's double-slit experiment and underlies all far-field (Fraunhofer) interference analysis.

🍃 Single Slit Diffraction

🍃 Single Slit Diffraction

Definition. When a plane wave passes through a narrow slit of width $a$, it undergoes diffraction — the wavefront fans out and produces a characteristic intensity pattern on a distant screen. Unlike the idealized point-source treatment of geometric optics, single-slit diffraction arises from the interference of infinitely many Huygens wavelets emanating from every point across the slit aperture. The condition for destructive interference (dark fringes) is

$$ a \sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \ldots $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength of light, $\theta$ is the angle from the central axis, and $m$ is a nonzero integer. The central maximum, centered at $\theta = 0$, is twice as wide as each secondary maximum.

Worked Example. A laser of wavelength $\lambda = 632.8\,\text{nm}$ illuminates a slit of width $a = 0.200\,\text{mm}$. A screen is placed $L = 1.50\,\text{m}$ away. Find the positions of the first and second dark fringes.

For the first minimum ($m = 1$):

$$ \sin\theta_1 = \frac{m\lambda}{a} = \frac{(1)(632.8 \times 10^{-9})}{0.200 \times 10^{-3}} = 3.164 \times 10^{-3} $$

Since $\theta_1$ is small, $\sin\theta_1 \approx \tan\theta_1$, so the lateral position is

$$ y_1 = L\tan\theta_1 \approx L\sin\theta_1 = (1.50)(3.164 \times 10^{-3}) = 4.75\,\text{mm} $$

For $m = 2$: $y_2 = 2y_1 = 9.49\,\text{mm}$. The full central maximum therefore spans $2y_1 = 9.49\,\text{mm}$.

Key Theorem. The normalized intensity distribution for single-slit diffraction is given by

$$ I(\theta) = I_0 \left(\frac{\sin\beta}{\beta}\right)^2, \quad \beta = \frac{\pi a \sin\theta}{\lambda} $$

This $\text{sinc}^2$ function predicts the exact location and relative intensity of all maxima and minima. Secondary maxima occur near $\beta = (m + \tfrac{1}{2})\pi$ and diminish rapidly in intensity — the first secondary maximum carries only about $4.7\%$ of the central peak's intensity.

Using a diode laser ($\lambda = 650\,\text{nm}$), a variable slit, and a screen at $L = 2.00\,\text{m}$: (1) measure $y_1$ for slit widths $a = 0.10,\, 0.20,\, 0.40\,\text{mm}$; (2) plot $y_1$ versus $1/a$ and extract $\lambda$ from the slope; (3) compare your result to the known value and compute the percent error. Verify that narrowing the slit widens the diffraction pattern, confirming the inverse relationship $y_1 \propto 1/a$.

🍃 Rayleigh Criterion

🍃 Rayleigh Criterion

Definition. The Rayleigh criterion establishes the minimum angular separation at which two point sources of light can be distinguished as distinct objects through a circular aperture. When light passes through a circular opening of diameter $D$, diffraction spreads each point source into an Airy disk pattern. Two sources are said to be just resolved when the central maximum of one pattern coincides with the first minimum of the other. This condition yields the minimum resolvable angle:

$$ \theta_{\min} = 1.22 \frac{\lambda}{D} $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength of light and $D$ is the aperture diameter. The factor $1.22$ arises from the first zero of the Bessel function $J_1$, which governs diffraction through a circular aperture rather than the familiar $\sin\theta = \lambda/a$ relation for a single slit.

Worked Example. A refracting telescope has an objective lens of diameter $D = 12\,\text{cm}$. Determine the minimum angular separation it can resolve for green light of wavelength $\lambda = 550\,\text{nm}$.

Applying the Rayleigh criterion:

$$ \theta_{\min} = 1.22 \frac{550 \times 10^{-9}\,\text{m}}{0.12\,\text{m}} = 1.22 \times 4.583 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{rad} \approx 5.6 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{rad} $$

Converting to arcseconds: $(5.6 \times 10^{-6})\times\frac{180°}{\pi}\times 3600 \approx 1.15''$. Two stars separated by less than approximately $1.15$ arcseconds would appear as a single blurred object through this instrument.

Key Theorem. The resolving power of a circular aperture scales linearly with diameter and inversely with wavelength. Consequently, doubling $D$ halves $\theta_{\min}$, while observing at shorter wavelengths improves resolution by the same ratio. This principle underlies the design of large-aperture telescopes and explains why ultraviolet or X-ray imaging systems achieve finer spatial resolution than visible-light instruments of equal aperture.

🍃 Resolving Power

🍃 Resolving Power

Definition. The resolving power of an optical instrument is its ability to distinguish two closely spaced point sources as separate objects rather than a single blurred image. For a diffraction grating, resolving power is defined quantitatively as

$$ R = \frac{\lambda}{\Delta\lambda} $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength being observed and $\Delta\lambda$ is the minimum wavelength difference that can be distinguished at that wavelength. A higher value of $R$ indicates finer spectral discrimination. For a diffraction grating with $N$ slits operating in order $m$, the resolving power is given by

$$ R = mN $$

This result follows from the Rayleigh criterion applied to grating diffraction: two spectral lines are just resolved when the principal maximum of one coincides with the first minimum of the other in the same diffraction order.

Worked Example. A diffraction grating contains 600 lines/mm over a ruled width of 50 mm. Determine whether it can resolve the sodium doublet, which consists of two lines at $\lambda_1 = 589.0\ \text{nm}$ and $\lambda_2 = 589.6\ \text{nm}$, when used in first order.

Solution. The total number of slits is $N = 600 \times 50 = 30{,}000$. In first order ($m = 1$):

$$ R = mN = (1)(30{,}000) = 30{,}000 $$

The required resolving power to separate the doublet is:

$$ R_{\text{required}} = \frac{\lambda}{\Delta\lambda} = \frac{589.0}{0.6} \approx 982 $$

Since $30{,}000 \operatorname{gg} 982$, the grating easily resolves the sodium doublet in first order.

Key Theorem. The resolving power $R = mN$ increases linearly with both the diffraction order and the total number of illuminated slits. Operating in higher orders multiplies resolving power at the cost of reduced intensity and potential order overlap, which must be managed with a cross-disperser or order-sorting filter.

🌸 Interference Microscopy

🌸 Interference Microscopy

Definition. Interference microscopy is an optical technique that exploits the wave nature of light to map nanoscale variations in optical path length across a specimen. When two coherent beams—a reference beam and a beam that has traversed the sample—are recombined, the resulting interference pattern encodes phase differences $\Delta\phi$ according to

$$ \Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\,\Delta(nd), $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength of illumination, $n$ is the refractive index of the medium, and $d$ is the physical thickness traversed. Because the eye is sensitive to intensity rather than phase, the interference pattern converts invisible phase shifts into measurable contrast. This capability is invaluable for imaging transparent biological specimens—living cells, for instance—that produce negligible amplitude contrast under conventional bright-field illumination.

Worked Example. A differential interference contrast (DIC) microscope operating at $\lambda = 550\text{ nm}$ examines a lipid bilayer of thickness $d = 7\text{ nm}$ and refractive index $n_s = 1.46$ immersed in an aqueous medium of refractive index $n_0 = 1.33$. The optical path difference introduced by the membrane is

$$ \text{OPD} = (n_s - n_0)\,d = (1.46 - 1.33)(7\times10^{-9}\text{ m}) = 9.1\times10^{-10}\text{ m} \approx 0.91\text{ nm}. $$

The corresponding phase shift is

$$ \Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{550\times10^{-9}}\times9.1\times10^{-10} \approx 0.010\text{ rad}. $$

Although this shift is well below the $\pi$ threshold for a bright fringe, phase-shifting variants of interference microscopy can detect changes as small as $0.001\text{ rad}$, making the bilayer readily observable.

Key Theorem (Rayleigh–Abbe Phase Sensitivity). For a two-beam interferometer with intensity $I = I_0\left[1 + \cos(\Delta\phi)\right]$, the minimum detectable phase shift is limited by photon shot noise:

$$ \delta\phi_{\min} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}, $$

where $N$ is the number of detected photons per pixel. High-coherence illumination maximizes fringe visibility $\mathcal{V} = (I_{\max}-I_{\min})/(I_{\max}+I_{\min})$, and peak sensitivity is achieved when the interferometer is biased to the quadrature point $\Delta\phi = \pi/2$.

Payoff 🎯

Interference microscopy stands as the natural culmination of wave optics because it transforms the phase of light — ordinarily invisible to any detector — into measurable intensity contrast. A conventional microscope records only the amplitude of a transmitted or reflected wavefront; biological cells, thin films, and polished surfaces that alter phase while leaving amplitude nearly unchanged appear as featureless, low-contrast images. Interference microscopy overcomes this limitation by splitting a coherent beam into a sample arm and a reference arm, then recombining them so that the optical path difference $\Delta = n\,d$ accumulated by the sample converts directly into a fringe shift $\delta x = \Delta\lambda / (2\pi)$, readable as a brightness variation across the field of view. The underlying principle is the same two-beam superposition studied throughout this chapter: wherever the path difference satisfies $\Delta = m\lambda$ the beams interfere constructively, and wherever $\Delta = (m+\tfrac{1}{2})\lambda$ they interfere destructively, mapping nanometer-scale thickness or refractive-index variations onto a visible fringe pattern.

Worked example. A Mach–Zehnder interference microscope illuminates a living cell ($n_{\text{cell}} \approx 1.38$) immersed in culture medium ($n_{\text{medium}} = 1.335$) with $\lambda = 550\,\text{nm}$ light. A region of the cell $d = 5\,\mu\text{m}$ thick introduces an extra optical path $\Delta = (n_{\text{cell}} - n_{\text{medium}})\,d = (0.045)(5000\,\text{nm}) = 225\,\text{nm}$. The resulting fringe shift is $\delta x / x_{\text{fringe}} = \Delta/\lambda = 225/550 \approx 0.41$ fringes — easily resolved with a calibrated eyepiece or CCD, and achievable without any staining or fixation that would alter living dynamics.

Key theorem. For a two-beam interferometer with reference intensity $I_r$ and sample intensity $I_s$, the recorded irradiance is

$$ I = I_r + I_s + 2\sqrt{I_r I_s}\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi\Delta}{\lambda} + \phi_0\right), $$

where $\phi_0$ is the static phase offset set by the reference arm. Phase-shifting interferometry — stepping $\phi_0$ through $0,\,\pi/2,\,\pi,\,3\pi/2$ and solving the resulting system — recovers $\Delta$ unambiguously at every pixel, yielding a quantitative phase image with sub-nanometer axial sensitivity.
