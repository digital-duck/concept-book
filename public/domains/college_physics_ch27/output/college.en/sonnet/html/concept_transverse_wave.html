🌱 Transverse Wave

🌱 Transverse Wave

Definition. A transverse wave is a mechanical or electromagnetic disturbance in which the displacement of the medium (or field oscillation) is perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. If the wave travels along the $+x$-axis, each particle of the medium oscillates along the $y$- or $z$-axis. The wave is characterised by its amplitude $A$, wavelength $\lambda$, frequency $f$, and wave speed $v$, related by

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

The one new quantity needed to write a compact displacement equation is the wave number $k = 2\pi/\lambda$, which counts radians of phase per metre of space. In terms of $k$ the instantaneous displacement is

$$ y(x,t) = A\sin\!\left(kx - 2\pi f\, t\right), $$

where the phase $kx - 2\pi f\,t$ advances by $2\pi$ over one full wavelength in space or one full period in time. The intensity of the wave is proportional to the square of the amplitude: $I \propto A^2$.

Worked Example. A transverse wave on a stretched string has amplitude $A = 0.030\ \text{m}$, frequency $f = 120\ \text{Hz}$, and wave speed $v = 24\ \text{m/s}$. Find the wavelength and write the displacement equation.

Solution. The wavelength is

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{24\ \text{m/s}}{120\ \text{Hz}} = 0.20\ \text{m}. $$

The wave number is therefore

$$ k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} = \frac{2\pi}{0.20} \approx 31.4\ \text{rad/m}, $$

giving

$$ y(x,t) = 0.030\sin(31.4\,x - 754\,t)\ \text{m}. $$

Key Theorem — Transverse Particle Speed. The speed at which a particle of the medium moves is distinct from the wave speed $v$. Differentiating $y(x,t)$ with respect to time,

$$ v_y(x,t) = \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = -A(2\pi f)\cos(kx - 2\pi f\,t). $$

The maximum particle speed is $A(2\pi f)$, which grows with both amplitude and frequency, whereas the wave speed $v = f\lambda$ depends only on the mechanical properties of the medium.

Lab Exercise. Attach a mechanical wave driver to a long string fixed at one end. Vary the driving frequency while keeping string tension constant. For at least five frequencies, measure the resulting wavelength with a meter stick and compute $v = f\lambda$ for each trial. Confirm that $v$ remains constant across trials, demonstrating that wave speed is a medium property independent of source frequency. Plot $f$ versus $1/\lambda$ and extract $v$ from the slope.
