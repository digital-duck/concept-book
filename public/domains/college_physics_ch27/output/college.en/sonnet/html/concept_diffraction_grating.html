🍃 Diffraction Grating

🍃 Diffraction Grating

A diffraction grating is an optical element consisting of a large number $N$ of equally spaced parallel slits, each separated by a distance $d$ called the grating spacing. When monochromatic light of wavelength $\lambda$ passes through the grating, constructive interference among waves from all slits occurs only when the path-length difference between adjacent slits equals an integer multiple of the wavelength. This condition yields the grating equation:

$$ d \sin\theta_m = m\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

where $\theta_m$ is the diffraction angle for order $m$. Because $N$ is typically several thousand slits per millimeter, gratings produce extremely sharp, well-resolved principal maxima separated by broad dark regions, making them far superior to double slits for spectroscopic work.

Worked Example. A diffraction grating has $6000$ lines/cm. Find the first- and second-order diffraction angles for sodium light at $\lambda = 589\,\text{nm}$.

The grating spacing is $d = 1/6000\,\text{cm} = 1.667\times10^{-4}\,\text{cm} = 1667\,\text{nm}$. Applying the grating equation:

$$ \sin\theta_1 = \frac{m\lambda}{d} = \frac{(1)(589)}{1667} = 0.3533 \implies \theta_1 \approx 20.7° $$ $$ \sin\theta_2 = \frac{(2)(589)}{1667} = 0.7066 \implies \theta_2 \approx 44.9° $$

A third-order maximum would require $\sin\theta_3 = 1.060 > 1$, which is physically impossible, so only two orders exist on each side of center.

Key Theorem — Resolving Power. The ability of a grating to distinguish two closely spaced wavelengths $\lambda$ and $\lambda + \Delta\lambda$ is characterized by the resolving power $R$:

$$ R = \frac{\lambda}{\Delta\lambda} = mN $$

where $m$ is the diffraction order and $N$ is the total number of illuminated slits. A grating with $N = 5000$ lines used in second order achieves $R = 10{,}000$, sufficient to resolve the sodium doublet ($\Delta\lambda \approx 0.6\,\text{nm}$).

Lab Exercise. Illuminate a 500 lines/mm transmission grating with a mercury vapor lamp. Measure the angles $\theta$ of at least three visible spectral lines in first order. Using $d\sin\theta = \lambda$, calculate each wavelength and compare your results to the accepted values of the mercury spectrum. Estimate the resolving power of your grating and determine the minimum wavelength separation it can resolve at $\lambda = 546\,\text{nm}$.
