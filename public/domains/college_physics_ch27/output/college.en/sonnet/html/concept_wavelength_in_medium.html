🍃 Wavelength In Medium

🍃 Wavelength In Medium

Definition. When a monochromatic electromagnetic wave propagates through a transparent medium of index of refraction $n$, its frequency $f$ remains unchanged (determined by the source), but its speed decreases from $c$ to $v = c/n$. Since the wave relation $v = f\lambda$ must hold in any medium, the wavelength shortens correspondingly:

$$ \lambda_n = \frac{\lambda}{n} $$

where $\lambda$ is the free-space (vacuum) wavelength and $\lambda_n$ is the wavelength inside the medium. Equivalently, $\lambda_n = v/f = c/(nf)$. This reduction is physically significant: the spatial phase accumulated over a given path length $L$ is $\phi = 2\pi L / \lambda_n = 2\pi n L / \lambda$, so a medium of higher refractive index packs more wave cycles into the same geometric distance.

Worked Example. Sodium yellow light has a vacuum wavelength $\lambda = 589\,\text{nm}$. Find its wavelength inside crown glass, where $n = 1.52$.

$$ \lambda_n = \frac{589\,\text{nm}}{1.52} = 387\,\text{nm} $$

The frequency is unchanged: $f = c/\lambda = (3.00 \times 10^8\,\text{m/s})/(589 \times 10^{-9}\,\text{m}) = 5.09 \times 10^{14}\,\text{Hz}$, and indeed $f = v/\lambda_n = (c/n)/\lambda_n = c/\lambda$, confirming consistency. Note that 387 nm falls in the near-ultraviolet, illustrating that the color experienced by an observer is governed by frequency, not by the wavelength inside the medium.

Key Theorem (Phase Accumulation). Because $\lambda_n = \lambda/n$, a wave traveling a geometric distance $L$ through a medium of index $n$ accumulates phase

$$ \phi = \frac{2\pi L}{\lambda_n} = \frac{2\pi n L}{\lambda} $$

This result follows directly from the definition above with no additional primitives. It shows that phase depends on the product $nL$, not on $L$ alone—the foundation of optical path length accounting in all wave-optics calculations.

Lab Exercise. A laser beam ($\lambda = 632.8\,\text{nm}$ in vacuum) passes normally through a glass plate of thickness $t = 3.00\,\text{mm}$ and index $n = 1.50$. (a) Find the wavelength of light inside the glass. (b) Determine how many complete wavelengths fit within the plate, both in vacuum and inside the glass, and compute the ratio of these two counts.
