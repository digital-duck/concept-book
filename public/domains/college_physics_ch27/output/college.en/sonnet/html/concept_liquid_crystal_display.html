🌸 Liquid Crystal Display

🌸 Liquid Crystal Display

Definition. A liquid crystal display (LCD) is an optical device that modulates transmitted or reflected light by exploiting the electrically controllable birefringence of liquid crystal (LC) materials. Liquid crystals occupy a mesophase between isotropic liquids and crystalline solids: their molecules possess long-range orientational order (characterized by the director field $\hat{n}$) but lack positional order. In the twisted nematic (TN) configuration—the basis of most LCDs—rod-shaped molecules are aligned in a helical structure that rotates the plane of polarized light by 90° in the field-off state.

The operating principle relies on the electro-optic effect. When no voltage is applied, incident linearly polarized light follows the molecular twist and exits rotated by 90°, passing through an orthogonally oriented analyzer (bright state). When a voltage $V$ exceeding the threshold

$$ V_{\text{th}} = \pi \sqrt{\frac{K_{11}}{\varepsilon_0 \Delta\varepsilon}} $$

is applied across the cell of thickness $d$, the electric field reorients the directors parallel to $\hat{E}$, unwinding the helix. Light then exits with its polarization unchanged and is blocked by the analyzer (dark state). Here $K_{11}$ is the splay elastic constant and $\Delta\varepsilon = \varepsilon_{\parallel} - \varepsilon_{\perp}$ is the dielectric anisotropy.

Worked Example. An LC cell has $K_{11} = 6.4 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{N}$, $\Delta\varepsilon = 10.0$, and $d = 5.0\ \mu\text{m}$. Find $V_{\text{th}}$.

$$ V_{\text{th}} = \pi\sqrt{\frac{6.4\times10^{-12}}{(8.85\times10^{-12})(10.0)}} = \pi\sqrt{0.0723} = \pi(0.2689) \approx 0.845\ \text{V} $$

Any applied voltage above $\approx 0.85\ \text{V}$ switches the pixel to dark; below it, the pixel remains bright.

Key Theorem (Mauguin Condition). For the polarization to adiabatically follow the molecular twist, the cell must satisfy $\Gamma \operatorname{gg} 1$, where $\Gamma = \pi \Delta n\, d / \lambda$ is the retardation parameter and $\Delta n$ is the birefringence. Typical cells use $\Delta n \approx 0.1$ and $d \approx 5\ \mu\text{m}$, giving $\Gamma \approx 1.6$ at $\lambda = 550\ \text{nm}$, which provides adequate light rotation.

Lab Exercise. Obtain two polarizing filters and an LCD screen. With the screen off and the filters crossed, observe how the brightness of individual pixels changes as you rotate one polarizer. Measure the angle at which maximum extinction occurs and compare it to the 90° twist predicted by TN theory. Record whether the off-state is bright or dark and deduce the polarizer orientation convention used in your display.
