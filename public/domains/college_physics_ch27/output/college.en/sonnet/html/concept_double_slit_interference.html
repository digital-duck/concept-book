🍃 Double Slit Interference

🍃 Double Slit Interference

Definition. When coherent monochromatic light of wavelength $\lambda$ passes through two narrow slits separated by a distance $d$, the overlapping spherical wavefronts produce a stable interference pattern on a distant screen. At any point $P$ on the screen, constructive interference occurs when the path length difference $\Delta\ell$ between the two waves is an integer multiple of the wavelength:

$$ \Delta\ell = d\sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

where $\theta$ is the angle from the central axis to point $P$. Destructive interference occurs when the path difference equals a half-integer multiple:

$$ d\sin\theta = \left(m + \frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

These conditions follow directly from the superposition principle: two waves with equal amplitudes and a phase difference of $\phi = 2\pi\Delta\ell/\lambda$ interfere constructively when $\phi = 2\pi m$ and destructively when $\phi = (2m+1)\pi$.

Worked Example. A double-slit apparatus uses slits separated by $d = 0.250\ \text{mm}$ and a screen placed $L = 1.00\ \text{m}$ away. Sodium light of wavelength $\lambda = 589\ \text{nm}$ illuminates the slits. Find the position of the second bright fringe ($m = 2$) on the screen.

Using the small-angle approximation $\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta = y/L$, the constructive-interference condition becomes:

$$ y_m = \frac{m\lambda L}{d} $$

Substituting values:

$$ y_2 = \frac{2 \times (589 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{m}) \times 1.00\ \text{m}}{0.250 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}} = 4.71 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m} \approx 4.71\ \text{mm} $$

The fringe spacing $\Delta y = \lambda L/d$ is uniform for small angles, so fringes appear as equally spaced bright bands.

Key Theorem. The intensity at angle $\theta$ in a double-slit pattern is:

$$ I(\theta) = I_0 \cos^2\!\left(\frac{\pi d \sin\theta}{\lambda}\right) $$

where $I_0$ is the maximum intensity at the central maximum. This result, derived by summing two phasors of equal amplitude, confirms that intensity varies smoothly between $I_0$ (bright fringes) and zero (dark fringes), with no energy lost — it is redistributed from dark regions to bright ones.

Lab Exercise. Set up a double-slit slide ($d \approx 0.2\ \text{mm}$) with a red laser ($\lambda \approx 650\ \text{nm}$) at $L = 2.0\ \text{m}$. Measure the fringe spacing $\Delta y$ with a ruler and calculate $\lambda_{\text{exp}} = d\Delta y/L$. Compare with the labeled laser wavelength and estimate the dominant source of experimental uncertainty.
