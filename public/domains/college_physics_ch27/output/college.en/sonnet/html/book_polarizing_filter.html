🌱 Electromagnetic Wave

🌱 Electromagnetic Wave

Definition. An electromagnetic wave is a self-propagating disturbance in the electromagnetic field, consisting of mutually perpendicular oscillating electric field $\vec{E}$ and magnetic field $\vec{B}$ vectors, both transverse to the direction of propagation. Starting from Maxwell's equations—which govern $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ as previously established—one can derive that these fields satisfy coupled wave equations in vacuum:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial x^2} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial t^2}, \qquad \frac{\partial^2 B}{\partial x^2} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 B}{\partial t^2} $$

Comparing with the standard wave equation $\partial^2 f/\partial x^2 = (1/v^2)\,\partial^2 f/\partial t^2$ identifies the propagation speed as:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}} \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s} $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ T·m/A and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}$ C²/(N·m²). At every point and instant the field amplitudes satisfy $E = cB$.

Worked Example. A plane electromagnetic wave propagates in the $+x$ direction with electric field amplitude $E_0 = 150$ V/m and wavelength $\lambda = 500$ nm. Find the magnetic field amplitude $B_0$ and the wave frequency $f$.

Solution. From $E = cB$:

$$ B_0 = \frac{E_0}{c} = \frac{150 \text{ V/m}}{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}} = 5.00 \times 10^{-7} \text{ T} $$

From the universal wave relation $c = f\lambda$:

$$ f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{500 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}} = 6.00 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz} $$

This frequency places the wave in the visible green portion of the electromagnetic spectrum.

Key Theorem — Energy Transport. Because $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ store energy, an electromagnetic wave transports energy through space. The power per unit area is quantified by the Poynting vector $\vec{S} = (\vec{E} \times \vec{B})/\mu_0$, whose time average over one cycle gives the intensity:

$$ I = \frac{E_0^2}{2\mu_0 c} = \frac{c\varepsilon_0 E_0^2}{2} $$

This result connects the observable wave amplitude directly to radiated power, and underpins antenna design, optical power budgets, and radiometric calibration.

(c) Explain why standing waves in the cavity can produce uneven heating and describe one engineering solution used in consumer ovens to mitigate this effect.

🌱 Transverse Wave

🌱 Transverse Wave

Definition. A transverse wave is a mechanical or electromagnetic disturbance in which the displacement of the medium (or field oscillation) is perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. If the wave travels along the $+x$-axis, each particle of the medium oscillates along the $y$- or $z$-axis. The wave is characterised by its amplitude $A$, wavelength $\lambda$, frequency $f$, and wave speed $v$, related by

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

The one new quantity needed to write a compact displacement equation is the wave number $k = 2\pi/\lambda$, which counts radians of phase per metre of space. In terms of $k$ the instantaneous displacement is

$$ y(x,t) = A\sin\!\left(kx - 2\pi f\, t\right), $$

where the phase $kx - 2\pi f\,t$ advances by $2\pi$ over one full wavelength in space or one full period in time. The intensity of the wave is proportional to the square of the amplitude: $I \propto A^2$.

Worked Example. A transverse wave on a stretched string has amplitude $A = 0.030\ \text{m}$, frequency $f = 120\ \text{Hz}$, and wave speed $v = 24\ \text{m/s}$. Find the wavelength and write the displacement equation.

Solution. The wavelength is

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{24\ \text{m/s}}{120\ \text{Hz}} = 0.20\ \text{m}. $$

The wave number is therefore

$$ k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} = \frac{2\pi}{0.20} \approx 31.4\ \text{rad/m}, $$

giving

$$ y(x,t) = 0.030\sin(31.4\,x - 754\,t)\ \text{m}. $$

Key Theorem — Transverse Particle Speed. The speed at which a particle of the medium moves is distinct from the wave speed $v$. Differentiating $y(x,t)$ with respect to time,

$$ v_y(x,t) = \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = -A(2\pi f)\cos(kx - 2\pi f\,t). $$

The maximum particle speed is $A(2\pi f)$, which grows with both amplitude and frequency, whereas the wave speed $v = f\lambda$ depends only on the mechanical properties of the medium.

Lab Exercise. Attach a mechanical wave driver to a long string fixed at one end. Vary the driving frequency while keeping string tension constant. Confirm that $v$ remains constant across trials, demonstrating that wave speed is a medium property independent of source frequency. Plot $f$ versus $1/\lambda$ and extract $v$ from the slope.

🍃 Polarization

🍃 Polarization

Definition. Polarization describes the orientation of the electric field vector in a transverse electromagnetic wave. An unpolarized wave contains electric field oscillations in all directions perpendicular to the propagation direction with equal probability. A linearly polarized wave has its electric field confined to a single plane containing the propagation direction. If $\hat{k}$ denotes the direction of propagation, a linearly polarized wave can be written as

$$ \vec{E}(z,t) = E_0 \cos(kz - \omega t)\,\hat{x}, $$

where $\hat{x}$ is the polarization axis. A polarizer (e.g., a sheet of Polaroid film) transmits only the component of $\vec{E}$ along its transmission axis, governed by Malus's Law:

$$ I = I_0 \cos^2\theta, $$

where $I_0$ is the incident intensity of polarized light and $\theta$ is the angle between the incident polarization direction and the transmission axis.

Worked Example. A beam of unpolarized light with intensity $I_0 = 80\,\text{W/m}^2$ passes through two polarizers. The first polarizer (the polarizer) has its axis set arbitrarily; the second (the analyzer) has its axis at $\theta = 30°$ relative to the first. Find the transmitted intensity.

Step 1. After the first polarizer, unpolarized light is reduced to half its original intensity: $I_1 = I_0/2 = 40\,\text{W/m}^2$.

Step 2. Apply Malus's Law at the analyzer:

$$ I_2 = I_1\cos^2(30°) = 40 \times \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)^2 = 40 \times \frac{3}{4} = 30\,\text{W/m}^2. $$

Key Theorem — Brewster's Angle. When unpolarized light strikes a dielectric interface at the Brewster angle $\theta_B$, the reflected beam is completely polarized perpendicular to the plane of incidence. The condition is

$$ \tan\theta_B = \frac{n_2}{n_1}, $$

where $n_1$ and $n_2$ are the refractive indices of the incident and transmitted media. At this angle, the reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular to each other ($\theta_B + \theta_r = 90°$), so the oscillating dipoles in the medium cannot radiate in the reflection direction for the parallel component.

Lab Exercise. Arrange three polarizers in series. Set the first and third with perpendicular axes ($\theta = 90°$) and verify that no light passes. Insert the second polarizer between them at $\theta = 45°$ to the first. Measure the transmitted intensity and compare with the theoretical prediction $I = I_0/8$. Discuss how the intermediate polarizer allows partial transmission despite the orthogonal outer polarizers.

🌸 Polarizing Filter

🌸 Polarizing Filter

Definition. A polarizing filter (or polarizer) is an optical element that transmits light whose electric-field oscillations are aligned with a preferred axis — the transmission axis — while absorbing or reflecting the orthogonal component. Natural light is unpolarized: its electric field vector $\mathbf{E}$ oscillates in all transverse directions with equal probability. A linear polarizer selects a single plane of oscillation, producing linearly polarized light. The transmitted intensity is governed by Malus's Law: if linearly polarized light of intensity $I_0$ strikes a polarizer whose transmission axis makes an angle $\theta$ with the incident polarization direction, the transmitted intensity is

$$ I = I_0 \cos^2\theta. $$

When unpolarized light of intensity $I_{\text{un}}$ passes through a first polarizer, the output intensity is $I_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}I_{\text{un}}$, since on average half the incident power lies along any given axis.

Worked Example. Unpolarized light of intensity $I_0 = 8.0\ \text{W/m}^2$ passes through two polarizers in sequence. The first polarizer is oriented vertically; the second is oriented at $\theta = 30°$ to the vertical. Find the final transmitted intensity.

Step 1. After the first polarizer: $I_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}(8.0) = 4.0\ \text{W/m}^2$.

Step 2. Apply Malus's Law at the second polarizer:

$$ I_2 = I_1\cos^2 30° = 4.0 \times \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)^2 = 4.0 \times 0.75 = 3.0\ \text{W/m}^2. $$

The final intensity is $3.0\ \text{W/m}^2$, a 62.5% reduction from the original beam.

Key Theorem. Malus's Law follows directly from the projection of the electric-field amplitude: $E_{\text{trans}} = E_0\cos\theta$. Since intensity is proportional to $E^2$, we obtain $I = I_0\cos^2\theta$. When $\theta = 90°$ (crossed polarizers), $I = 0$; inserting a third polarizer at an intermediate angle restores a nonzero transmitted intensity — a counterintuitive consequence of sequential projection.

Lab Exercise. Arrange three polarizing filters so that the first and third have orthogonal transmission axes. Vary the angle $\phi$ of the middle filter from $0°$ to $90°$ in $15°$ increments and record the transmitted intensity. Plot $I$ versus $\phi$ and verify that the result follows $I = \tfrac{1}{8}I_0\sin^2 2\phi$, derived by applying Malus's Law twice.

Payoff 🎯

A polarizing filter is an optical element that transmits only the component of electromagnetic radiation whose electric field oscillates along a single privileged axis, called the transmission axis. When unpolarized light of intensity $I_0$ passes through an ideal linear polarizer, the transmitted intensity is $I_0/2$, because each polarization component carries equal energy on average. When polarized light of intensity $I$ strikes a second polarizer whose transmission axis makes an angle $\theta$ with the incoming polarization direction, Malus's Law governs the output:

$$ I = I_0 \cos^2\theta. $$

This deceptively simple relationship is the natural endpoint of Chapter 27 because it unifies the wave nature of light, the vector character of the electric field, and the interaction of structured matter with electromagnetic radiation. Every concept in the chapter — wave superposition, interference, diffraction — prepares the student to understand that light carries directional information, and the polarizing filter is the instrument that reads and controls it.

Worked example. Unpolarized light of intensity $8.0\ \text{W m}^{-2}$ passes through a polarizer, then strikes an analyzer at $\theta = 60°$. After the first filter, $I_1 = 4.0\ \text{W m}^{-2}$. Applying Malus's Law: $I_2 = 4.0\cos^2(60°) = 4.0 \times 0.25 = 1.0\ \text{W m}^{-2}$. The analyzer reduces transmitted irradiance by 75%.

Key theorem. For $N$ ideal polarizers in series, each rotated by $\Delta\theta = \pi/(2N)$ relative to its predecessor, the transmitted intensity approaches $I_0/2$ as $N \to \infty$, even though crossed polarizers ($\theta = 90°$) transmit nothing. This limiting result follows from $\cos^{2N}(\pi/2N) \to 1$.

Connection to Liquid Crystal Displays. An LCD exploits precisely this configuration. Each pixel is sandwiched between two crossed polarizers. A liquid-crystal layer, whose molecules rotate the polarization direction by up to $90°$ under an applied voltage, acts as a voltage-tunable wave plate. When the voltage is off, the crystal rotates polarization by $90°$ and light passes through the analyzer; when the voltage is on, the crystal is reoriented and the analyzer blocks the light. Gray levels are encoded by intermediate angles via Malus's Law, turning every pixel into a continuously adjustable light valve.
