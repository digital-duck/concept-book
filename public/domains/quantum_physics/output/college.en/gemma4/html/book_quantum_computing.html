🌱 Observable

🌱 Observable

An observable is a function that maps elements from an underlying probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ to a measurable space $(E, \mathcal{E})$. Intuitively, if $\Omega$ represents all possible outcomes of a random experiment (e.g., the path of a particle), then the observable $X: \Omega \to E$ provides a quantifiable measurement of that outcome—for instance, its position or momentum at a specific time. Formally, an observable must be measurable with respect to $\mathcal{F}$, meaning that for any measurable set $A \in \mathcal{E}$ in the codomain $E$, the preimage $X^{-1}(A) = \{\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in A\}$ must belong to the sigma-algebra $\mathcal{F}$. The measurability condition is crucial because it ensures that the probability of the observable falling into any defined set $A$ can be computed using the underlying measure $P$: $P(X \in A) = P(X^{-1}(A))$.

Consider a simple worked example involving continuous time processes. Suppose $\Omega = [0, T]$ represents the time interval for a Wiener process (Brownian motion), and let the observable $X_T$ be the position of the particle at time $T$. We define $X_T: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ by $X_T(\omega) = \omega$. The measurable space is $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$, where $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ are the Borel sets. Given that the path measure on $[0, T]$ induces a standard probability distribution on $\mathbb{R}$, $X_T$ is clearly an observable because for any Borel set $A$, $\{ \omega : X_T(\omega) \in A \} = A \cap [0, T]$, which is measurable under the natural sigma-algebra on $[0, T]$.

The key theorem connecting observables and probability theory is the expectation identity. If $X: (\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P) \to (E, \mathcal{E})$ is a real-valued observable such that $\mathbb{E}[|X|] < \infty$, then its expected value can be computed as the integral of the indicator function over its preimage: $\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{\Omega} X(\omega) dP(\omega)$. This definition allows us to calculate statistical moments (like variance) directly from the underlying probability measure $P$.

🍃 Schrodinger Equation

🍃 Schrodinger Equation

The Schrödinger equation is a fundamental partial differential equation of quantum mechanics that describes how the quantum state of a physical system changes over time. It operates on the wave function, $\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)$, which contains all possible information about the particle. In its full form, it relates the temporal evolution of this wave function to the Hamiltonian operator ($\hat{H}$), which represents the total energy of the system:

$$ i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \hat{H} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) $$

Here, $i$ is the imaginary unit, $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant ($h/2\pi$), and $\hat{H}$ includes kinetic energy ($\hat{T}$) and potential energy ($\hat{V}$): $\hat{H} = \hat{T} + \hat{V} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\mathbf{r}, t)$. For time-independent systems, where the potential $V$ does not explicitly depend on time, we separate the wave function into a product of space and time components ($\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \psi(\mathbf{r})e^{-iEt/\hbar}$), leading to the stationary form known as the time-independent Schrödinger equation (TISE):

$$ \hat{H} \psi(\mathbf{r}) = E\psi(\mathbf{r}) $$

This is an eigenvalue equation, where $\psi$ are the eigenfunctions and $E$ are the corresponding allowed energy eigenvalues.

Consider a particle of mass $m$ confined in one dimension by a potential well defined by $V(x)$. The TISE becomes:

$$ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi(x) = E\psi(x) $$

A concrete worked example is the infinite square well of width $L$, where $V(x)=0$ for $0 \le x \le L$ and $V(x)=\infty$ otherwise. Applying the boundary conditions ($\psi(0)=\psi(L)=0$), solving this equation yields quantized energy eigenvalues:

$$ E_n = \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2mL^2}, \quad n=1, 2, 3, \dots $$

The resulting wave functions are $\psi_n(x) = A\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)$.

A key conceptual theorem related to this equation is the Born Interpretation. This theorem states that the probability density of finding a particle at position $\mathbf{r}$ at time $t$ is given by the square magnitude of the wave function, $|\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)|^2$. Since the Schrödinger equation governs the evolution of $\Psi$, it implicitly dictates how this probability distribution changes over time.

🍃 Superposition

🍃 Superposition

Superposition is a fundamental principle that governs the behavior of linear systems—whether those systems are electrical circuits, differential equations, or physical oscillators. Informally, it states that if multiple sources or inputs act upon a system, the resulting total output is simply the algebraic sum of the outputs that would have been produced by each input acting individually. Mathematically, this principle applies exclusively to linearity; any system characterized by linear behavior adheres to superposition. For a general system $S$ subjected to a set of independent input signals $\{x_1(t), x_2(t), \dots, x_n(t)\}$, the output response $y(t)$ is given by:

$$ y(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i(t) $$

where $y_i(t)$ is the response of the system to the input $x_i(t)$. This principle requires the governing differential equations (or transfer function representation) to be linear, meaning they satisfy both additivity and homogeneity.

Consider a simple series DC circuit containing two independent voltage sources, $V_1$ and $V_2$, connected across an unknown load resistor, $R$. To find the total current through $R$, we can apply superposition. We first calculate the current due only to $V_1$ ($I_{V1}$) by setting $V_2=0$, and then calculate the current due only to $V_2$ ($I_{V2}$) by setting $V_1=0$. The total current is simply the sum: $I_{\text{total}} = I_{V1} + I_{V2}$. For example, if $R=6\ \Omega$, $V_1=3\ \text{V}$, and $V_2=9\ \text{V}$, then $I_{V1} = 3/6 = 0.5\ \text{A}$ and $I_{V2} = 9/6 = 1.5\ \text{A}$. Therefore, $I_{\text{total}} = 0.5\ \text{A} + 1.5\ \text{A} = 2.0\ \text{A}$.

The validity of superposition is formally guaranteed by the property that a linear operator $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies:

$$ \mathcal{L}[a x_1(t) + b x_2(t)] = a \cdot \mathcal{L}[x_1(t)] + b \cdot \mathcal{L}[x_2(t)] $$

where $a$ and $b$ are arbitrary constants. This linear combination property is critical for solving complex system responses by decomposing them into simpler, manageable components.

Key Theorem: Linearity of System Response. A physical or mathematical system can be analyzed using the superposition principle if and only if its governing differential equations (or transfer function in the Laplace domain) are linear with respect to the input variables. If non-linear terms (e.g., $V^2$, $\sin(I)$) are present, superposition generally does not apply.

🍃 Energy Quantization

Energy quantization is a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics asserting that certain physical properties, such as energy or angular momentum, are not continuous variables but can only take on discrete values, referred to as quanta. Classically, a system could possess any amount of energy within a given range; however, the principles governing microscopic interactions dictate that energy levels are strictly allowed and forbidden. This discreteness arises primarily when particles are confined to specific potential wells or interact via fundamental quantum fields. The transition from continuous classical mechanics to discrete quantum states marked the revolutionary shift in 20th-century physics.

A concrete worked example illustrating quantization involves the energy of electromagnetic radiation, such as light. Classically, light intensity could vary smoothly; however, Planck and Einstein demonstrated that light energy is carried in discrete packets called photons. The relationship defining this quantization is given by:

$$ E = h\nu $$

where $E$ is the energy of a single photon (measured in Joules), $\nu$ is the frequency of the radiation (measured in $\text{Hz}$ or $\text{s}^{-1}$), and $h$ is Planck's constant ($h \approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J}\cdot\text{s}$). This equation shows that if the frequency ($\nu$) is quantized, the energy ($E$) must also be quantized, preventing continuous energy transfer at the subatomic level.

🍃 Measurement

🍃 Measurement

Measurement is the fundamental process of assigning a quantitative value—a number accompanied by an associated unit—to a physical property or quantity. Conceptually, it involves comparing an unknown magnitude ($\mathcal{A}$) against a standardized reference magnitude ($\mathcal{R}$), resulting in the ratio $\frac{\mathcal{A}}{\mathcal{R}}$. This ensures dimensional consistency; the derived numerical value is meaningful only when paired with its correct dimensions (e.g., meters, kilograms). All quantitative measurements inherently contain uncertainty ($\Delta x$), which quantifies the potential deviation between the measured value and the true value due to instrumental limitations or experimental conditions. Therefore, a complete measurement must always be expressed as $x \pm \Delta x$.

Worked Example: Consider the determination of the density $\rho$ of an unknown solid object using mass ($m$) and volume ($V$). The relationship is $\rho = m/V$. Suppose we measure the mass to be $m = 50.0 \pm 0.1 \text{ g}$ and the volume using displacement to be $V = (10.0 \pm 0.2) \text{ cm}^3$. To find the density, we perform the calculation $\rho = \frac{50.0}{10.0}$. However, due to the uncertainty in both inputs, the error in the final result must be calculated using propagation rules for division:

$$ \left(\frac{\Delta \rho}{\rho}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{\Delta m}{m}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\Delta V}{V}\right)^2 $$

Solving this yields a rigorous estimate of the uncertainty in $\rho$, ensuring that our final reported density value is physically accurate.

🍃 Entanglement

🍃 Entanglement

Entanglement is a non-classical correlation between two or more quantum systems such that the collective state of the system cannot be written as a tensor product of the states of its individual subsystems. If two particles, A and B, are entangled, measuring an observable on particle A instantaneously restricts the possible outcomes for measurements on particle B, regardless of the spatial separation between them. Mathematically, a pure bipartite state $|\psi\rangle$ of System $A \otimes B$ is separable if it can be written as $|\psi\rangle = |\psi_A\rangle \otimes |\psi_B\rangle$. Conversely, an entangled state cannot be factored in this manner. The degree of entanglement quantifies how far the joint state deviates from a product state.

Consider two qubits (spin-1/2 particles) prepared in the Bell state $|\Phi^+\rangle$, which is a maximally entangled state:

$$ |\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle_A |0\rangle_B + |1\rangle_A |1\rangle_B) $$

If we measure the spin ($\sigma_z$) on particle A and find it to be $|0\rangle$, the state immediately collapses. Since the particles are entangled, the remaining state of particle B must be $|0\rangle$ as well, even if no physical interaction occurs between the measurement apparatuses. This correlation is stronger than any classical correlation (e.g., hidden variable theories), leading to a violation of Bell inequalities.

A key theorem characterizing entanglement is the Peres-Horodecki criterion (often simplified for small systems). For a state $\rho$ to be entangled, its partial transposition ($\rho^{T_A}$) must have at least one negative eigenvalue. This provides a necessary condition for separability; if the partial transpose results in negative eigenvalues, the state is definitively non-separable and thus entangled.

🍃 Perturbation Theory

🍃 Perturbation Theory

Perturbation theory is a mathematical technique used to find approximate solutions to problems involving differential equations, wave mechanics, or physical systems that cannot be solved exactly. It operates on the principle of decomposing a complex system (or equation) into two parts: a solvable, unperturbed component and a small correction, or "perturbation." We assume that the overall behavior is dominated by the simple, solvable part, and the deviation from this solution—the perturbation—is small enough to be treated using power series expansions. Mathematically, if we have an equation of the form $\mathcal{L}x = f$, and we can rewrite it as $(\mathcal{L}_0 + \epsilon \mathcal{V})x = f$, where $\mathcal{L}_0$ is the unperturbed operator, $\mathcal{V}$ is the perturbation potential, and $\epsilon$ is a small dimensionless parameter (often called the expansion parameter), the solution $x$ can be expanded as a power series in $\epsilon$:

$$ x(\epsilon) = x_0 + \epsilon x_1 + \epsilon^2 x_2 + O(\epsilon^3) $$

Here, $x_0$ is the solution to the unperturbed system ($\epsilon=0$), and subsequent terms ($x_1, x_2, \dots$) represent increasing orders of correction due to the perturbation. Solving for these coefficients iteratively allows us to approximate the full solution with controllable error.

Consider a simple quantum mechanical example: the anharmonic oscillator. The Hamiltonian for an ideal harmonic oscillator is $H_0 = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}k x^2$. When we introduce an anharmonic term, say $\lambda x^3$, the full Hamiltonian becomes $H = H_0 + \epsilon V(x)$, where $V(x) = x^3$ and $\epsilon$ is small. Using time-independent perturbation theory, the energy shift for the first excited state ($n=1$) can be approximated by calculating the expectation value of the perturbation operator in the unperturbed basis:

$$ E^{(1)} \approx E_0 + \langle \psi_0 | V(x) | \psi_0 \rangle $$

This method provides a systematic way to calculate how the energy levels shift due to the presence of the weak, perturbing force $V(x)$.

The foundational principle governing this approach is often summarized by Rayleigh-Schrödinger perturbation theory. The core idea is that if $\epsilon$ is sufficiently small, the solution can be accurately represented by a truncated Taylor expansion in $\epsilon$. This expansion converts the difficult problem into an infinite sequence of solvable problems: finding $x_0$, then solving for $x_1$ using $x_0$, then solving for $x_2$ using $x_0$ and $x_1$, and so on. The accuracy increases with each calculated order, provided the series converges rapidly.

🍃 Spin

Spin ($\mathbf{S}$) represents an intrinsic form of angular momentum carried by elementary particles, such as electrons and quarks. Unlike orbital angular momentum ($\mathbf{L}$), which arises from a particle's spatial motion around an axis, spin is an inherent quantum number independent of the particle's trajectory or external fields. For a particle with spin quantum number $s$, the components are restricted to discrete eigenvalues $m_s = -s, -s+1, \dots, +s$. The corresponding magnitude of the total angular momentum is fixed at $\langle \mathbf{S}}^2\rangle = \hbar^2 s(s+1)$.

🍃 Many Body States

🍃 Many Body States

A many-body state describes the collective quantum mechanical behavior of a system composed of multiple interacting particles. Unlike single-particle states, where the wavefunction $\psi(\mathbf{r}_1)$ depends only on one coordinate, a many-body state is represented by a multi-particle wavefunction $\Psi( \mathbf{r}_1, \dots, \mathbf{r}_N)$, which depends explicitly on the coordinates of all $N$ constituent particles. The complex structure of this wavefunction dictates the system's energy spectrum and observable properties. For identical particles (such as electrons), the total wavefunction must obey specific symmetry requirements—namely, it must be symmetric under exchange for bosons or antisymmetric for fermions—a constraint formalized by the Pauli Exclusion Principle.

Worked Example: Simple Harmonic Oscillator Model. Consider a system of $N$ non-interacting particles, each modeled as a one-dimensional simple harmonic oscillator (SHO). The total Hamiltonian is separable: $\hat{H} = \sum_{i=1}^N \hat{h}_i$, where $\hat{h}_i = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i^2} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 x_i^2$. Since the Hamiltonian is a sum of independent operators, the total wavefunction $\Psi$ is simply a product of single-particle eigenstates:

$$ \Psi(x_1, \dots, x_N) = \prod_{i=1}^N \psi_n(x_i) $$

The resulting energy eigenvalue $E$ is the sum of the individual particle energies: $E = \sum_{i=1}^N \hbar\omega (n_i + 1/2)$. This simple product form illustrates how non-interacting systems lead to easily separable many-body states.

Key Theorem: Slater Determinants and Fermions. For interacting fermionic systems, the general wavefunction is prohibitively complex. However, for single-particle interactions, we can use the method of Slater determinants as a basis set. If $\{\phi_1, \dots, \phi_N\}$ are $N$ orthonormal single-particle orbitals (eigenstates of the one-body Hamiltonian), the corresponding many-body wavefunction $\Psi_{\text{SD}}$ is defined by the determinant:

$$ \Psi_{\text{SD}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \det[\phi_i(\mathbf{r}_j)] $$

This construction automatically ensures that the resulting state is completely antisymmetric under particle exchange, thereby satisfying the Pauli Exclusion Principle and providing a highly effective variational ansatz for many quantum chemistry calculations.

🌸 Quantum Computing

🌸 Quantum Computing

Quantum computing represents a revolutionary paradigm shift from classical computation by leveraging fundamental principles of quantum mechanics—namely superposition and entanglement—to process information. Unlike classical bits, which must exist in a definite state of $|0\rangle$ or $|1\rangle$, the foundational unit of quantum information, the qubit, can exist in a linear combination of both states simultaneously. Mathematically, a single qubit state $|\psi\rangle$ is represented as:

$$ |\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle $$

where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are complex probability amplitudes satisfying the normalization condition $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$. The power of quantum computation scales exponentially with the number of qubits, allowing parallel exploration of vast state spaces impossible for classical machines.

Consider a concrete worked example illustrating superposition using the Hadamard gate ($H$). If we initialize a qubit in the ground state $|0\rangle$, applying $H$ transforms it into an equal superposition:

$$ |0\rangle \xrightarrow{H} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) $$

This demonstrates that the resulting state is not simply 'both 0 and both 1', but rather a coherent combination of possibilities. Entanglement, where two or more qubits share a joint quantum state such that measuring one instantaneously determines the state of another regardless of separation, enables complex operations like those found in multi-qubit gates (e.g., CNOT).

A crucial theoretical underpinning is Shor's algorithm. This polynomial-time algorithm provides an exponential speedup over the best known classical methods for factoring large integers. Its existence demonstrates that certain problems believed to be intractable classically—such as integer factorization—can be solved efficiently using quantum resources, posing a significant threat to current public-key cryptography systems like RSA.

Payoff 🎯

Quantum computing ($\text{quantum\_computing}$) represents the culmination of computational theory and quantum mechanics, moving beyond classical limitations by harnessing superposition and entanglement. Fundamentally, it achieves an exponential speedup for specific classes of problems—those that scale poorly with the number of variables in a classical Turing machine model. This emergence is not merely incremental; it is a paradigm shift because the underlying physics dictates that certain computational processes (like factoring large numbers or simulating molecular interactions) cannot be efficiently mapped onto classical bits ($\{0, 1\}$). Instead, they require the complex Hilbert space spanned by qubits $(\{|0\rangle, |1\rangle\})$ to process information natively.

The advantage is most clearly demonstrated when considering optimization problems. Classically, finding the optimal solution for a large set of constraints requires exploring an exponentially growing search space $N$. In contrast, quantum algorithms like Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) allow the exploration of this landscape via amplitude amplification and adiabatic evolution. For instance, in computational chemistry, determining the ground state energy of a molecule ($\mathcal{H}$) is classically approximated using methods like Density Functional Theory (DFT). $\text{quantum\_computing}$ provides a direct simulation pathway through algorithms such as Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE), which estimates eigenvalues by mapping the Hamiltonian operator to a measurable frequency. This capability directly links to materials science, allowing the precise design of novel catalysts or superconductive alloys that would be intractable to model using classical supercomputers due to time and memory constraints.

This computational power connects universally: in finance, it optimizes complex portfolio risk models; in drug discovery, it simulates protein folding with unprecedented accuracy; and in cryptography, Shor's algorithm threatens current public-key infrastructure by efficiently factoring large prime numbers $N$, necessitating the development of Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC).

Key Theorem (The Computational Gap): The complexity class $\text{BQP}$ (Bounded-error Quantum Polynomial time) is believed to be strictly larger than $\text{BPP}$ (Bounded-error Probabilistic Polynomial time), meaning that quantum computers can solve problems fundamentally faster than any classical computer, provided sufficient physical resources are available.
