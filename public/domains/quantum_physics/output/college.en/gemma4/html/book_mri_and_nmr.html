🌱 Quantum State

The quantum state $\operatorname{ket}{\psi}$ is the fundamental mathematical object used within quantum mechanics to completely characterize a physical system. Mathematically, it is realized as a normalized vector belonging to a complex Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$. This abstract vector encapsulates all possible information—both deterministic potential and probabilistic likelihoods—about the system being observed. The requirement that the state be normalized, expressed by $\operatorname{braket}{\psi|\psi} = 1$, mandates that summing the probabilities of finding the system in any complete basis set must yield unity. When a physical observable quantity (such as position or momentum) is measured, the act of measurement causes the state vector to undergo instantaneous collapse from its initial superposition into an eigenstate corresponding precisely to the measured eigenvalue.

🌱 Observable

An observable is a function that maps elements from an underlying probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ to a measurable space $(E, \mathcal{E})$. Intuitively, if $\Omega$ represents all possible outcomes of a random experiment (e.g., the path of a particle), then the observable $X: \Omega \to E$ provides a quantifiable measurement of that outcome—for instance, its position or momentum at a specific time. Formally, an observable must be measurable with respect to $\mathcal{F}$, meaning that for any measurable set $A \in \mathcal{E}$ in the codomain $E$, the preimage $X^{-1}(A) = \{\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in A\}$ must belong to the sigma-algebra $\mathcal{F}$. The measurability condition is crucial because it ensures that the probability of the observable falling into any defined set $A$ can be computed using the underlying measure $P$: $P(X \in A) = P(X^{-1}(A))$.

Consider a simple worked example involving continuous time processes. Suppose $\Omega = [0, T]$ represents the time interval for a Wiener process (Brownian motion), and let the observable $X_T$ be the position of the particle at time $T$. We define $X_T: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ by $X_T(\omega) = \omega$. The measurable space is $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$, where $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ are the Borel sets. Given that the path measure on $[0, T]$ induces a standard probability distribution on $\mathbb{R}$, $X_T$ is clearly an observable because for any Borel set $A$, $\{ \omega : X_T(\omega) \in A \} = A \cap [0, T]$, which is measurable under the natural sigma-algebra on $[0, T]$.

The key theorem connecting observables and probability theory is the expectation identity. If $X: (\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P) \to (E, \mathcal{E})$ is a real-valued observable such that $\mathbb{E}[|X|] < \infty$, then its expected value can be computed as the integral of the indicator function over its preimage: $\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{\Omega} X(\omega) dP(\omega)$. This definition allows us to calculate statistical moments (like variance) directly from the underlying probability measure $P$.

🌱 Planck Constant

For convenience in advanced derivations, one often uses the reduced Planck constant, $\hbar$, defined as $\hbar = h/(2\pi)$. The existence of this constant necessitated abandoning classical assumptions of continuous energy exchange and establishing that physical quantities like angular momentum ($\vec{L}$) are quantized.

🍃 Wave Function

The wave function, denoted by $\psi(\mathbf{r}, t)$, is perhaps the most central concept in non-relativistic quantum mechanics. It serves as the fundamental mathematical description of a quantum system (such as a particle or an atom) and contains all possible information about that system. Crucially, $\psi$ itself is generally not directly measurable; rather, its physical significance lies in the Born interpretation. The probability density of finding the particle at position $\mathbf{r}$ and time $t$, denoted by $\rho(\mathbf{r}, t)$, is given by the square of the magnitude of the wave function: $\rho(\mathbf{r}, t) = |\psi(\mathbf{r}, t)|^2$. Thus, the probability of finding the particle within an infinitesimal volume $d\tau$ is $\text{Prob} \, d\tau = |\psi|^2 d\tau$. The evolution of $\psi$ in time and space is governed by the time-dependent Schrödinger Equation:

🍃 Schrodinger Equation

🍃 Schrodinger Equation

The Schrödinger equation is a fundamental partial differential equation of quantum mechanics that describes how the quantum state of a physical system changes over time. It operates on the wave function, $\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)$, which contains all possible information about the particle. In its full form, it relates the temporal evolution of this wave function to the Hamiltonian operator ($\hat{H}$), which represents the total energy of the system:

$$ i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \hat{H} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) $$

Here, $i$ is the imaginary unit, $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant ($h/2\pi$), and $\hat{H}$ includes kinetic energy ($\hat{T}$) and potential energy ($\hat{V}$): $\hat{H} = \hat{T} + \hat{V} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\mathbf{r}, t)$. For time-independent systems, where the potential $V$ does not explicitly depend on time, we separate the wave function into a product of space and time components ($\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \psi(\mathbf{r})e^{-iEt/\hbar}$), leading to the stationary form known as the time-independent Schrödinger equation (TISE):

$$ \hat{H} \psi(\mathbf{r}) = E\psi(\mathbf{r}) $$

This is an eigenvalue equation, where $\psi$ are the eigenfunctions and $E$ are the corresponding allowed energy eigenvalues.

Consider a particle of mass $m$ confined in one dimension by a potential well defined by $V(x)$. The TISE becomes:

$$ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi(x) = E\psi(x) $$

A concrete worked example is the infinite square well of width $L$, where $V(x)=0$ for $0 \le x \le L$ and $V(x)=\infty$ otherwise. Applying the boundary conditions ($\psi(0)=\psi(L)=0$), solving this equation yields quantized energy eigenvalues:

$$ E_n = \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2mL^2}, \quad n=1, 2, 3, \dots $$

The resulting wave functions are $\psi_n(x) = A\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)$.

A key conceptual theorem related to this equation is the Born Interpretation. This theorem states that the probability density of finding a particle at position $\mathbf{r}$ at time $t$ is given by the square magnitude of the wave function, $|\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)|^2$. Since the Schrödinger equation governs the evolution of $\Psi$, it implicitly dictates how this probability distribution changes over time.

🍃 Energy Quantization

Energy quantization is a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics asserting that certain physical properties, such as energy or angular momentum, are not continuous variables but can only take on discrete values, referred to as quanta. Classically, a system could possess any amount of energy within a given range; however, the principles governing microscopic interactions dictate that energy levels are strictly allowed and forbidden. This discreteness arises primarily when particles are confined to specific potential wells or interact via fundamental quantum fields. The transition from continuous classical mechanics to discrete quantum states marked the revolutionary shift in 20th-century physics.

A concrete worked example illustrating quantization involves the energy of electromagnetic radiation, such as light. Classically, light intensity could vary smoothly; however, Planck and Einstein demonstrated that light energy is carried in discrete packets called photons. The relationship defining this quantization is given by:

$$ E = h\nu $$

where $E$ is the energy of a single photon (measured in Joules), $\nu$ is the frequency of the radiation (measured in $\text{Hz}$ or $\text{s}^{-1}$), and $h$ is Planck's constant ($h \approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J}\cdot\text{s}$). 🍃 Spin

Spin ($\mathbf{S}$) represents an intrinsic form of angular momentum carried by elementary particles, such as electrons and quarks. Unlike orbital angular momentum ($\mathbf{L}$), which arises from a particle's spatial motion around an axis, spin is an inherent quantum number independent of the particle's trajectory or external fields. Mathematically, it is described using vector operators $\mathbf{S} = (S_x, S_y, S_z)$. The magnitude of the total angular momentum is quantized by $s$, where $s$ can take values $\{0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, \dots\}$. For a particle with spin quantum number $s$, the components are restricted to discrete eigenvalues $m_s = -s, -s+1, \dots, +s$. The corresponding magnitude of the total angular momentum is fixed at $\langle \mathbf{S}}^2\rangle = \hbar^2 s(s+1)$.

🌸 Mri And Nmr

Magnetic Resonance (MR) techniques, including Nuclear Magnetic Resonance ($\text{NMR}$) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging ($\text{MRI}$), exploit the quantum mechanical property of nuclear spin ($\mathbf{I}$). Nuclei possessing non-zero spin, such as ${}^1\text{H}$ or ${}^{13}\text{C}$, behave like tiny magnets (dipoles). When placed in a strong static magnetic field $\mathbf{B}_0$, these dipoles align either parallel (lower energy) or anti-parallel (higher energy) to the field. This alignment creates a net bulk magnetization vector, $\mathbf{M}$. By applying time-varying radiofrequency ($\text{RF}$) pulses at the Larmor frequency ($\omega_L$), we can excite this system. The transfer of energy causes the spins to precess and tip the net magnetization away from the $z$-axis. This induced precession generates a measurable voltage in receiver coils, allowing us to probe the quantum state of the nuclei.

Consider an $\text{NMR}$ spectroscopy experiment using a sample containing protons (${}^1\text{H}$). The resonance condition dictates that energy ($\Delta E$) is absorbed when the applied RF frequency ($\omega$) matches the Larmor frequency:

$$ \hbar \omega = \gamma B_{net} $$

where $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant, $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio (a unique property of the nucleus), and $B_{net}$ is the total magnetic field strength. The resulting signal intensity is measured as the $\text{Free Induction Decay}$ ($\text{FID}$) after the exciting pulse is turned off, which decays due to spin-spin ($T_2$) and spin-lattice ($T_1$) relaxation processes.

A concrete worked example involves determining the chemical shift of a methyl group ($\text{-CH}_3$). The local electronic environment shields the nucleus from $B_0$, reducing the effective field strength experienced by the proton, $B_{local}$. This difference is quantified by $\sigma$:

$$ \omega_{\text{obs}} = \omega_0 (1 - \sigma) $$

By measuring the observed frequency ($\omega_{\text{obs}}$) and knowing the spectrometer's operating frequency ($\omega_0$), one can calculate the shielding constant $\sigma$, which provides detailed information about the electron density around the nucleus.

The cornerstone principle governing these measurements is the assumption of $T_1$ and $T_2$ relaxation mechanisms, which dictate how quickly the system returns to thermal equilibrium. The key theorem utilized in analyzing $\text{NMR}$ data is the relationship between the observed echo decay rate and the spin-spin relaxation time:

$$ \frac{1}{T_{2}^{*}} = \frac{1}{T_2} + \frac{1}{T_{2}^{*}} $$

where $1/T_2^*$ encompasses both true molecular interactions ($1/T_2$) and measurement artifacts due to magnetic field inhomogeneity. Understanding this distinction is critical for accurate structural elucidation.

Payoff 🎯

The culmination of concepts in magnetic resonance physics—specifically Nuclear Magnetic Resonance ($\text{NMR}$) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging ($\text{MRI}$)—represents a profound leap in analytical methodology, transforming physical principles into non-invasive molecular mapping tools. Fundamentally, $\text{mri\_and\_nmr}$ allows us to interrogate the local electronic environment of atomic nuclei (most commonly ${}^1\text{H}$, ${}^{13}\text{C}$, and ${}^{15}\text{N}$) by exploiting their quantum mechanical spin behavior in a powerful external magnetic field $\mathbf{B}_0$. The system achieves its power by measuring the characteristic frequency ($\omega$) at which these spins precess, providing chemical-shift information that is unique to the surrounding molecular structure.

The core achievement of this concept is spatial and structural elucidation without destructive sampling. Unlike spectroscopic methods that require purified samples or techniques limited to macroscopic observation, $\text{mri\_and\_nmr}$ provides a continuum of data: from global macromolecular conformation (structural biology) to localized chemical bond identification (chemistry) and physiological state assessment (medicine).

This unified framework connects seamlessly across diverse applied domains. In Structural Biology, the measurement of dipolar couplings allows for the determination of precise distances between atoms in proteins, forming the backbone for understanding enzyme kinetics. For Medicine, $\text{MRI}$ translates these spin-level interactions into spatial maps of hydrogen density and relaxation times ($T_1$ and $T_2$), enabling soft tissue contrast that is unparalleled by other diagnostic methods.
