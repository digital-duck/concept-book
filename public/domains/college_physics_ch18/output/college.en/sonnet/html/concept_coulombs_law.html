🍃 Coulombs Law

🍃 Coulombs Law

Definition. Coulomb's Law describes the electrostatic force between two stationary point charges. If two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ are separated by a distance $r$ in a vacuum, the magnitude of the force each exerts on the other is

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9 \, \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant, related to the permittivity of free space by $k_e = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$, with $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{C}^2/(\text{N·m}^2)$. The force is directed along the line joining the two charges: like charges repel, opposite charges attract. In vector form,

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r}_{12} $$

where $\hat{r}_{12}$ points from charge 1 toward charge 2. The law holds precisely for point charges at rest and remains an excellent approximation for charged objects whose separation greatly exceeds their physical dimensions.

Worked Example. A proton ($q_1 = +1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C) and an electron ($q_2 = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C) are separated by the Bohr radius $r = 5.29 \times 10^{-11}$ m. Find the magnitude of the electrostatic force.

$$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})^2}{(5.29 \times 10^{-11})^2} $$ $$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{2.566 \times 10^{-38}}{2.798 \times 10^{-21}} \approx 8.23 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{N} $$

This attractive force ($\approx 82$ nN) is roughly $10^{39}$ times stronger than the gravitational attraction between the same two particles, illustrating the dominant role of electrostatics at atomic scales.

Key Theorem — Superposition Principle. The total electrostatic force on a charge $q$ due to a collection of charges $q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_n$ is the vector sum of the individual Coulomb forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} k_e \frac{q \, q_i}{r_i^2} \hat{r}_i $$

This linearity is foundational; it permits the analysis of complex charge distributions by decomposition into point-charge contributions.

Lab Exercise. Place three charges in a line: $q_1 = +3.0 \, \mu\text{C}$ at $x = 0$, $q_2 = -2.0 \, \mu\text{C}$ at $x = 4.0$ cm, and $q_3 = +1.5 \, \mu\text{C}$ at $x = 9.0$ cm. Calculate the net electrostatic force (magnitude and direction) on $q_2$ due to $q_1$ and $q_3$, applying the superposition principle. Verify that your sign conventions for attraction and repulsion are consistent with the vector form of Coulomb's Law.
