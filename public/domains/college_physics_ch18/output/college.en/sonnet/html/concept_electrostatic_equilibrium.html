🍃 Electrostatic Equilibrium

🍃 Electrostatic Equilibrium

Definition. A conductor is said to be in electrostatic equilibrium when the free charges within it have ceased to move, so that the macroscopic electric current density everywhere inside the material is zero. This condition imposes four fundamental consequences that follow directly from Gauss's law and the properties of conductors:

  1. The electric field E inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium is zero everywhere.
  2. Any net charge resides entirely on the outer surface of the conductor.
  3. The electric field at the surface is perpendicular to that surface (no tangential component).
  4. On an irregularly shaped conductor, surface charge density $\sigma$ is greatest where the radius of curvature is smallest.

These results are not empirical observations but logical necessities. If E were nonzero inside, free electrons would accelerate, contradicting the equilibrium assumption. By Gauss's law applied to a Gaussian surface just inside the conductor, $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0$ implies $Q_{\text{enc}} = 0$, so all charge migrates to the surface.

Worked Example. A solid copper sphere of radius $R = 0.10\ \text{m}$ carries a net charge $Q = +5.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Find the electric field at $r = 0.05\ \text{m}$ (inside) and at $r = 0.15\ \text{m}$ (outside).

Inside ($r < R$): Because the conductor is in electrostatic equilibrium, $\mathbf{E} = 0$.

Outside ($r > R$): By Gauss's law with a concentric spherical surface,

$$ E \cdot 4\pi r^2 = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0} \implies E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(5.0\times10^{-6})}{(0.15)^2} \approx 2.0\times10^6\ \text{N/C} $$

directed radially outward.

Key Theorem. The Faraday cage effect is a direct corollary: any conductor surrounding a hollow interior completely shields that interior from external electrostatic fields. If $\mathbf{E}_{\text{ext}}$ exists outside, induced surface charges redistribute until the superposition of the external field and the induced-charge field cancels exactly inside the cavity.

Lab Exercise. Using an electroscope and a hollow metallic shell, charge the exterior of the shell and lower an uncharged metal ball suspended by a nylon thread into the interior without touching the walls. Measure any deflection of the electroscope leaves when the ball contacts the inner surface; then repeat for the outer surface. Explain the asymmetry of charge transfer in each case using the equilibrium conditions stated above.
