🍃 Charging By Induction

🍃 Charging By Induction

Definition. Charging by induction is the process of redistributing charge within a conductor—or transferring net charge to a conductor—through the influence of an external electric field, without any direct contact with a charged object. The mechanism exploits the free mobility of conduction electrons in metals: an external charge polarizes the conductor, and if a ground connection is made and then broken while the inducing charge remains in place, a net charge of opposite sign is left on the object.

More precisely, consider a neutral metallic sphere. When a negatively charged rod is brought near (without touching), the free electrons in the sphere experience a repulsive Coulomb force and migrate to the far side, leaving a positive region on the near side. The sphere is now polarized but still electrically neutral overall. If the sphere is momentarily grounded—connected to Earth via a conducting path—electrons flow off the sphere into the ground, since the ground acts as an effectively infinite reservoir of charge. When the ground connection is severed first, and then the charged rod is removed, the sphere retains a net positive charge distributed uniformly over its surface.

Worked Example. A neutral aluminum sphere of radius $r = 0.05\ \text{m}$ is grounded while a rod carrying charge $Q = -3\ \mu\text{C}$ is held $d = 0.10\ \text{m}$ from the sphere's center. Estimate the induced surface charge density after the procedure is complete.

The induced charge on the sphere is approximately $+3\ \mu\text{C}$ (assuming complete induction for simplicity). The surface area of the sphere is:

$$ A = 4\pi r^2 = 4\pi (0.05)^2 \approx 3.14 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{m}^2 $$

The average surface charge density is:

$$ \sigma = \frac{Q}{A} = \frac{3 \times 10^{-6}}{3.14 \times 10^{-2}} \approx 9.55 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{C/m}^2 $$

Key Theorem. By Gauss's Law, the electric field inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium is zero: $\mathbf{E}_{\text{inside}} = 0$. Consequently, any excess charge resides entirely on the outer surface, and the surface charge density $\sigma$ at any point satisfies $E_{\perp} = \sigma/\varepsilon_0$ just outside the conductor.

Lab Exercise. Bring a charged PVC rod near one end of a pair of touching metal spheres. While the rod remains in place, separate the spheres. Remove the rod. Using an electroscope, verify that the sphere nearest the rod acquired a charge opposite in sign to the rod, and the far sphere acquired the same sign as the rod.
