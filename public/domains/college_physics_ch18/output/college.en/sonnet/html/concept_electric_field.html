🍃 Electric Field

Definition. The electric field $\mathbf{E}$ at a point in space is defined as the force per unit positive test charge placed at that point:

$$ \mathbf{E} = \frac{\mathbf{F}}{q_0} $$

where $\mathbf{F}$ is the electrostatic force experienced by a small positive test charge $q_0$. The SI unit of electric field is newtons per coulomb ($\text{N/C}$), equivalently volts per meter ($\text{V/m}$). The field is a vector quantity — its direction coincides with the direction of force on a positive test charge. For a point charge $Q$, the field at distance $r$ is given by Coulomb's law:

$$ \mathbf{E} = k_e \frac{Q}{r^2}\hat{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant and $\hat{r}$ is the unit vector pointing radially outward from $Q$.

Worked Example. A proton (charge $q = +1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$) is located at the origin. Find the magnitude and direction of the electric field at a point $P$ located $0.50\ \text{m}$ to the right of the proton.

$$ E = k_e \frac{q}{r^2} = \left(8.99 \times 10^9\right)\frac{1.60 \times 10^{-19}}{(0.50)^2} $$ $$ E = \frac{1.438 \times 10^{-9}}{0.25} = 5.75 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{N/C} $$

The field points in the $+x$ direction, away from the positive source charge.

Key Theorem — Superposition Principle. For a collection of point charges $Q_1, Q_2, \ldots, Q_n$, the total electric field at any point is the vector sum of the individual fields:

$$ \mathbf{E}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{E}_i = \sum_{i=1}^{n} k_e \frac{Q_i}{r_i^2}\hat{r}_i $$

This linearity is fundamental: it allows complex charge distributions to be analyzed by decomposition into simpler elements, and underpins the calculation of fields from continuous charge distributions via integration.

Lab Exercise. Using a charged Van de Graaff generator and a calibrated field mill, measure the electric field magnitude at distances of $10\ \text{cm}$, $20\ \text{cm}$, and $30\ \text{cm}$ from the sphere's center. Plot $E$ versus $r$ on a log-log scale. Determine the slope and verify that it equals $-2$, confirming the inverse-square dependence predicted by Coulomb's law. Estimate $k_e$ from the $y$-intercept and compare with the accepted value.
