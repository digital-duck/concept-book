🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: all observable free charges are integer multiples of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb, the SI unit of charge. There exist exactly two polarities: positive (carried by protons, $q = +e$) and negative (carried by electrons, $q = -e$). The net charge of an isolated system is conserved—charge can be transferred between objects but cannot be created or destroyed. Mathematically, conservation of charge is written as

$$ \frac{dQ_{\text{system}}}{dt} = 0 $$

for any closed system, where $Q_{\text{system}} = \sum_i q_i$ is the algebraic sum of all constituent charges.

Worked Example. A glass rod is rubbed with a silk cloth, transferring $3.0 \times 10^{10}$ electrons from the glass to the silk. Find the charge acquired by each object.

Each electron carries $q_e = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$. The silk gains

$$ Q_{\text{silk}} = (3.0 \times 10^{10})(−1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}) = -4.81 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{C} \approx -4.81\ \text{nC}. $$

By conservation of charge, the glass rod acquires an equal and opposite charge: $Q_{\text{glass}} = +4.81\ \text{nC}$. Note that total charge before and after rubbing remains zero, consistent with both objects beginning electrically neutral.

Key Theorem — Quantization of Charge. Every observable free charge satisfies $q = ne$ for some $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. This was established experimentally by Millikan's oil-drop experiment (1909), in which electrically charged droplets suspended against gravity were found to carry charges only in discrete steps of $e$, never fractional multiples. Although quarks carry charges of $\pm e/3$ and $\pm 2e/3$, they are permanently confined within hadrons and have never been detected as free particles, so the quantization rule holds universally for all directly observable charged objects.

Lab Exercise. Obtain two identical conducting spheres on insulating stands. Charge one sphere to $Q = +10\ \text{nC}$ using a Van de Graaff generator. Touch it briefly to the second sphere and separate them. Using conservation of charge and the symmetry of identical conductors, predict the charge on each sphere after contact. Verify your prediction with an electroscope and record whether the spheres subsequently attract or repel each other when brought close together without touching.
