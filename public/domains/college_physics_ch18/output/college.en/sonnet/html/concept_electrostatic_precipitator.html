🌸 Electrostatic Precipitator

🌸 Electrostatic Precipitator

Definition. An electrostatic precipitator (ESP) is a pollution-control device that removes particulate matter—dust, smoke, ash, and aerosols—from a gas stream by exploiting the force exerted on charged particles in an electric field. In its most common configuration, a high-voltage wire (the discharge electrode) runs along the central axis of a cylindrical or plate-shaped grounded collector. A potential difference of 25–100 kV is applied, generating a nonuniform electric field strong enough near the wire to ionize the surrounding gas, creating a corona discharge. Gas molecules in this corona region lose electrons, producing positive ions and free electrons. The free electrons attach to neutral particulates, rendering them negatively charged, whereupon the electric force $\vec{F} = q\vec{E}$ drives them toward the grounded collector plates, where they deposit and are periodically removed by mechanical rapping.

Worked Example. A cylindrical ESP has an inner wire radius $r_a = 1.0 \text{ mm}$ and outer collector radius $r_b = 10.0 \text{ cm}$. The applied voltage is $V_0 = 50 \text{ kV}$. Find the electric field at the wire surface.

For a coaxial geometry, the field at radius $r$ is

$$ E(r) = \frac{V_0}{r \ln(r_b/r_a)}. $$

Substituting $r = r_a = 1.0 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}$:

$$ E(r_a) = \frac{5.0 \times 10^4}{(1.0 \times 10^{-3})\ln(100)} = \frac{5.0 \times 10^4}{4.605 \times 10^{-3}} \approx 1.09 \times 10^7 \ \text{V/m}. $$

This field (≈ 10.9 MV/m) exceeds the breakdown threshold of air (~3 MV/m), initiating the corona near the wire while leaving the bulk gas stream intact.

Key Theorem. The collection efficiency $\eta$ of an ESP is described by the Deutsch–Anderson equation:

$$ \eta = 1 - e^{-w A / Q}, $$

where $w$ is the particle drift velocity toward the collector, $A$ is the total collector plate area, and $Q$ is the volumetric gas flow rate. Efficiency increases with larger collection area or slower gas flow, providing a quantitative design criterion.

Lab Exercise. Connect a Van de Graaff generator to a cylindrical metal can (collector). Introduce smoke into the can and apply a high-voltage central wire. Measure particle count upstream and downstream with a particle counter over 60-second intervals at three voltage settings (10 kV, 20 kV, 30 kV). Plot $-\ln(1-\eta)$ versus voltage and discuss whether the trend is consistent with the Deutsch–Anderson model, identifying which parameter $w$ depends on.
