🍃 Charging By Contact

🍃 Charging By Contact

Definition. Charging by contact (also called conduction charging) is the process by which an electrically neutral object acquires a net charge through direct physical contact with a charged object. When two conductors touch, free electrons redistribute across the combined system until the electric potential is equalized. If the charging object carries a net charge $Q$ and has capacitance $C_1$, while the neutral object has capacitance $C_2$, the charge redistributes such that the final charges satisfy

$$ Q_1' = Q \cdot \frac{C_1}{C_1 + C_2}, \qquad Q_2' = Q \cdot \frac{C_2}{C_1 + C_2} $$

with $Q_1' + Q_2' = Q$, confirming conservation of charge. For two identical conductors, each retains exactly half the original charge. Unlike induction, conduction transfers charge permanently and requires no subsequent grounding step to preserve the induced charge.

Worked Example. A metal sphere A carries a charge of $+6.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and is brought into contact with an identical uncharged metal sphere B. After contact and separation, sphere A retains $Q_A' = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and sphere B acquires $Q_B' = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Now sphere B is brought into contact with a third identical sphere C (also initially uncharged). The charge splits again:

$$ Q_B'' = Q_C' = \frac{3.0\ \mu\text{C}}{2} = 1.5\ \mu\text{C}. $$

Sphere A remains at $+3.0\ \mu\text{C}$; spheres B and C each carry $+1.5\ \mu\text{C}$. Total charge: $3.0 + 1.5 + 1.5 = 6.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Charge is conserved throughout.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Charge). In any isolated system, the total electric charge is invariant:

$$ \sum_i Q_i = \text{constant}. $$

Conduction charging is consistent with this principle — charge is never created or destroyed, only redistributed. The sign of the transferred charge matches that of the source: a negatively charged rod produces a negatively charged recipient.

Lab Exercise. Charge an aluminum rod negatively by rubbing it with fur. Touch it briefly to each of three identical small metal spheres arranged in a line, then measure the charge on each sphere using an electroscope or charge sensor. Record the charge after each contact event, verify that the total charge is conserved, and determine whether the redistribution follows the equal-sharing prediction for identical conductors. Discuss sources of charge leakage that may cause discrepancies.
