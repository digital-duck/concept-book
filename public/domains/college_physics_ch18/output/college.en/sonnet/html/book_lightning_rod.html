🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: all observable free charges are integer multiples of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb, the SI unit of charge. There exist exactly two polarities: positive (carried by protons, $q = +e$) and negative (carried by electrons, $q = -e$). The net charge of an isolated system is conserved—charge can be transferred between objects but cannot be created or destroyed. Mathematically, conservation of charge is written as

$$ \frac{dQ_{\text{system}}}{dt} = 0 $$

for any closed system, where $Q_{\text{system}} = \sum_i q_i$ is the algebraic sum of all constituent charges.

Worked Example. A glass rod is rubbed with a silk cloth, transferring $3.0 \times 10^{10}$ electrons from the glass to the silk. Find the charge acquired by each object.

Each electron carries $q_e = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$. The silk gains

$$ Q_{\text{silk}} = (3.0 \times 10^{10})(−1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}) = -4.81 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{C} \approx -4.81\ \text{nC}. $$

By conservation of charge, the glass rod acquires an equal and opposite charge: $Q_{\text{glass}} = +4.81\ \text{nC}$. Note that total charge before and after rubbing remains zero, consistent with both objects beginning electrically neutral.

Key Theorem — Quantization of Charge. Every observable free charge satisfies $q = ne$ for some $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. This was established experimentally by Millikan's oil-drop experiment (1909), in which electrically charged droplets suspended against gravity were found to carry charges only in discrete steps of $e$, never fractional multiples. Although quarks carry charges of $\pm e/3$ and $\pm 2e/3$, they are permanently confined within hadrons and have never been detected as free particles, so the quantization rule holds universally for all directly observable charged objects.

Touch it briefly to the second sphere and separate them. Using conservation of charge and the symmetry of identical conductors, predict the charge on each sphere after contact. Verify your prediction with an electroscope and record whether the spheres subsequently attract or repel each other when brought close together without touching.

🌱 Electron

🌱 Electron

Definition. The electron is a fundamental subatomic particle carrying a charge of $q_e = -e$, where $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$ is the elementary charge. Its rest mass is $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, making it approximately 1836 times lighter than the proton. Electrons are classified as leptons — point-like particles with no known internal structure — and they occupy quantized energy states in atomic orbitals. In the context of electrostatics and circuit theory, the electron serves as the primary charge carrier in conducting materials, where conduction electrons move quasi-freely through a metallic lattice under an applied electric field.

Worked Example. Consider a copper wire carrying a steady current $I = 2.0\ \text{A}$. How many electrons pass through a cross-sectional plane of the wire each second?

Current is defined as charge per unit time:

$$ I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} \implies \Delta Q = I\,\Delta t. $$

In one second, $\Delta Q = 2.0\ \text{C}$. The number of electrons is:

$$ N = \frac{\Delta Q}{e} = \frac{2.0\ \text{C}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}} \approx 1.25 \times 10^{19}\ \text{electrons}. $$

This enormous number underscores the collective nature of macroscopic electric current; no single electron traverses the entire wire in one second, but the coordinated drift of the electron ensemble produces a measurable, continuous current.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Electron Charge). The charge of an isolated electron is invariant: $q_e = -e$ regardless of the electron's kinetic energy, reference frame, or chemical environment. This invariance, confirmed to at least one part in $10^{21}$ by precision spectroscopy, is the empirical foundation for treating $e$ as a universal constant. Consequently, the total charge in any closed system is conserved because each electron contributes exactly $-e$, with no partial values possible.

Lab Exercise. Using a cathode-ray tube apparatus, apply a known electric field $\vec{E}$ perpendicular to the electron beam and a magnetic field $\vec{B}$ parallel to it. Adjust $B$ until the beam travels undeflected, satisfying $eE = evB$, giving $v = E/B$. Then remove $\vec{B}$ and measure the deflection $y$ of the beam to determine $e/m_e$ via:

$$ \frac{e}{m_e} = \frac{2yE}{B^2 L^2}, $$

where $L$ is the length of the deflecting field region. Compare your result to the accepted value $e/m_e = 1.759 \times 10^{11}\ \text{C\,kg}^{-1}$.

🍃 Conductor

🍃 Conductor

Definition. A conductor is a material in which electric charges—typically electrons—are free to move throughout the bulk of the material under the influence of an applied electric field. In metals, the outermost valence electrons are delocalized, forming a "sea" of conduction electrons that can respond collectively to external forces. Formally, a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium satisfies three fundamental conditions: (1) the electric field $\mathbf{E}$ inside the conductor is identically zero, $\mathbf{E}_{\text{int}} = \mathbf{0}$; (2) any net charge resides exclusively on the outer surface; and (3) the electric field at the surface is perpendicular to that surface, with magnitude $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0$, where $\sigma$ is the local surface charge density and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{C}^2/(\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)$.

Worked Example. A solid copper sphere of radius $R = 0.10\ \text{m}$ carries a net charge of $Q = +5.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Determine the electric field at $r = 0.05\ \text{m}$ (inside) and at $r = 0.15\ \text{m}$ (outside).

Inside ($r < R$): Because copper is a conductor in equilibrium, $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{0}$ everywhere within the material. No calculation is needed—the conducting electrons redistribute until they cancel any interior field.

Outside ($r > R$): By Gauss's law with a spherical surface of radius $r$,

$$ \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0} \implies E(4\pi r^2) = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}, $$ $$ E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(5.0\times10^{-6})}{(0.15)^2} \approx 2.0\times10^6\ \text{N/C}. $$

The field points radially outward.

Key Theorem (Electrostatic Shielding). The interior of any closed conducting shell is completely shielded from external electric fields. This follows directly from Gauss's law: a Gaussian surface drawn entirely within the conductor encloses zero net field, so external charge distributions produce no field inside the cavity. This principle underlies Faraday cages used in sensitive electronic equipment.

Then insert a charged rod into the cavity without touching the walls and record whether the leaves deflect. Reconcile your observations with the shielding theorem and the condition $\mathbf{E}_{\text{int}} = \mathbf{0}$.

🍃 Coulombs Law

🍃 Coulombs Law

Definition. Coulomb's Law describes the electrostatic force between two stationary point charges. If two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ are separated by a distance $r$ in a vacuum, the magnitude of the force each exerts on the other is

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9 \, \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant, related to the permittivity of free space by $k_e = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$, with $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{C}^2/(\text{N·m}^2)$. The force is directed along the line joining the two charges: like charges repel, opposite charges attract. In vector form,

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r}_{12} $$

where $\hat{r}_{12}$ points from charge 1 toward charge 2. The law holds precisely for point charges at rest and remains an excellent approximation for charged objects whose separation greatly exceeds their physical dimensions.

Worked Example. A proton ($q_1 = +1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C) and an electron ($q_2 = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C) are separated by the Bohr radius $r = 5.29 \times 10^{-11}$ m. Find the magnitude of the electrostatic force.

$$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})^2}{(5.29 \times 10^{-11})^2} $$ $$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{2.566 \times 10^{-38}}{2.798 \times 10^{-21}} \approx 8.23 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{N} $$

This attractive force ($\approx 82$ nN) is roughly $10^{39}$ times stronger than the gravitational attraction between the same two particles, illustrating the dominant role of electrostatics at atomic scales.

Key Theorem — Superposition Principle. The total electrostatic force on a charge $q$ due to a collection of charges $q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_n$ is the vector sum of the individual Coulomb forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} k_e \frac{q \, q_i}{r_i^2} \hat{r}_i $$

This linearity is foundational; it permits the analysis of complex charge distributions by decomposition into point-charge contributions.

Lab Exercise. Place three charges in a line: $q_1 = +3.0 \, \mu\text{C}$ at $x = 0$, $q_2 = -2.0 \, \mu\text{C}$ at $x = 4.0$ cm, and $q_3 = +1.5 \, \mu\text{C}$ at $x = 9.0$ cm. Calculate the net electrostatic force (magnitude and direction) on $q_2$ due to $q_1$ and $q_3$, applying the superposition principle. Verify that your sign conventions for attraction and repulsion are consistent with the vector form of Coulomb's Law.

🍃 Electric Field

🍃 Electric Field

Definition. The electric field $\mathbf{E}$ at a point in space is defined as the force per unit positive test charge placed at that point:

$$ \mathbf{E} = \frac{\mathbf{F}}{q_0} $$

where $\mathbf{F}$ is the electrostatic force experienced by a small positive test charge $q_0$. The SI unit of electric field is newtons per coulomb ($\text{N/C}$), equivalently volts per meter ($\text{V/m}$). The field is a vector quantity — its direction coincides with the direction of force on a positive test charge. For a point charge $Q$, the field at distance $r$ is given by Coulomb's law:

$$ \mathbf{E} = k_e \frac{Q}{r^2}\hat{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant and $\hat{r}$ is the unit vector pointing radially outward from $Q$.

Worked Example. A proton (charge $q = +1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$) is located at the origin. Find the magnitude and direction of the electric field at a point $P$ located $0.50\ \text{m}$ to the right of the proton.

$$ E = k_e \frac{q}{r^2} = \left(8.99 \times 10^9\right)\frac{1.60 \times 10^{-19}}{(0.50)^2} $$ $$ E = \frac{1.438 \times 10^{-9}}{0.25} = 5.75 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{N/C} $$

The field points in the $+x$ direction, away from the positive source charge.

Key Theorem — Superposition Principle. For a collection of point charges $Q_1, Q_2, \ldots, Q_n$, the total electric field at any point is the vector sum of the individual fields:

$$ \mathbf{E}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{E}_i = \sum_{i=1}^{n} k_e \frac{Q_i}{r_i^2}\hat{r}_i $$

This linearity is fundamental: it allows complex charge distributions to be analyzed by decomposition into simpler elements, and underpins the calculation of fields from continuous charge distributions via integration.

🍃 Polarization

🍃 Polarization

Definition. Electromagnetic waves are transverse waves in which the electric field vector $\vec{E}$ oscillates perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Polarization describes the orientation of this oscillation. An unpolarized wave, such as light emitted by an incandescent bulb, contains electric field vectors oriented randomly in all transverse directions. A linearly polarized wave confines $\vec{E}$ to a single plane, called the plane of polarization. Circular and elliptical polarization arise when two perpendicular components of $\vec{E}$ oscillate with a fixed phase difference; these are the more general cases.

Polarization is achieved by several mechanisms: selective absorption (dichroism) in polarizing filters, reflection at a dielectric surface (Brewster's angle), scattering by small particles (Rayleigh scattering), and birefringence in anisotropic crystals.

Worked Example. Unpolarized light of intensity $I_0$ passes through two ideal linear polarizers in series. The transmission axis of the second polarizer makes an angle $\theta = 30°$ with respect to the first. Find the transmitted intensity.

After the first polarizer, the light is linearly polarized and its intensity is reduced to $I_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}I_0$ (each polarizer transmits exactly half the power of unpolarized light). The second polarizer transmits only the component of $\vec{E}$ aligned with its axis, so by Malus's Law:

$$ I_2 = I_1 \cos^2\theta = \frac{I_0}{2}\cos^2(30°) = \frac{I_0}{2}\cdot\frac{3}{4} = \frac{3I_0}{8}. $$

Key Theorem — Malus's Law. When linearly polarized light of intensity $I_1$ passes through an ideal polarizer whose transmission axis makes angle $\theta$ with the polarization direction, the transmitted intensity is

$$ I = I_1 \cos^2\theta. $$

This result follows directly from projecting $\vec{E}$ onto the transmission axis: $E_{\text{trans}} = E_0\cos\theta$, and since intensity is proportional to $|\vec{E}|^2$, the $\cos^2\theta$ factor emerges immediately.

Lab Exercise. Arrange three polarizing filters in series. Set the first and third at $90°$ (crossed), confirming that no light passes through. Now insert the second filter at $45°$ between them. Measure the transmitted intensity and compare to the theoretical prediction $I = \tfrac{1}{8}I_0$. Record how removing the middle filter restores the dark condition, illustrating that polarization states cannot simply "add back" once extinguished.

🍃 Charging By Induction

🍃 Charging By Induction

Definition. Charging by induction is the process of redistributing charge within a conductor—or transferring net charge to a conductor—through the influence of an external electric field, without any direct contact with a charged object. The mechanism exploits the free mobility of conduction electrons in metals: an external charge polarizes the conductor, and if a ground connection is made and then broken while the inducing charge remains in place, a net charge of opposite sign is left on the object.

More precisely, consider a neutral metallic sphere. When a negatively charged rod is brought near (without touching), the free electrons in the sphere experience a repulsive Coulomb force and migrate to the far side, leaving a positive region on the near side. The sphere is now polarized but still electrically neutral overall. If the sphere is momentarily grounded—connected to Earth via a conducting path—electrons flow off the sphere into the ground, since the ground acts as an effectively infinite reservoir of charge. When the ground connection is severed first, and then the charged rod is removed, the sphere retains a net positive charge distributed uniformly over its surface.

Worked Example. A neutral aluminum sphere of radius $r = 0.05\ \text{m}$ is grounded while a rod carrying charge $Q = -3\ \mu\text{C}$ is held $d = 0.10\ \text{m}$ from the sphere's center. Estimate the induced surface charge density after the procedure is complete.

The induced charge on the sphere is approximately $+3\ \mu\text{C}$ (assuming complete induction for simplicity). The surface area of the sphere is:

$$ A = 4\pi r^2 = 4\pi (0.05)^2 \approx 3.14 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{m}^2 $$

The average surface charge density is:

$$ \sigma = \frac{Q}{A} = \frac{3 \times 10^{-6}}{3.14 \times 10^{-2}} \approx 9.55 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{C/m}^2 $$

Key Theorem. By Gauss's Law, the electric field inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium is zero: $\mathbf{E}_{\text{inside}} = 0$. Consequently, any excess charge resides entirely on the outer surface, and the surface charge density $\sigma$ at any point satisfies $E_{\perp} = \sigma/\varepsilon_0$ just outside the conductor.

Lab Exercise. Bring a charged PVC rod near one end of a pair of touching metal spheres. While the rod remains in place, separate the spheres. Remove the rod. Using an electroscope, verify that the sphere nearest the rod acquired a charge opposite in sign to the rod, and the far sphere acquired the same sign as the rod.

🍃 Electrostatic Equilibrium

🍃 Electrostatic Equilibrium

Definition. A conductor is said to be in electrostatic equilibrium when the free charges within it have ceased to move, so that the macroscopic electric current density everywhere inside the material is zero. This condition imposes four fundamental consequences that follow directly from Gauss's law and the properties of conductors:

  1. The electric field E inside a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium is zero everywhere.
  2. Any net charge resides entirely on the outer surface of the conductor.
  3. The electric field at the surface is perpendicular to that surface (no tangential component).
  4. On an irregularly shaped conductor, surface charge density $\sigma$ is greatest where the radius of curvature is smallest.

These results are not empirical observations but logical necessities. If E were nonzero inside, free electrons would accelerate, contradicting the equilibrium assumption. By Gauss's law applied to a Gaussian surface just inside the conductor, $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0$ implies $Q_{\text{enc}} = 0$, so all charge migrates to the surface.

Worked Example. A solid copper sphere of radius $R = 0.10\ \text{m}$ carries a net charge $Q = +5.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Find the electric field at $r = 0.05\ \text{m}$ (inside) and at $r = 0.15\ \text{m}$ (outside).

Inside ($r < R$): Because the conductor is in electrostatic equilibrium, $\mathbf{E} = 0$.

Outside ($r > R$): By Gauss's law with a concentric spherical surface,

$$ E \cdot 4\pi r^2 = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0} \implies E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(5.0\times10^{-6})}{(0.15)^2} \approx 2.0\times10^6\ \text{N/C} $$

directed radially outward.

Key Theorem. The Faraday cage effect is a direct corollary: any conductor surrounding a hollow interior completely shields that interior from external electrostatic fields. If $\mathbf{E}_{\text{ext}}$ exists outside, induced surface charges redistribute until the superposition of the external field and the induced-charge field cancels exactly inside the cavity.

🌸 Lightning Rod

🌸 Lightning Rod

Definition. A lightning rod (also called a lightning conductor) is a grounded metallic conductor, typically a pointed rod mounted at the apex of a structure, connected by a low-resistance cable to an earth ground. Its function rests on two electrostatic principles: (1) the concentration of electric field at regions of high curvature, and (2) the provision of a preferential low-resistance discharge path to ground. By Gauss's law, the surface charge density $\sigma$ on a conductor is related to the local electric field by

$$ E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0} $$

For a conductor of non-uniform geometry, charge accumulates preferentially at regions of small radius of curvature $r$. The electric field near a pointed tip scales approximately as $E \sim \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$, so a sharply tapered rod sustains a field many orders of magnitude stronger than a flat surface at the same total charge. This intense field ionizes surrounding air molecules, creating a conducting plasma channel — the corona discharge — that continuously bleeds charge between the cloud and ground, reducing the probability of a catastrophic stroke.

Worked Example. A spherical terminal of radius $r_1 = 1\,\text{cm}$ and a pointed rod tip approximated as a sphere of radius $r_2 = 0.5\,\text{mm}$ are both connected to the same conductor at potential $V = 300\,\text{kV}$ above ground. Using $V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r}$, the surface charge densities are

$$ \sigma_i = \varepsilon_0 \frac{V}{r_i} $$

yielding $\sigma_1 = (8.85\times10^{-12})(3\times10^5)/(0.01) \approx 0.27\,\mu\text{C/m}^2$ and $\sigma_2 \approx 5.3\,\mu\text{C/m}^2$ — a factor of roughly 20 enhancement at the tip, driving $E_2 \approx 600\,\text{MV/m}$, well above the dielectric breakdown threshold of air ($\approx 3\,\text{MV/m}$).

Key Theorem (Earnshaw's Theorem Corollary). A conductor in electrostatic equilibrium has zero internal field and carries all excess charge on its outer surface. The grounding cable ensures the rod remains at zero potential relative to earth, so any accumulated charge flows harmlessly to ground rather than building to destructive potential differences.

Payoff 🎯

The lightning rod represents the culmination of electrostatic principles developed throughout this chapter, synthesizing charge distribution, electric field geometry, and conductor behavior into a single elegant engineering solution. Its operation rests on a precise physical fact: the surface charge density on a conductor is greatest where curvature is sharpest. For a conductor with local radius of curvature $r$, the electric field magnitude near the surface scales as

$$ E \propto \sigma \propto \frac{1}{r}, $$

so a sharply pointed tip concentrates field lines to intensities far exceeding those at flat or rounded surfaces. This is not merely qualitative; solving the boundary-value problem for an isolated conducting sphere of radius $R$ held at potential $V$ gives $E_{\text{surface}} = V/R$, confirming that reducing $R$ by an order of magnitude raises the local field by the same factor.

A lightning rod exploits this concentration deliberately. The intense field at the tip ionizes surrounding air molecules, producing a conducting plasma channel — a corona discharge — that bleeds charge continuously from the structure to the atmosphere, suppressing the large-scale charge separation that would otherwise culminate in a destructive lightning strike. Alternatively, should a strike occur regardless, the rod provides a low-resistance path to ground, channeling current safely away from the building's structure. In either regime, the rod's function is inseparable from Gauss's law applied to conductor surfaces and the relationship between geometry and field intensity.

This concept anchors the broader landscape of applied electrostatics. The same tip-enhancement principle governs the operation of electrostatic precipitators, where needle electrodes ionize particulate-laden air to remove pollutants; field-emission electron microscopes, where atomically sharp tips emit electron beams for nanoscale imaging; and Van de Graaff generators, where controlled charge accumulation demonstrates potential differences in the megavolt range. In each case, geometry is the design variable, and the electric field is the quantity to be shaped.

To appreciate the full quantitative depth of this principle, consider tracing the path from Gauss's law through the solution of Laplace's equation in spherical coordinates to the explicit field map around a conductor of arbitrary shape. That investigation reveals not just how lightning rods work, but why sharpness is a universal tool for field control — an invitation to the richer territory of electromagnetic boundary-value problems that awaits in the next course.
