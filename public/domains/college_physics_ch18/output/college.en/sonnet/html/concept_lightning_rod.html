🌸 Lightning Rod

🌸 Lightning Rod

Definition. A lightning rod (also called a lightning conductor) is a grounded metallic conductor, typically a pointed rod mounted at the apex of a structure, connected by a low-resistance cable to an earth ground. Its function rests on two electrostatic principles: (1) the concentration of electric field at regions of high curvature, and (2) the provision of a preferential low-resistance discharge path to ground. By Gauss's law, the surface charge density $\sigma$ on a conductor is related to the local electric field by

$$ E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0} $$

For a conductor of non-uniform geometry, charge accumulates preferentially at regions of small radius of curvature $r$. The electric field near a pointed tip scales approximately as $E \sim \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$, so a sharply tapered rod sustains a field many orders of magnitude stronger than a flat surface at the same total charge. This intense field ionizes surrounding air molecules, creating a conducting plasma channel — the corona discharge — that continuously bleeds charge between the cloud and ground, reducing the probability of a catastrophic stroke.

Worked Example. A spherical terminal of radius $r_1 = 1\,\text{cm}$ and a pointed rod tip approximated as a sphere of radius $r_2 = 0.5\,\text{mm}$ are both connected to the same conductor at potential $V = 300\,\text{kV}$ above ground. Using $V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r}$, the surface charge densities are

$$ \sigma_i = \varepsilon_0 \frac{V}{r_i} $$

yielding $\sigma_1 = (8.85\times10^{-12})(3\times10^5)/(0.01) \approx 0.27\,\mu\text{C/m}^2$ and $\sigma_2 \approx 5.3\,\mu\text{C/m}^2$ — a factor of roughly 20 enhancement at the tip, driving $E_2 \approx 600\,\text{MV/m}$, well above the dielectric breakdown threshold of air ($\approx 3\,\text{MV/m}$).

Key Theorem (Earnshaw's Theorem Corollary). A conductor in electrostatic equilibrium has zero internal field and carries all excess charge on its outer surface. The grounding cable ensures the rod remains at zero potential relative to earth, so any accumulated charge flows harmlessly to ground rather than building to destructive potential differences.

Lab Exercise. Using a Van de Graaff generator and two identical metal spheres — one smooth, one fitted with a sharp needle — measure the spark discharge distance as a function of applied voltage. Record the critical voltage at which corona discharge begins at the needle tip versus the smooth sphere, and compare the ratio of discharge voltages to the inverse ratio of effective radii. Quantify how geometry alone controls the onset of electrical breakdown.
