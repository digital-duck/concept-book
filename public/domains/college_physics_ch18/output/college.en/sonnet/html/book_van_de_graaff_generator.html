🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: all observable free charges are integer multiples of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb, the SI unit of charge. There exist exactly two polarities: positive (carried by protons, $q = +e$) and negative (carried by electrons, $q = -e$). The net charge of an isolated system is conserved—charge can be transferred between objects but cannot be created or destroyed. Mathematically, conservation of charge is written as

$$ \frac{dQ_{\text{system}}}{dt} = 0 $$

for any closed system, where $Q_{\text{system}} = \sum_i q_i$ is the algebraic sum of all constituent charges.

Worked Example. A glass rod is rubbed with a silk cloth, transferring $3.0 \times 10^{10}$ electrons from the glass to the silk. Find the charge acquired by each object.

Each electron carries $q_e = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$. The silk gains

$$ Q_{\text{silk}} = (3.0 \times 10^{10})(−1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}) = -4.81 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{C} \approx -4.81\ \text{nC}. $$

By conservation of charge, the glass rod acquires an equal and opposite charge: $Q_{\text{glass}} = +4.81\ \text{nC}$. Note that total charge before and after rubbing remains zero, consistent with both objects beginning electrically neutral.

Key Theorem — Quantization of Charge. Every observable free charge satisfies $q = ne$ for some $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. This was established experimentally by Millikan's oil-drop experiment (1909), in which electrically charged droplets suspended against gravity were found to carry charges only in discrete steps of $e$, never fractional multiples. Although quarks carry charges of $\pm e/3$ and $\pm 2e/3$, they are permanently confined within hadrons and have never been detected as free particles, so the quantization rule holds universally for all directly observable charged objects.

🌱 Electron

🌱 Electron

Definition. The electron is a fundamental subatomic particle carrying a charge of $q_e = -e$, where $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$ is the elementary charge. Its rest mass is $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, making it approximately 1836 times lighter than the proton. Electrons are classified as leptons — point-like particles with no known internal structure — and they occupy quantized energy states in atomic orbitals. In the context of electrostatics and circuit theory, the electron serves as the primary charge carrier in conducting materials, where conduction electrons move quasi-freely through a metallic lattice under an applied electric field.

Worked Example. Consider a copper wire carrying a steady current $I = 2.0\ \text{A}$. How many electrons pass through a cross-sectional plane of the wire each second?

Current is defined as charge per unit time:

$$ I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} \implies \Delta Q = I\,\Delta t. $$

In one second, $\Delta Q = 2.0\ \text{C}$. The number of electrons is:

$$ N = \frac{\Delta Q}{e} = \frac{2.0\ \text{C}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}} \approx 1.25 \times 10^{19}\ \text{electrons}. $$

This enormous number underscores the collective nature of macroscopic electric current; no single electron traverses the entire wire in one second, but the coordinated drift of the electron ensemble produces a measurable, continuous current.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Electron Charge). The charge of an isolated electron is invariant: $q_e = -e$ regardless of the electron's kinetic energy, reference frame, or chemical environment. This invariance, confirmed to at least one part in $10^{21}$ by precision spectroscopy, is the empirical foundation for treating $e$ as a universal constant. Consequently, the total charge in any closed system is conserved because each electron contributes exactly $-e$, with no partial values possible.

Lab Exercise. Using a cathode-ray tube apparatus, apply a known electric field $\vec{E}$ perpendicular to the electron beam and a magnetic field $\vec{B}$ parallel to it. Adjust $B$ until the beam travels undeflected, satisfying $eE = evB$, giving $v = E/B$. Then remove $\vec{B}$ and measure the deflection $y$ of the beam to determine $e/m_e$ via:

$$ \frac{e}{m_e} = \frac{2yE}{B^2 L^2}, $$

where $L$ is the length of the deflecting field region. Compare your result to the accepted value $e/m_e = 1.759 \times 10^{11}\ \text{C\,kg}^{-1}$.

🍃 Conductor

🍃 Conductor

Definition. A conductor is a material in which electric charges—typically electrons—are free to move throughout the bulk of the material under the influence of an applied electric field. In metals, the outermost valence electrons are delocalized, forming a "sea" of conduction electrons that can respond collectively to external forces. Formally, a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium satisfies three fundamental conditions: (1) the electric field $\mathbf{E}$ inside the conductor is identically zero, $\mathbf{E}_{\text{int}} = \mathbf{0}$; (2) any net charge resides exclusively on the outer surface; and (3) the electric field at the surface is perpendicular to that surface, with magnitude $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0$, where $\sigma$ is the local surface charge density and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{C}^2/(\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)$.

Worked Example. A solid copper sphere of radius $R = 0.10\ \text{m}$ carries a net charge of $Q = +5.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Determine the electric field at $r = 0.05\ \text{m}$ (inside) and at $r = 0.15\ \text{m}$ (outside).

Inside ($r < R$): Because copper is a conductor in equilibrium, $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{0}$ everywhere within the material. No calculation is needed—the conducting electrons redistribute until they cancel any interior field.

Outside ($r > R$): By Gauss's law with a spherical surface of radius $r$,

$$ \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0} \implies E(4\pi r^2) = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}, $$ $$ E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(5.0\times10^{-6})}{(0.15)^2} \approx 2.0\times10^6\ \text{N/C}. $$

The field points radially outward.

Key Theorem (Electrostatic Shielding). The interior of any closed conducting shell is completely shielded from external electric fields. This follows directly from Gauss's law: a Gaussian surface drawn entirely within the conductor encloses zero net field, so external charge distributions produce no field inside the cavity. This principle underlies Faraday cages used in sensitive electronic equipment.

🍃 Charging By Contact

🍃 Charging By Contact

Definition. Charging by contact (also called conduction charging) is the process by which an electrically neutral object acquires a net charge through direct physical contact with a charged object. When two conductors touch, free electrons redistribute across the combined system until the electric potential is equalized. If the charging object carries a net charge $Q$ and has capacitance $C_1$, while the neutral object has capacitance $C_2$, the charge redistributes such that the final charges satisfy

$$ Q_1' = Q \cdot \frac{C_1}{C_1 + C_2}, \qquad Q_2' = Q \cdot \frac{C_2}{C_1 + C_2} $$

with $Q_1' + Q_2' = Q$, confirming conservation of charge. For two identical conductors, each retains exactly half the original charge. Unlike induction, conduction transfers charge permanently and requires no subsequent grounding step to preserve the induced charge.

Worked Example. A metal sphere A carries a charge of $+6.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and is brought into contact with an identical uncharged metal sphere B. After contact and separation, sphere A retains $Q_A' = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and sphere B acquires $Q_B' = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Now sphere B is brought into contact with a third identical sphere C (also initially uncharged). The charge splits again:

$$ Q_B'' = Q_C' = \frac{3.0\ \mu\text{C}}{2} = 1.5\ \mu\text{C}. $$

Sphere A remains at $+3.0\ \mu\text{C}$; spheres B and C each carry $+1.5\ \mu\text{C}$. Total charge: $3.0 + 1.5 + 1.5 = 6.0\ \mu\text{C}$. Charge is conserved throughout.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Charge). In any isolated system, the total electric charge is invariant:

$$ \sum_i Q_i = \text{constant}. $$

Conduction charging is consistent with this principle — charge is never created or destroyed, only redistributed. The sign of the transferred charge matches that of the source: a negatively charged rod produces a negatively charged recipient.

🍃 Insulator

🍃 Insulator

Definition. An insulator (also called a dielectric in the context of electric fields) is a material in which the outermost electrons of its constituent atoms are tightly bound to their respective nuclei and are therefore not free to migrate through the bulk of the material under an applied electric field. As a consequence, insulators possess an extremely low density of charge carriers, resulting in negligible macroscopic electric current even when placed in a strong external field. Quantitatively, the electrical conductivity $\sigma$ of a good insulator satisfies $\sigma \operatorname{ll} 1 \, \mathrm{S \cdot m^{-1}}$, in contrast to metallic conductors where $\sigma \sim 10^7 \, \mathrm{S \cdot m^{-1}}$. Common examples include rubber, glass, dry wood, and most polymers.

Worked Example. A glass rod of length $L = 0.25 \, \mathrm{m}$ and cross-sectional area $A = 1.0 \times 10^{-4} \, \mathrm{m}^2$ has a resistivity $\rho = 1.0 \times 10^{12} \, \mathrm{\Omega \cdot m}$ at room temperature. The resistance of the rod is given by

$$ R = \frac{\rho L}{A} = \frac{(1.0 \times 10^{12})(0.25)}{1.0 \times 10^{-4}} = 2.5 \times 10^{15} \, \mathrm{\Omega}. $$

If a potential difference of $V = 1000 \, \mathrm{V}$ is applied across the rod, the resulting current is

$$ I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{1000}{2.5 \times 10^{15}} = 4.0 \times 10^{-13} \, \mathrm{A}, $$

confirming that the current is vanishingly small.

Key Theorem — Charge Localization on Insulators. Unlike a conductor, an excess charge deposited on an insulator remains localized at the point of deposition rather than redistributing over the surface. This follows directly from the absence of free charge carriers: without mobile electrons or ions, Coulomb repulsion cannot drive redistribution. Formally, the charge density $\rho_{\mathrm{f}}(\mathbf{r})$ at any free surface point satisfies $\partial \rho_{\mathrm{f}} / \partial t \approx 0$ over experimentally relevant timescales.

Lab Exercise. Charge a plastic comb by running it through dry hair. Bring the comb close to small pieces of paper. Record where charge appears to reside on the comb over a 60-second interval using a grounded probe, and verify that the charge distribution does not spread. Compare the decay time with that observed for a metallic rod charged under identical conditions.

🌸 Van De Graaff Generator

🌸 Van De Graaff Generator

Definition

A Van de Graaff generator is an electrostatic device that accumulates large amounts of electric charge on a hollow metallic sphere by exploiting the principle that charge resides on the outer surface of conductors. The mechanism consists of a non-conducting conveyor belt driven between two rollers—one near the base and one inside the dome—with a pair of metallic combs positioned at each roller. The lower comb acquires charge from an external high-voltage source (or through triboelectric contact) and sprays charge onto the moving belt. The upper comb, located inside the grounded interior of the sphere, strips this charge off the belt and deposits it onto the sphere's outer surface. Because the interior of a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium has zero net charge and zero electric field, charge continuously migrates to the exterior regardless of how much has already accumulated, allowing the potential to build up without theoretical limit (in practice limited by corona discharge and breakdown of surrounding air).

Worked Example

Suppose a spherical dome of radius $R = 0.15\ \text{m}$ has accumulated a charge $Q = 1.0\ \mu\text{C}$. The electric potential at the surface is:

$$ V = \frac{kQ}{R} = \frac{(8.99 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2)(1.0 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{C})}{0.15\ \text{m}} \approx 6.0 \times 10^4\ \text{V} $$

The surface electric field is $E = V/R \approx 4.0 \times 10^5\ \text{V/m}$. Since air begins to ionize at roughly $3 \times 10^6\ \text{V/m}$, this dome is well below breakdown, but a dome of the same charge at $R = 0.02\ \text{m}$ would exceed the threshold.

Key Theorem

By Gauss's law applied to a closed surface just inside the conducting shell:

$$ \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0} = 0 $$

since $Q_{\text{enc}} = 0$ in the interior. Consequently, all charge distributes on the outer surface, and the potential everywhere inside equals the surface potential—a result central to the generator's operation and to electrostatic shielding (Faraday cage).

Payoff 🎯

The Van de Graaff generator represents the culminating synthesis of electrostatics: a machine that transforms mechanical work directly into an arbitrarily large, controllable electrostatic potential. Every concept encountered throughout this course—charge quantization, Coulomb's law, electric fields, Gauss's law, electric potential, capacitance, and dielectric breakdown—converges in this single device. It is the natural endpoint precisely because it does not merely illustrate one principle in isolation but instead demands that all principles operate simultaneously and coherently.

What the Generator Achieves

A Van de Graaff generator accumulates charge on a hollow conducting sphere by exploiting two foundational facts: charge deposited on a conductor resides entirely on its outer surface, and the electric field inside a closed conductor is zero. A moving belt continuously carries charge from a lower comb (where corona discharge transfers charge to the belt) to an upper comb inside the sphere. Because the interior field vanishes, each new increment of charge is repelled outward onto the sphere's exterior regardless of how much has already accumulated. The potential on the sphere of radius $R$ carrying total charge $Q$ grows without limit as

$$ V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 R}, $$

constrained in practice only by dielectric breakdown of the surrounding medium, which occurs in air at approximately $E_{\text{max}} \approx 3 \times 10^6\ \text{V/m}$.

Connections to Application Domains

Though no explicit downstream application domains were enumerated for this chapter, the generator's principles open three essential directions. First, it anchors particle acceleration: by placing a charged particle at potential $V$, one imparts kinetic energy $K = qV$, the operating principle of every early nuclear accelerator. Second, it illuminates electrostatic shielding and safety engineering, since the zero-interior-field property underpins Faraday cage design in sensitive instrumentation. Third, it makes spark and discharge phenomena quantitative, directly connecting to lightning-rod physics and high-voltage safety protocols.
