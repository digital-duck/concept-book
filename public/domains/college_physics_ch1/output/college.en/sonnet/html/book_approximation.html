🌱 Measurement

🌱 Measurement

Definition. Measurement is the quantitative comparison of a physical quantity against a defined standard unit, yielding a numerical value paired with that unit. Formally, any measured quantity $Q$ is expressed as

$$ Q = n \cdot [Q] $$

where $n$ is the numerical measure and $[Q]$ denotes the unit. In the SI system, all physical quantities reduce to combinations of seven base units: meter (m), kilogram (kg), second (s), ampere (A), kelvin (K), mole (mol), and candela (cd). Precision refers to the reproducibility of repeated measurements, while accuracy describes how closely a measurement agrees with the accepted true value. Significant figures encode the precision of a measured result: the number of reliably known digits plus one estimated digit.

Worked Example. A student measures the diameter of a steel cylinder five times and records: 2.43 cm, 2.45 cm, 2.44 cm, 2.43 cm, 2.45 cm. The mean is

$$ \bar{d} = \frac{2.43 + 2.45 + 2.44 + 2.43 + 2.45}{5} = \frac{12.20}{5} = 2.44 \text{ cm}. $$

The accepted value is 2.50 cm. Precision is assessed by the range $\Delta d = 2.45 - 2.43 = 0.02$ cm (or equivalently the standard deviation $\approx 0.01$ cm), indicating high reproducibility. Accuracy is assessed by the percent error:

$$ \text{\% error} = \frac{|2.44 - 2.50|}{2.50} \times 100\% = 2.4\%. $$

The measurements are precise but not fully accurate, suggesting a systematic error in the instrument or procedure.

Key Theorem (Propagation of Uncertainty). If a quantity $f$ depends on independently measured variables $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$, the absolute uncertainty propagates as

$$ \delta f = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}\delta x_1\right)^2 + \cdots + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\delta x_n\right)^2}. $$

This result follows from a first-order Taylor expansion and the statistical independence of the errors. It ensures that uncertainties do not simply add but combine in quadrature, preventing overestimation of total error.

🍃 Physical Quantity

🍃 Physical Quantity

Definition. A physical quantity is any property of a physical system that can be measured and expressed as the product of a numerical value and a unit. Formally, a physical quantity $Q$ is written as

$$ Q = \{Q\} \cdot [Q], $$

where $\{Q\}$ denotes the numerical measure and $[Q\}$ denotes the chosen unit. Physical quantities are categorized as scalar (characterized by magnitude alone, e.g., temperature $T$, mass $m$) or vector (characterized by both magnitude and direction, e.g., velocity $\vec{v}$, force $\vec{F}$). A complete, unambiguous description of any physical quantity requires specifying both the number and the unit; a number without a unit is physically meaningless.

Worked Example. A student measures the length of a laboratory bench and records $\ell = 2.47 \, \text{m}$. Here $\{Q\} = 2.47$ and $[Q] = \text{metre}$. If the student wishes to express the same length in centimetres, the numerical value changes while the physical quantity remains invariant:

$$ \ell = 2.47 \, \text{m} = 2.47 \times 10^{2} \, \text{cm} = 247 \, \text{cm}. $$

This illustrates the principle of unit invariance: the physical reality described by $Q$ does not depend on the choice of unit system. Consequently, any valid equation relating physical quantities must be dimensionally homogeneous — every additive term must share the same dimensions.

Key Theorem (Dimensional Homogeneity). In any physically valid equation $A = B$, the dimensions of $A$ and $B$ must be identical. That is, if $[A] = \mathsf{M}^a \mathsf{L}^b \mathsf{T}^c$, then necessarily $[B] = \mathsf{M}^a \mathsf{L}^b \mathsf{T}^c$, where $\mathsf{M}$, $\mathsf{L}$, $\mathsf{T}$ denote the base dimensions of mass, length, and time respectively. This theorem provides a powerful consistency check: an equation that fails dimensional analysis is guaranteed to be incorrect.

Lab Exercise. The kinematic equation for displacement under constant acceleration is $x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$. Using dimensional analysis, verify that each term carries dimensions of length $[\mathsf{L}]$. Then, given $x_0 = 0$, $v_0 = 3.0 \, \text{m/s}$, and $a = 2.0 \, \text{m/s}^2$, compute $x$ at $t = 4.0 \, \text{s}$ and confirm that your result has the correct unit.

🍃 Order Of Magnitude

🍃 Order Of Magnitude

Definition. The order of magnitude of a quantity is the power of ten closest to that quantity when expressed in scientific notation. Formally, if a quantity $Q$ satisfies

$$ 10^{n-1/2} \leq Q < 10^{n+1/2} $$

for some integer $n$, then the order of magnitude of $Q$ is $10^n$. In practice, one writes $Q \approx 10^n$ and treats $n$ as a rough logarithmic measure of scale. Two quantities share the same order of magnitude if their ratio lies between $10^{-1/2} \approx 0.316$ and $10^{1/2} \approx 3.16$; they differ by one order of magnitude if that ratio is approximately 10.

This concept underpins dimensional analysis and estimation: before solving a problem precisely, a physicist checks whether the expected answer is $10^{-3}$ or $10^{3}$, catching unit errors and unreasonable results early.

Worked Example. Estimate the number of heartbeats in a human lifetime.

  • Average heart rate: $\approx 70 \text{ beats/min} \approx 10^2 \text{ beats/min}$
  • Minutes per year: $60 \times 24 \times 365 \approx 5 \times 10^5 \text{ min/yr}$
  • Human lifespan: $\approx 80 \text{ yr} \approx 10^2 \text{ yr}$

Total beats $\approx 10^2 \times 5 \times 10^5 \times 10^2 = 5 \times 10^9$.

The order of magnitude is $10^{10}$ (since $5 \times 10^9 \approx 10^{9.7}$, which rounds to $10^{10}$). A precise calculation yields roughly $3 \times 10^9$, confirming our estimate is correct to within one order of magnitude.

Key Theorem (Logarithmic Additivity). If quantity $A$ has order of magnitude $10^a$ and quantity $B$ has order of magnitude $10^b$, then their product $AB$ has order of magnitude $10^{a+b}$. This follows directly from the logarithm rule $\log_{10}(AB) = \log_{10} A + \log_{10} B$, and justifies why order-of-magnitude estimates can be chained through multiplication without compounding large errors.

🍃 Units

🍃 Units

Definition. A unit is a standardized quantity used as a reference against which other quantities of the same kind are measured. The International System of Units (SI) defines seven base units from which all other physical quantities are derived: the metre (m) for length, the kilogram (kg) for mass, the second (s) for time, the ampere (A) for electric current, the kelvin (K) for thermodynamic temperature, the mole (mol) for amount of substance, and the candela (cd) for luminous intensity. Every physical quantity $Q$ can be expressed as a numerical value multiplied by a unit: $Q = \{Q\} \cdot [Q]$, where $\{Q\}$ is the numerical measure and $[Q]$ denotes the unit. A change in unit produces a corresponding inverse change in numerical value, preserving the physical quantity itself.

Worked Example. Convert a speed of $v = 90\,\text{km/h}$ to SI base units (m/s). Using the conversion factors $1\,\text{km} = 10^3\,\text{m}$ and $1\,\text{h} = 3600\,\text{s}$:

$$ v = 90\,\frac{\text{km}}{\text{h}} \times \frac{10^3\,\text{m}}{1\,\text{km}} \times \frac{1\,\text{h}}{3600\,\text{s}} = \frac{90 \times 10^3}{3600}\,\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}} = 25\,\text{m/s}. $$

Each conversion factor equals unity, so the physical quantity is unchanged; only its representation shifts.

Key Theorem (Dimensional Homogeneity). Any valid physical equation must be dimensionally homogeneous: every additive term must carry identical dimensions, and both sides of an equality must share the same dimensional form. Formally, if $[A] \neq [B]$, then the expression $A + B$ is physically meaningless. This principle, sometimes called dimensional analysis, provides a necessary (though not sufficient) condition for correctness and serves as a powerful consistency check. For instance, the kinematic equation $v = v_0 + at$ is verified by confirming $[\text{m/s}] = [\text{m/s}] + [\text{m/s}^2][\text{s}] = [\text{m/s}]$.

🌸 Approximation

🌸 Approximation

Definition. In physics, an approximation is a deliberate simplification of a physical model that retains the essential behavior of a system while discarding contributions whose magnitudes fall below a chosen threshold of significance. Formally, if a quantity $Q$ depends on a small dimensionless parameter $\varepsilon \operatorname{ll} 1$, then retaining only the leading-order terms in a Taylor expansion,

$$ Q(\varepsilon) \approx Q(0) + \varepsilon\,Q'(0) + \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon^2 Q''(0) + \cdots, $$

constitutes a perturbative approximation. The truncation is justified when the neglected terms are smaller than the experimental or contextual precision required. Physical approximations may also involve geometric idealization (point masses, ideal strings) or limiting cases (frictionless surfaces, massless ropes).

Worked Example. Consider a simple pendulum of length $L = 1.00\ \text{m}$ released from a small angle $\theta_0 = 5.00°$. The exact equation of motion is $\ddot{\theta} + (g/L)\sin\theta = 0$, which has no closed-form solution. Applying the small-angle approximation, $\sin\theta \approx \theta$ (valid when $\theta \operatorname{ll} 1\ \text{rad}$), the equation reduces to the simple harmonic form $\ddot{\theta} + (g/L)\theta = 0$, yielding the period

$$ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{1.00}{9.81}} \approx 2.007\ \text{s}. $$

The exact period (computed numerically) is $T_{\text{exact}} \approx 2.008\ \text{s}$, a discrepancy of less than $0.05\%$, well within typical experimental uncertainty for introductory laboratory work.

Key Theorem (Taylor's Theorem with Remainder). If $f$ is $(n+1)$-times differentiable on an interval containing $a$ and $x$, then

$$ f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k + R_n(x), $$

where the Lagrange remainder satisfies $|R_n(x)| \leq \frac{M}{(n+1)!}|x-a|^{n+1}$ for some bound $M$ on $|f^{(n+1)}|$. This theorem provides rigorous error control for any truncated approximation.

Payoff 🎯

Approximation is not a concession to ignorance — it is a disciplined strategy for extracting exact answers to the questions that actually matter. Throughout Chapter 1, the tools of dimensional analysis, order-of-magnitude estimation, and significant figures have been assembled one by one. Approximation is where those tools converge into a single principle: every physical model is an idealization, and the physicist's craft lies in knowing which idealizations to make and how much they cost in precision.

What approximation achieves. A physical problem rarely arrives in a form that submits immediately to exact solution. Friction is neglected; air resistance is linearized; a curved surface is treated as flat over a small patch. Each such simplification is justified by a dimensionless ratio being small. If the ratio $\varepsilon = v/c \operatorname{ll} 1$, relativistic corrections of order $\varepsilon^2$ are negligible at the precision the experiment can resolve. Making this argument rigorous — and knowing when it breaks down — is the payoff of mastering approximation.

Worked example. Estimate the speed at which a falling raindrop strikes the ground. A naive free-fall calculation gives $v = \sqrt{2g h}$; for $h = 2{,}000\ \text{m}$ this yields roughly $200\ \text{m/s}$, far above observed values. Introducing a terminal-velocity approximation, where drag force balances gravity, gives $v_t = \sqrt{mg/b}$ for a linear drag coefficient $b$. For a droplet of mass $m \approx 3 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{kg}$ and $b \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{kg/s}$, this gives $v_t \approx \sqrt{(3\times10^{-5})(9.8)/(1.6\times10^{-4})} \approx 1.4\ \text{m/s}$, consistent with measurement. The approximation discarded the transient phase of acceleration — legitimate because the time constant $\tau = m/b \approx 0.2\ \text{s}$ is tiny compared with a multi-second fall.

Key theorem. For any quantity $f(\varepsilon)$ analytic near $\varepsilon = 0$, the Taylor expansion $f(\varepsilon) = f(0) + f'(0)\,\varepsilon + \tfrac{1}{2}f''(0)\,\varepsilon^2 + \cdots$ provides a systematic hierarchy of approximations whose error is bounded by the first neglected term. This underpins every perturbative method in classical and quantum physics.
