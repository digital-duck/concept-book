Scientific Method

Every day, engineers test whether a new drug works, physicists search for hidden particles, and climate scientists forecast future temperatures. What separates a reliable conclusion from an educated guess? The answer is a shared procedure for turning observations into knowledge: the scientific method.

Definition. The scientific method is a cyclic reasoning framework:

$$\text{Observe} \;\to\; \text{Hypothesize} \;\to\; \text{Predict} \;\to\; \text{Experiment} \;\to\; \text{Conclude} \;\to\; \text{Revise}$$

A *hypothesis* $H$ is a falsifiable statement about the world. An *experiment* produces data $D$. We then evaluate how well $H$ explains $D$, often via a conditional probability $P(D \mid H)$ — the *likelihood* of observing $D$ if $H$ were true.

Worked example. Suppose you hypothesize that a coin is fair: $H_0: p = 0.5$, where $p$ is the probability of heads. You flip it $n = 100$ times and observe $k = 68$ heads. Under $H_0$, the expected number of heads is $\mu = np = 50$ with standard deviation $\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)} = 5$. The standardized test statistic is

$$z = \frac{k - \mu}{\sigma} = \frac{68 - 50}{5} = 3.6.$$

Because $|z| = 3.6$ far exceeds the conventional threshold of $1.96$ (for a $5\%$ significance level), you *reject* $H_0$: the evidence is inconsistent with a fair coin.

Why it works. The method is self-correcting. By demanding that hypotheses make *testable predictions*, it creates a feedback loop: failed predictions force revision. No single experiment is definitive, but repeated independent tests that survive scrutiny converge on descriptions of nature that are progressively more accurate.
