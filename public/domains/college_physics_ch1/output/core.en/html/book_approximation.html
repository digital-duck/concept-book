Physical Quantity

Every measurement you have ever made — the length of a table, the temperature of a room, the time it takes to walk to class — combines two pieces of information: a number and a unit. Together they form a physical quantity, the fundamental language through which physics describes the natural world.

Definition. A physical quantity is a property of a material or system that can be quantified by measurement. It is expressed as

$$Q = n \cdot [Q],$$

where $n$ is the numerical value (a pure number) and $[Q]$ is the unit of measurement. Neither part is meaningful alone: saying "the mass is 5" is incomplete, and saying "the mass is kilograms" is nonsensical.

Worked Example. A car travels $d = 150\;\mathrm{km}$ in $t = 2.0\;\mathrm{h}$. Its average speed is

$$v = \frac{d}{t} = \frac{150\;\mathrm{km}}{2.0\;\mathrm{h}} = 75\;\mathrm{km\,h^{-1}}.$$

Here two physical quantities — a distance and a time — combine to produce a third. Notice that the units combine algebraically just like the numbers do. This is not a coincidence but a defining feature of how physical quantities work: you can multiply, divide, and exponentiate them, and the units track every operation.

Why it's true. The decomposition $Q = n \cdot [Q]$ is more than notation; it encodes the requirement that physics does not depend on human-chosen units. If you switch from kilometers to meters, the numerical value changes ($150\;\mathrm{km} = 1.50 \times 10^5\;\mathrm{m}$) but the physical quantity remains the same. This unit invariance guarantees that physical laws hold regardless of the measurement system — a cornerstone of dimensional analysis and a powerful tool for checking equations.

Practice Problem. The density of water is $\rho = 1000\;\mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$. Identify the numerical value and the unit. Then convert $\rho$ to $\mathrm{g\,cm^{-3}}$ and verify that the physical quantity is unchanged.

Precision

When a medical test screens for a rare disease, every positive result matters. If the test flags 100 people but only 10 actually have the disease, doctors waste resources on 90 false alarms. Precision measures exactly this: of all the items a system labels as positive, how many truly are?

Definition. Given a binary classifier, let $TP$ (true positives) be the number of correctly predicted positives and $FP$ (false positives) be the number of negatives incorrectly predicted as positive. The precision is

$$P = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}.$$

Precision ranges over $[0, 1]$. A precision of $1$ means every positive prediction was correct; a precision near $0$ means the classifier raises mostly false alarms.

Worked Example. A spam filter processes 200 emails. It flags 50 as spam. On inspection, 40 of those are genuinely spam and 10 are legitimate messages wrongly flagged. Here $TP = 40$ and $FP = 10$, so

$$P = \frac{40}{40 + 10} = \frac{40}{50} = 0.80.$$

Eighty percent of the emails the filter called spam actually were spam.

Why it's true (and when it matters). Precision answers the question "Can I trust a positive prediction?" It is especially important when false positives carry high cost — blocking a legitimate email, accusing an innocent person, or ordering an unnecessary medical procedure. Notice that precision says nothing about the positives the classifier missed; that role belongs to recall ($TP / (TP + FN)$). A trivially cautious model that predicts positive only once, and gets it right, achieves $P = 1$ but catches almost nothing. This is why precision is rarely used alone; the $F_1$ score, defined as $F_1 = 2 \cdot \frac{P \cdot R}{P + R}$, balances precision $P$ and recall $R$ into a single metric.

Practice Problem. A fraud detection system examines 1{,}000 transactions and flags 80 as fraudulent. Of those 80, only 60 are actual fraud. Compute the precision. Then suppose the system is made stricter, flagging only 50 transactions, of which 48 are actual fraud. What is the new precision, and what trade-off might the stricter system introduce?

Uncertainty

Every measurement carries a story of its own limitations. When a pharmacist weighs a dose of medication, the reading might show 250 mg — but is it exactly 250.000… mg? The true value hides behind the finite precision of every instrument, and uncertainty is how we quantify that gap.

Definition. The uncertainty $\delta x$ of a measured quantity $x$ represents the range within which the true value is reasonably expected to lie. A measurement is reported as

$$x_{\text{best}} \pm \delta x,$$

where $x_{\text{best}}$ is the best estimate (often the mean of repeated trials) and $\delta x$ bounds the probable deviation from that estimate.

Worked Example. A student measures the length of a table five times, obtaining values (in cm): $120.2,\; 120.4,\; 120.1,\; 120.3,\; 120.5$. The best estimate is the mean:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{120.2 + 120.4 + 120.1 + 120.3 + 120.5}{5} = 120.3 \;\text{cm}.$$

One common estimate of uncertainty is the standard deviation of the mean. First, the sample standard deviation:

$$s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{N-1}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.01 + 0.01 + 0.04 + 0.00 + 0.04}{4}} = \sqrt{0.025} \approx 0.16 \;\text{cm}.$$

The standard uncertainty in the mean is $\delta x = \dfrac{s}{\sqrt{N}} = \dfrac{0.16}{\sqrt{5}} \approx 0.07\;\text{cm}.$ We report the length as $120.30 \pm 0.07\;\text{cm}.$

Why it's true. Averaging reduces random scatter: each independent measurement contributes new information, and the central limit theorem guarantees that the distribution of the mean tightens by a factor of $1/\sqrt{N}$. This is why repeating a measurement genuinely improves precision — not by changing the instrument, but by letting statistics work in your favor.

Practice Problem. A stopwatch records five lap times (in seconds): $48.2,\; 47.9,\; 48.4,\; 48.1,\; 48.0$. Compute the mean, the sample standard deviation, and the uncertainty in the mean. Report the result in the form $\bar{t} \pm \delta t$.

Order Of Magnitude

How can a physicist quickly compare the size of an atom ($10^{-10}$ m) with the diameter of the Earth ($10^{7}$ m)? Rather than wrestling with long strings of digits, scientists use orders of magnitude — powers of ten that capture the essential scale of a quantity.

Definition. The order of magnitude of a positive number $x$ is the integer $n$ such that

$$10^{n} \leq x < 10^{n+1},$$

or more precisely, $n = \lfloor \log_{10} x \rfloor$. We say two quantities differ by $k$ orders of magnitude when the ratio between them is approximately $10^{k}$.

Worked Example. Estimate the order of magnitude of $x = 7{,}400$.

  • Express $x$ in scientific notation: $7{,}400 = 7.4 \times 10^{3}$.
  • Compute $\log_{10}(7{,}400) = \log_{10}(7.4) + 3 \approx 0.87 + 3 = 3.87$.
  • Take the floor: $n = \lfloor 3.87 \rfloor = 3$.

    • So $7{,}400$ is of order $10^{3}$. Compare this with $x = 52 \approx 5.2 \times 10^{1}$, which has order $10^{1}$. The two numbers differ by about $3 - 1 = 2$ orders of magnitude — a factor of roughly $100$.

    Why it works. The logarithm converts multiplication into addition and maps the positive reals onto a uniform scale where each unit step corresponds to a factor of ten. Because $\log_{10}$ is strictly increasing, the floor of $\log_{10} x$ partitions the positive reals into bands $[10^{n},\, 10^{n+1})$. Every number in one band shares the same leading power of ten, which is exactly the information an order-of-magnitude estimate preserves. This is why quick "back-of-the-envelope" calculations — where factors of $2$ or $3$ are noise — remain reliable: errors smaller than a full power of ten do not change the order.

    Practice Problem. The speed of light is $c \approx 3.0 \times 10^{8}$ m/s and the speed of sound in air is $v \approx 343$ m/s. By how many orders of magnitude does light outpace sound? Verify by computing $\lfloor \log_{10}(c/v) \rfloor$.

    Significant Figures

    When NASA engineers calculated the trajectory for the Mars Climate Orbiter in 1999, a unit mismatch led to the spacecraft's destruction — a stark reminder that how we report numbers matters as much as the numbers themselves. Significant figures are the digits in a measurement that carry real information about its precision.

    Definition. The significant figures (sig figs) of a number are all digits known with certainty plus one estimated digit. The rules for identifying them:

  • All nonzero digits are significant: $4307$ has 4 sig figs.
  • Zeros between nonzero digits are significant: $2.005$ has 4 sig figs.
  • Leading zeros are never significant: $0.0042$ has 2 sig figs.
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant: $8.100$ has 4 sig figs.
  • Trailing zeros in a whole number without a decimal point are ambiguous: $1500$ may have 2, 3, or 4 sig figs. Scientific notation resolves this — $1.50 \times 10^3$ has 3.

    • Worked Example. A student measures a rod as $\ell = 12.34 \, \text{cm}$ (4 sig figs) and its diameter as $d = 0.250 \, \text{cm}$ (3 sig figs). What is the cross-sectional area reported with proper sig figs?

    The radius is $r = d/2 = 0.125 \, \text{cm}$. Then

    $$A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.125)^2 = 0.049087 \ldots \, \text{cm}^2.$$

    The multiplication rule states: the result carries as many sig figs as the least precise input. Since $d$ has 3 sig figs, we round to 3 sig figs:

    $$A = 0.0491 \, \text{cm}^2.$$

    Why it works. Significant figures propagate the weakest link in a chain of measurements. If a quantity is known only to $\pm 1$ in the third digit, no arithmetic can conjure fourth-digit certainty. The rounding rules are a simplified form of full uncertainty propagation — they guarantee the final digit is meaningful without requiring a formal error analysis at every step.

    Practice Problem. Evaluate $(3.20 \times 10^2) \times (1.5 \times 10^{-1})$ and express the result with the correct number of significant figures. How many sig figs does your answer have, and why?

    Approximation

    Every engineer, scientist, and navigator faces the same reality: exact answers are often impossible or impractical to obtain. When you estimate that a road trip will take "about three hours," you are approximating — replacing a complex, exact quantity with a simpler one that is close enough to be useful.

    Definition. An approximation of a quantity $Q$ is a value $\tilde{Q}$ chosen so that the error $|Q - \tilde{Q}|$ is acceptably small for a given purpose. We often measure quality by the relative error:

    $$\text{relative error} = \frac{|Q - \tilde{Q}|}{|Q|}$$

    A good approximation balances accuracy against simplicity.

    Worked Example. Approximate $\sqrt{50}$ without a calculator.

    We know $7^2 = 49$, so $\sqrt{50} \approx 7$. For a better estimate, use the linear approximation $\sqrt{a + h} \approx \sqrt{a} + \frac{h}{2\sqrt{a}}$ with $a = 49$ and $h = 1$:

    $$\sqrt{50} \approx 7 + \frac{1}{2 \cdot 7} = 7 + \frac{1}{14} \approx 7.0714$$

    The true value is $\sqrt{50} = 7.07107\ldots$, giving a relative error of

    $$\frac{|7.07107 - 7.0714|}{7.07107} \approx 0.00005 = 0.005\%.$$

    From a rough guess to a result accurate within five thousandths of a percent — two lines of arithmetic.

    Why it works. The linear approximation rests on the tangent-line idea: near a known point, a smooth curve is nearly straight. Formally, the Taylor expansion of $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ around $x = a$ gives $f(a+h) = f(a) + f'(a)\,h + O(h^2)$. When $h$ is small relative to $a$, the dropped $O(h^2)$ terms are tiny, so the first-order term captures almost all of the change. This principle — controlled truncation with bounded error — underlies virtually every approximation method in mathematics.

    Practice Problem. Using $\ln(1+x) \approx x$ for small $x$, estimate $\ln(1.03)$. Then compute the relative error given that $\ln(1.03) = 0.02956\ldots$

    Payoff

    Every measurement you have ever taken, every prediction you have ever trusted, rests on approximation. When a bridge engineer rounds steel tolerances to the nearest millimetre, when a physicist replaces $\sin\theta$ with $\theta$ for small angles, when a programmer halts an iterative solver after fifty steps — each is making a deliberate trade: perfect exactness for something far more valuable, a usable answer now.

    What approximation achieves. Throughout this book, you have built a toolkit of exact definitions, logical structures, and proof techniques. Approximation is the natural endpoint because it is where that toolkit meets the real world. Most equations that describe nature have no closed-form solution. Most datasets are noisy. Most decisions must be made under uncertainty. Approximation is the discipline of replacing an intractable problem with a tractable one while controlling the error — and that control is what separates a guess from mathematics.

    Formally, to approximate a quantity $A$ by $\hat{A}$ means to guarantee that

    $$|A - \hat{A}| \leq \varepsilon$$

    for a chosen tolerance $\varepsilon > 0$, and to understand how $\varepsilon$ shrinks as you invest more computation, data, or refinement.

    Why this is the capstone. Every concept you have studied — algebraic manipulation, function behaviour, limits, convergence — feeds directly into the two core questions of approximation: How close am I? and How do I get closer? Taylor's theorem answers the first for smooth functions; iterative methods like Newton's answer the second. Error bounds give rigour; convergence rates give efficiency. Without the earlier concepts, approximation would be hand-waving. With them, it becomes precise engineering.

    An invitation. Choose one domain that interests you — climate modelling, medical imaging, financial pricing, orbital mechanics — and investigate which approximation method it relies on most. You will find the ideas from this book not at the margins but at the centre, doing the work that makes the impossible merely difficult.
