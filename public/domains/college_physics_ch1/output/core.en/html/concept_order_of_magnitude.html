Order Of Magnitude

How can a physicist quickly compare the size of an atom ($10^{-10}$ m) with the diameter of the Earth ($10^{7}$ m)? Rather than wrestling with long strings of digits, scientists use orders of magnitude — powers of ten that capture the essential scale of a quantity.

Definition. The order of magnitude of a positive number $x$ is the integer $n$ such that

$$10^{n} \leq x < 10^{n+1},$$

or more precisely, $n = \lfloor \log_{10} x \rfloor$. We say two quantities differ by $k$ orders of magnitude when the ratio between them is approximately $10^{k}$.

Worked Example. Estimate the order of magnitude of $x = 7{,}400$.

  • Express $x$ in scientific notation: $7{,}400 = 7.4 \times 10^{3}$.
  • Compute $\log_{10}(7{,}400) = \log_{10}(7.4) + 3 \approx 0.87 + 3 = 3.87$.
  • Take the floor: $n = \lfloor 3.87 \rfloor = 3$.

    • So $7{,}400$ is of order $10^{3}$. Compare this with $x = 52 \approx 5.2 \times 10^{1}$, which has order $10^{1}$. The two numbers differ by about $3 - 1 = 2$ orders of magnitude — a factor of roughly $100$.

    Why it works. The logarithm converts multiplication into addition and maps the positive reals onto a uniform scale where each unit step corresponds to a factor of ten. Because $\log_{10}$ is strictly increasing, the floor of $\log_{10} x$ partitions the positive reals into bands $[10^{n},\, 10^{n+1})$. Every number in one band shares the same leading power of ten, which is exactly the information an order-of-magnitude estimate preserves. This is why quick "back-of-the-envelope" calculations — where factors of $2$ or $3$ are noise — remain reliable: errors smaller than a full power of ten do not change the order.

    Practice Problem. The speed of light is $c \approx 3.0 \times 10^{8}$ m/s and the speed of sound in air is $v \approx 343$ m/s. By how many orders of magnitude does light outpace sound? Verify by computing $\lfloor \log_{10}(c/v) \rfloor$.
