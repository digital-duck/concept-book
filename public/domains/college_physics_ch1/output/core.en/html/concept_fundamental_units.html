🍃 Fundamental Units

🍃 Fundamental Units

When you buy carpet, the salesperson quotes a price per square meter. When you check your car's fuel efficiency, you see kilometers per liter. These composite quantities feel different from a raw length or a raw time — and that intuition points to something deep: there exists a small set of irreducible building blocks from which every other measurable quantity is constructed.

Definition. A fundamental unit (or base unit) is a unit of measurement that is defined independently, not derived from any combination of other units. In the International System (SI), there are exactly seven:

$$\text{meter (m)},\quad \text{kilogram (kg)},\quad \text{second (s)},\quad \text{ampere (A)},\quad \text{kelvin (K)},\quad \text{mole (mol)},\quad \text{candela (cd)}$$

Every other physical quantity — derived units — is expressed as a product of powers of these seven:

$$[Q] = \mathrm{m}^{a}\,\mathrm{kg}^{b}\,\mathrm{s}^{c}\,\mathrm{A}^{d}\,\mathrm{K}^{e}\,\mathrm{mol}^{f}\,\mathrm{cd}^{g}, \qquad a,b,\ldots,g \in \mathbb{Z}.$$

Worked example. What is the unit of force? Newton's second law states $F = ma$. Mass has dimension $\mathrm{kg}$ and acceleration has dimension $\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$, so:

$$[F] = \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}} = \mathrm{kg\,m\,s^{-2}} =: \mathrm{N}.$$

The newton is not fundamental — it collapses into three base units with specific exponents $(b=1,\,a=1,\,c=-2)$.

Why it's true. Dimensional analysis is essentially linear algebra over the integers. Each physical quantity is a vector in $\mathbb{Z}^{7}$, and the seven fundamental units form a basis for that space. Because they are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Z}$, every derived quantity has a unique dimensional signature — which is why a dimensional check can catch errors in any formula.

Practice problem. Express the SI unit of pressure (force per area) entirely in fundamental units. What is the exponent of $\mathrm{s}$?
