🌱 Physical Quantity

🌱 Physical Quantity

When a doctor says your temperature is 38.5°C or a pilot announces cruising altitude at 35,000 feet, they are doing something mathematically precise: they are attaching a number to a measurable property of the world. This pairing of measurement and unit is the foundation of all quantitative science.

Definition. A physical quantity is an ordered pair $(n, [u])$, where $n \in \mathbb{R}$ is the numerical value and $[u]$ denotes a unit of measurement drawn from an agreed system (e.g., SI). Two physical quantities are equal if and only if they represent the same magnitude under any consistent unit conversion:

$$Q = n \cdot [u]$$

A quantity without a unit is called dimensionless (e.g., the refractive index $n = 1.5$); all other quantities belong to a dimension such as length $\mathsf{L}$, mass $\mathsf{M}$, or time $\mathsf{T}$.

Worked example. Suppose a car travels $d = 90 \text{ km}$ in $t = 1.5 \text{ h}$. Its average speed is:

$$v = \frac{d}{t} = \frac{90 \text{ km}}{1.5 \text{ h}} = 60 \; \frac{\text{km}}{\text{h}}$$

Converting to SI units ($1 \text{ km} = 1000 \text{ m}$, $1 \text{ h} = 3600 \text{ s}$):

$$v = 60 \times \frac{1000}{3600} \; \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}} \approx 16.7 \; \text{m s}^{-1}$$

The numerical value changed, but the physical quantity — the speed of the car — did not.

Why this matters. The rule $Q = n \cdot [u]$ guarantees that equations involving physical quantities are dimensionally homogeneous: every term on both sides must share the same dimension. This constraint eliminates entire classes of errors before any arithmetic begins, and underpins dimensional analysis as a lightweight proof technique.

Practice problem. A 5 kg block accelerates at $2 \; \text{m s}^{-2}$. Using Newton's second law $F = ma$, express the force in Newtons and verify that $1 \; \text{N} = 1 \; \text{kg} \cdot \text{m} \cdot \text{s}^{-2}$ by expanding the dimensions on both sides.
