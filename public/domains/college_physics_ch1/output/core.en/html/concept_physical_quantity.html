🌱 Physical Quantity

🌱 Physical Quantity

When a nurse checks your temperature and says "38.5 degrees Celsius," she is not just saying a number — she is communicating a measurement with a unit. Strip away either piece and the statement becomes meaningless: "38.5" alone tells us nothing, and "Celsius" alone tells us nothing. This pairing of number and unit is the foundation of all quantitative science.

Definition. A physical quantity is an attribute of a physical phenomenon that can be expressed as the product of a numerical value and a unit of measurement:

$$Q = \{Q\} \cdot [Q]$$

where $\{Q\}$ denotes the numerical value and $[Q]$ denotes the unit. Two quantities are equal if and only if both their numerical values agree and their units are compatible.

Worked example. Suppose a car travels a distance $d = 90 \text{ km}$ in a time $t = 1.5 \text{ h}$. Its average speed is:

$$v = \frac{d}{t} = \frac{90 \text{ km}}{1.5 \text{ h}} = 60 \;\frac{\text{km}}{\text{h}}$$

Notice that the units divide just like numbers: $\text{km} / \text{h}$ is itself a valid unit. Carrying units through every step of a calculation is the primary tool for catching arithmetic errors in physics.

Why it works. Physical laws are relationships between quantities, not bare numbers. Because any valid law must hold regardless of which unit system we choose (SI, CGS, imperial), both sides of every equation must carry the same units. This requirement turns unit tracking from bookkeeping into a consistency check: if the units on the left and right don't match, the equation cannot be correct.

Practice problem. A cyclist rides $d = 15 \text{ km}$ at a constant speed of $v = 25 \;\text{km/h}$. Write the time $t$ as a physical quantity with appropriate units and verify that the units on both sides of $t = d/v$ are consistent.
