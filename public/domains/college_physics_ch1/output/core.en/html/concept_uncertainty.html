🍃 Uncertainty

When a weather app says there is a 70% chance of rain tomorrow, it is not admitting ignorance — it is *quantifying* ignorance. That number encodes everything the model knows and everything it does not. Uncertainty is not the absence of knowledge; it is knowledge about the limits of knowledge.

Definition. Given a discrete random variable $X$ taking values in $\{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}$ with probability mass function $p(x_i) = P(X = x_i)$, the Shannon entropy measures the uncertainty of $X$:

$$H(X) = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} p(x_i) \log_2 p(x_i)$$

$H(X)$ is measured in *bits*. A larger value means more uncertainty; knowing the outcome gives you more information.

Worked example. Suppose you flip a fair coin: $P(\text{heads}) = P(\text{tails}) = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Then

$$H(X) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\log_2\tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{2}\log_2\tfrac{1}{2} = -2\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot(-1)\right) = 1 \text{ bit}.$$

Now suppose the coin is biased: $P(\text{heads}) = 0.9$, $P(\text{tails}) = 0.1$. Then

$$H(X) = -0.9\log_2 0.9 - 0.1\log_2 0.1 \approx 0.137 + 0.332 = 0.469 \text{ bits}.$$

Less uncertainty — before the flip, you already have a strong prior guess.

Why it's true. Entropy is maximized when all outcomes are equally likely, because no single outcome is more "expected" than another. As one probability approaches 1, $p \log_2 p \to 0$, and the sum collapses toward zero — perfect certainty carries zero bits of surprise.

Practice problem. A six-sided die is loaded so that face 6 appears with probability $\tfrac{1}{2}$, and each of the other five faces appears with probability $\tfrac{1}{10}$. Compute $H(X)$ and compare it to the entropy of a fair die ($\approx 2.585$ bits). What does the difference tell you?
