Uncertainty

Every measurement carries a story of its own limitations. When a pharmacist weighs a dose of medication, the reading might show 250 mg — but is it exactly 250.000… mg? The true value hides behind the finite precision of every instrument, and uncertainty is how we quantify that gap.

Definition. The uncertainty $\delta x$ of a measured quantity $x$ represents the range within which the true value is reasonably expected to lie. A measurement is reported as

$$x_{\text{best}} \pm \delta x,$$

where $x_{\text{best}}$ is the best estimate (often the mean of repeated trials) and $\delta x$ bounds the probable deviation from that estimate.

Worked Example. A student measures the length of a table five times, obtaining values (in cm): $120.2,\; 120.4,\; 120.1,\; 120.3,\; 120.5$. The best estimate is the mean:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{120.2 + 120.4 + 120.1 + 120.3 + 120.5}{5} = 120.3 \;\text{cm}.$$

One common estimate of uncertainty is the standard deviation of the mean. First, the sample standard deviation:

$$s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{N-1}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.01 + 0.01 + 0.04 + 0.00 + 0.04}{4}} = \sqrt{0.025} \approx 0.16 \;\text{cm}.$$

The standard uncertainty in the mean is $\delta x = \dfrac{s}{\sqrt{N}} = \dfrac{0.16}{\sqrt{5}} \approx 0.07\;\text{cm}.$ We report the length as $120.30 \pm 0.07\;\text{cm}.$

Why it's true. Averaging reduces random scatter: each independent measurement contributes new information, and the central limit theorem guarantees that the distribution of the mean tightens by a factor of $1/\sqrt{N}$. This is why repeating a measurement genuinely improves precision — not by changing the instrument, but by letting statistics work in your favor.

Practice Problem. A stopwatch records five lap times (in seconds): $48.2,\; 47.9,\; 48.4,\; 48.1,\; 48.0$. Compute the mean, the sample standard deviation, and the uncertainty in the mean. Report the result in the form $\bar{t} \pm \delta t$.
