Order Of Magnitude

Order Of Magnitude

Everyday example. How far is it to the grocery store? Maybe 2 kilometres. How far is it to the Moon? About 384,000 kilometres. You don't need the exact numbers to feel the enormous gap between them — one is a short walk, the other is a space voyage. That "gap" is what scientists call a difference in order of magnitude.

Picture this. Imagine a ruler where each mark is ten times bigger than the last: $1, 10, 100, 1{,}000, 10{,}000, \dots$ Every time you step one mark to the right, you have jumped one order of magnitude. The distance to the Moon ($\approx 10^{5.6}$ km) is roughly five orders of magnitude larger than a trip to the store ($\approx 10^{0.3}$ km), because $10^{5.6} / 10^{0.3} \approx 10^{5}$.

Simple rule. To find the order of magnitude of a number, ask: *"What power of ten is closest?"* We write this as $10^n$. A number around $500$ is closest to $10^{2.7}$, so its order of magnitude is roughly $3$ (we often round to the nearest integer). Two quantities are "the same order of magnitude" when their ratio is less than $10$.

Try it yourself. A housefly weighs about $0.012$ grams and a house cat about $4{,}000$ grams. How many orders of magnitude apart are they? Hint: divide, then count the powers of ten.
