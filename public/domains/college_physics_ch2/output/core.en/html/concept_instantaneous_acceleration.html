Instantaneous Acceleration

A car merging onto a highway doesn't speed up at a perfectly steady rate — the driver presses the pedal harder at some moments than others. To capture how the velocity is changing right now, at a single instant, we need instantaneous acceleration.

Definition. If $v(t)$ is the velocity of an object at time $t$, the instantaneous acceleration is the derivative of velocity with respect to time:

$$a(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{v(t + \Delta t) - v(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dv}{dt}.$$

Because velocity is itself the derivative of position $x(t)$, instantaneous acceleration is equivalently the second derivative of position:

$$a(t) = \frac{d^2 x}{dt^2}.$$

The units are $\mathrm{m/s^2}$ in SI. A positive value means the velocity is increasing; a negative value (sometimes called deceleration) means it is decreasing.

Worked example. A particle moves along a line with position $x(t) = 3t^3 - 2t + 1$ (metres, with $t$ in seconds). Find the instantaneous acceleration at $t = 2\;\mathrm{s}$.

  • Differentiate once: $v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 9t^2 - 2$.
  • Differentiate again: $a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = 18t$.
  • Evaluate: $a(2) = 18 \times 2 = 36\;\mathrm{m/s^2}$.

    • Why it's true. Average acceleration $\bar{a} = \Delta v / \Delta t$ tells you how velocity changes over an interval, but it hides the variation within that interval. By shrinking $\Delta t$ to zero, the limit isolates the rate of change at a single moment — precisely the idea behind the derivative. This is the same passage from "average" to "instantaneous" that connects average velocity to instantaneous velocity, extended one derivative further.

    Practice problem. An object has velocity $v(t) = 4t^2 - 5t + 3\;\mathrm{m/s}$. Determine the instantaneous acceleration at $t = 3\;\mathrm{s}$, and state whether the object is speeding up or slowing down at that moment. (Hint: check the signs of both $v(3)$ and $a(3)$.)
