Concept Book: Instantaneous Velocity

Instantaneous Velocity

A car's speedometer doesn't tell you how fast you traveled over your entire trip — it tells you how fast you're moving *right now*. That reading is your instantaneous velocity: the velocity of an object at a single moment in time.

Definition. The instantaneous velocity $v(t)$ at time $t$ is the limit of the average velocity as the time interval shrinks to zero:

$$v(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{x(t + \Delta t) - x(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt}$$

where $x(t)$ is the position as a function of time. Instantaneous velocity is the derivative of position with respect to time.

Worked Example. Suppose a ball's position is given by $x(t) = 3t^2 + 2t$, measured in meters with $t$ in seconds. Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 4\,\text{s}$.

We compute the derivative:

$$v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t + 2$$

Evaluating at $t = 4$:

$$v(4) = 6(4) + 2 = 26\,\text{m/s}$$

At exactly four seconds, the ball is moving at $26\,\text{m/s}$.

Why it's true. Average velocity $\bar{v} = \Delta x / \Delta t$ describes motion over a finite interval, but motion can change within that interval. By taking $\Delta t$ progressively smaller — say from $1\,\text{s}$ to $0.1\,\text{s}$ to $0.001\,\text{s}$ — the average velocity converges to a single value. The limit process eliminates the ambiguity of choosing an interval, giving the unique velocity at one instant. This is precisely what differentiation formalizes: replacing a secant line on the position–time graph with the tangent line at a point. The slope of that tangent line is $v(t)$.

Practice Problem. A particle moves along the $x$-axis with position $x(t) = t^3 - 5t + 1$ (meters, seconds). Find its instantaneous velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$, and determine whether the particle is moving in the positive or negative $x$-direction at that moment.
