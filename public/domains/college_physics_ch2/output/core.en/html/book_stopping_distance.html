Coordinate System

Every map you have ever used works because someone agreed on a reference frame — a starting point and a pair of directions. Without that agreement, "three blocks east" means nothing. A coordinate system does the same thing for mathematics: it gives us a precise language to describe where things are.

Definition. A coordinate system in the plane is a pair of perpendicular number lines, called axes, that intersect at a common zero point called the origin $O$. The horizontal axis is typically labeled $x$ and the vertical axis $y$. Every point $P$ in the plane is uniquely identified by an ordered pair $(a, b)$, where $a$ is the signed distance from the $y$-axis and $b$ is the signed distance from the $x$-axis. This framework is called the Cartesian coordinate system, after René Descartes.

The axes divide the plane into four quadrants, numbered counterclockwise:

QuadrantSign of $x$Sign of $y$
I$+$$+$
II$-$$+$
III$-$$-$
IV$+$$-$

Worked example. Plot the point $P = (-3, 4)$ and determine its quadrant. Starting at the origin, move $3$ units left along the $x$-axis (since $-3 < 0$) and then $4$ units up parallel to the $y$-axis (since $4 > 0$). Because $x < 0$ and $y > 0$, the point lies in Quadrant II.

Why it works. The key property is uniqueness: every point corresponds to exactly one ordered pair, and every ordered pair corresponds to exactly one point. This one-to-one correspondence is what lets us translate geometric problems — distances, intersections, slopes — into algebraic equations, and vice versa. It is the bridge between geometry and algebra.

Practice problem. A triangle has vertices $A = (1, 2)$, $B = (5, 2)$, and $C = (1, 6)$. In which quadrant does each vertex lie? What is the length of side $AB$?

Position

Every navigation app on your phone answers one fundamental question: where are you right now? Physics formalizes this idea with the concept of position — the precise specification of an object's location relative to a chosen reference point.

Definition. The position of a particle in one dimension is written as $x$, representing a signed distance along a chosen axis from a designated origin. The sign encodes direction: positive values lie one way from the origin, negative values the other. Position carries units of length (meters in SI).

Crucially, position is relative. It has no meaning without a reference point — an origin and an axis direction. Change the origin, and every position value changes with it, even though the physical situation is identical.

Worked Example. A student stands $3\,\text{m}$ east of a lamppost. If we place the origin at the lamppost with the positive $x$-axis pointing east, her position is

$$x = +3\,\text{m}.$$

If we instead place the origin at a bench $5\,\text{m}$ east of the lamppost, the same student's position becomes

$$x' = 3\,\text{m} - 5\,\text{m} = -2\,\text{m}.$$

Nothing physical changed — only the coordinate system. This illustrates why stating your reference point is essential.

Why it's true. Position must be a signed quantity because location inherently involves direction from the origin, not just distance. An unsigned distance $d = |x|$ would make the student at $+3\,\text{m}$ indistinguishable from one at $-3\,\text{m}$, collapsing two distinct physical locations into one number. The sign preserves the full geometric information: not only how far from the origin, but which side.

Practice Problem. A car sits $12\,\text{m}$ west of a traffic light. Define east as positive and place the origin at the light. Write the car's position. Then rewrite it using an origin placed $4\,\text{m}$ west of the light. Confirm that the difference between the car's position and the light's position is the same in both coordinate systems.

Time

Every motion you observe — a ball arcing through the air, a planet sweeping around the Sun — unfolds over some duration. Physics needs a way to quantify that duration precisely, and that quantity is time.

Definition

Time is a fundamental scalar quantity that measures the progression of events from past to future. In the International System of Units (SI), the base unit of time is the second (s), defined by the frequency of radiation emitted by a cesium-133 atom. We denote an instant of time by $t$ and a duration — an interval between two instants — by

$$\Delta t = t_f - t_i,$$

where $t_i$ is the initial instant and $t_f$ is the final instant. Because $\Delta t$ represents a duration, we require $\Delta t \geq 0$ for physically meaningful intervals measured in the forward direction.

Worked Example

A sprinter crosses the start line at $t_i = 0.00\;\text{s}$ and the finish line at $t_f = 9.58\;\text{s}$. What is the elapsed time?

$$\Delta t = t_f - t_i = 9.58\;\text{s} - 0.00\;\text{s} = 9.58\;\text{s}.$$

This single number — 9.58 seconds — captures everything we need to know about how long the run lasted. Whenever we later build rates or compare motions, $\Delta t$ will be the foundation.

Why It Matters

Time enters physics as an independent variable: we express position and all quantities derived from it as functions of $t$. This is not merely convention. Without a well-defined, uniformly progressing time coordinate, we would have no way to describe how things change — only that they changed. Defining $\Delta t$ carefully ensures that every rate we construct from it will be unambiguous, and that comparing two different motions is always grounded in the same universal clock.

Practice Problem

A traffic light turns green at $t_i = 4.50\;\text{s}$ on a stopwatch and turns yellow at $t_f = 34.50\;\text{s}$. What is the duration of the green phase?

$$\Delta t = t_f - t_i = 34.50\;\text{s} - 4.50\;\text{s} = \;?$$

Compute $\Delta t$ and express your answer in seconds.

Vector

Every GPS coordinate, every force acting on a bridge, every pixel's color on your screen encodes more than a single number — it carries both magnitude and direction, or multiple components at once. The mathematical object that captures this idea is the vector.

A vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is an ordered list of $n$ real numbers, written as

$$\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^n.$$

We call each $v_i$ a component. Two vectors are equal if and only if all their corresponding components are equal. Vectors support two fundamental operations: addition $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}$, performed component-wise, and scalar multiplication $c\mathbf{v}$, which scales every component by $c \in \mathbb{R}$.

Worked example. Let $\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Then

$$\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 3+2 \\ -1+4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad 2\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

Geometrically, $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}$ is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$, and $2\mathbf{u}$ points in the same direction as $\mathbf{u}$ but twice as far from the origin.

Why these operations matter. Addition and scalar multiplication satisfy eight axioms — commutativity, associativity, distributivity, and the existence of a zero vector $\mathbf{0}$ and additive inverses — that together make $\mathbb{R}^n$ a vector space. These axioms are not arbitrary rules; they guarantee that any linear combination $c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k$ behaves predictably regardless of how we group or reorder terms. This algebraic stability is what allows us to solve systems of equations, describe physical motion, and build higher structures like matrices and transformations on a trustworthy foundation.

Practice problem. Given $\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$, compute $3\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b}$ and verify that the result lies in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Displacement

When you walk from your front door to the mailbox and back, you have covered some distance — but you end up exactly where you started. A physicist would say your displacement is zero. Understanding why requires separating how far you traveled from where you ended up relative to where you began.

Definition. Displacement is the change in position of an object. For an object moving from position $x_i$ to position $x_f$, the displacement is

$$\Delta x = x_f - x_i.$$

Displacement is a vector quantity: it carries both magnitude and direction. In one dimension we encode direction with a sign — positive for one way, negative for the other. In higher dimensions, displacement becomes a vector $\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i$.

Worked Example. A cyclist starts at position $x_i = 2\;\text{m}$, rides to $x = 8\;\text{m}$, then turns around and stops at $x_f = 5\;\text{m}$. Find the displacement and the total distance traveled.

  • Displacement: $\Delta x = x_f - x_i = 5 - 2 = +3\;\text{m}$. The cyclist ends up $3\;\text{m}$ in the positive direction from the start.
  • Distance: The cyclist travels $|8 - 2| + |5 - 8| = 6 + 3 = 9\;\text{m}$.

    • Notice that displacement depends only on the starting and ending positions, while distance accumulates every meter of the path.

    Why it's true. Displacement ignores the winding details of a journey because position is a state variable — it depends only on where you are, not how you got there. The difference of two states erases intermediate history. This property makes displacement fundamental in physics: velocity, for instance, is defined as the rate of change of displacement, not distance.

    Practice Problem. A dog runs $12\;\text{m}$ east, then $5\;\text{m}$ north. What is the magnitude of the dog's displacement? (Hint: sketch the path and apply the Pythagorean theorem.)

    Elapsed Time

    Every morning you glance at the clock when you leave home and again when you arrive at school. The difference between those two readings — that's elapsed time. It measures how much time has passed between two events, and it is one of the most practical quantities in both everyday life and physics.

    Definition. If an event begins at time $t_1$ and ends at time $t_2$, the elapsed time is

    $$\Delta t = t_2 - t_1$$

    where $\Delta t \geq 0$. The Greek letter $\Delta$ (delta) denotes a change in a quantity. Elapsed time is always measured as a final reading minus an initial reading, so it is independent of when we choose to start our clock — only the interval matters.

    Worked Example. A runner crosses the start line at $t_1 = 12\!:\!15\!:\!30$ (hours : minutes : seconds) and the finish line at $t_2 = 12\!:\!42\!:\!10$. Find the elapsed time.

    $$\Delta t = 12\!:\!42\!:\!10 - 12\!:\!15\!:\!30$$

    Subtract each unit: $42 - 15 = 27$ minutes, $10 - 30 = -20$ seconds. Because the seconds are negative, borrow one minute:

    $$\Delta t = 26 \text{ min } 40 \text{ s} = 1600 \text{ s}$$

    Converting to a single unit (seconds) is often useful in calculations: $26 \times 60 + 40 = 1600\;\text{s}$.

    Why it's true. Elapsed time inherits the properties of subtraction on the real number line. Because $t_2 \geq t_1$ for any event that ends after it starts, $\Delta t$ is non-negative. It is also translation-invariant: shifting both $t_1$ and $t_2$ by the same constant $c$ leaves $\Delta t$ unchanged, since $(t_2 + c) - (t_1 + c) = t_2 - t_1$. This is why two stopwatches started at different moments still agree on the duration of the same race.

    Practice Problem. A chemistry experiment begins at $t_1 = 9\!:\!07\!:\!45$ and ends at $t_2 = 9\!:\!50\!:\!20$. Calculate $\Delta t$ in seconds.

    Average Velocity

    A car drives 120 km north in 2 hours, then 60 km south in 1 hour. It has covered 180 km total, yet it ends up only 60 km from where it started. To describe how its position changed over time — not just how far the odometer reads — we need average velocity.

    Definition. Average velocity is the displacement divided by the elapsed time:

    $$\vec{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{x}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{x}_f - \vec{x}_i}{t_f - t_i}$$

    Because displacement $\Delta \vec{x}$ is a vector (it has direction), average velocity is also a vector. This distinguishes it from average speed, which uses the total distance traveled (a scalar) in the numerator.

    Worked example. A jogger runs east along a straight path: at $t = 0$ she is at position $x_i = 2\;\text{m}$, and at $t = 5\;\text{s}$ she reaches $x_f = 17\;\text{m}$.

    $$v_{\text{avg}} = \frac{17\;\text{m} - 2\;\text{m}}{5\;\text{s} - 0\;\text{s}} = \frac{15\;\text{m}}{5\;\text{s}} = 3\;\text{m/s}\;\text{(east)}$$

    The positive sign confirms motion in the positive (east) direction.

    Why it works. Average velocity captures the net effect of all motion during an interval. Geometrically, if you plot position versus time, $v_{\text{avg}}$ is the slope of the straight line connecting the initial and final points on the curve. This slope tells you the single constant velocity that would carry you from $\vec{x}_i$ to $\vec{x}_f$ in exactly $\Delta t$. Return to the opening example: displacement is 60 km north in 3 hours, so $v_{\text{avg}} = 20\;\text{km/h north}$ — even though the car's speedometer read higher values along the way. Average velocity filters out the back-and-forth and reports only the net rate of position change.

    Practice problem. A cyclist rides along a straight road. At $t = 1\;\text{s}$ her position is $x = 5\;\text{m}$; at $t = 4\;\text{s}$ her position is $x = -7\;\text{m}$. Calculate her average velocity. What does the sign tell you about her direction of motion?

    Average Acceleration

    A car merging onto a highway speeds up from 30 km/h to 90 km/h over 8 seconds. A cyclist braking at a red light slows from 20 km/h to rest in 4 seconds. In both cases, velocity changes over time — and the single number that captures how quickly that change happens is average acceleration.

    Definition. Average acceleration is the change in velocity divided by the elapsed time:

    $$\vec{a}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{t_f - t_i}$$

    Because velocity is a vector, acceleration is also a vector. Its SI unit is metres per second squared ($\text{m/s}^2$). A positive value along a chosen axis means speeding up in that direction; a negative value means slowing down (or speeding up in the opposite direction).

    Worked example. A train traveling east at $v_i = 10\;\text{m/s}$ accelerates to $v_f = 25\;\text{m/s}$ over $\Delta t = 6.0\;\text{s}$. Find its average acceleration.

    $$a_{\text{avg}} = \frac{25\;\text{m/s} - 10\;\text{m/s}}{6.0\;\text{s}} = \frac{15\;\text{m/s}}{6.0\;\text{s}} = 2.5\;\text{m/s}^2 \text{ (east)}$$

    The train's velocity increases by $2.5\;\text{m/s}$ every second, on average.

    Why it's true. Average acceleration is the constant acceleration that would produce the same total velocity change in the same time interval. The actual acceleration may have varied from moment to moment, but $\vec{a}_{\text{avg}}$ guarantees that $\vec{v}_i + \vec{a}_{\text{avg}}\,\Delta t = \vec{v}_f$. You can verify this with the train: $10\;\text{m/s} + (2.5\;\text{m/s}^2)(6.0\;\text{s}) = 25\;\text{m/s}$, exactly the final velocity. This makes average acceleration a reliable single-number summary of how velocity evolved over any interval, no matter how complicated the motion in between.

    Practice problem. A runner heading north at $8.0\;\text{m/s}$ comes to a complete stop in $2.5\;\text{s}$. What is her average acceleration? State its magnitude and direction, and explain what the negative sign (relative to north) tells you physically.

    Instantaneous Acceleration

    A car merging onto a highway doesn't speed up at a perfectly steady rate — the driver presses the pedal harder at some moments than others. To capture how the velocity is changing right now, at a single instant, we need instantaneous acceleration.

    Definition. If $v(t)$ is the velocity of an object at time $t$, the instantaneous acceleration is the derivative of velocity with respect to time:

    $$a(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{v(t + \Delta t) - v(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dv}{dt}.$$

    Because velocity is itself the derivative of position $x(t)$, instantaneous acceleration is equivalently the second derivative of position:

    $$a(t) = \frac{d^2 x}{dt^2}.$$

    The units are $\mathrm{m/s^2}$ in SI. A positive value means the velocity is increasing; a negative value (sometimes called deceleration) means it is decreasing.

    Worked example. A particle moves along a line with position $x(t) = 3t^3 - 2t + 1$ (metres, with $t$ in seconds). Find the instantaneous acceleration at $t = 2\;\mathrm{s}$.

  • Differentiate once: $v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 9t^2 - 2$.
  • Differentiate again: $a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = 18t$.
  • Evaluate: $a(2) = 18 \times 2 = 36\;\mathrm{m/s^2}$.

    • Why it's true. Average acceleration $\bar{a} = \Delta v / \Delta t$ tells you how velocity changes over an interval, but it hides the variation within that interval. By shrinking $\Delta t$ to zero, the limit isolates the rate of change at a single moment — precisely the idea behind the derivative. This is the same passage from "average" to "instantaneous" that connects average velocity to instantaneous velocity, extended one derivative further.

    Practice problem. An object has velocity $v(t) = 4t^2 - 5t + 3\;\mathrm{m/s}$. Determine the instantaneous acceleration at $t = 3\;\mathrm{s}$, and state whether the object is speeding up or slowing down at that moment. (Hint: check the signs of both $v(3)$ and $a(3)$.)

    Concept Book: Instantaneous Velocity

    Instantaneous Velocity

    A car's speedometer doesn't tell you how fast you traveled over your entire trip — it tells you how fast you're moving right now. That reading is your instantaneous velocity: the velocity of an object at a single moment in time.

    Definition. The instantaneous velocity $v(t)$ at time $t$ is the limit of the average velocity as the time interval shrinks to zero:

    $$v(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{x(t + \Delta t) - x(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt}$$

    where $x(t)$ is the position as a function of time. Instantaneous velocity is the derivative of position with respect to time.

    Worked Example. Suppose a ball's position is given by $x(t) = 3t^2 + 2t$, measured in meters with $t$ in seconds. Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 4\,\text{s}$.

    We compute the derivative:

    $$v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t + 2$$

    Evaluating at $t = 4$:

    $$v(4) = 6(4) + 2 = 26\,\text{m/s}$$

    At exactly four seconds, the ball is moving at $26\,\text{m/s}$.

    Why it's true. Average velocity $\bar{v} = \Delta x / \Delta t$ describes motion over a finite interval, but motion can change within that interval. By taking $\Delta t$ progressively smaller — say from $1\,\text{s}$ to $0.1\,\text{s}$ to $0.001\,\text{s}$ — the average velocity converges to a single value. The limit process eliminates the ambiguity of choosing an interval, giving the unique velocity at one instant. This is precisely what differentiation formalizes: replacing a secant line on the position–time graph with the tangent line at a point. The slope of that tangent line is $v(t)$.

    Practice Problem. A particle moves along the $x$-axis with position $x(t) = t^3 - 5t + 1$ (meters, seconds). Find its instantaneous velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$, and determine whether the particle is moving in the positive or negative $x$-direction at that moment.

    Constant Acceleration

    Drop a ball from your hand. It doesn't just move — it moves faster every instant, gaining roughly $9.8\;\text{m/s}$ of speed each second. That steady increase is constant acceleration: the velocity changes by the same amount in every equal time interval.

    Definition. An object has constant acceleration $a$ when its velocity $v$ changes linearly with time:

    $$a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \text{const.}$$

    From this single condition, two kinematic equations follow. Starting from position $x_0$ with initial velocity $v_0$ at $t = 0$:

    $$v(t) = v_0 + at$$ $$x(t) = x_0 + v_0\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}\,a\,t^2$$

    Worked Example. A car accelerates from rest ($v_0 = 0$) at $a = 3\;\text{m/s}^2$. How far does it travel in $4\;\text{s}$?

    $$x = 0 + 0\cdot4 + \tfrac{1}{2}(3)(4)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 3\cdot 16 = 24\;\text{m}$$

    Its final speed is $v = 0 + 3(4) = 12\;\text{m/s}$.

    Why it's true. If acceleration is constant, velocity is a linear function of time, so $v(t) = v_0 + at$. Position is the integral of velocity:

    $$x(t) = x_0 + \int_0^t (v_0 + a\,t')\,dt' = x_0 + v_0\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}\,a\,t^2$$

    The $\tfrac{1}{2}$ arises because the velocity contribution grows linearly — geometrically, it is the area of a triangle sitting on top of a rectangle in a $v$-vs-$t$ graph. This derivation requires only that $a$ does not change; the moment acceleration varies, these neat formulas no longer apply directly.

    Practice Problem. A stone is thrown straight upward with $v_0 = 15\;\text{m/s}$. Taking $a = -9.8\;\text{m/s}^2$ (directed downward), find the time it takes to reach its highest point and the maximum height above the launch position.

    Deceleration

    A car approaching a red light doesn't stop instantly — the driver applies the brakes, and the vehicle gradually slows down. This everyday experience captures the essence of deceleration: acceleration that acts opposite to the direction of motion, reducing an object's speed over time.

    Definition. Deceleration occurs when an object's acceleration vector points opposite to its velocity vector, causing the object's speed to decrease. If an object moves in the positive direction with velocity $v > 0$, deceleration corresponds to a negative acceleration $a < 0$. The relationship between velocity, acceleration, and time remains governed by the kinematic equation:

    $$v_f = v_i + a\,t$$

    where $v_i$ is the initial velocity, $v_f$ is the final velocity, $a$ is the (negative) acceleration, and $t$ is the elapsed time.

    Worked Example. A cyclist traveling at $v_i = 12\;\text{m/s}$ applies the brakes and comes to rest in $t = 4\;\text{s}$. Find the deceleration.

    Setting $v_f = 0$ and solving:

    $$a = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t} = \frac{0 - 12}{4} = -3\;\text{m/s}^2$$

    The acceleration is $-3\;\text{m/s}^2$, so the cyclist decelerates at a magnitude of $3\;\text{m/s}^2$. Each second, the cyclist's speed drops by $3\;\text{m/s}$ — from $12$ to $9$ to $6$ to $3$ to $0$.

    Why it's true. Newton's second law, $F = ma$, tells us that a net force opposite to motion produces a negative acceleration. The brakes exert a friction force against the wheel's rotation, and this opposing force is what creates the deceleration. The kinematic equation $v_f = v_i + at$ follows directly from integrating constant acceleration over time, so a negative $a$ necessarily yields a decreasing speed when $v_i > 0$.

    Practice Problem. A train moving at $v_i = 30\;\text{m/s}$ decelerates uniformly at $a = -2\;\text{m/s}^2$. How long does it take to stop, and what distance does it cover while braking? Use $v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2a\,d$ for the distance.

    Kinematic Equations

    A car merging onto a highway accelerates steadily from 20 m/s to 30 m/s over 5 seconds. How far does it travel in that time? Questions like this — connecting position, velocity, acceleration, and time — arise constantly in physics and engineering. The kinematic equations give us a compact toolkit to answer them.

    Definition. For motion with constant acceleration $a$, the four kinematic equations relate displacement $\Delta x$, initial velocity $v_0$, final velocity $v$, and elapsed time $t$:

    $$v = v_0 + at$$ $$\Delta x = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$ $$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x$$ $$\Delta x = \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)\,t$$

    Each equation omits exactly one of the five quantities $\{v, v_0, a, \Delta x, t\}$, so you choose the equation that matches the three knowns and one unknown in your problem.

    Worked example. Returning to the merging car: $v_0 = 20$ m/s, $v = 30$ m/s, $t = 5$ s. We want $\Delta x$. The fourth equation needs no acceleration:

    $$\Delta x = \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)\,t = \tfrac{1}{2}(20 + 30)(5) = 125 \text{ m}.$$

    Why it's true. Velocity under constant acceleration is a linear function of time: $v(t) = v_0 + at$. Displacement is the area under the $v$-$t$ graph. That area is a trapezoid with parallel sides $v_0$ and $v$ and width $t$, giving $\Delta x = \frac{1}{2}(v_0+v)t$. Substituting $v = v_0 + at$ into this yields $\Delta x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2$. Eliminating $t$ instead produces $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x$. All four equations are algebraic rearrangements of the same linear velocity assumption — no new physics is introduced.

    Practice problem. A ball is thrown straight upward with $v_0 = 15$ m/s. Taking $a = -9.8\;\text{m/s}^2$, find the maximum height it reaches. (Hint: at the peak, $v = 0$.)

    Stopping Distance

    A car travels at highway speed when the driver spots debris ahead. From the moment they hit the brakes, how far does the car travel before it comes to rest? This quantity — the stopping distance — determines safe following gaps, road-sign placement, and speed-limit policy.

    Definition. Stopping distance is the displacement $d$ a vehicle covers while decelerating from initial speed $v_0$ to rest ($v = 0$) under constant braking force. Applying the kinematic equation $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,d$ with $v = 0$ and braking deceleration $a < 0$, we solve for

    $$d = \frac{v_0^2}{2\,|a|}$$

    where $|a|$ is the magnitude of the deceleration. Notice that $d$ grows with the square of the initial speed: doubling your speed quadruples your stopping distance.

    Worked Example. A car brakes from $v_0 = 30\;\text{m/s}$ (about 108 km/h) with a deceleration of $|a| = 8\;\text{m/s}^2$. Find the stopping distance.

    $$d = \frac{(30)^2}{2 \times 8} = \frac{900}{16} = 56.25\;\text{m}$$

    The car needs over 56 meters — roughly half a football field — to stop.

    Why It's True. The work-energy theorem provides the justification. The net work done by friction equals the change in kinetic energy:

    $$F_{\text{brake}} \cdot d = \tfrac{1}{2}\,m\,v_0^2$$

    Since $F_{\text{brake}} = m\,|a|$ by Newton's second law, substituting gives

    $$m\,|a|\,d = \tfrac{1}{2}\,m\,v_0^2 \implies d = \frac{v_0^2}{2\,|a|}$$

    The mass cancels, confirming that stopping distance depends only on speed and deceleration — not on how heavy the vehicle is (assuming the same braking deceleration).

    Practice Problem. A cyclist traveling at $v_0 = 12\;\text{m/s}$ applies brakes that produce $|a| = 4\;\text{m/s}^2$. Calculate the stopping distance, then predict how far the cyclist would need if they were moving at $24\;\text{m/s}$ instead. Verify the quadratic relationship.

    Payoff

    Every time a driver sees a hazard and hits the brakes, physics renders its verdict in meters of asphalt. Stopping distance is where the entire toolkit of kinematics — velocity, acceleration, reaction time, friction — converges on a single, consequential number. It is the natural endpoint of this book because it demands every concept you have built: translating a real scenario into equations, combining them, and interpreting the result under constraints that matter.

    What stopping distance achieves. The total stopping distance $d_{\text{stop}}$ unifies two phases of motion into one expression:

    $$d_{\text{stop}} = v_0 \, t_r + \frac{v_0^2}{2\mu g}$$

    where $v_0$ is the initial speed, $t_r$ is the driver's reaction time, $\mu$ is the coefficient of kinetic friction, and $g \approx 9.8\;\text{m/s}^2$. The first term is uniform motion during reaction; the second is deceleration under friction. Neither term alone tells the full story — stopping distance is the synthesis.

    Worked example. A car travels at $v_0 = 20\;\text{m/s}$ with $t_r = 1.2\;\text{s}$ and $\mu = 0.7$.

    $$d_{\text{stop}} = (20)(1.2) + \frac{20^2}{2(0.7)(9.8)} = 24 + \frac{400}{13.72} \approx 24 + 29.2 = 53.2\;\text{m}$$

    Notice how doubling speed would quadruple the braking term — the nonlinearity encoded in $v_0^2$ is precisely why speed limits exist.

    Why it's true. The reaction-distance term follows directly from $d = vt$ at constant velocity. The braking term comes from $v^2 = v_0^2 - 2a\,d$ with $a = \mu g$, setting $v = 0$ and solving for $d$. Each piece rests on definitions and principles you have already proved.

    Where to go next. This single formula opens doors to traffic engineering (setting safe following distances), autonomous vehicle design (sensor-to-actuator latency replaces $t_r$), forensic accident reconstruction (solving for $v_0$ from skid marks), and road surface engineering (optimizing $\mu$ in wet conditions). Pick one that intrigues you — the physics you now command is the entry ticket.

    Practice. Accident investigators measure $38\;\text{m}$ of skid marks on dry pavement ($\mu = 0.75$). Assuming $t_r = 0$, what was the car's speed at the moment braking began? Convert your answer to km/h and compare it to a typical urban speed limit.
