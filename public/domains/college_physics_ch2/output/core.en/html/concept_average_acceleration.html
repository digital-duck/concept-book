Average Acceleration

A car merging onto a highway speeds up from 30 km/h to 90 km/h over 8 seconds. A cyclist braking at a red light slows from 20 km/h to rest in 4 seconds. In both cases, velocity changes over time — and the single number that captures how quickly that change happens is average acceleration.

Definition. Average acceleration is the change in velocity divided by the elapsed time:

$$\vec{a}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{t_f - t_i}$$

Because velocity is a vector, acceleration is also a vector. Its SI unit is metres per second squared ($\text{m/s}^2$). A positive value along a chosen axis means speeding up in that direction; a negative value means slowing down (or speeding up in the opposite direction).

Worked example. A train traveling east at $v_i = 10\;\text{m/s}$ accelerates to $v_f = 25\;\text{m/s}$ over $\Delta t = 6.0\;\text{s}$. Find its average acceleration.

$$a_{\text{avg}} = \frac{25\;\text{m/s} - 10\;\text{m/s}}{6.0\;\text{s}} = \frac{15\;\text{m/s}}{6.0\;\text{s}} = 2.5\;\text{m/s}^2 \text{ (east)}$$

The train's velocity increases by $2.5\;\text{m/s}$ every second, on average.

Why it's true. Average acceleration is the constant acceleration that would produce the same total velocity change in the same time interval. The actual acceleration may have varied from moment to moment, but $\vec{a}_{\text{avg}}$ guarantees that $\vec{v}_i + \vec{a}_{\text{avg}}\,\Delta t = \vec{v}_f$. You can verify this with the train: $10\;\text{m/s} + (2.5\;\text{m/s}^2)(6.0\;\text{s}) = 25\;\text{m/s}$, exactly the final velocity. This makes average acceleration a reliable single-number summary of how velocity evolved over any interval, no matter how complicated the motion in between.

Practice problem. A runner heading north at $8.0\;\text{m/s}$ comes to a complete stop in $2.5\;\text{s}$. What is her average acceleration? State its magnitude and direction, and explain what the negative sign (relative to north) tells you physically.
