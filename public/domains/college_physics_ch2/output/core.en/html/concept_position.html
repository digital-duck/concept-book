Concept Book: Position

Position

Every navigation app on your phone answers one fundamental question: where are you right now? Physics formalizes this idea with the concept of position — the precise specification of an object's location relative to a chosen reference point.

Definition. The position of a particle in one dimension is written as $x$, representing a signed distance along a chosen axis from a designated origin. The sign encodes direction: positive values lie one way from the origin, negative values the other. Position carries units of length (meters in SI).

Crucially, position is relative. It has no meaning without a reference point — an origin and an axis direction. Change the origin, and every position value changes with it, even though the physical situation is identical.

Worked Example. A student stands $3\,\text{m}$ east of a lamppost. If we place the origin at the lamppost with the positive $x$-axis pointing east, her position is

$$x = +3\,\text{m}.$$

If we instead place the origin at a bench $5\,\text{m}$ east of the lamppost, the same student's position becomes

$$x' = 3\,\text{m} - 5\,\text{m} = -2\,\text{m}.$$

Nothing physical changed — only the coordinate system. This illustrates why stating your reference point is essential.

Why it's true. Position must be a signed quantity because location inherently involves direction from the origin, not just distance. An unsigned distance $d = |x|$ would make the student at $+3\,\text{m}$ indistinguishable from one at $-3\,\text{m}$, collapsing two distinct physical locations into one number. The sign preserves the full geometric information: not only how far from the origin, but which side.

Practice Problem. A car sits $12\,\text{m}$ west of a traffic light. Define east as positive and place the origin at the light. Write the car's position. Then rewrite it using an origin placed $4\,\text{m}$ west of the light. Confirm that the difference between the car's position and the light's position is the same in both coordinate systems.
