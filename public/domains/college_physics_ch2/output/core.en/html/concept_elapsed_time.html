Concept Book: Elapsed Time

Elapsed Time

Every morning you glance at the clock when you leave home and again when you arrive at school. The difference between those two readings — that's elapsed time. It measures how much time has passed between two events, and it is one of the most practical quantities in both everyday life and physics.

Definition. If an event begins at time $t_1$ and ends at time $t_2$, the elapsed time is

$$\Delta t = t_2 - t_1$$

where $\Delta t \geq 0$. The Greek letter $\Delta$ (delta) denotes a change in a quantity. Elapsed time is always measured as a final reading minus an initial reading, so it is independent of when we choose to start our clock — only the interval matters.

Worked Example. A runner crosses the start line at $t_1 = 12\!:\!15\!:\!30$ (hours : minutes : seconds) and the finish line at $t_2 = 12\!:\!42\!:\!10$. Find the elapsed time.

$$\Delta t = 12\!:\!42\!:\!10 - 12\!:\!15\!:\!30$$

Subtract each unit: $42 - 15 = 27$ minutes, $10 - 30 = -20$ seconds. Because the seconds are negative, borrow one minute:

$$\Delta t = 26 \text{ min } 40 \text{ s} = 1600 \text{ s}$$

Converting to a single unit (seconds) is often useful in calculations: $26 \times 60 + 40 = 1600\;\text{s}$.

Why it's true. Elapsed time inherits the properties of subtraction on the real number line. Because $t_2 \geq t_1$ for any event that ends after it starts, $\Delta t$ is non-negative. It is also translation-invariant: shifting both $t_1$ and $t_2$ by the same constant $c$ leaves $\Delta t$ unchanged, since $(t_2 + c) - (t_1 + c) = t_2 - t_1$. This is why two stopwatches started at different moments still agree on the duration of the same race.

Practice Problem. A chemistry experiment begins at $t_1 = 9\!:\!07\!:\!45$ and ends at $t_2 = 9\!:\!50\!:\!20$. Calculate $\Delta t$ in seconds.
