🌸 Graphical Analysis Of Motion

🌸 Graphical Analysis Of Motion

Definition. Graphical analysis of motion is the systematic extraction of kinematic information—position, velocity, and acceleration—directly from plots of motion data. The three fundamental graphs are the position–time graph ($x$ vs. $t$), the velocity–time graph ($v$ vs. $t$), and the acceleration–time graph ($a$ vs. $t$). Each graph encodes specific physical quantities in its geometry: the slope of a curve at any point equals the instantaneous rate of change of the vertical axis quantity with respect to time, and the area under a curve between two times equals the net change in the antiderivative quantity over that interval.

Formally, if $x(t)$ is a differentiable position function, then the instantaneous velocity is $v(t) = dx/dt$, and the instantaneous acceleration is $a(t) = dv/dt$. Conversely, $\Delta x = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} v(t)\, dt$ and $\Delta v = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} a(t)\, dt$.

Worked Example. A car's velocity–time data yields a straight line from $v = 4\ \text{m/s}$ at $t = 0\ \text{s}$ to $v = 16\ \text{m/s}$ at $t = 6\ \text{s}$. First, the slope gives the (constant) acceleration:

$$ a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{16 - 4}{6 - 0} = 2\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

Second, the displacement during this interval equals the area of the trapezoid beneath the line:

$$ \Delta x = \frac{1}{2}(v_0 + v_f)\Delta t = \frac{1}{2}(4 + 16)(6) = 60\ \text{m}. $$

Key Theorem. For uniformly accelerated motion, the $x$–$t$ graph is a parabola of the form $x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$, whose curvature is proportional to $a$. A concave-up parabola indicates positive acceleration; concave-down indicates negative acceleration. A linear $x$–$t$ segment implies zero acceleration (constant velocity), while a horizontal $v$–$t$ segment likewise confirms uniform motion.

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor, record position versus time for a cart released from rest on an inclined track. Plot $x(t)$ and use a graphing tool to compute the numerical derivative at five equally spaced times, constructing the $v$–$t$ curve. Fit a linear trendline to $v(t)$; the slope should match the acceleration obtained from the $x$–$t$ parabola fit. Quantify the agreement by computing the percent difference between the two acceleration values.
