🍃 Laminar Flow

🍃 Laminar Flow

Definition. Laminar flow is a regime of fluid motion in which every fluid particle travels along a smooth, well-defined path called a streamline, and adjacent layers of fluid slide past one another without macroscopic mixing. In laminar flow the velocity field is steady: at any fixed point in space, the fluid velocity $\mathbf{v}$ does not fluctuate in time. This orderly behavior stands in contrast to turbulent flow, where chaotic eddies cause rapid, irregular velocity fluctuations throughout the fluid.

The dimensionless Reynolds number governs the transition between the two regimes:

$$ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\eta} $$

where $\rho$ is the fluid density $(\text{kg/m}^3)$, $v$ the mean flow speed $(\text{m/s})$, $D$ the characteristic length (pipe diameter for internal flow, in meters), and $\eta$ the dynamic viscosity $(\text{Pa·s})$. For flow through a cylindrical pipe, laminar conditions persist when $Re \lesssim 2000$; above roughly $4000$ the flow becomes fully turbulent.

Worked Example. Water ($\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3$, $\eta = 1.00 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{Pa·s}$) flows through a tube of diameter $D = 4.0\ \text{mm}$ at a mean speed $v = 0.30\ \text{m/s}$. Determine whether the flow is laminar.

$$ Re = \frac{(1000)(0.30)(4.0 \times 10^{-3})}{1.00 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1.2}{1.00 \times 10^{-3}} = 1200 $$

Since $Re = 1200 < 2000$, the flow is laminar. Under these conditions the velocity profile across the tube cross-section is parabolic, with fluid at the centerline moving fastest and fluid at the wall stationary (no-slip condition):

$$ v(r) = \frac{\Delta P}{4\eta L}\left(R^2 - r^2\right) $$

where $R$ is the tube radius, $r$ the radial distance from the centerline, $L$ the tube length, and $\Delta P$ the pressure difference driving the flow.

Key Theorem (Hagen–Poiseuille Law). For steady, incompressible, laminar flow through a cylindrical tube of radius $R$ and length $L$, integrating the parabolic profile over the cross-section yields the volumetric flow rate:

$$ Q = \frac{\pi R^4 \Delta P}{8 \eta L} $$

The fourth-power dependence on radius is clinically significant: halving a blood vessel's radius reduces flow by a factor of 16 for the same driving pressure.

Lab Exercise. Fill a long glass tube ($D \approx 5\ \text{mm}$, $L \approx 1\ \text{m}$) with glycerin and inject a thin thread of dye at the inlet while controlling flow rate with an adjustable clamp. Measure the volume collected over a fixed time interval to determine $Q$, then compute $\Delta P$ via the Hagen–Poiseuille law and compare it with a manometer reading. Increase $Q$ gradually until the dye thread breaks up, recording the flow rate at which turbulence first appears, and use it to estimate the critical Reynolds number experimentally.
