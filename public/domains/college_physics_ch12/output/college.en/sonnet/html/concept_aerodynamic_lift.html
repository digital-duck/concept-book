🌸 Aerodynamic Lift

🌸 Aerodynamic Lift

Definition. Aerodynamic lift is the component of the net fluid-dynamic force acting on a body that is perpendicular to the direction of the oncoming flow. For a wing moving through air, lift arises from the pressure difference between the lower and upper surfaces, quantified by Bernoulli's principle in conjunction with the geometry of the airfoil. For an incompressible, inviscid, steady flow along a streamline, Bernoulli's equation states:

$$ P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant} $$

where $P$ is the static pressure, $\rho$ is the fluid density, $v$ is the flow speed, and $h$ is the elevation. Because the curved upper surface of a wing forces air to travel a longer path—and hence at greater speed—than air beneath the flatter lower surface, the upper pressure $P_{\text{top}}$ is reduced relative to the lower pressure $P_{\text{bottom}}$. The resulting net upward pressure force per unit area is $\Delta P = P_{\text{bottom}} - P_{\text{top}}$, and the total lift force over wing area $A$ is:

$$ F_L = \Delta P \cdot A = \frac{1}{2}\rho (v_{\text{top}}^2 - v_{\text{bottom}}^2)\, A $$

Worked Example. Consider a wing of planform area $A = 25\ \text{m}^2$ in level flight. Air of density $\rho = 1.20\ \text{kg/m}^3$ flows over the upper surface at $v_{\text{top}} = 72.0\ \text{m/s}$ and under the lower surface at $v_{\text{bottom}} = 60.0\ \text{m/s}$. Applying the lift formula:

$$ F_L = \frac{1}{2}(1.20)\bigl(72.0^2 - 60.0^2\bigr)(25) = \frac{1}{2}(1.20)(5184 - 3600)(25) $$ $$ F_L = \frac{1}{2}(1.20)(1584)(25) = 23{,}760\ \text{N} \approx 2.38 \times 10^4\ \text{N} $$

This lift force supports an aircraft mass of approximately $2430\ \text{kg}$.

Key Theorem — Pressure–Speed Relation for Lift. Applying Bernoulli's equation at constant elevation to the upper and lower streamlines and rearranging directly yields:

$$ \Delta P = P_{\text{bottom}} - P_{\text{top}} = \frac{1}{2}\rho\bigl(v_{\text{top}}^2 - v_{\text{bottom}}^2\bigr) $$

This result establishes that lift scales quadratically with airspeed: doubling the free-stream speed quadruples the pressure difference and hence the total lift force, all else equal. It also shows that lift vanishes when the flow speeds above and below the wing are equal, which occurs when the wing has no camber and is at zero angle of attack.

Lab Exercise. Using a wind tunnel with an adjustable-angle NACA 0012 airfoil (chord $c = 0.15\ \text{m}$, span $\ell = 0.30\ \text{m}$), measure the vertical force $F_L$ at angles of attack $\alpha = 0°, 4°, 8°, 12°, 16°$ at a fixed free-stream speed of $v_\infty = 20.0\ \text{m/s}$ and air density $\rho = 1.20\ \text{kg/m}^3$. At each angle, also record the Pitot-tube speeds above and below the wing and compute the predicted $F_L$ from the pressure–speed relation above. Compare measured and predicted values, and note the angle at which the measured lift begins to decrease despite increasing $\alpha$.
