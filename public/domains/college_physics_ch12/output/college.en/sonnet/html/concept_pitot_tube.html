🌸 Pitot Tube

🌸 Pitot Tube

A pitot tube is a pressure-measurement device used to determine the flow velocity of a fluid by exploiting the relationship between static and dynamic pressure encoded in Bernoulli's equation. Named after Henri Pitot, the instrument consists of two concentric tubes: an inner tube with an opening facing directly into the oncoming flow and an outer annular channel with side ports perpendicular to the flow. The inner opening brings the fluid to rest, creating a stagnation point where all kinetic energy converts to pressure energy. The outer ports sample the undisturbed static pressure $P_s$, and the difference between the two measurements encodes the kinetic energy per unit volume of the moving fluid.

Definition. For an incompressible, inviscid fluid in steady flow, Bernoulli's equation applied between a free-stream point and the stagnation point gives

$$ P_s + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 = P_0, $$

where $\rho$ is the fluid density, $v$ is the free-stream speed, and $P_0$ is the stagnation pressure recorded at the inner opening. Solving for $v$:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{2(P_0 - P_s)}{\rho}}. $$

Worked Example. An aircraft pitot tube records $P_0 = 1.080 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$ and $P_s = 1.013 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$. Taking the density of air at cruise altitude as $\rho = 0.909\ \text{kg/m}^3$, the airspeed is

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{2(1.080 \times 10^5 - 1.013 \times 10^5)}{0.909}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(6700)}{0.909}} = \sqrt{14{,}741} \approx 121\ \text{m/s}. $$

This corresponds to approximately 437 km/h, a typical subsonic cruising speed.

Key Theorem (Stagnation Pressure). At the stagnation point, all kinetic energy converts to pressure energy with zero velocity, so $P_0$ is always the maximum pressure the fluid can exert on any surface in the flow. For compressible flow at Mach numbers $M < 0.3$, the incompressible Bernoulli result remains accurate to within 2%; corrections using isentropic relations are required at higher Mach numbers.

Lab Exercise. Set up a wind tunnel with a pitot-static probe connected to a differential manometer. Record pressure differences at five fan speeds. For each trial, compute the air velocity using the formula above and compare your result to a calibrated anemometer reading placed beside the probe. Quantify the percentage discrepancy and discuss possible sources of error, including misalignment of the tube axis relative to the flow direction and turbulent boundary-layer effects near the side ports.
