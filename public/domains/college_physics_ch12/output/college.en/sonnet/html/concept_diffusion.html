🍃 Diffusion

🍃 Diffusion

Definition. Diffusion is the net transport of particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, driven by random thermal motion rather than by bulk fluid flow. For a one-dimensional system, the molar flux $J$ (mol m$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$) is governed by Fick's First Law:

$$ J = -D \frac{dC}{dx} $$

where $D$ (m$^2$ s$^{-1}$) is the diffusion coefficient characteristic of the diffusing species and medium, $C$ is the molar concentration (mol m$^{-3}$), and $x$ is position. The negative sign reflects that transport occurs down the concentration gradient. When $D$ and the geometry are time-independent, conservation of mass yields Fick's Second Law:

$$ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} $$

describing how concentration profiles evolve in time.

Worked Example. A thin slab of muscle tissue ($L = 2.0 \times 10^{-3}$ m) separates two well-stirred compartments. The left side maintains an oxygen concentration $C_1 = 8.0 \times 10^{-2}$ mol m$^{-3}$; the right side is kept at $C_2 = 2.0 \times 10^{-2}$ mol m$^{-3}$. The diffusion coefficient of O$_2$ in tissue is $D = 1.0 \times 10^{-9}$ m$^2$ s$^{-1}$. Under steady-state conditions the concentration profile is linear, so:

$$ J = -D \frac{C_2 - C_1}{L} = -\left(1.0 \times 10^{-9}\right)\frac{(2.0 - 8.0)\times 10^{-2}}{2.0 \times 10^{-3}} = 3.0 \times 10^{-8} \ \text{mol m}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1} $$

The positive value confirms net flux directed from left to right, consistent with the concentration gradient.

Key Theorem. The mean squared displacement of a diffusing particle grows linearly in time:

$$ \langle x^2 \rangle = 2Dt $$

This result, derivable from the random-walk model of Brownian motion, shows that diffusion is inherently slow over macroscopic distances; doubling the distance requires quadrupling the time.

Lab Exercise. Fill a graduated cylinder with still water and introduce a drop of food coloring at the bottom. Record the color boundary position $x(t)$ every 5 minutes for 30 minutes. Plot $x^2$ versus $t$ and determine the slope; compare the extracted $D$ with the literature value for the dye in water at room temperature ($\approx 5 \times 10^{-10}$ m$^2$ s$^{-1}$). Identify sources of systematic error, including convective currents from temperature gradients.
