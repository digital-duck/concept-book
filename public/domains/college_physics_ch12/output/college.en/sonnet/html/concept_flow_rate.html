🍃 Flow Rate

🍃 Flow Rate

Definition. The flow rate (also called volume flow rate) of a fluid is the volume of fluid passing through a cross-sectional area per unit time. For a fluid moving with uniform velocity $v$ through a pipe of cross-sectional area $A$, the flow rate $Q$ is defined by

$$ Q = Av, $$

where $Q$ has SI units of cubic meters per second ($\text{m}^3/\text{s}$). More generally, when the velocity is not uniform across the cross-section, $Q$ is obtained by integrating the velocity field over the area. The mass flow rate $\dot{m}$ is related to volume flow rate through the fluid density $\rho$:

$$ \dot{m} = \rho Q = \rho A v. $$

Worked Example. Water flows through a cylindrical pipe of inner diameter $d = 4.0\ \text{cm}$ at a speed of $v = 2.5\ \text{m/s}$. Determine the volume flow rate and the mass flow rate (take $\rho_{\text{water}} = 1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3$).

Solution. The cross-sectional area of the pipe is

$$ A = \pi \left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi (0.020\ \text{m})^2 = 1.257 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2. $$

The volume flow rate is therefore

$$ Q = Av = (1.257 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2)(2.5\ \text{m/s}) = 3.14 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3/\text{s}. $$

The mass flow rate is

$$ \dot{m} = \rho Q = (1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3)(3.14 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3/\text{s}) = 3.14\ \text{kg/s}. $$

Key Theorem — Equation of Continuity. For an incompressible fluid in steady flow, conservation of mass requires that the flow rate be constant along any streamtube:

$$ A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2 = Q = \text{const.} $$

This result, known as the continuity equation, implies that fluid accelerates when a pipe narrows and decelerates when it widens — a foundational principle underlying the Venturi effect and many engineering applications.

Lab Exercise. Using a garden hose fitted with a nozzle, measure the time $t$ required to fill a container of known volume $V$. Compute $Q = V/t$. Then measure the hose inner diameter $d_1$ and nozzle exit diameter $d_2$, and use the continuity equation to predict the exit speed $v_2$ given a measured inlet speed $v_1$. Compare your prediction with a direct timed measurement, and calculate the percent discrepancy.
