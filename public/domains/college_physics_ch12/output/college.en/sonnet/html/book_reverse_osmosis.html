🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is a vector quantity that describes the rate of change of position with respect to time. For a particle whose position is given by the vector $\vec{r}(t)$, the instantaneous velocity is defined as

$$ \vec{v}(t) = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t}. $$

The magnitude $|\vec{v}|$ is called the speed, and the direction of $\vec{v}$ is tangent to the trajectory at each instant. In one dimension, velocity reduces to $v = dx/dt$, which may be positive or negative depending on the chosen positive axis direction. The SI unit of velocity is meters per second ($\text{m/s}$).

Average velocity over a finite interval $[t_1, t_2]$ is

$$ \vec{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\vec{r}(t_2) - \vec{r}(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1} = \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t}. $$

Note that average velocity depends only on the net displacement, not the total path length traveled.

Worked Example. A particle moves along the $x$-axis with position $x(t) = 3t^2 - 6t + 1$ (meters, $t$ in seconds). Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$ and determine when the particle is momentarily at rest.

Differentiating: $v(t) = dx/dt = 6t - 6$. At $t = 2\,\text{s}$,

$$ v(2) = 6(2) - 6 = 6\,\text{m/s}. $$

The particle is at rest when $v(t) = 0$, i.e., $6t - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow t = 1\,\text{s}$.

Key Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus applied to kinematics). If the velocity $\vec{v}(t)$ is integrable on $[t_0, t]$, the displacement is recovered by

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \int_{t_0}^{t} \vec{v}(t')\, dt'. $$

This integral relation is the kinematic inverse of differentiation: knowing $\vec{v}(t)$ completely determines the trajectory up to an initial condition $\vec{r}(t_0)$. For constant acceleration $\vec{a}$, this yields $\vec{v}(t) = \vec{v}_0 + \vec{a}t$ and $\vec{r}(t) = \vec{r}_0 + \vec{v}_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\vec{a}t^2$.

🍃 Diffusion

🍃 Diffusion

Definition. Diffusion is the net transport of particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, driven by random thermal motion rather than by bulk fluid flow. For a one-dimensional system, the molar flux $J$ (mol m$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$) is governed by Fick's First Law:

$$ J = -D \frac{dC}{dx} $$

where $D$ (m$^2$ s$^{-1}$) is the diffusion coefficient characteristic of the diffusing species and medium, $C$ is the molar concentration (mol m$^{-3}$), and $x$ is position. The negative sign reflects that transport occurs down the concentration gradient. When $D$ and the geometry are time-independent, conservation of mass yields Fick's Second Law:

$$ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} $$

describing how concentration profiles evolve in time.

Worked Example. A thin slab of muscle tissue ($L = 2.0 \times 10^{-3}$ m) separates two well-stirred compartments. The left side maintains an oxygen concentration $C_1 = 8.0 \times 10^{-2}$ mol m$^{-3}$; the right side is kept at $C_2 = 2.0 \times 10^{-2}$ mol m$^{-3}$. The diffusion coefficient of O$_2$ in tissue is $D = 1.0 \times 10^{-9}$ m$^2$ s$^{-1}$. Under steady-state conditions the concentration profile is linear, so:

$$ J = -D \frac{C_2 - C_1}{L} = -\left(1.0 \times 10^{-9}\right)\frac{(2.0 - 8.0)\times 10^{-2}}{2.0 \times 10^{-3}} = 3.0 \times 10^{-8} \ \text{mol m}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1} $$

The positive value confirms net flux directed from left to right, consistent with the concentration gradient.

Key Theorem. The mean squared displacement of a diffusing particle grows linearly in time:

$$ \langle x^2 \rangle = 2Dt $$

This result, derivable from the random-walk model of Brownian motion, shows that diffusion is inherently slow over macroscopic distances; doubling the distance requires quadrupling the time.

Lab Exercise. Fill a graduated cylinder with still water and introduce a drop of food coloring at the bottom. Record the color boundary position $x(t)$ every 5 minutes for 30 minutes. Identify sources of systematic error, including convective currents from temperature gradients.

🍃 Semipermeable Membrane

🍃 Semipermeable Membrane

Definition. A semipermeable membrane is a thin barrier that permits the passage of certain molecules—typically solvent molecules such as water—while selectively blocking the passage of larger solute particles or ions. The selective permeability arises from the membrane's pore geometry and chemical affinity: pores are sized so that small solvent molecules diffuse freely while solute species are excluded by steric hindrance or electrostatic repulsion. Semipermeable membranes are central to the phenomenon of osmosis, in which a net solvent flux is driven across the membrane from a region of lower solute concentration to one of higher solute concentration.

The thermodynamic driving force is the difference in chemical potential of the solvent on each side. For an ideal dilute solution, the osmotic pressure $\Pi$ required to halt net solvent flow is given by the van 't Hoff equation:

$$ \Pi = iMRT $$

where $i$ is the van 't Hoff factor (number of particles per formula unit), $M$ is the molar concentration of the solute, $R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvin.

Worked Example. A cell membrane separates an interior solution of $0.30\ \text{mol/L}$ NaCl ($i = 2$) from pure water at $T = 310\ \text{K}$ (normal body temperature). Calculate the osmotic pressure across the membrane.

$$ \Pi = iMRT = (2)(0.30\ \text{mol/L})(8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1})(310\ \text{K}) $$

Converting: $1\ \text{mol/L} = 1000\ \text{mol/m}^3$, so $M = 300\ \text{mol/m}^3$.

$$ \Pi = (2)(300)(8.314)(310) \approx 1.55 \times 10^6\ \text{Pa} \approx 15.3\ \text{atm} $$

This large pressure illustrates why cells require active regulation of solute concentrations to maintain structural integrity.

Key Theorem (Osmotic Equilibrium). At equilibrium, no net solvent flux occurs when the hydrostatic pressure difference $\Delta P$ across the membrane satisfies $\Delta P = \Pi$. Any departure from this balance produces a net flux proportional to the hydraulic conductivity of the membrane.

Plot mass versus time and extrapolate to determine the effective osmotic pressure, comparing your result to the van 't Hoff prediction at room temperature.

🍃 Osmosis

🍃 Osmosis

Definition. Osmosis is the net diffusion of solvent molecules (typically water) across a semipermeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration (higher solvent chemical potential) to a region of higher solute concentration (lower solvent chemical potential). The membrane permits passage of solvent molecules while blocking solute particles. The process continues until thermodynamic equilibrium is achieved or until sufficient pressure opposes further flow. The minimum pressure required to halt osmotic flow is the osmotic pressure, given by the van 't Hoff equation:

$$ \Pi = iMRT $$

where $\Pi$ is the osmotic pressure (Pa), $i$ is the van 't Hoff factor accounting for solute dissociation, $M$ is the molar concentration of solute (mol·L$^{-1}$), $R = 8.314\ \text{J·mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature (K).

Worked Example. A solution of sodium chloride (NaCl) with concentration $M = 0.15\ \text{mol·L}^{-1} = 150\ \text{mol·m}^{-3}$ is separated from pure water by a semipermeable membrane at $T = 310\ \text{K}$ (approximate body temperature). NaCl dissociates fully into Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$, giving $i = 2$. The osmotic pressure is:

$$ \Pi = (2)(150\ \text{mol·m}^{-3})(8.314\ \text{J·mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1})(310\ \text{K}) \approx 7.73 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa} \approx 7.6\ \text{atm} $$

This value closely matches the osmotic pressure of blood plasma, explaining why isotonic saline solutions used in medicine must match this concentration.

Key Theorem. For dilute solutions, osmotic pressure obeys the formal analogy with the ideal gas law: $\Pi V = nRT$, where $n$ is the number of moles of solute and $V$ is the solution volume. This result, derived rigorously from chemical potential equality at membrane equilibrium ($\mu_{\text{solvent}}^{\text{left}} = \mu_{\text{solvent}}^{\text{right}}$), confirms that osmotic pressure depends solely on solute particle number density, not on the chemical identity of the solute.

Lab Exercise. Place a dialysis bag filled with $0.30\ \text{mol·L}^{-1}$ sucrose solution into a beaker of distilled water. Record the bag mass every five minutes for thirty minutes. Plot mass versus time and extrapolate to determine the equilibrium osmotic pressure. Compare your experimental value to the theoretical prediction from $\Pi = MRT$ (sucrose does not dissociate, so $i = 1$) at measured room temperature.

🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the normal force exerted per unit area on a surface. For a uniform force $F$ applied perpendicularly over an area $A$, the pressure is

$$ P = \frac{F}{A}. $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; it acts equally in all directions at a point within a static fluid. For a fluid at rest, the pressure at depth $h$ below the surface is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface, $\rho$ is the fluid density, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. This relationship shows that pressure increases linearly with depth and depends on the fluid's density but not on the total volume of fluid.

Worked Example. A diver descends to a depth of $h = 30\,\text{m}$ in seawater ($\rho = 1025\,\text{kg/m}^3$). The surface pressure is atmospheric, $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. The absolute pressure at that depth is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1025)(9.81)(30). $$

Computing the second term: $(1025)(9.81)(30) = 3.015 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Therefore

$$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 3.015 \times 10^5 = 4.028 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa} \approx 4.0\,\text{atm}. $$

The gauge pressure, defined as the pressure in excess of atmospheric, is $P_\text{gauge} = \rho g h \approx 3.0 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if the applied pressure changes by $\Delta P$, then every location in the fluid experiences the same increment $\Delta P$. This underpins hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small piston area produces a large force over a large piston area, since $F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2$.

Quantify any discrepancy and identify likely sources of systematic error.

🍃 Osmotic Pressure

🍃 Osmotic Pressure

Definition. Osmotic pressure is the hydrostatic pressure that must be applied to a solution to halt the net flow of solvent molecules across a semipermeable membrane separating the solution from pure solvent. Quantitatively, for a dilute solution obeying ideal-solution behavior, the van 't Hoff equation gives

$$ \Pi = iMRT, $$

where $\Pi$ is the osmotic pressure (in pascals), $i$ is the van 't Hoff factor accounting for solute dissociation, $M$ is the molar concentration of the solute (mol m$^{-3}$), $R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature (K). The structural analogy with the ideal-gas law $P = nRT/V$ is not coincidental: both expressions arise from the statistical tendency of particles to distribute uniformly throughout an accessible volume.

Worked Example. A physiological saline solution contains $0.154\ \text{mol L}^{-1}$ NaCl at $37^\circ\text{C}\ (T = 310\ \text{K})$. Because NaCl dissociates completely into Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$, the van 't Hoff factor is $i = 2$. Converting concentration: $M = 154\ \text{mol m}^{-3}$. Applying the van 't Hoff equation:

$$ \Pi = (2)(154\ \text{mol m}^{-3})(8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1})(310\ \text{K}) \approx 7.93 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa} \approx 7.83\ \text{atm}. $$

This result matches the osmotic pressure of blood plasma, explaining why intravenous fluids must be isotonic to prevent cell lysis or crenation.

Key Theorem. For a solution in osmotic equilibrium across a membrane, the chemical potential of the solvent is equal on both sides. Specifically, the lowering of solvent chemical potential by the solute, $\Delta\mu = -RT\ln x_s$ (where $x_s$ is the solvent mole fraction), is exactly compensated by the applied pressure increment $\Delta\mu = V_m \Pi$ (with $V_m$ the molar volume of the solvent). Setting these equal and applying the dilute-solution approximation $\ln x_s \approx -x_\text{solute} \approx -n_\text{solute}/n_\text{solvent}$ recovers the van 't Hoff equation.

🌸 Reverse Osmosis

🌸 Reverse Osmosis

Definition. Osmosis is the net diffusion of solvent molecules through a semipermeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration to one of higher solute concentration. The osmotic pressure $\Pi$ required to halt this flow is given by the van 't Hoff equation:

$$ \Pi = iMRT $$

where $i$ is the van 't Hoff factor (number of dissolved particles per formula unit), $M$ is the molar concentration of the solute (mol/L), $R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvin. Reverse osmosis (RO) occurs when an external pressure $P_{\text{ext}} > \Pi$ is applied to the high-concentration side, forcing solvent molecules back through the membrane against the concentration gradient — effectively purifying the solution.

Worked Example. Seawater contains approximately $35\ \text{g/L}$ of NaCl ($M_r = 58.44\ \text{g/mol}$), which dissociates fully so $i = 2$. At $T = 298\ \text{K}$:

$$ M = \frac{35}{58.44} \approx 0.599\ \text{mol/L} $$ $$ \Pi = (2)(0.599)(8.314 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1})(298\ \text{K}) \approx 2.97\ \text{MPa} \approx 29.3\ \text{atm} $$

Industrial RO desalination plants operate at $5$–$8\ \text{MPa}$ to overcome both osmotic pressure and membrane resistance, producing potable water with ion rejection rates exceeding $99\%$.

Key Theorem (Starling–Kedem equation, simplified). The volumetric flux $J_v$ of solvent through the membrane is:

$$ J_v = L_p\!\left(P_{\text{ext}} - \Pi\right) $$

where $L_p$ is the hydraulic permeability of the membrane (m s$^{-1}$ Pa$^{-1}$). Flux is positive (pure solvent crosses toward the high-pressure side) only when $P_{\text{ext}} > \Pi$; at $P_{\text{ext}} = \Pi$ the system is in osmotic equilibrium and net flux vanishes.

Lab Exercise. Fill one side of a U-tube osmometer with $1.0\ \text{mol/L}$ sucrose solution and the other with pure water, separated by a dialysis membrane. Record the equilibrium height difference $\Delta h$ and calculate $\Pi = \rho g \Delta h$. Then apply compressed air ($P_{\text{ext}} = 1.5\Pi$) to the sucrose side and observe the direction of solvent flow. Compare your measured $\Pi$ with the van 't Hoff prediction, and identify sources of deviation such as membrane impermeability assumptions and solute activity coefficients.

Payoff 🎯

Reverse osmosis represents the deliberate inversion of a spontaneous thermodynamic process, and in doing so it crystallizes every major theme developed throughout this chapter: pressure, fluid statics, osmotic equilibrium, and the interplay between entropy and mechanical work. Where natural osmosis drives solvent across a semipermeable membrane from low to high solute concentration until equilibrium is reached, reverse osmosis applies an external pressure $P_{\text{ext}}$ that exceeds the osmotic pressure $\Pi$ of the solution, forcing solvent back through the membrane against its chemical potential gradient. The governing condition is simply

$$ P_{\text{ext}} > \Pi = iMRT, $$

where $i$ is the van 't Hoff factor, $M$ the molar concentration of solutes, $R$ the universal gas constant, and $T$ the absolute temperature. When this inequality holds, pure solvent is expelled from the concentrated side, leaving solutes behind.

Worked example. Seawater contains roughly $0.60\ \text{mol/L}$ of dissolved ions (treating sodium chloride as fully dissociated, $i = 2$, gives $M_{\text{eff}} \approx 0.60\ \text{mol/L}$). At $T = 298\ \text{K}$:

$$ \Pi = (2)(0.60)(8.314)(298) \approx 2975\ \text{kPa} \approx 29\ \text{atm}. $$

An industrial desalination plant must sustain feed pressures of $55$–$70\ \text{atm}$ to maintain adequate flux through the membrane, accounting for concentration polarization and frictional losses in the flow channels.

Key theorem (osmotic pressure–flux relation). The volumetric flux $J_v$ of solvent through a membrane of hydraulic permeability $L_p$ is given by the Kedem–Katchalsky expression:

$$ J_v = L_p \left( \Delta P - \sigma \Delta \Pi \right), $$

where $\Delta P$ is the applied transmembrane pressure, $\Delta \Pi$ is the osmotic pressure difference across the membrane, and $\sigma \in [0,1]$ is the reflection coefficient quantifying how effectively the membrane rejects a given solute. Perfect rejection ($\sigma = 1$) and $\Delta P > \Delta \Pi$ guarantee net solvent flow toward the low-concentration permeate side.

Reverse osmosis is the natural endpoint of this chapter because it demands simultaneous command of fluid pressure, thermodynamic equilibrium, and membrane transport — each a pillar developed in the preceding sections. Mastering it opens access to desalination engineering, pharmaceutical purification, and wastewater reclamation, where clean water production underpins public health at a global scale.
