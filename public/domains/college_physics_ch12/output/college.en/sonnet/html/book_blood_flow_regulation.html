🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is a vector quantity that describes the rate of change of position with respect to time. For a particle whose position is given by the vector $\vec{r}(t)$, the instantaneous velocity is defined as

$$ \vec{v}(t) = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t}. $$

The magnitude $|\vec{v}|$ is called the speed, and the direction of $\vec{v}$ is tangent to the trajectory at each instant. In one dimension, velocity reduces to $v = dx/dt$, which may be positive or negative depending on the chosen positive axis direction. The SI unit of velocity is meters per second ($\text{m/s}$).

Average velocity over a finite interval $[t_1, t_2]$ is

$$ \vec{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\vec{r}(t_2) - \vec{r}(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1} = \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t}. $$

Note that average velocity depends only on the net displacement, not the total path length traveled.

Worked Example. A particle moves along the $x$-axis with position $x(t) = 3t^2 - 6t + 1$ (meters, $t$ in seconds). Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$ and determine when the particle is momentarily at rest.

Differentiating: $v(t) = dx/dt = 6t - 6$. At $t = 2\,\text{s}$,

$$ v(2) = 6(2) - 6 = 6\,\text{m/s}. $$

The particle is at rest when $v(t) = 0$, i.e., $6t - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow t = 1\,\text{s}$.

Key Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus applied to kinematics). If the velocity $\vec{v}(t)$ is integrable on $[t_0, t]$, the displacement is recovered by

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \int_{t_0}^{t} \vec{v}(t')\, dt'. $$

This integral relation is the kinematic inverse of differentiation: knowing $\vec{v}(t)$ completely determines the trajectory up to an initial condition $\vec{r}(t_0)$. For constant acceleration $\vec{a}$, this yields $\vec{v}(t) = \vec{v}_0 + \vec{a}t$ and $\vec{r}(t) = \vec{r}_0 + \vec{v}_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\vec{a}t^2$.

🌱 Cross Sectional Area

🌱 Cross Sectional Area

Definition. The cross-sectional area of an object is the area of the two-dimensional shape obtained by cutting the object with a plane perpendicular to a specified axis—typically the axis along which a fluid flows or a force is applied. For a pipe or channel carrying a fluid, it is the area $A$ of the opening through which the fluid passes. The SI unit of cross-sectional area is the square meter ($\text{m}^2$).

For a circular cross section, the area is:

$$ A = \pi r^2 $$

where $r$ is the inner radius of the pipe. This is the geometry encountered most frequently in fluid mechanics problems.

Worked Example. A horizontal pipe transitions from a wide section to a narrow section. The wide section has an inner diameter of $d_1 = 8.0\,\text{cm}$, and the narrow section has an inner diameter of $d_2 = 4.0\,\text{cm}$. Calculate the cross-sectional areas of each section.

For the wide section:

$$ A_1 = \pi\!\left(\frac{d_1}{2}\right)^2 = \pi\!\left(0.040\,\text{m}\right)^2 = 5.03 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2 $$

For the narrow section:

$$ A_2 = \pi\!\left(\frac{d_2}{2}\right)^2 = \pi\!\left(0.020\,\text{m}\right)^2 = 1.26 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2 $$

The ratio $A_1/A_2 = 4$, reflecting the fourfold reduction in area when the diameter is halved.

Key Theorem — Equation of Continuity. For an incompressible fluid in steady flow, the cross-sectional area is central to the continuity equation:

$$ A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2 $$

where $v_1$ and $v_2$ are the fluid speeds at cross sections $A_1$ and $A_2$, respectively. This expresses conservation of mass: the volumetric flow rate $Q = Av$ is constant along any streamtube. Consequently, fluid speed increases where the cross section narrows and decreases where it widens.

Discuss sources of discrepancy, such as viscous losses and turbulence at the constriction.

🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. A force is a vector quantity representing an interaction that, when applied to a body, alters or tends to alter its state of motion or its shape. Formally, Newton's Second Law defines the net force $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$ acting on a body of mass $m$ as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}, $$

where $\vec{a}$ is the body's acceleration. The SI unit of force is the newton (N), defined as $1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Force is inherently relational: by Newton's Third Law, every force exerted by body $A$ on body $B$ is accompanied by an equal and opposite force exerted by $B$ on $A$, so forces always arise in action–reaction pairs. Common force types appearing in mechanics include gravitational force $\vec{F}_g = m\vec{g}$, normal force $\vec{N}$, tension $\vec{T}$, friction $\vec{f}$, and applied forces.

Worked Example. A crate of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ rests on a horizontal floor. A horizontal applied force $F_{\text{app}} = 80\,\text{N}$ acts on the crate, while kinetic friction opposes the motion with coefficient $\mu_k = 0.25$. Find the acceleration.

Step 1. Identify the normal force. Since there is no vertical acceleration, $\vec{N} = mg = (20)(9.8) = 196\,\text{N}$.

Step 2. Compute kinetic friction: $f_k = \mu_k N = (0.25)(196) = 49\,\text{N}$.

Step 3. Apply Newton's Second Law horizontally:

$$ F_{\text{net}} = F_{\text{app}} - f_k = 80 - 49 = 31\,\text{N}. $$

Step 4. Solve for acceleration:

$$ a = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{31}{20} = 1.55\,\text{m/s}^2. $$

Key Theorem (Newton's Second Law in Component Form). For a body subject to forces $\vec{F}_1, \vec{F}_2, \ldots, \vec{F}_n$, the equations of motion decompose independently along orthogonal axes:

$$ \sum F_x = ma_x, \qquad \sum F_y = ma_y, \qquad \sum F_z = ma_z. $$

This decomposition permits analysis of complex multi-force problems by treating each spatial direction independently, provided inertial reference frames are used throughout.

🍃 Flow Rate

🍃 Flow Rate

Definition. The flow rate (also called volume flow rate) of a fluid is the volume of fluid passing through a cross-sectional area per unit time. For a fluid moving with uniform velocity $v$ through a pipe of cross-sectional area $A$, the flow rate $Q$ is defined by

$$ Q = Av, $$

where $Q$ has SI units of cubic meters per second ($\text{m}^3/\text{s}$). More generally, when the velocity is not uniform across the cross-section, $Q$ is obtained by integrating the velocity field over the area. The mass flow rate $\dot{m}$ is related to volume flow rate through the fluid density $\rho$:

$$ \dot{m} = \rho Q = \rho A v. $$

Worked Example. Water flows through a cylindrical pipe of inner diameter $d = 4.0\ \text{cm}$ at a speed of $v = 2.5\ \text{m/s}$. Determine the volume flow rate and the mass flow rate (take $\rho_{\text{water}} = 1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3$).

Solution. The cross-sectional area of the pipe is

$$ A = \pi \left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi (0.020\ \text{m})^2 = 1.257 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2. $$

The volume flow rate is therefore

$$ Q = Av = (1.257 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2)(2.5\ \text{m/s}) = 3.14 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3/\text{s}. $$

The mass flow rate is

$$ \dot{m} = \rho Q = (1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3)(3.14 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3/\text{s}) = 3.14\ \text{kg/s}. $$

Key Theorem — Equation of Continuity. For an incompressible fluid in steady flow, conservation of mass requires that the flow rate be constant along any streamtube:

$$ A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2 = Q = \text{const.} $$

This result, known as the continuity equation, implies that fluid accelerates when a pipe narrows and decelerates when it widens — a foundational principle underlying the Venturi effect and many engineering applications.

🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the normal force exerted per unit area on a surface. For a uniform force $F$ applied perpendicularly over an area $A$, the pressure is

$$ P = \frac{F}{A}. $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; it acts equally in all directions at a point within a static fluid. For a fluid at rest, the pressure at depth $h$ below the surface is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface, $\rho$ is the fluid density, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. This relationship shows that pressure increases linearly with depth and depends on the fluid's density but not on the total volume of fluid.

Worked Example. A diver descends to a depth of $h = 30\,\text{m}$ in seawater ($\rho = 1025\,\text{kg/m}^3$). The surface pressure is atmospheric, $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. The absolute pressure at that depth is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1025)(9.81)(30). $$

Computing the second term: $(1025)(9.81)(30) = 3.015 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Therefore

$$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 3.015 \times 10^5 = 4.028 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa} \approx 4.0\,\text{atm}. $$

The gauge pressure, defined as the pressure in excess of atmospheric, is $P_\text{gauge} = \rho g h \approx 3.0 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if the applied pressure changes by $\Delta P$, then every location in the fluid experiences the same increment $\Delta P$. This underpins hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small piston area produces a large force over a large piston area, since $F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2$.

Quantify any discrepancy and identify likely sources of systematic error.

🍃 Viscosity

🍃 Viscosity

Definition. Viscosity is a measure of a fluid's internal resistance to flow, arising from intermolecular cohesion and momentum transfer between adjacent fluid layers moving at different velocities. For a Newtonian fluid, the shear stress $\tau$ between fluid layers is proportional to the velocity gradient perpendicular to the flow direction:

$$ \tau = \eta \frac{dv}{dy} $$

where $\eta$ is the dynamic viscosity (SI unit: Pa·s = kg·m⁻¹·s⁻¹), $v$ is the fluid velocity, and $y$ is the coordinate perpendicular to flow. A larger $\eta$ means greater resistance: honey ($\eta \approx 10\ \mathrm{Pa{\cdot}s}$) resists flow far more than water ($\eta \approx 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{Pa{\cdot}s}$) under the same velocity gradient.

Worked Example. Two horizontal plates are separated by a gap $d = 1.0\ \mathrm{mm}$ filled with engine oil ($\eta = 0.20\ \mathrm{Pa{\cdot}s}$). The upper plate moves at $v = 0.50\ \mathrm{m/s}$ while the lower plate remains stationary, establishing a linear velocity profile. Find the shear stress on the lower plate.

The velocity gradient is:

$$ \frac{dv}{dy} = \frac{v - 0}{d} = \frac{0.50\ \mathrm{m/s}}{1.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m}} = 500\ \mathrm{s^{-1}} $$

Applying Newton's viscosity law:

$$ \tau = \eta \frac{dv}{dy} = (0.20\ \mathrm{Pa{\cdot}s})(500\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}) = 100\ \mathrm{Pa} $$

Key Theorem — Poiseuille's Law. For steady laminar flow of a viscous fluid through a cylindrical pipe of radius $R$ and length $L$ under pressure difference $\Delta P$, the volumetric flow rate $Q$ is given by:

$$ Q = \frac{\pi R^4 \Delta P}{8 \eta L} $$

This result is derived by integrating the parabolic velocity profile $v(r) = \frac{\Delta P}{4\eta L}(R^2 - r^2)$ over the pipe cross-section. The fourth-power dependence on radius is the key consequence: halving the pipe radius reduces flow rate by a factor of sixteen at fixed $\Delta P$ and $\eta$, which explains why even modest arterial narrowing substantially restricts blood flow.

🍃 Flow Resistance

🍃 Flow Resistance

Definition. Flow resistance, often called hydraulic resistance, quantifies the opposition a fluid encounters as it moves through a conduit. For viscous, incompressible flow through a cylindrical tube under steady-state conditions, the resistance $R$ is defined by the Hagen–Poiseuille relation:

$$ R = \frac{8\eta L}{\pi r^4} $$

where $\eta$ is the dynamic viscosity of the fluid (in Pa·s), $L$ is the tube length, and $r$ is the inner radius. This expression arises from balancing the pressure-driven force against viscous drag at the tube wall. The volumetric flow rate $Q$ relates to the pressure difference $\Delta P$ across the tube through the hydraulic analogue of Ohm's law:

$$ Q = \frac{\Delta P}{R} $$

This structural parallel with electrical circuits—where flow rate corresponds to current and pressure difference to voltage—allows series and parallel resistance combinations to be analyzed using identical rules.

Worked Example. Blood flows through an arteriole of radius $r = 2.0 \times 10^{-5}$ m and length $L = 1.5 \times 10^{-3}$ m. The viscosity of blood is $\eta = 2.7 \times 10^{-3}$ Pa·s. Compute the flow resistance and the volumetric flow rate if the pressure drop across the arteriole is $\Delta P = 400$ Pa.

$$ R = \frac{8(2.7 \times 10^{-3})(1.5 \times 10^{-3})}{\pi (2.0 \times 10^{-5})^4} = \frac{3.24 \times 10^{-5}}{\pi \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 6.45 \times 10^{13} \, \text{Pa·s/m}^3 $$ $$ Q = \frac{400}{6.45 \times 10^{13}} \approx 6.2 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{m}^3/\text{s} $$

Key Theorem. The fourth-power dependence on radius is the central result of the Hagen–Poiseuille analysis. A factor-of-two reduction in vessel radius increases resistance by a factor of sixteen, which explains why arterial narrowing (stenosis) dramatically reduces blood flow even when the change in diameter appears modest.

🍃 Laminar Flow

🍃 Laminar Flow

Definition. Laminar flow is a regime of fluid motion in which every fluid particle travels along a smooth, well-defined path called a streamline, and adjacent layers of fluid slide past one another without macroscopic mixing. In laminar flow the velocity field is steady: at any fixed point in space, the fluid velocity $\mathbf{v}$ does not fluctuate in time. This orderly behavior stands in contrast to turbulent flow, where chaotic eddies cause rapid, irregular velocity fluctuations throughout the fluid.

The dimensionless Reynolds number governs the transition between the two regimes:

$$ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\eta} $$

where $\rho$ is the fluid density $(\text{kg/m}^3)$, $v$ the mean flow speed $(\text{m/s})$, $D$ the characteristic length (pipe diameter for internal flow, in meters), and $\eta$ the dynamic viscosity $(\text{Pa·s})$. For flow through a cylindrical pipe, laminar conditions persist when $Re \lesssim 2000$; above roughly $4000$ the flow becomes fully turbulent.

Worked Example. Water ($\rho = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3$, $\eta = 1.00 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{Pa·s}$) flows through a tube of diameter $D = 4.0\ \text{mm}$ at a mean speed $v = 0.30\ \text{m/s}$. Determine whether the flow is laminar.

$$ Re = \frac{(1000)(0.30)(4.0 \times 10^{-3})}{1.00 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1.2}{1.00 \times 10^{-3}} = 1200 $$

Since $Re = 1200 < 2000$, the flow is laminar. Under these conditions the velocity profile across the tube cross-section is parabolic, with fluid at the centerline moving fastest and fluid at the wall stationary (no-slip condition):

$$ v(r) = \frac{\Delta P}{4\eta L}\left(R^2 - r^2\right) $$

where $R$ is the tube radius, $r$ the radial distance from the centerline, $L$ the tube length, and $\Delta P$ the pressure difference driving the flow.

Key Theorem (Hagen–Poiseuille Law). For steady, incompressible, laminar flow through a cylindrical tube of radius $R$ and length $L$, integrating the parabolic profile over the cross-section yields the volumetric flow rate:

$$ Q = \frac{\pi R^4 \Delta P}{8 \eta L} $$

The fourth-power dependence on radius is clinically significant: halving a blood vessel's radius reduces flow by a factor of 16 for the same driving pressure.

🍃 Poiseuilles Law

🍃 Poiseuille's Law

Definition. Poiseuille's Law describes the volumetric flow rate of a viscous, incompressible fluid undergoing steady laminar flow through a cylindrical pipe. For a pipe of radius $R$ and length $L$ with a pressure difference $\Delta P$ across its ends and a fluid of dynamic viscosity $\eta$, the volumetric flow rate $Q$ is:

$$ Q = \frac{\pi R^4 \Delta P}{8 \eta L} $$

This result follows from solving the Navier–Stokes equations under the assumptions of axial symmetry, no-slip boundary conditions at the pipe wall, and fully developed laminar flow. The velocity profile takes a parabolic form: $v(r) = \frac{\Delta P}{4\eta L}(R^2 - r^2)$, where $r$ is the radial distance from the centerpiece. Integrating this profile over the cross-section yields Poiseuille's Law.

Worked Example. Blood flows through an arteriole of radius $R = 0.50 \times 10^{-3}$ m and length $L = 0.020$ m. The pressure difference is $\Delta P = 400$ Pa, and blood's dynamic viscosity is $\eta = 2.5 \times 10^{-3}$ Pa·s. Find the volumetric flow rate.

$$ Q = \frac{\pi (0.50 \times 10^{-3})^4 (400)}{8 (2.5 \times 10^{-3})(0.020)} $$ $$ Q = \frac{\pi (6.25 \times 10^{-13})(400)}{4.0 \times 10^{-4}} = \frac{\pi \times 2.5 \times 10^{-10}}{4.0 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 1.96 \times 10^{-6} \ \text{m}^3/\text{s} $$

Key Theorem: Fourth-Power Radius Dependence. The most important feature of Poiseuille's Law is its $R^4$ dependence. Doubling a pipe's radius increases flow rate by a factor of $2^4 = 16$ for the same pressure gradient. This has profound physiological implications: arterial constriction dramatically reduces blood flow, and small changes in vessel radius require large compensatory pressure changes to maintain perfusion.

Lab Exercise. Set up a horizontal tube connected to a fluid reservoir at fixed height $h$ (so $\Delta P = \rho g h$). Plot $\ln Q$ versus $\ln R$ and verify the slope equals $4.0 \pm 0.1$, confirming the fourth-power law.

🌸 Blood Flow Regulation

🌸 Blood Flow Regulation

Definition. Blood flow regulation refers to the mechanisms by which the cardiovascular system maintains appropriate volumetric flow rates through vascular networks in response to physiological demands. From a fluid mechanics perspective, blood flow through a vessel is governed by the Hagen–Poiseuille law:

$$ Q = \frac{\pi r^4 \Delta P}{8 \eta L} $$

where $Q$ is the volumetric flow rate (m³/s), $r$ is the vessel radius, $\Delta P$ is the pressure difference across the vessel length $L$, and $\eta$ is the dynamic viscosity of blood (approximately $3 \times 10^{-3}$ Pa·s for whole blood). The critical observation is the fourth-power dependence on radius: small changes in vessel diameter produce dramatic changes in flow rate. The body exploits this sensitivity through vasodilation and vasoconstriction—smooth muscle contractions that alter $r$ in arterioles to redirect blood according to metabolic need.

Worked Example. Suppose an arteriole of radius $r_0 = 0.10$ mm supplies a resting muscle with flow rate $Q_0$. During exercise, local metabolites trigger vasodilation, increasing the radius by 19% to $r_1 = 0.119$ mm. Holding $\Delta P$, $\eta$, and $L$ constant, the new flow rate is:

$$ \frac{Q_1}{Q_0} = \left(\frac{r_1}{r_0}\right)^4 = (1.19)^4 \approx 2.00 $$

A modest 19% increase in radius doubles the blood supply to the active tissue, illustrating how efficiently the body meets increased oxygen demand without large pressure changes.

Key Theorem. Vascular resistance is defined as $R = 8\eta L / (\pi r^4)$, so that $Q = \Delta P / R$ (the fluid analogue of Ohm's law). For vessels arranged in parallel—as arterioles supplying a capillary bed—total resistance satisfies $1/R_{\text{total}} = \sum_i 1/R_i$. Selective vasodilation of a subset of parallel arterioles therefore reduces total resistance and redistributes flow, explaining how organs receive preferential perfusion during stress or digestion.

Payoff 🎯

Blood flow regulation stands as the unifying capstone of fluid dynamics applied to living systems, synthesizing every principle developed across this chapter — pressure gradients, viscous flow, the Hagen-Poiseuille relation, Bernoulli's equation, and the mechanics of elastic vessels — into a single, coherent physiological framework. The central achievement of this concept is explaining how the cardiovascular system maintains adequate perfusion of every tissue under wildly varying metabolic demands, from rest to maximal exertion, through precise, layered control of vascular resistance.

The governing relationship is derived from Poiseuille's law for laminar viscous flow through a cylindrical tube:

$$ Q = \frac{\pi r^4 \Delta P}{8 \eta L} $$

where $Q$ is volumetric flow rate, $r$ the vessel radius, $\Delta P$ the pressure difference across the vessel segment, $\eta$ the dynamic viscosity of blood, and $L$ the vessel length. Because flow scales as the fourth power of radius, even a modest 19% vasoconstriction — reducing $r$ by a factor of $0.81$ — halves blood flow through that vessel ($0.81^4 \approx 0.43$). Smooth muscle tone in arteriolar walls is therefore the dominant control variable; the nervous and endocrine systems modulate it continuously.

Worked example. During moderate exercise, skeletal muscle oxygen consumption rises tenfold. Metabolic byproducts ($\text{CO}_2$, $\text{H}^+$, adenosine) trigger local arteriolar dilation, increasing $r$ by 30%. Compute the factor by which local $Q$ increases, assuming $\Delta P$ is held constant by cardiac output adjustment:

$$ \frac{Q_\text{new}}{Q_\text{rest}} = \left(\frac{1.30\,r}{r}\right)^4 = 1.30^4 \approx 2.86 $$

Flow nearly triples in the active muscle while the cardiac output redistribution simultaneously reduces flow to the viscera — a coordinated systemic response emergent from local physics.

Key theorem (flow-resistance analogy). The total vascular bed obeys a circuit analogy: vessels in series add resistances $R_i = 8\eta L_i / \pi r_i^4$, while parallel branches reduce net resistance, exactly as electrical resistors do. Mean arterial pressure $\bar{P} = Q_\text{cardiac} \cdot R_\text{total}$ connects macroscopic hemodynamics to microscopic vessel geometry.
