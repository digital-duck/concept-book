🍃 Bernoulli's Equation

Definition. Bernoulli's equation is a statement of energy conservation applied to steady, incompressible, non-viscous flow along a streamline. For a fluid of density $\rho$ moving with speed $v$ at height $y$ and pressure $P$, the equation states:

$$ P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g y = \text{constant} $$

Equivalently, between any two points along a streamline:

$$ P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g y_1 = P_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g y_2 $$

The three terms represent, respectively, the static pressure energy per unit volume, the kinetic energy per unit volume, and the gravitational potential energy per unit volume. The equation is derived by applying the work-energy theorem to a fluid parcel: the net work done by pressure forces equals the change in kinetic and potential energy of that parcel.

Proof sketch. Consider a fluid element of volume $\Delta V$ moving from point 1 to point 2. The pressure force at the inlet does positive work $P_1 \Delta V$, while the pressure at the outlet does negative work $-P_2 \Delta V$. By the work-energy theorem, the net work equals the change in mechanical energy:

$$ P_1 \Delta V - P_2 \Delta V = \Delta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\rho \Delta V\, v^2\right) + \Delta(\rho \Delta V\, g y) $$

Dividing through by $\Delta V$ and rearranging yields Bernoulli's equation.

Worked example. Water flows through a horizontal pipe that narrows from a cross-sectional area $A_1 = 0.04\ \text{m}^2$ to $A_2 = 0.01\ \text{m}^2$. The inlet speed is $v_1 = 2\ \text{m/s}$ and the inlet gauge pressure is $P_1 = 3.0 \times 10^4\ \text{Pa}$. Find $P_2$.

By continuity, $v_2 = v_1 A_1/A_2 = 2 \times 4 = 8\ \text{m/s}$. Since $y_1 = y_2$:

$$ P_2 = P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(v_1^2 - v_2^2) = 3.0\times10^4 + \tfrac{1}{2}(1000)(4 - 64) = 3.0\times10^4 - 3.0\times10^4 = 0\ \text{Pa} $$

The pressure drops to zero gauge pressure at the constriction, illustrating the inverse relationship between speed and pressure.

Key theorem. Bernoulli's equation subsumes free-fall kinematics as a special case. For a tank open to the atmosphere with a small orifice at depth $h$ below the surface, setting $P_1 = P_2 = P_\text{atm}$ and $v_1 \approx 0$ (large reservoir) reduces the equation to $\rho g h = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2$, giving the efflux speed $v = \sqrt{2gh}$—identical to the speed acquired by a body falling freely through height $h$.

Lab exercise. Connect two manometer taps to a converging–diverging nozzle carrying water at a measured flow rate. Record $P$, $v$, and $y$ at three cross-sections and verify that $P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g y$ remains constant to within experimental uncertainty.
