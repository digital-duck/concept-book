🌸 Normal Modes

A normal mode of oscillation describes a specific pattern of vibration or motion that occurs within a linear, coupled physical system such that all components oscillate independently at a single, characteristic frequency. When a system is excited in one of its normal modes, the resulting motion is purely harmonic and requires no continuous external forcing to maintain that specific pattern. Mathematically, these modes correspond to the eigenvectors of the generalized mass-stiffness matrix governing the system's dynamics, and their associated frequencies are the eigenvalues. The existence of normal coordinates allows us to decouple a set of coupled equations of motion into a set of independent, uncoupled simple harmonic oscillator equations.

Worked Example: Coupled Oscillators. Consider two masses, $m_1$ and $m_2$, connected by springs with stiffness constants $k_1, k_2,$ and $k_{coupling}$, respectively. Let the displacements be $x_1(t)$ and $x_2(t)$. The equations of motion are coupled second-order differential equations:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} m_1 & 0 \\ 0 & m_2 \end{pmatrix} \ddot{\mathbf{x}} = - \left[ \begin{pmatrix} k_1 & -k_{coupling} \\ -k_{coupling} & k_2 \end{pmatrix} \right] \mathbf{x} $$

We seek solutions of the form $\mathbf{x}(t) = \mathbf{A} \cos(\omega t)$. This yields a characteristic equation involving the stiffness and mass matrices, which must be satisfied by the normal mode frequencies $\omega_n^2$. The resulting eigenvectors $\mathbf{A}_n$ define the spatial pattern (the amplitudes $A_{1,n}$ and $A_{2,n}$) for that specific normal mode.
