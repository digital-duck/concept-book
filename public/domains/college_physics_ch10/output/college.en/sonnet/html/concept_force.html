🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. In classical mechanics, a force is a vector quantity that describes an interaction capable of changing the state of motion of an object. Formally, Newton's Second Law defines net force as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}, $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass (kg) and $\vec{a}$ is the acceleration of the object ($\text{m/s}^2$). The SI unit of force is the newton: $1\ \text{N} = 1\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Force is additive; when multiple forces act on a body, the resultant is their vector sum $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_i \vec{F}_i$.

Worked Example. A 5.0 kg block rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}$. Find the acceleration.

Step 1 — compute the net force:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (12 - 4)\ \hat{x}\ \text{N} = 8\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}. $$

Step 2 — apply Newton's Second Law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8\ \hat{x}}{5.0}\ \text{m/s}^2 = 1.6\ \hat{x}\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

The block accelerates at $1.6\ \text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction.

Key Theorem — Newton's Third Law. For every force exerted by object $A$ on object $B$, there exists an equal and opposite reaction force exerted by $B$ on $A$:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A}. $$

This law is not a statement about equilibrium; the paired forces act on different bodies and therefore do not cancel in any single free-body diagram. Identifying third-law pairs correctly is essential when analyzing systems of interacting objects, such as a rocket expelling exhaust or a person pushing against a wall.

Lab Exercise. Mount a low-friction dynamics cart on a level track and attach a string over a pulley to a hanging mass $m_h$. Using a force sensor on the cart and a motion detector at the track's end, record the net force and resulting acceleration for three different hanging masses. Plot $F_{\text{net}}$ versus $a$ and determine the cart's inertial mass from the slope. Compare this value with a direct balance measurement and compute the percent discrepancy, discussing potential sources of error such as pulley friction and string mass.
