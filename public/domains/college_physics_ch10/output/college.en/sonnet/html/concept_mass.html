🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. Mass is a fundamental scalar quantity that measures the amount of matter contained in an object and quantifies its resistance to changes in translational motion. In classical mechanics, two distinct but empirically equivalent concepts of mass arise. Inertial mass $m_i$ is defined operationally through Newton's second law,

$$ \mathbf{F} = m_i \mathbf{a}, $$

where $\mathbf{F}$ is the net applied force and $\mathbf{a}$ is the resulting acceleration. Gravitational mass $m_g$ characterizes the strength of an object's coupling to a gravitational field, appearing in Newton's law of universal gravitation:

$$ F_g = G\frac{m_g M_g}{r^2}. $$

The equivalence principle, confirmed experimentally to one part in $10^{12}$ by Eötvös-type experiments, asserts $m_i = m_g$, a cornerstone underlying general relativity. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg), defined since 2019 by fixing the Planck constant $h = 6.62607015 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$.

Worked Example. A block of unknown mass rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A constant horizontal force $F = 12.0\ \text{N}$ is applied, and the block accelerates at $a = 3.00\ \text{m/s}^2$. Applying Newton's second law:

$$ m = \frac{F}{a} = \frac{12.0\ \text{N}}{3.00\ \text{m/s}^2} = 4.00\ \text{kg}. $$

The weight of this block near Earth's surface ($g = 9.81\ \text{m/s}^2$) is then $W = mg = (4.00)(9.81) = 39.2\ \text{N}$, illustrating the distinction between mass (intrinsic, invariant) and weight (context-dependent force).

Key Theorem — Conservation of Mass (Non-relativistic). In any closed system undergoing collisions or interactions at speeds $v \operatorname{ll} c$, the total mass is conserved:

$$ \sum_i m_i = \text{constant}. $$

This follows from Newton's third law and the additivity of inertial mass. At relativistic speeds, mass conservation is subsumed by conservation of the total relativistic energy–momentum four-vector.

Lab Exercise. Using a dynamics cart, a pulley, and a set of calibrated hanging masses on an air track, measure the acceleration of the system for five different hanging-mass values while keeping the total system mass constant. Plot $a$ versus the net driving force $F = m_\text{hang} g$, and extract the total system inertial mass from the slope $m = \Delta F / \Delta a$. Compare this value to the directly measured mass using a digital balance and compute the percent discrepancy.
