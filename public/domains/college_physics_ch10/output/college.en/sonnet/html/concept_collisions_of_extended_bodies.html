🌸 Collisions Of Extended Bodies

Definition. When two extended rigid bodies collide, the interaction transfers both linear momentum and angular momentum. Unlike point-particle collisions, extended bodies possess rotational degrees of freedom, so a collision can simultaneously alter the center-of-mass velocity and the angular velocity of each body. The single new quantity entering this analysis is the total angular momentum of the system, which decomposes into a spin part and an orbital part:

$$ \vec{L}_\text{total} = \sum_i I_i \omega_i + \sum_i m_i (\vec{r}_{cm,i} \times \vec{v}_{cm,i}) $$

For an isolated system, both total linear momentum and total angular momentum are independently conserved. For a perfectly inelastic off-center collision in which object 2 is initially at rest, these two laws apply simultaneously:

$$ mv_0 = (m_1 + m_2)v_{cm}', \qquad L_{cm} = I_{total}\,\omega' $$

Worked Example. A uniform thin rod of mass $M = 2.0\ \text{kg}$ and length $L = 1.0\ \text{m}$ lies at rest on a frictionless surface. A clay ball of mass $m = 0.50\ \text{kg}$ traveling at $v_0 = 4.0\ \text{m/s}$ strikes the rod's end and sticks to it. Find the post-collision center-of-mass speed and angular velocity about the new center of mass.

The new center of mass lies at $d = mL/[2(M+m)] = (0.50)(0.50)/2.5 = 0.10\ \text{m}$ from the rod's original center. Linear momentum conservation gives:

$$ v_{cm}' = \frac{mv_0}{M + m} = \frac{(0.50)(4.0)}{2.5} = 0.80\ \text{m/s} $$

Angular momentum about the new center of mass is conserved (no external torques). The ball strikes at $r_{ball} = L/2 - d = 0.50 - 0.10 = 0.40\ \text{m}$ from the new center of mass:

$$ mv_0\,r_{ball} = I_{total}\,\omega' $$ $$ I_{total} = \tfrac{1}{12}ML^2 + M(0.10)^2 + m(0.40)^2 = 0.167 + 0.020 + 0.080 = 0.267\ \text{kg·m}^2 $$ $$ \omega' = \frac{(0.50)(4.0)(0.40)}{0.267} \approx 3.0\ \text{rad/s} $$

Key Theorem. In any isolated collision of extended bodies, both total linear momentum and total angular momentum about any fixed point are independently conserved, regardless of whether the collision is elastic or inelastic.

Lab Exercise. Mount a pivoted meter stick horizontally on an air table. Launch a steel ball to strike the stick at varying distances from the pivot. Measure $\omega'$ with a photogate and compare against the prediction $\omega' = mv_0 d / I_{pivot}$. Identify which impact point maximizes $\omega'$ and explain physically why collisions at the "sweet spot" produce no impulsive pivot reaction.
