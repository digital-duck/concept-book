🍃 Conservation Of Angular Momentum

🍃 Conservation Of Angular Momentum

Definition. The angular momentum of a system about a fixed axis is defined as $L = I\omega$, where $I$ is the moment of inertia and $\omega$ is the angular velocity. The conservation of angular momentum states that if the net external torque acting on a system is zero, the total angular momentum of the system remains constant in time:

$$ \frac{dL}{dt} = \tau_{\text{net}} \implies \tau_{\text{net}} = 0 \Rightarrow L_i = L_f $$

That is, $I_i \omega_i = I_f \omega_f$. This follows directly from Newton's second law in rotational form, $\tau_{\text{net}} = dL/dt$, applied to an isolated or torque-free system. The law holds for both rigid bodies and deformable systems, provided all internal torques cancel by Newton's third law.

Worked Example. A figure skater begins a spin with arms extended, giving a moment of inertia $I_i = 4.0\ \text{kg·m}^2$ and angular velocity $\omega_i = 2.0\ \text{rad/s}$. She pulls her arms inward, reducing her moment of inertia to $I_f = 1.0\ \text{kg·m}^2$. Find her final angular velocity.

Since no external torque acts (friction at the skate blade is negligible and acts through the axis), angular momentum is conserved:

$$ I_i \omega_i = I_f \omega_f \implies \omega_f = \frac{I_i \omega_i}{I_f} = \frac{(4.0)(2.0)}{1.0} = 8.0\ \text{rad/s} $$

Her spin rate quadruples. Note that her rotational kinetic energy $K = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$ increases from $8.0\ \text{J}$ to $32.0\ \text{J}$; this energy comes from the internal work done by her muscles.

Key Theorem. For a system of particles, the total angular momentum $\mathbf{L} = \sum_i \mathbf{r}_i \times \mathbf{p}_i$ is conserved whenever $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net,ext}} = \mathbf{0}$. This is a vector statement — each component of $\mathbf{L}$ is independently conserved along axes where the corresponding torque component vanishes.

Lab Exercise. Mount a freely rotating platform (low-friction turntable) with a student holding two dumbbells at arm's length. Measure $\omega_i$ with a tachometer. Have the student pull the dumbbells to their chest and record $\omega_f$. Estimate $I_i$ and $I_f$ from body measurements, then verify $I_i\omega_i \approx I_f\omega_f$ and compute the percentage discrepancy attributable to bearing friction.
