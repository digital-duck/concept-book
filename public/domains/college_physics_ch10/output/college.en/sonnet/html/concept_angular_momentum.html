🍃 Angular Momentum

🍃 Angular Momentum

Definition. The angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ of a particle about a reference point $O$ is defined as the cross product

$$ \mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}, $$

where $\mathbf{r}$ is the position vector from $O$ to the particle and $\mathbf{p}$ is the particle's linear momentum. The SI unit of angular momentum is $\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s}$. The magnitude is $L = p\,r\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{p}$. For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis with moment of inertia $I$ and angular velocity $\omega$, the scalar form reduces to

$$ L = I\omega. $$

Worked Example. A figure skater of moment of inertia $I_1 = 4.0\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$ spins at $\omega_1 = 2.0\ \text{rad/s}$ with arms extended. She pulls her arms inward, reducing her moment of inertia to $I_2 = 1.6\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$. Find her new angular velocity $\omega_2$.

Applying conservation of angular momentum (no external torques act):

$$ I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2 $$ $$ \omega_2 = \frac{I_1\omega_1}{I_2} = \frac{(4.0)(2.0)}{1.6} = 5.0\ \text{rad/s}. $$

Her spin rate increases by a factor of 2.5, illustrating that reducing $I$ forces $\omega$ upward to conserve $L$.

Key Theorem — Conservation of Angular Momentum. If the net external torque on a system is zero,

$$ \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt} = \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{L} = \text{constant}. $$

This follows directly from Newton's second law in rotational form: $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} = d\mathbf{L}/dt$. The conservation law is independent of internal forces and holds even when the moment of inertia changes, making it powerful for analyzing systems with variable geometry such as collapsing stars, divers, and gyroscopes.

Lab Exercise. Mount a bicycle wheel on a frictionless axle and spin it to a measured $\omega$. Calculate $L = I\omega$ using the wheel's known moment of inertia. Then tilt the spin axis by $90°$ while seated on a rotating platform and observe the platform's response. Measure the platform's angular velocity and verify that the total angular momentum of the wheel–platform system remains conserved throughout the maneuver.
