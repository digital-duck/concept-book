🌸 Gyroscopic Precession

🌸 Gyroscopic Precession

Definition. When a spinning body (a gyroscope) experiences an external torque $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ perpendicular to its angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$, the spin axis does not tilt in the direction of the torque; instead, it rotates about a perpendicular axis in a phenomenon called gyroscopic precession. The precession angular velocity is

$$ \boldsymbol{\Omega} = \frac{\boldsymbol{\tau}}{L} = \frac{\boldsymbol{\tau}}{I\omega}, $$

where $I$ is the moment of inertia of the rotor about its spin axis and $\omega$ is its spin rate. More precisely, Newton's second law for rotation gives $\boldsymbol{\tau} = d\mathbf{L}/dt$, and for a torque that remains perpendicular to $\mathbf{L}$, the magnitude $L = I\omega$ is constant while the direction of $\mathbf{L}$ changes at the rate $\Omega = \tau / L$.

Worked Example. A bicycle wheel of mass $m = 1.5\ \text{kg}$ and radius $r = 0.35\ \text{m}$ spins at $\omega = 120\ \text{rad s}^{-1}$. Its axle is held horizontal, with one end resting on a pivot located $d = 0.25\ \text{m}$ from the wheel's center. Find the precession rate.

The gravitational torque about the pivot is $\tau = mgd = (1.5)(9.8)(0.25) = 3.675\ \text{N·m}$. Modeling the wheel as a thin ring, $I = mr^2 = (1.5)(0.35)^2 = 0.184\ \text{kg·m}^2$, so $L = I\omega = (0.184)(120) = 22.1\ \text{kg·m}^2\text{s}^{-1}$. The precession rate is

$$ \Omega = \frac{\tau}{L} = \frac{3.675}{22.1} \approx 0.166\ \text{rad s}^{-1} \approx 9.5\ \text{°/s}. $$

The axle sweeps slowly around a horizontal circle — far slower than the spin itself.

Key Theorem. The precession angular velocity $\Omega = \tau / (I\omega)$ is inversely proportional to both $I$ and $\omega$. Consequently, a faster or more massive rotor precesses more slowly under the same applied torque — a result that underpins the stability of spinning tops, gyrocompasses, and spacecraft attitude control systems.

Lab Exercise. Mount a spinning bicycle wheel on a rope attached to one end of its axle. Measure $\Omega$ with a stopwatch over several complete horizontal circuits. Vary $\omega$ by changing how hard you spin the wheel and record $\Omega$ at each spin rate. Plot $\Omega$ versus $1/\omega$ and verify that the relationship is linear with slope $\tau/I$, confirming the theoretical prediction.
