🍃 Torque Angular Momentum Relation

🍃 Torque Angular Momentum Relation

Definition. Recall that the angular momentum of a particle about a fixed point is $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}$, and for a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis, $L = I\omega$. The single new primitive introduced here is the torque–angular momentum relation: the net external torque acting on a system equals the time rate of change of its total angular momentum,

$$ \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt}. $$

This is the rotational analogue of Newton's second law $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = d\mathbf{p}/dt$, and it is derived from that law by differentiating $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}$ with respect to time and using the fact that $\dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \mathbf{p} = \mathbf{0}$ (since $\dot{\mathbf{r}} \parallel \mathbf{p}$). For a rigid body with constant moment of inertia, the relation reduces to $\tau_{\text{net}} = I\alpha$, where $\alpha = d\omega/dt$.

Worked Example. A solid disk of mass $M = 4.0\ \text{kg}$ and radius $R = 0.30\ \text{m}$ is initially at rest. A constant tangential force $F = 10\ \text{N}$ is applied at its rim. Find the angular momentum after $t = 3.0\ \text{s}$.

The moment of inertia of a solid disk is $I = \tfrac{1}{2}MR^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(4.0)(0.09) = 0.18\ \text{kg·m}^2$. The applied torque is $\tau = FR = (10)(0.30) = 3.0\ \text{N·m}$. Since $\tau = dL/dt$ and $L(0) = 0$:

$$ L(t) = \tau \cdot t = (3.0)(3.0) = 9.0\ \text{kg·m}^2/\text{s}. $$

Key Theorem. When the net external torque on a system is zero ($\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} = \mathbf{0}$), the torque–angular momentum relation immediately gives $d\mathbf{L}/dt = \mathbf{0}$, so $\mathbf{L} = \text{const}$. Conservation of angular momentum follows as a direct corollary and governs phenomena from spinning ice skaters to orbital mechanics — if a skater pulls in their arms, $I$ decreases, so $\omega$ must increase to maintain constant $L$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a bicycle wheel on a frictionless axle and measure its angular velocity $\omega_0$ using a stroboscope. Apply a known tangential force $F$ at radius $R$ for a measured time interval $\Delta t$. Predict the new angular momentum $L_f = I\omega_0 + \tau\,\Delta t$ and compare with the measured $\omega_f$. Assess whether systematic deviations correlate with bearing friction, quantifying it as an opposing torque.
