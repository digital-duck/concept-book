🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. Mass is a fundamental scalar quantity that measures the amount of matter contained in an object and quantifies its resistance to changes in translational motion. In classical mechanics, two distinct but empirically equivalent concepts of mass arise. Inertial mass $m_i$ is defined operationally through Newton's second law,

$$ \mathbf{F} = m_i \mathbf{a}, $$

where $\mathbf{F}$ is the net applied force and $\mathbf{a}$ is the resulting acceleration. Gravitational mass $m_g$ characterizes the strength of an object's coupling to a gravitational field, appearing in Newton's law of universal gravitation:

$$ F_g = G\frac{m_g M_g}{r^2}. $$

The equivalence principle, confirmed experimentally to one part in $10^{12}$ by Eötvös-type experiments, asserts $m_i = m_g$, a cornerstone underlying general relativity. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg), defined since 2019 by fixing the Planck constant $h = 6.62607015 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$.

Worked Example. A block of unknown mass rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A constant horizontal force $F = 12.0\ \text{N}$ is applied, and the block accelerates at $a = 3.00\ \text{m/s}^2$. Applying Newton's second law:

$$ m = \frac{F}{a} = \frac{12.0\ \text{N}}{3.00\ \text{m/s}^2} = 4.00\ \text{kg}. $$

The weight of this block near Earth's surface ($g = 9.81\ \text{m/s}^2$) is then $W = mg = (4.00)(9.81) = 39.2\ \text{N}$, illustrating the distinction between mass (intrinsic, invariant) and weight (context-dependent force).

Key Theorem — Conservation of Mass (Non-relativistic). In any closed system undergoing collisions or interactions at speeds $v \operatorname{ll} c$, the total mass is conserved:

$$ \sum_i m_i = \text{constant}. $$

This follows from Newton's third law and the additivity of inertial mass. At relativistic speeds, mass conservation is subsumed by conservation of the total relativistic energy–momentum four-vector.

🌱 Angular Velocity

🌱 Angular Velocity

Definition. Angular velocity describes the rate at which a rigid body rotates about a fixed axis. For a particle moving along a circular path, let $\theta$ denote the angular position measured in radians from a reference direction. The instantaneous angular velocity $\omega$ is defined as

$$ \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt}, $$

with SI units of radians per second ($\text{rad/s}$). Angular velocity is a vector quantity; its direction is given by the right-hand rule—curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of rotation, and the extended thumb points along $\boldsymbol{\omega}$. For rotation in the $xy$-plane, counterclockwise motion yields $\boldsymbol{\omega} = \omega\,\hat{k}$ (positive), while clockwise motion yields a negative component along $\hat{k}$.

When $\omega$ is constant, the motion is called uniform circular motion, and the angular displacement over a time interval $\Delta t$ is simply $\Delta\theta = \omega\,\Delta t$.

Worked Example. A grinding wheel starts from rest and reaches an angular speed of $120\,\text{rpm}$ in $4.0\,\text{s}$ under constant angular acceleration. Convert the final angular velocity to SI units and find the angular displacement.

Converting: $\omega_f = 120 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 4\pi \approx 12.6\,\text{rad/s}$.

Since $\omega_0 = 0$ and acceleration is constant, the average angular velocity is $\bar{\omega} = \frac{\omega_0 + \omega_f}{2} = 2\pi\,\text{rad/s}$. The total angular displacement is

$$ \Delta\theta = \bar{\omega}\,\Delta t = 2\pi \times 4.0 = 8\pi \approx 25.1\,\text{rad}, $$

corresponding to exactly four complete revolutions.

Key Theorem. The relationship between angular velocity and the linear (tangential) speed $v$ of a point at radius $r$ from the rotation axis is

$$ v = r\omega. $$

This follows directly from the arc-length definition $s = r\theta$, differentiated with respect to time. Points farther from the axis move faster, even though all points on a rigid body share the same $\omega$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a bicycle wheel on a frictionless axle and mark a point on the rim. Using a stroboscope or high-speed camera, measure the time $T$ for ten complete revolutions. Compute $\omega = 2\pi/T$ and compare it to the linear speed of the rim point measured by timing the passage of the mark across a fixed reference. Verify that $v = r\omega$ holds to within experimental uncertainty, and discuss sources of error including axle friction and parallax in angle measurement.

🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. In classical mechanics, a force is a vector quantity that describes an interaction capable of changing the state of motion of an object. Formally, Newton's Second Law defines net force as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}, $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass (kg) and $\vec{a}$ is the acceleration of the object ($\text{m/s}^2$). The SI unit of force is the newton: $1\ \text{N} = 1\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Force is additive; when multiple forces act on a body, the resultant is their vector sum $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_i \vec{F}_i$.

Worked Example. A 5.0 kg block rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}$. Find the acceleration.

Step 1 — compute the net force:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (12 - 4)\ \hat{x}\ \text{N} = 8\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}. $$

Step 2 — apply Newton's Second Law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8\ \hat{x}}{5.0}\ \text{m/s}^2 = 1.6\ \hat{x}\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

The block accelerates at $1.6\ \text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction.

Key Theorem — Newton's Third Law. For every force exerted by object $A$ on object $B$, there exists an equal and opposite reaction force exerted by $B$ on $A$:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A}. $$

This law is not a statement about equilibrium; the paired forces act on different bodies and therefore do not cancel in any single free-body diagram. Identifying third-law pairs correctly is essential when analyzing systems of interacting objects, such as a rocket expelling exhaust or a person pushing against a wall.

🍃 Moment Of Inertia

🍃 Moment Of Inertia

Definition. The moment of inertia $I$ of a rigid body about a specified axis is the rotational analog of mass in translational mechanics. For a discrete system of $n$ point masses, it is defined as

$$ I = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i r_i^2, $$

where $m_i$ is the mass of the $i$-th particle and $r_i$ is its perpendicular distance from the rotation axis. For a continuous body with mass density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$, this sum becomes an integral:

$$ I = \int r^2 \, dm = \int r^2 \rho(\mathbf{r}) \, dV. $$

The SI unit of moment of inertia is $\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$. Unlike mass, $I$ depends critically on the choice of axis; the same object can have very different moments of inertia about different axes.

Worked Example. Consider a uniform thin rod of mass $M$ and length $L$ rotating about an axis perpendicular to the rod through one end. Let $x$ measure distance from the pivot, so a differential element $dx$ carries mass $dm = (M/L)\,dx$. Then

$$ I_{\text{end}} = \int_0^L x^2 \frac{M}{L}\,dx = \frac{M}{L}\cdot\frac{L^3}{3} = \frac{1}{3}ML^2. $$

Had the axis passed through the center of mass instead, the integration limits shift to $-L/2$ to $L/2$, yielding $I_{\text{cm}} = \frac{1}{12}ML^2$, a smaller value—confirming that rotating about a more central axis requires less torque for the same angular acceleration.

Key Theorem (Parallel-Axis Theorem). If $I_{\text{cm}}$ is the moment of inertia about an axis through the center of mass, then the moment of inertia about any parallel axis displaced by distance $d$ is

$$ I = I_{\text{cm}} + Md^2. $$

This follows directly from expanding $r^2 = |\mathbf{r}_{\text{cm}} + \mathbf{d}|^2$ in the integral and noting that the cross term vanishes by definition of the center of mass. The theorem eliminates the need to re-derive $I$ from scratch for every possible axis.

Lab Exercise. Set a solid disk (mass $M$, radius $R$) on a low-friction axle through its center. Hang a known mass $m$ from a string wrapped around the axle (radius $r_0$). Measure the linear acceleration $a$ of the hanging mass using a motion sensor. Apply Newton's second law to the hanging mass ($mg - T = ma$) and the rotational equation ($Tr_0 = I\alpha$, with $\alpha = a/r_0$) to solve for $I_{\text{exp}}$. Compare to the theoretical value $I_{\text{theory}} = \frac{1}{2}MR^2$ and compute the percent discrepancy, accounting for axle friction if necessary.

🍃 Angular Momentum

🍃 Angular Momentum

Definition. The angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ of a particle about a reference point $O$ is defined as the cross product

$$ \mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}, $$

where $\mathbf{r}$ is the position vector from $O$ to the particle and $\mathbf{p}$ is the particle's linear momentum. The SI unit of angular momentum is $\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s}$. The magnitude is $L = p\,r\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{p}$. For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis with moment of inertia $I$ and angular velocity $\omega$, the scalar form reduces to

$$ L = I\omega. $$

Worked Example. A figure skater of moment of inertia $I_1 = 4.0\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$ spins at $\omega_1 = 2.0\ \text{rad/s}$ with arms extended. She pulls her arms inward, reducing her moment of inertia to $I_2 = 1.6\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$. Find her new angular velocity $\omega_2$.

Applying conservation of angular momentum (no external torques act):

$$ I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2 $$ $$ \omega_2 = \frac{I_1\omega_1}{I_2} = \frac{(4.0)(2.0)}{1.6} = 5.0\ \text{rad/s}. $$

Her spin rate increases by a factor of 2.5, illustrating that reducing $I$ forces $\omega$ upward to conserve $L$.

Key Theorem — Conservation of Angular Momentum. If the net external torque on a system is zero,

$$ \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt} = \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{L} = \text{constant}. $$

This follows directly from Newton's second law in rotational form: $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} = d\mathbf{L}/dt$. The conservation law is independent of internal forces and holds even when the moment of inertia changes, making it powerful for analyzing systems with variable geometry such as collapsing stars, divers, and gyroscopes.

Lab Exercise. Mount a bicycle wheel on a frictionless axle and spin it to a measured $\omega$. Calculate $L = I\omega$ using the wheel's known moment of inertia. Then tilt the spin axis by $90°$ while seated on a rotating platform and observe the platform's response. Measure the platform's angular velocity and verify that the total angular momentum of the wheel–platform system remains conserved throughout the maneuver.

🍃 Torque

🍃 Torque

Definition. Torque is the rotational analog of force, quantifying the tendency of an applied force to produce angular acceleration about a specified axis. For a force $\vec{F}$ applied at position vector $\vec{r}$ measured from the pivot, the torque vector is defined by the cross product

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}. $$

Its magnitude is

$$ \tau = rF\sin\theta, $$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$. The SI unit of torque is the newton-meter ($\text{N·m}$). The factor $r\sin\theta$ is called the moment arm (or lever arm) $\ell = r\sin\theta$, the perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of action of the force, so equivalently $\tau = F\ell$.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of length $L = 3.0\ \text{m}$ is pinned at its left end. A downward force $F = 40\ \text{N}$ is applied at the beam's right end. Find the torque about the pin.

Here $r = 3.0\ \text{m}$ and $\theta = 90°$ (force perpendicular to the beam), giving

$$ \tau = rF\sin\theta = (3.0\ \text{m})(40\ \text{N})\sin 90° = 120\ \text{N·m}. $$

By the right-hand rule, this torque points out of the page (counterclockwise tendency). If instead the force is applied at $\theta = 30°$ from the beam axis, the torque reduces to $(3.0)(40)\sin 30° = 60\ \text{N·m}$, illustrating that only the perpendicular component of force is effective.

Key Theorem — Newton's Second Law for Rotation. For a rigid body with moment of inertia $I$ about a fixed axis, the net torque produces angular acceleration $\alpha$ according to

$$ \sum \tau = I\alpha. $$

This is the rotational counterpart of $\sum F = ma$. It implies that for a given net torque, a larger moment of inertia results in smaller angular acceleration, analogous to inertial resistance in linear dynamics.

Lab Exercise. Mount a disk of known moment of inertia on a frictionless axle. Attach a string of radius $r$ wound around the axle, suspending a hanging mass $m$. Measure the angular acceleration $\alpha$ using a rotary encoder. Verify that $mgr = I\alpha + mr^2\alpha$, thereby confirming the rotational form of Newton's second law and extracting an experimental value of $I$.

🍃 Right Hand Rule

🍃 Right Hand Rule

Definition. The right-hand rule is a mnemonic convention for determining the direction of a vector quantity that arises as a cross product. Given two vectors $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, their cross product $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}$ is a vector perpendicular to the plane containing $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, with magnitude $|\mathbf{C}| = |\mathbf{A}||\mathbf{B}|\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the vectors. To find the direction of $\mathbf{C}$, point the fingers of the right hand along $\mathbf{A}$, curl them toward $\mathbf{B}$ through the smaller angle $\theta$, and the extended thumb points in the direction of $\mathbf{C}$. This convention is foundational in rotational mechanics, where angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$, torque $\boldsymbol{\tau}$, and angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ are all axial vectors whose orientation is fixed by the right-hand rule.

Worked Example. A disk rotates counterclockwise when viewed from above, completing one revolution every $2.0\ \text{s}$. Determine the direction and magnitude of the angular velocity vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}$.

The angular speed is $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = \frac{2\pi}{2.0} = \pi\ \text{rad/s}$. To find the direction, curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of rotation (counterclockwise as seen from above). The thumb points upward, along the positive $z$-axis. Therefore:

$$ \boldsymbol{\omega} = \pi\,\hat{z}\ \text{rad/s} $$

Key Theorem. For a rigid body rotating with angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$, the linear velocity of any point at position $\mathbf{r}$ from the rotation axis is:

$$ \mathbf{v} = \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r} $$

The direction of $\mathbf{v}$ follows directly from the right-hand rule applied to $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and $\mathbf{r}$, confirming that points on opposite sides of the axis move in opposite directions—consistent with physical rotation. This relation also underlies the torque equation $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}$, where the order of the cross product matters: reversing it negates the result.

Lab Exercise. Hold a bicycle wheel by its axle with the wheel spinning counterclockwise as viewed from your right. Apply the right-hand rule to determine the direction of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$. Now tilt the axle so the top moves toward you. Using $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}$, predict which way the wheel will precess. Record your prediction, then observe the actual precession direction and reconcile any discrepancy using the angular momentum relation $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt}$.

🌸 Gyroscopic Precession

🌸 Gyroscopic Precession

Definition. When a spinning body (a gyroscope) experiences an external torque $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ perpendicular to its angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$, the spin axis does not tilt in the direction of the torque; instead, it rotates about a perpendicular axis in a phenomenon called gyroscopic precession. The precession angular velocity is

$$ \boldsymbol{\Omega} = \frac{\boldsymbol{\tau}}{L} = \frac{\boldsymbol{\tau}}{I\omega}, $$

where $I$ is the moment of inertia of the rotor about its spin axis and $\omega$ is its spin rate. More precisely, Newton's second law for rotation gives $\boldsymbol{\tau} = d\mathbf{L}/dt$, and for a torque that remains perpendicular to $\mathbf{L}$, the magnitude $L = I\omega$ is constant while the direction of $\mathbf{L}$ changes at the rate $\Omega = \tau / L$.

Worked Example. A bicycle wheel of mass $m = 1.5\ \text{kg}$ and radius $r = 0.35\ \text{m}$ spins at $\omega = 120\ \text{rad s}^{-1}$. Its axle is held horizontal, with one end resting on a pivot located $d = 0.25\ \text{m}$ from the wheel's center. Find the precession rate.

The gravitational torque about the pivot is $\tau = mgd = (1.5)(9.8)(0.25) = 3.675\ \text{N·m}$. Modeling the wheel as a thin ring, $I = mr^2 = (1.5)(0.35)^2 = 0.184\ \text{kg·m}^2$, so $L = I\omega = (0.184)(120) = 22.1\ \text{kg·m}^2\text{s}^{-1}$. The precession rate is

$$ \Omega = \frac{\tau}{L} = \frac{3.675}{22.1} \approx 0.166\ \text{rad s}^{-1} \approx 9.5\ \text{°/s}. $$

The axle sweeps slowly around a horizontal circle — far slower than the spin itself.

Key Theorem. The precession angular velocity $\Omega = \tau / (I\omega)$ is inversely proportional to both $I$ and $\omega$. Consequently, a faster or more massive rotor precesses more slowly under the same applied torque — a result that underpins the stability of spinning tops, gyrocompasses, and spacecraft attitude control systems.

Lab Exercise. Mount a spinning bicycle wheel on a rope attached to one end of its axle. Measure $\Omega$ with a stopwatch over several complete horizontal circuits. Vary $\omega$ by changing how hard you spin the wheel and record $\Omega$ at each spin rate. Plot $\Omega$ versus $1/\omega$ and verify that the relationship is linear with slope $\tau/I$, confirming the theoretical prediction.

Payoff 🎯

Gyroscopic precession stands as the natural culmination of rotational dynamics because it unifies every major thread of the chapter — angular momentum, torque, moment of inertia, and the vector nature of rotation — into a single, counter-intuitive phenomenon that challenges and refines physical intuition. A spinning gyroscope subjected to an external torque does not fall in the direction the torque would naively suggest; instead, the spin axis migrates perpendicular to both the angular momentum vector $\mathbf{L}$ and the applied torque $\boldsymbol{\tau}$. The governing relation,

$$ \boldsymbol{\Omega}_p = \frac{\tau}{L} = \frac{\tau}{I\omega}, $$

where $\boldsymbol{\Omega}_p$ is the precession angular velocity, $I$ the moment of inertia about the spin axis, and $\omega$ the spin rate, encodes the complete interplay of these quantities in one elegant expression.

Worked example. A gyroscope of mass $m = 0.50\ \text{kg}$ has its center of mass located $r = 0.12\ \text{m}$ from the pivot. The rotor spins at $\omega = 150\ \text{rad/s}$ about an axle with $I = 3.0\times10^{-3}\ \text{kg\,m}^2$. Gravity supplies the torque: $\tau = mgr = (0.50)(9.8)(0.12) = 0.588\ \text{N\,m}$. The angular momentum is $L = I\omega = 0.45\ \text{kg\,m}^2/\text{s}$. Substituting,

$$ \Omega_p = \frac{\tau}{L} = \frac{0.588}{0.45} \approx 1.31\ \text{rad/s}. $$

The axis sweeps one full horizontal circle in roughly $T = 2\pi/\Omega_p \approx 4.8\ \text{s}$ — slow, stately, and measurably distinct from free fall.

Key theorem. For torque-free precession (Euler's equations regime), the body-fixed symmetry axis traces a cone about the fixed angular momentum vector at the body precession rate $\Omega = (I_3 - I_1)\omega_3/I_1$, where $I_3$ is the axial moment and $I_1$ the transverse moment. This result underlies the wobble of non-spherical planets and the nutation observed in Earth's own rotation.

Lab exercise. Mount a bicycle wheel on a frictionless axle suspended by a string at one end. Spin the wheel to a measured $\omega$ using a tachometer, then record the precession period with a stopwatch. Compute the predicted $\Omega_p$ from the wheel's measured $I$ and compare; quantify how $\Omega_p$ scales as $\omega$ is halved.

The physics of gyroscopic precession is far from a textbook curiosity. It governs the stability of spacecraft attitude-control systems, the operation of inertial navigation gyroscopes in aircraft, and the torque-steering behavior of motorcycles during cornering. If one application invites deeper pursuit, consider inertial navigation: explore how a laser-ring gyroscope replaces the mechanical rotor with counter-propagating light beams and the Sagnac effect, extending the principle of angular momentum conservation into the domain of relativistic optics.
