🌱 月

🌱 月

Everyday example. Think about how you track time each month — maybe you flip a calendar page, or your phone reminds you it's the first of a new month. In Chinese, that word month comes directly from the word for moon, because ancient people counted time by watching the moon grow full and dark again. One complete lunar cycle — from new moon to new moon — takes roughly $29.5$ days, which is where our idea of a "month" was born.

Picture this. The character 月 is a tiny picture. Imagine a crescent moon with two short horizontal strokes tucked inside it, like a window letting you peek at the night sky. Early Chinese scribes drew exactly that: a slim, curved shape representing the moon's familiar crescent silhouette. Over centuries of writing and printing, the curves were straightened into the tidy rectangular form we see today — but the moon is still hiding inside.

Simple rule. 月 carries two meanings that work together: moon (the object in the sky) and month (the unit of time). Whenever you see 月 in a Chinese word, ask yourself: is this about the moon itself, or about measuring time? For example, 三月 (sān yuè) means "March" — literally "third month," $3 \times$ month.

Try it yourself. Say the sound aloud: yuè. Now count: 一月 (yī yuè) is January, 二月 (èr yuè) is February. Can you guess what 十二月 (shí èr yuè) — literally $12 \times$ month — means?
