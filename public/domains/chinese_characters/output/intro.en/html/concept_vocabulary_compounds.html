Vocabulary Compounds

Everyday example. Think about how English builds words from smaller parts: "sun" + "flower" = "sunflower," "book" + "shelf" = "bookshelf." Each piece carries meaning, and together they create something new yet logical. Chinese works the same way — but it does this all the time.

Picture this. You already know the character 火 (fire) and the character 山 (mountain). Place them side by side — 火山 — and you get "volcano." That's a compound word (复合词, fùhécí): two or more characters joined to express a single idea. The meaning often follows naturally from its parts: fire + mountain = a mountain that produces fire.

Now let's learn one new character: 车 (vehicle). Watch how it combines with characters you already know:

| Characters | Literal parts | Compound meaning | |---|---|---| | 火 + 车 | fire + vehicle | train | | 山 + 火 | mountain + fire | wildfire |

Picture the first trains, powered by steam and fire — a fire vehicle!

Simple rule. Most Chinese vocabulary is built by compounding. Once you recognize individual characters, you can often guess a compound's meaning — and you'll be right surprisingly often.

Try it yourself. You know 水 (water) and now 车 (vehicle). What do you think 水车 means? Hint: imagine a vehicle that moves water. What word does that give you?
