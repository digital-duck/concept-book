好

Everyday example. You bite into a perfectly ripe peach and smile. In Chinese, the single word you need is 好 (hǎo) — "good." It is one of the first characters anyone learns, and you will meet it dozens of times a day: 好吃 (hǎo chī, "delicious"), 好看 (hǎo kàn, "good-looking"), 你好 (nǐ hǎo, "hello" — literally "you good").

Picture this. Look at 好 closely and you will see it is made of two smaller pieces sitting side by side. On the left is 女 (nǚ, "woman"). On the right is 子 (zǐ, "child"). A woman beside her child — an image the ancient writers chose to represent something "good," a picture of harmony and completeness. Characters built by combining two meaningful pieces like this are called compound ideographs (会意字, huìyì zì): each half contributes an idea, and together they form a new meaning.

Simple rule. When you see an unfamiliar character, ask: Can I spot smaller pieces I already know? With 好, once you recognize 女 and 子, the character stops being a mysterious squiggle and becomes a tiny story — mother and child, therefore good.

Try it yourself. Cover the right half of 好. Can you write 女 from memory? Now cover the left half and write 子. Finally, combine them. Congratulations — you have just written your first compound ideograph.
