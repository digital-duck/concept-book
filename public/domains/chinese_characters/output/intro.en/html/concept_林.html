🍃 林

🍃 林

Everyday example. Walk to the edge of a city park and look out at a cluster of trees standing together. That's not just "a tree" — it's a grove or a small woods. In Chinese, a single tree is written 木 (mù). When you want to say "woods," you simply place two trees side by side: 林 (lín).

Picture this. Imagine two stick-figure trees standing next to each other: 木 + 木 = 林. The character literally looks like a pair of trees. This is one of the most intuitive combinations in the entire writing system — more trees means more forest.

Simple rule. 林 is built by doubling the component 木. This doubling technique is a common pattern in Chinese characters: repeating an element intensifies or multiplies its meaning. One tree → wood; two trees → woods. (Three trees, 森 (sēn), means a dense forest — but that's a story for another section.)

The character 林 is pronounced lín (second tone, rising pitch) and appears in everyday words like 森林 (sēnlín, "forest") and as one of the most common Chinese surnames.

Try it yourself. Write 木 twice, side by side, keeping both halves roughly equal in width. Notice how the left 木 squeezes slightly narrower to make room for its neighbor — this is how balanced characters share space.
