🍃 林

🍃 林

Everyday example. Think about a park where someone planted not just one tree, but a whole cluster of them side by side. That collection of trees has a different feel than a single tree standing alone — it's a grove, a small forest, a place where shade multiplies and birds gather.

Picture this. You already know 木 ($\text{mù}$), the character for "tree" — a trunk with branches above and roots below. Now imagine placing two of those trees right next to each other:

$$\text{木} + \text{木} = \text{林}$$

That's exactly how 林 ($\text{lín}$) is written. Two trees, shoulder to shoulder. The character is its meaning: more than one tree makes a grove.

Simple rule. In Chinese, doubling a character often intensifies or multiplies its base idea. One tree ($\text{木}$) → two trees side by side ($\text{林}$, grove) → three trees stacked together ($\text{森}$, dense forest). Each step adds more of the same element to grow the concept:

$$\text{木} \operatorname{xrightarrow}{\times 2} \text{林} \operatorname{xrightarrow}{+\text{木}} \text{森}$$

Try it yourself. Look at the character 林 and trace it slowly — left tree first, then right tree. Ask yourself: which part of the stroke tells you it's a tree? Once you see the two hidden 木 shapes, you'll never unsee them. That's the magic of building meaning from simpler pieces.
