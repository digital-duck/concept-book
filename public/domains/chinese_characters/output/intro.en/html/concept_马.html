🌱 马

🌱 马

Everyday example. Think about the last time you saw a horse — maybe in a movie, a park, or a picture book. That powerful animal with flowing mane and four strong legs has inspired humans for thousands of years. In Chinese, the word for horse is 马 (pronounced mǎ, with a dipping-then-rising tone).

Picture this. The character 马 is one of Chinese writing's great treasures: it started as a pictograph, a tiny drawing of a real horse. Ancient versions, carved on oracle bones around 3,000 years ago, showed a horse with a head, mane, legs, and tail. Over centuries, artists simplified those strokes into the compact symbol we use today. If you squint at 马, you can still spot the horse's head at the top and its legs sweeping downward.

Simple rule. 马 belongs to a special group of characters called radicals — building blocks that appear inside hundreds of other characters. Whenever you see 马 hiding inside a more complex character, there is almost always a connection to horses, riding, or movement. For example, 骑 (qí) means to ride, and 马 lives right inside it.

Try it yourself. Trace 马 slowly with your finger: start with the head strokes, then the body, then the four short strokes at the bottom representing legs. Count them — exactly four, just like a real horse. How many horse-related words can you guess might contain 马?
