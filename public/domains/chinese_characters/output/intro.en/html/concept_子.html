子

Everyday example. Think of a toddler bundled in a winter coat, arms stretched wide for a hug. That simple image — a small person reaching out — is exactly what the ancient Chinese captured when they drew the character 子.

Picture this. In its oldest form, 子 showed a baby with a large head and two little arms. Over centuries the strokes simplified, but the meaning stayed: child, son, offspring. Today you write it in just three strokes — a short horizontal dash, a curving hook downward, and a horizontal line crossing through. The big head on top and the body below still echo that original baby picture.

Simple rule. 子 carries two jobs in Chinese. As a standalone word it means "child" or "son" (儿子, érzi). As a building block inside other characters, it often signals something small, young, or related to people — for example, 好 ("good") pairs 女 (woman) with 子 (child), painting the idea that a mother and child together mean "good." Whenever you spot 子 tucked inside a more complex character, ask yourself: where is the child in this meaning?

Try it yourself. Write 子 three times slowly, paying attention to the hook stroke. Then look at these characters that contain 子: 学 (learn), 字 (written character), 孩 (child). Can you find where 子 hides in each one?
