🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the vector quantity that describes the rate of change of an object's position with respect to time. For a particle whose position vector is $\vec{r}(t)$, the instantaneous velocity is defined as the limit

$$ \vec{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt}. $$

In one dimension this reduces to $v = dx/dt$, while in two or three dimensions the velocity vector has components $\vec{v} = \dot{x}\,\hat{i} + \dot{y}\,\hat{j} + \dot{z}\,\hat{k}$. The magnitude $|\vec{v}|$ is the speed, a scalar quantity. Velocity carries both magnitude and direction; two objects moving at the same speed in different directions possess distinct velocities.

Worked Example. A particle moves along the $x$-axis according to $x(t) = 3t^2 - 2t + 5$ (SI units). Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$ and the average velocity over the interval $[1,\,3]\,\text{s}$.

Instantaneous velocity:

$$ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 2 \implies v(2) = 6(2) - 2 = 10\,\text{m/s}. $$

Average velocity:

$$ \bar{v} = \frac{x(3) - x(1)}{3 - 1} = \frac{(27 - 6 + 5) - (3 - 2 + 5)}{2} = \frac{26 - 6}{2} = 10\,\text{m/s}. $$

The numerical equality here is not coincidental—it holds whenever $x(t)$ is a polynomial of degree two (uniform acceleration), a consequence of the mean value theorem applied to quadratic functions.

Key Theorem — Mean Value Theorem for Motion. If $x(t)$ is differentiable on $[t_1, t_2]$, then there exists at least one instant $t^* \in (t_1, t_2)$ such that

$$ v(t^*) = \bar{v} = \frac{x(t_2) - x(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1}. $$

This guarantees that the instantaneous velocity equals the average velocity at some moment in every smooth motion—a result with direct implications for analyzing experimental position data.

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor, record position versus time for a cart released from rest on a frictionless track. From the data, compute the average velocity over successive 0.1 s intervals and plot $\bar{v}$ as a function of the midpoint time. Fit a linear model to the resulting points, identify the slope, and compare it against the accepted gravitational acceleration component along the track.
