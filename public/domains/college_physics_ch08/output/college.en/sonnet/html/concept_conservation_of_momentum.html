🍃 Conservation Of Momentum

🍃 Conservation Of Momentum

Definition. The law of conservation of momentum states that the total linear momentum of an isolated system remains constant in time. For a system of $n$ particles with masses $m_i$ and velocities $\mathbf{v}_i$, the total momentum is

$$ \mathbf{p}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i \mathbf{v}_i. $$

If the net external force on the system is zero, $\sum \mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}} = \mathbf{0}$, then by Newton's second law in its momentum form, $\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}} = d\mathbf{p}_{\text{total}}/dt = \mathbf{0}$, so $\mathbf{p}_{\text{total}}$ is constant. Internal forces between particles always occur in action-reaction pairs (Newton's third law) and cancel when summed over the entire system, leaving only external forces capable of changing total momentum.

Worked Example. A 1500 kg car traveling east at $20\ \text{m/s}$ collides with a stationary 2000 kg truck on a frictionless surface. The vehicles lock together after impact. Find the velocity of the combined wreckage.

Taking east as positive, the initial momentum of the system is

$$ p_i = (1500\ \text{kg})(20\ \text{m/s}) + (2000\ \text{kg})(0) = 3.00 \times 10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}. $$

Since no external horizontal force acts, $p_f = p_i$. With the combined mass $M = 3500\ \text{kg}$,

$$ v_f = \frac{p_i}{M} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}}{3500\ \text{kg}} \approx 8.57\ \text{m/s (east)}. $$

Because the vehicles merge into a single body, kinetic energy is not conserved; some is converted to deformation and heat. Momentum conservation, however, holds regardless of what happens internally.

Key Theorem. For any two-body collision, conservation of momentum gives

$$ m_1 \mathbf{v}_{1i} + m_2 \mathbf{v}_{2i} = m_1 \mathbf{v}_{1f} + m_2 \mathbf{v}_{2f}. $$

This relation is exact whenever the net external impulse during the collision is negligible — a condition well approximated when collision forces are large and the interaction time is short. It holds for all collision types and constrains the final momenta even when internal energy changes are unknown.

Lab Exercise. Using two low-friction carts on a track equipped with force sensors, arrange a collision between a moving cart of mass $m_1 = 0.50\ \text{kg}$ (initial speed $0.80\ \text{m/s}$) and a stationary cart of mass $m_2 = 1.00\ \text{kg}$. Attach a Velcro pad so the carts stick together. Predict $v_f$ using momentum conservation, then measure the post-collision velocity with a motion detector. Compute the percentage discrepancy and identify possible sources of systematic error, such as track inclination or friction at the wheels.
