🍃 Rocket Thrust

🍃 Rocket Thrust

Definition. Rocket thrust is the reaction force exerted on a rocket by the high-velocity exhaust gases expelled from its engine. It arises directly from Newton's third law and the conservation of linear momentum. For a rocket of instantaneous mass $m$ expelling exhaust at speed $v_e$ relative to the rocket, the thrust force is

$$ F_{\text{thrust}} = -v_e \frac{dm}{dt}, $$

where $dm/dt$ is the rate of change of the rocket's mass (negative, since mass is being lost). Because $dm/dt < 0$, the thrust $F_{\text{thrust}}$ is positive — directed opposite to the exhaust flow. This expression is the foundation of the rocket equation, also known as the Tsiolkovsky equation.

Worked Example. A rocket in deep space (no gravity, no drag) has an initial mass $m_0 = 2.0 \times 10^4\ \text{kg}$ and burns fuel at a constant rate $|\dot{m}| = 80\ \text{kg/s}$ with an exhaust speed $v_e = 2500\ \text{m/s}$.

Step 1 — Thrust:

$$ F_{\text{thrust}} = v_e \left|\frac{dm}{dt}\right| = (2500)(80) = 2.0 \times 10^5\ \text{N}. $$

Step 2 — Initial acceleration (using $F = ma$ at the moment of ignition):

$$ a_0 = \frac{F_{\text{thrust}}}{m_0} = \frac{2.0 \times 10^5}{2.0 \times 10^4} = 10\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

Note that as fuel is consumed the mass decreases, so acceleration increases over time even though thrust remains constant.

Key Theorem — Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation. Integrating the equation of motion $m\,dv = -v_e\,dm$ over the burn from initial mass $m_0$ to final mass $m_f$ yields the ideal velocity increment:

$$ \Delta v = v_e \ln\!\left(\frac{m_0}{m_f}\right). $$

The ratio $m_0/m_f$ is the mass ratio; achieving large $\Delta v$ requires either a high exhaust speed or a large mass ratio, which explains why multistage rockets are used for orbital missions.

Lab Exercise. A model rocket carries $m_{\text{fuel}} = 0.050\ \text{kg}$ of propellant and burns it in $t = 1.2\ \text{s}$ with $v_e = 300\ \text{m/s}$. Given an unfueled mass of $0.150\ \text{kg}$, calculate (a) the average thrust, (b) the mass ratio, and (c) the theoretical $\Delta v$ assuming the burn occurs in free space. Compare your computed $\Delta v$ with the impulse-momentum approach using the average thrust and discuss any discrepancy.
