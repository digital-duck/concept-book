🍃 Rocket Acceleration

🍃 Rocket Acceleration

Definition. A rocket accelerates by expelling mass (exhaust gases) at high velocity, converting stored chemical energy into momentum. Because no external agent pushes the rocket, this is a purely internal process governed by conservation of linear momentum. For a rocket of instantaneous mass $m$ moving at velocity $v$, with exhaust expelled at speed $v_e$ relative to the rocket, the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation gives the thrust force

$$ F_{\text{thrust}} = -v_e \frac{dm}{dt}, $$

where $dm/dt < 0$ because the rocket is losing mass. Applying Newton's second law to the rocket–exhaust system yields the equation of motion

$$ m \frac{dv}{dt} = -v_e \frac{dm}{dt}. $$

Integrating from initial mass $m_0$ and initial velocity $v_0$ to final mass $m_f$ and final velocity $v_f$ gives

$$ \Delta v = v_f - v_0 = v_e \ln\!\left(\frac{m_0}{m_f}\right). $$

This result is remarkable: the velocity gain depends only on the exhaust speed and the mass ratio, not on the burn duration.

Worked Example. A rocket of initial mass $m_0 = 20{,}000\ \text{kg}$ (including $15{,}000\ \text{kg}$ of fuel) fires its engine in deep space, expelling gas at $v_e = 2{,}500\ \text{m/s}$. Find the final velocity, starting from rest.

The final mass after all fuel is burned is $m_f = 20{,}000 - 15{,}000 = 5{,}000\ \text{kg}$. The mass ratio is $m_0/m_f = 4$. Applying the rocket equation:

$$ \Delta v = 2{,}500 \cdot \ln(4) = 2{,}500 \times 1.386 \approx 3{,}466\ \text{m/s}. $$

The rocket reaches approximately $3.47\ \text{km/s}$ from rest — over three times the exhaust speed.

Key Theorem. In the absence of external forces, the center of mass of the rocket–exhaust system moves at constant velocity. The rocket's acceleration at any instant is

$$ a = \frac{v_e}{m}\left|\frac{dm}{dt}\right|, $$

which increases as propellant is consumed and $m$ decreases, even if the thrust force remains constant.

Lab Exercise. A model rocket ($m_0 = 0.150\ \text{kg}$, $m_f = 0.080\ \text{kg}$) produces an average thrust of $12\ \text{N}$ over a $0.50\ \text{s}$ burn. Estimate $v_e$ from the thrust and mass-flow rate, then apply the Tsiolkovsky equation to predict $\Delta v$.
