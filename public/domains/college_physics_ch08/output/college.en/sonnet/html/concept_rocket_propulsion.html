🌸 Rocket Propulsion

🌸 Rocket Propulsion

Definition. Rocket propulsion is a direct application of conservation of linear momentum in a system with variable mass. A rocket accelerates by expelling mass (exhaust gases) at high velocity in one direction; the reaction force propels the rocket in the opposite direction. Because no external horizontal force acts on an idealized rocket in free space, the total momentum of the rocket-plus-exhaust system remains constant. Letting $M$ denote the instantaneous rocket mass and $v_{ex}$ the speed of exhaust relative to the rocket, the equation of motion is the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation:

$$ \Delta v = v_{ex} \ln\!\left(\frac{M_i}{M_f}\right) $$

where $M_i$ is the initial mass (rocket plus propellant) and $M_f$ is the final mass after burnout. The quantity $v_{ex}$ is often characterized by the specific impulse $I_{sp} = v_{ex}/g_0$, measured in seconds.

Worked Example. A rocket has an initial mass $M_i = 2.00 \times 10^4\ \text{kg}$, of which $1.50 \times 10^4\ \text{kg}$ is propellant. The exhaust speed relative to the rocket is $v_{ex} = 2500\ \text{m/s}$. Find the final speed gained from rest in free space.

$$ M_f = 2.00\times10^4 - 1.50\times10^4 = 5.00\times10^3\ \text{kg} $$ $$ \Delta v = 2500\,\ln\!\left(\frac{2.00\times10^4}{5.00\times10^3}\right) = 2500\,\ln(4) \approx 2500 \times 1.386 \approx 3470\ \text{m/s} $$

The rocket reaches approximately $3.47\ \text{km/s}$ — nearly four times the exhaust speed — because the mass ratio compounds logarithmically.

Key Theorem (Momentum Thrust). The instantaneous thrust force on the rocket is

$$ F_{thrust} = -v_{ex}\,\frac{dM}{dt} $$

Since $dM/dt < 0$ (mass is leaving the system), the thrust is positive in the forward direction. This result follows directly from Newton's second law applied to a variable-mass system; it shows that thrust depends on both exhaust velocity and mass flow rate, not on atmospheric pressure or an external medium — a rocket operates equally well in vacuum.

Lab Exercise. Using a dynamics cart on a frictionless air track, attach a CO₂ cartridge oriented rearward. Measure the cart's initial and final masses and record position versus time with a motion sensor. From $\Delta v$ and the measured mass ratio, calculate the effective exhaust speed $v_{ex}$ and compare it with the value inferred from the cartridge's rated thrust and flow rate. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including non-constant $dM/dt$ and friction.
