🌱 Net External Force

🌱 Net External Force

Definition. The net external force acting on a system is the vector sum of all forces exerted on that system by agents outside the system boundary. If a system is subject to external forces $\vec{F}_1, \vec{F}_2, \ldots, \vec{F}_k$, then

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \vec{F}_i. $$

Internal forces—those between constituents within the system—cancel in pairs by Newton's third law and therefore contribute nothing to $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$. Newton's second law for the system then reads

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = M\vec{a}, $$

where $M$ is the total mass and $\vec{a}$ is the acceleration of the system.

Worked Example. A 3.0 kg block rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A horizontal applied force $\vec{F}_A = 12\,\hat{i}\ \text{N}$ acts to the right, while a second horizontal force $\vec{F}_B = -4.0\,\hat{i}\ \text{N}$ acts to the left. The normal force $\vec{N} = 29.4\,\hat{j}\ \text{N}$ and gravity $\vec{W} = -29.4\,\hat{j}\ \text{N}$ act vertically. Summing all external forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (12 - 4.0)\,\hat{i} + (29.4 - 29.4)\,\hat{j} = 8.0\,\hat{i}\ \text{N}. $$

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{M} = \frac{8.0\ \text{N}}{3.0\ \text{kg}} = 2.7\ \text{m/s}^2\ (\text{to the right}). $$

Key Theorem. If and only if $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{0}$, the system moves with constant velocity (Newton's first law as a corollary). Equivalently, the total linear momentum $\vec{p} = M\vec{v}$ is conserved whenever the net external force vanishes:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} = \vec{0} \implies \vec{p} = \text{constant}. $$

This result forms the foundation of momentum conservation in collision analysis.

Lab Exercise. Set up an air track with a glider of mass $m_1 = 0.500\ \text{kg}$ connected by a string over a pulley to a hanging mass $m_2 = 0.100\ \text{kg}$. Treat both masses together as the system. List every external force acting on this combined system—gravity on $m_2$, the normal force on $m_1$, and any friction—and confirm that the string tension is internal to the system and therefore absent from $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$. Predict the acceleration using $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (m_1 + m_2)\vec{a}$, then measure the actual acceleration with a photogate and compute the percentage discrepancy between prediction and measurement.
