🍃 Energy Stored In Capacitors

🍃 Energy Stored In Capacitors

Definition. A capacitor stores energy in the electric field established between its plates. When a capacitor of capacitance $C$ is charged to a potential difference $V$, the energy stored is

$$ U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}QV = \frac{Q^2}{2C}, $$

where $Q = CV$ is the charge on the capacitor. The three equivalent forms follow directly from substituting $Q = CV$. This energy derives from the work done by an external agent moving charge against the growing electric field: as each infinitesimal charge element $dq$ is transferred at instantaneous voltage $v = q/C$, the incremental work $dW = v\,dq = (q/C)\,dq$, and integrating from $0$ to $Q$ yields $U = Q^2/2C$.

Worked Example. A parallel-plate capacitor with $C = 4.00\,\mu\text{F}$ is connected to a $12.0\,\text{V}$ battery until fully charged, then disconnected.

  1. Energy stored: $$ U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}(4.00\times10^{-6}\,\text{F})(12.0\,\text{V})^2 = 2.88\times10^{-4}\,\text{J}. $$

  2. Charge on plates: $$ Q = CV = (4.00\times10^{-6})(12.0) = 4.80\times10^{-5}\,\text{C}. $$

  3. Verification via $Q^2/2C$: $$ U = \frac{(4.80\times10^{-5})^2}{2(4.00\times10^{-6})} = \frac{2.304\times10^{-9}}{8.00\times10^{-6}} = 2.88\times10^{-4}\,\text{J}.\checkmark $$

Key Theorem — Energy Density. For a uniform electric field $E$ between parallel plates separated by distance $d$ and area $A$, the energy can be expressed as a volume density:

$$ u = \frac{U}{Ad} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2, $$

where $\epsilon_0 = 8.85\times10^{-12}\,\text{C}^2/(\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)$. This result generalizes: the electric field itself is the seat of the stored energy, not the plates. The energy density $u$ is proportional to $E^2$ and applies to any electric field configuration, not just parallel-plate geometries.

Lab Exercise. Charge a $10.0\,\mu\text{F}$ capacitor to $9.00\,\text{V}$, then connect it in parallel with an uncharged $10.0\,\mu\text{F}$ capacitor. Predict the final voltage across both capacitors using charge conservation, then calculate the initial and final stored energies. Account for the energy discrepancy and identify the physical mechanism responsible for the loss.
