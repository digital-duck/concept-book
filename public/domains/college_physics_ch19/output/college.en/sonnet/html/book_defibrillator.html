🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how a particle interacts with electromagnetic fields. Charge is quantized: any observable charge $Q$ is an integer multiple of the elementary charge,

$$ Q = ne, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z}, \quad e = 1.602 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{C}, $$

where $e$ is the magnitude of the electron's charge. Charge is also conserved: in any isolated system, the net charge remains constant. Two sign conventions apply — positive charge (protons, $q = +e$) and negative charge (electrons, $q = -e$). Like charges repel; unlike charges attract, as described by Coulomb's Law.

Worked Example. A glass rod is rubbed with silk, transferring $1.5 \times 10^{10}$ electrons from the rod to the silk. Find the charge acquired by each object.

The number of electrons transferred is $n = 1.5 \times 10^{10}$. The charge transferred is:

$$ \Delta Q = n \cdot e = (1.5 \times 10^{10})(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}) = 2.40 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{C} = 2.40\ \text{nC}. $$

The silk acquires charge $Q_{\text{silk}} = -2.40\ \text{nC}$ (gains electrons), and the rod acquires $Q_{\text{rod}} = +2.40\ \text{nC}$ (loses electrons). The total charge of the system remains zero, consistent with conservation of charge.

Key Theorem — Conservation of Charge. For any closed system,

$$ \frac{dQ_{\text{total}}}{dt} = 0. $$

This is not merely an empirical observation but reflects a deep symmetry of nature: invariance of physical laws under global phase transformations of quantum fields (Noether's theorem applied to $\text{U}(1)$ gauge symmetry). In every known particle interaction — including pair production ($\gamma \to e^+ + e^-$) and beta decay — the net charge before and after the event is identical.

🌱 Coulomb Force

🌱 Coulomb Force

Definition. The Coulomb force is the electrostatic interaction between two point charges at rest. If charge $q_1$ is located at the origin and charge $q_2$ is separated from it by a distance $r$, the magnitude of the force each charge exerts on the other is

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}, $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9\ \mathrm{N \cdot m^2 \cdot C^{-2}}$ is Coulomb's constant, related to the permittivity of free space by $k_e = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}$, with $\epsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{C^2 \cdot N^{-1} \cdot m^{-2}}$. The force is repulsive when the charges share the same sign and attractive when they are opposite in sign. In vector form,

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r}_{12}, $$

where $\hat{r}_{12}$ is the unit vector pointing from $q_1$ toward $q_2$.

Worked Example. Two point charges, $q_1 = +3.0\ \mu\mathrm{C}$ and $q_2 = -5.0\ \mu\mathrm{C}$, are separated by $r = 0.20\ \mathrm{m}$ in vacuum. Find the magnitude of the force between them.

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(5.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-11}}{4.0 \times 10^{-2}} = (8.988 \times 10^9)(3.75 \times 10^{-10}) \approx 3.4\ \mathrm{N}. $$

Because the charges are opposite, this force is attractive, pulling each charge toward the other.

Key Theorem — Superposition Principle. For a system of $n$ point charges, the net Coulomb force on charge $q$ is the vector sum of the individual pairwise forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\mathrm{net}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \vec{F}_i = k_e q \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{q_i}{r_i^2}\hat{r}_i. $$

This linearity means fields and forces from multiple sources add independently, a cornerstone of classical electrostatics.

🌱 Potential Energy

🌱 Potential Energy

Definition. In classical mechanics, potential energy is the energy stored in a system by virtue of the configuration or position of its constituent parts relative to a force field. For a conservative force $\vec{F}$, the potential energy $U$ is defined through the work–energy relation:

$$ \Delta U = -W = -\int_{\vec{r}_i}^{\vec{r}_f} \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} $$

This definition ensures that when the system moves from configuration $A$ to configuration $B$, the work done by the conservative force equals the decrease in potential energy: $W_{A \to B} = U_A - U_B$. The primary form introduced here is gravitational potential energy $U_g = mgh$, valid near Earth's surface, where $h$ is measured upward from an arbitrary reference level and $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$.

Worked Example. A 2.0 kg block is released from rest at the top of a frictionless ramp 5.0 m above the ground. Determine the block's speed at the bottom.

Taking the ground as the reference level ($U = 0$ there), the initial state has $K_i = 0$ and $U_i = mgh = (2.0)(9.8)(5.0) = 98\ \text{J}$. At the bottom, $U_f = 0$, so conservation of mechanical energy gives:

$$ K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f \implies 0 + 98 = \tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)v^2 + 0 $$ $$ v = \sqrt{\frac{2(98)}{2.0}} = \sqrt{98} \approx 9.9\ \text{m/s} $$

Key Theorem. For any conservative force, mechanical energy $E_{\text{mech}} = K + U$ is conserved in the absence of non-conservative forces. More precisely, $\frac{dE_{\text{mech}}}{dt} = 0$, which follows directly from the work–energy theorem and the path-independence of work done by conservative forces. This theorem fails when friction or other dissipative forces are present; in such cases the work–energy theorem must account for the additional energy transfer.

🍃 Electric Potential Energy

🍃 Electric Potential Energy

Definition. Electric potential energy is the work done by an external agent against the electric force to assemble a configuration of charges, stored as potential energy in the system. For a pair of point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$, the electric potential energy is

$$ U = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N \cdot m^2 / C^2}$ is Coulomb's constant. By convention, $U = 0$ when the charges are infinitely separated. For like-sign charges, $U > 0$ (energy must be supplied to bring them together); for opposite-sign charges, $U < 0$ (the system releases energy as the charges approach). More generally, for a charge $q_0$ moved through a region of electric field $\vec{E}$, the change in potential energy is related to the work done by the field:

$$ \Delta U = -W_{\text{field}} = -\int_A^B q_0 \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} $$

Worked Example. Two protons ($q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \, \mathrm{C}$) are initially separated by $r_i = 2.00 \, \mathrm{nm}$ and are released from rest. Find their common speed when their separation doubles to $r_f = 4.00 \, \mathrm{nm}$.

Using energy conservation ($\Delta K + \Delta U = 0$) and noting each proton gains identical kinetic energy:

$$ \Delta U = U_f - U_i = k_e \frac{q^2}{r_f} - k_e \frac{q^2}{r_i} = k_e q^2 \left(\frac{1}{r_f} - \frac{1}{r_i}\right) $$ $$ \Delta U = (8.99 \times 10^9)(1.60 \times 10^{-19})^2\left(\frac{1}{4.00 \times 10^{-9}} - \frac{1}{2.00 \times 10^{-9}}\right) = -5.75 \times 10^{-20} \, \mathrm{J} $$

Each proton gains $K = 2.88 \times 10^{-20} \, \mathrm{J}$, giving $v = \sqrt{2K/m_p} \approx 5.87 \times 10^3 \, \mathrm{m/s}$.

Key Theorem. The electric force is conservative: the work done moving a charge between two points is path-independent and depends only on the endpoints. Consequently, $\oint q_0 \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} = 0$ for any closed path. This path-independence is precisely what makes electric potential energy a well-defined state function of the charge configuration, analogous to gravitational potential energy in a uniform field.

🍃 Electric Potential

🍃 Electric Potential

Definition. The electric potential $V$ at a point in space is the electric potential energy per unit charge that a positive test charge $q_0$ would possess at that location:

$$ V = \frac{U}{q_0} $$

More precisely, the potential difference between two points $A$ and $B$ is the work done by the electric force per unit charge when moving the test charge from $A$ to $B$:

$$ V_B - V_A = -\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} $$

where $\vec{E}$ is the electric field and $d\vec{s}$ is the infinitesimal displacement element along the path. Because the electrostatic field is conservative, this integral is path-independent. The SI unit of electric potential is the volt (V), where $1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C}$. For a point charge $q$ at distance $r$, the potential reduces to:

$$ V = \frac{kq}{r} = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} $$

where $k \approx 8.99 \times 10^9\,\text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$.

Worked Example. Two point charges are fixed in place: $q_1 = +3.0\,\mu\text{C}$ at the origin and $q_2 = -1.5\,\mu\text{C}$ at $x = 0.40\,\text{m}$. Find the electric potential at point $P$ located at $x = 0.20\,\text{m}$.

Because potential is a scalar quantity, contributions from multiple charges add algebraically (superposition):

$$ V_P = \frac{kq_1}{r_1} + \frac{kq_2}{r_2} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(3.0\times10^{-6})}{0.20} + \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(-1.5\times10^{-6})}{0.20} $$ $$ V_P = 134{,}850\,\text{V} - 67{,}425\,\text{V} \approx 6.7 \times 10^4\,\text{V} $$

Note that $r_2 = 0.20\,\text{m}$ because $P$ lies midway between the two charges.

Key Theorem. The electric field and electric potential are related by:

$$ \vec{E} = -\nabla V \qquad \left(E_x = -\frac{dV}{dx} \text{ in one dimension}\right) $$

This gradient relationship means equipotential surfaces are always perpendicular to field lines, and no net work is done moving a charge along an equipotential surface. The theorem allows one to recover the full vector field from the scalar potential, which is often algebraically simpler to compute first.

🍃 Capacitor

🍃 Capacitor

Definition. A capacitor is a passive two-terminal electrical device consisting of two conducting plates (or surfaces) separated by an insulating medium called the dielectric. When a potential difference $V$ is applied across its terminals, equal and opposite charges $+Q$ and $-Q$ accumulate on the plates. The capacitance $C$ is defined as the ratio of the stored charge to the applied voltage:

$$ C = \frac{Q}{V} $$

The SI unit of capacitance is the farad (F), where $1\,\text{F} = 1\,\text{C/V}$. For a parallel-plate capacitor with plate area $A$, plate separation $d$, and dielectric constant $\kappa$, the capacitance is:

$$ C = \frac{\kappa \varepsilon_0 A}{d} $$

where $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{F/m}$ is the permittivity of free space. The electric field between the plates (assumed uniform) is $E = V/d = Q/(\kappa \varepsilon_0 A)$.

Worked Example. A parallel-plate capacitor has square plates of side $L = 0.10\,\text{m}$, separation $d = 2.0\,\text{mm} = 2.0 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}$, and a mica dielectric with $\kappa = 5.4$. Find the capacitance and the charge stored when $V = 120\,\text{V}$.

$$ A = (0.10)^2 = 1.0 \times 10^{-2}\,\text{m}^2 $$ $$ C = \frac{(5.4)(8.85 \times 10^{-12})(1.0 \times 10^{-2})}{2.0 \times 10^{-3}} = 2.39 \times 10^{-10}\,\text{F} \approx 239\,\text{pF} $$ $$ Q = CV = (2.39 \times 10^{-10})(120) \approx 2.87 \times 10^{-8}\,\text{C} = 28.7\,\text{nC} $$

Key Theorem — Energy Stored. The work done in charging a capacitor from zero to charge $Q$ equals the electrostatic energy stored in the electric field:

$$ U = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}QV $$

This energy resides in the electric field with volumetric energy density $u = \frac{1}{2}\kappa\varepsilon_0 E^2$.

🍃 Potential Difference

🍃 Potential Difference

Definition. The electric potential difference between two points A and B in an electric field is defined as the work done per unit positive charge by an external agent in moving a test charge quasi-statically from A to B:

$$ \Delta V = V_B - V_A = -\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{\ell} $$

The SI unit is the volt (V), where $1\ \text{V} = 1\ \text{J/C}$. Equivalently, the work done on a charge $q$ moved through a potential difference $\Delta V$ is:

$$ W = q\,\Delta V $$

Note the sign convention: a positive charge moving from high to low potential loses potential energy and gains kinetic energy; work done by the field is $W_{\text{field}} = -q\,\Delta V$.

Worked Example. A uniform electric field $E = 500\ \text{V/m}$ points in the $+x$ direction. Find the potential difference $V_B - V_A$ between point A at $x_A = 0$ and point B at $x_B = 0.30\ \text{m}$.

$$ V_B - V_A = -\int_0^{0.30} E\,dx = -E\,(x_B - x_A) = -(500)(0.30) = -150\ \text{V} $$

Point B is at lower potential than A. If a proton ($q = 1.6\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$) moves from A to B, the work done by the field is:

$$ W = q(V_A - V_B) = (1.6\times10^{-19})(150) = 2.4\times10^{-17}\ \text{J} $$

This work appears as kinetic energy gained by the proton, consistent with energy conservation.

Key Theorem (Equipotential Surfaces). For a static electric field, the work done in moving a charge along any path lying entirely on an equipotential surface — a surface on which $V$ is constant — is zero, because $\Delta V = 0$ implies $W = q\,\Delta V = 0$. It follows directly that $\vec{E}$ is always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces, since no work is done by a force acting perpendicular to displacement.

🍃 Capacitance

🍃 Capacitance

Definition. A capacitor is any pair of conductors separated by an insulating medium (or vacuum) that stores energy in an electric field. The capacitance $C$ of such a system is defined as the ratio of the magnitude of charge $Q$ stored on either conductor to the magnitude of the potential difference $V$ between them:

$$ C = \frac{Q}{V} $$

The SI unit of capacitance is the farad (F), where $1\,\text{F} = 1\,\text{C/V}$. Capacitance is a purely geometric property of the conductor configuration; it is independent of $Q$ and $V$ individually, depending only on the shape, size, and separation of the conductors and the permittivity of the intervening medium.

Worked Example. Consider a parallel-plate capacitor with plate area $A = 2.00 \times 10^{-2}\,\text{m}^2$ and plate separation $d = 1.00\,\text{mm} = 1.00 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}$, with vacuum between the plates. The electric field between infinite parallel plates is uniform, so the potential difference satisfies $V = Ed$. Using Gauss's law, the field is $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0 = Q/(\varepsilon_0 A)$, giving:

$$ C = \frac{Q}{V} = \frac{Q}{Ed} = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} = \frac{(8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{F/m})(2.00 \times 10^{-2}\,\text{m}^2)}{1.00 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}} = 1.77 \times 10^{-10}\,\text{F} \approx 177\,\text{pF} $$

If this capacitor is charged to $V = 12.0\,\text{V}$, the stored charge is $Q = CV = (1.77 \times 10^{-10}\,\text{F})(12.0\,\text{V}) = 2.12\,\text{nC}$.

Key Theorem. For a parallel-plate capacitor with a dielectric of permittivity $\varepsilon = \kappa\varepsilon_0$ filling the gap, the capacitance generalizes to:

$$ C = \frac{\kappa\varepsilon_0 A}{d} $$

where $\kappa \geq 1$ is the dielectric constant. Inserting a dielectric increases $C$ by the factor $\kappa$ because polarization of the dielectric reduces the effective electric field, lowering $V$ for the same stored charge.

🍃 Energy Stored In Capacitors

🍃 Energy Stored In Capacitors

Definition. A capacitor stores energy in the electric field established between its plates. When a capacitor of capacitance $C$ is charged to a potential difference $V$, the energy stored is

$$ U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}QV = \frac{Q^2}{2C}, $$

where $Q = CV$ is the charge on the capacitor. The three equivalent forms follow directly from substituting $Q = CV$. This energy derives from the work done by an external agent moving charge against the growing electric field: as each infinitesimal charge element $dq$ is transferred at instantaneous voltage $v = q/C$, the incremental work $dW = v\,dq = (q/C)\,dq$, and integrating from $0$ to $Q$ yields $U = Q^2/2C$.

Worked Example. A parallel-plate capacitor with $C = 4.00\,\mu\text{F}$ is connected to a $12.0\,\text{V}$ battery until fully charged, then disconnected.

  1. Energy stored: $$ U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}(4.00\times10^{-6}\,\text{F})(12.0\,\text{V})^2 = 2.88\times10^{-4}\,\text{J}. $$

  2. Charge on plates: $$ Q = CV = (4.00\times10^{-6})(12.0) = 4.80\times10^{-5}\,\text{C}. $$

  3. Verification via $Q^2/2C$: $$ U = \frac{(4.80\times10^{-5})^2}{2(4.00\times10^{-6})} = \frac{2.304\times10^{-9}}{8.00\times10^{-6}} = 2.88\times10^{-4}\,\text{J}.\checkmark $$

Key Theorem — Energy Density. For a uniform electric field $E$ between parallel plates separated by distance $d$ and area $A$, the energy can be expressed as a volume density:

$$ u = \frac{U}{Ad} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2, $$

where $\epsilon_0 = 8.85\times10^{-12}\,\text{C}^2/(\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)$. This result generalizes: the electric field itself is the seat of the stored energy, not the plates. The energy density $u$ is proportional to $E^2$ and applies to any electric field configuration, not just parallel-plate geometries.

🌸 Defibrillator

🌸 Defibrillator

Definition. A defibrillator is a medical device that delivers a controlled, high-voltage electrical shock to the heart to restore normal cardiac rhythm when the heart is in ventricular fibrillation or another life-threatening arrhythmia. Physically, a defibrillator operates as an RC discharge circuit: a capacitor is charged to a high potential difference (typically 1,000–5,000 V) by an internal power source, then rapidly discharged through the patient's chest via electrode paddles. The energy stored in the capacitor is given by

$$ U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C} $$

where $C$ is the capacitance and $V$ is the charging voltage. The discharge delivers a brief current pulse (lasting a few milliseconds) sufficient to depolarize a large fraction of cardiac muscle cells simultaneously, allowing the sinoatrial node to reassert pacemaker control.

Worked Example. A defibrillator capacitor of $C = 32\,\mu\text{F}$ is charged to $V_0 = 5{,}000\,\text{V}$. Find (a) the energy stored and (b) the peak current through a patient whose transthoracic resistance is $R = 50\,\Omega$.

(a) Stored energy:

$$ U = \frac{1}{2}CV_0^2 = \frac{1}{2}(32 \times 10^{-6})(5000)^2 = 400\,\text{J} $$

(b) At the instant of discharge, all voltage appears across the resistance, so the peak current is

$$ I_{\text{peak}} = \frac{V_0}{R} = \frac{5000}{50} = 100\,\text{A} $$

The discharge follows an exponential decay $I(t) = I_{\text{peak}}\,e^{-t/\tau}$ with time constant $\tau = RC = (50)(32\times10^{-6}) \approx 1.6\,\text{ms}$, confirming the pulse is short compared with the cardiac cycle.

Key Theorem. For an RC discharge circuit, the fraction of initial energy dissipated in the load resistance after one time constant is $1 - e^{-2} \approx 86\%$. This ensures rapid, nearly complete energy transfer to the patient rather than sustained current flow.

Payoff 🎯

The defibrillator stands as the definitive applied synthesis of the electrical principles developed throughout this chapter—capacitance, charge storage, potential difference, and the controlled discharge of energy through a resistive medium. Understanding the defibrillator is not merely an exercise in applied physics; it is a demonstration that the abstract language of electric fields and circuit equations has immediate, life-saving consequences.

What the Defibrillator Achieves

A defibrillator stores electrical energy in a capacitor bank charged to a potential difference $V$, typically in the range of 200–360 V for external defibrillators, accumulating an energy

$$ U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 $$

where capacitance $C$ is on the order of 32–175 $\mu\text{F}$. When ventricular fibrillation causes the heart's electrical conduction system to degrade into chaotic, uncoordinated depolarizations, the defibrillator delivers a precisely shaped current pulse through the thoracic cavity, simultaneously depolarizing a critical mass of cardiac cells. This momentary electrical reset allows the sinoatrial node to reassert coordinated rhythm.

Worked Example

A defibrillator is charged to $V = 300\,\text{V}$ with a capacitance of $C = 100\,\mu\text{F}$. The stored energy is:

$$ U = \frac{1}{2}(100 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{F})(300\,\text{V})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(10^{-4})(9 \times 10^4)\,\text{J} = 4.5\,\text{J} $$

If this energy is discharged through a patient with effective thoracic resistance $R = 50\,\Omega$, the peak current is $I_0 = V/R = 6\,\text{A}$, and the time constant of discharge is $\tau = RC = (50)(10^{-4}) = 5\,\text{ms}$, consistent with clinically effective pulse durations.

Key Theorem

The energy-voltage relationship $U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$ is the governing design equation: doubling the voltage quadruples the stored energy, while the RC time constant $\tau$ controls pulse duration independently of stored energy. Effective defibrillation requires optimizing both $U$ and $\tau$ for the patient's impedance.
