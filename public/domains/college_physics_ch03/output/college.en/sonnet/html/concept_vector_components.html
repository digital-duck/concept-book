🍃 Vector Components

🍃 Vector Components

Definition. A vector $\vec{A}$ in two dimensions can be uniquely decomposed into mutually perpendicular parts along chosen coordinate axes. If $\theta$ denotes the angle $\vec{A}$ makes with the positive $x$-axis (measured counterclockwise), then the scalar components of $\vec{A}$ are

$$ A_x = A\cos\theta, \qquad A_y = A\sin\theta, $$

where $A = |\vec{A}|$ is the magnitude. The vector is then written

$$ \vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j}, $$

with $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$ the unit vectors along $x$ and $y$, respectively. Conversely, given components, the magnitude and direction are recovered by

$$ A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}, \qquad \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{A_y}{A_x}\right), $$

where the quadrant of $\theta$ must be resolved using the signs of both components.

Worked Example. A displacement vector has magnitude $|\vec{d}| = 35.0\text{ m}$ directed at $\theta = 127°$ from the positive $x$-axis. Find its components.

$$ d_x = (35.0\text{ m})\cos 127° = (35.0)(-0.602) = -21.1\text{ m}, $$ $$ d_y = (35.0\text{ m})\sin 127° = (35.0)(0.799) = +28.0\text{ m}. $$

The negative $x$-component confirms the vector points into the second quadrant, consistent with $90° < 127° < 180°$. To verify: $\sqrt{(-21.1)^2+(28.0)^2} = \sqrt{445+784} = \sqrt{1229} \approx 35.1\text{ m}$, matching the original magnitude within rounding.

Key Theorem (Component Addition). Vector addition reduces to independent scalar addition along each axis. For $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$,

$$ R_x = A_x + B_x, \qquad R_y = A_y + B_y. $$

This follows directly from the distributive property of scalar multiplication over vector addition and the orthogonality of $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$. The theorem extends straightforwardly to three dimensions by appending $R_z = A_z + B_z$.

Lab Exercise. Using a force table, apply three concurrent forces: $\vec{F}_1 = 2.00\text{ N}$ at $0°$, $\vec{F}_2 = 1.50\text{ N}$ at $60.0°$, and $\vec{F}_3 = 1.00\text{ N}$ at $135°$. (a) Resolve each force into $x$- and $y$-components. (b) Compute the resultant $\vec{R}$ by summing components, and determine its magnitude and direction angle. (c) Reposition one of the three masses on the force table to match your computed $R_x$ and $R_y$, and verify that the adjusted configuration displaces the ring in the predicted direction.
