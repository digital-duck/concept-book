🌱 Reference Frame

🌱 Reference Frame

Definition. A reference frame is a coordinate system, together with a clock, with respect to which the position, velocity, and acceleration of objects are measured. Formally, a reference frame $S$ consists of an origin $O$, three mutually orthogonal spatial axes, and a time coordinate $t$. The position of a particle is then specified by the vector $\mathbf{r}(t) = x\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + y\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} + z\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ relative to $O$. A reference frame is called inertial if Newton's first law holds within it—that is, if a body subject to no net force moves with constant velocity. Any frame translating at constant velocity relative to an inertial frame is itself inertial; accelerating frames require fictitious forces and are not treated here.

Worked Example. A passenger on a train moving eastward at $v_T = 20\,\mathrm{m/s}$ throws a ball forward (eastward) at $v_{B,T} = 5\,\mathrm{m/s}$ relative to the train. Determine the ball's velocity as measured by an observer stationary on the ground.

Let $S$ denote the ground frame and $S'$ the train frame. The Galilean velocity addition formula gives

$$ \mathbf{v}_{B,S} = \mathbf{v}_{B,S'} + \mathbf{v}_{S',S}. $$

Substituting magnitudes (all eastward, so one-dimensional):

$$ v_{B,S} = 5\,\mathrm{m/s} + 20\,\mathrm{m/s} = 25\,\mathrm{m/s}\;\text{(eastward)}. $$

The same ball, observed from $S'$, travels at only $5\,\mathrm{m/s}$—illustrating that velocity is frame-dependent.

Key Theorem (Galilean Transformation). For two inertial frames $S$ and $S'$ where $S'$ moves with constant velocity $\mathbf{V}$ relative to $S$, and their origins coincide at $t = 0$, the coordinates transform as

$$ \mathbf{r}' = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{V}t, \qquad t' = t. $$

Differentiating once yields the velocity addition rule used above; differentiating twice shows that $\mathbf{a}' = \mathbf{a}$, so Newton's second law $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$ takes the same form in all inertial frames. This invariance of acceleration under Galilean transformation is the mathematical expression of Newtonian relativity.

Lab Exercise. Set up two dynamics carts on a low-friction track: cart $A$ at rest and cart $B$ moving at a measured constant velocity $V$. Attach a motion sensor to each cart. Record the position of a rolling ball as a function of time from both sensors simultaneously. Verify numerically that $x_A(t) - x_B(t) = Vt$ for all recorded times, confirming the Galilean position transformation and the frame-dependence of position measurements.
