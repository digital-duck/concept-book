🌱 Displacement

🌱 Displacement

Definition. Displacement is the vector quantity that describes the change in position of an object. If a particle occupies position $\vec{r}_1$ at time $t_1$ and position $\vec{r}_2$ at time $t_2$, its displacement is defined as

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_2 - \vec{r}_1. $$

In one dimension, with positions given by coordinates $x_1$ and $x_2$ along a chosen axis, displacement reduces to the scalar

$$ \Delta x = x_2 - x_1. $$

Displacement differs fundamentally from distance traveled: displacement depends only on the initial and final positions, whereas distance accumulates the total path length traversed. A runner who completes one full lap of a 400 m track has traveled 400 m but has zero displacement.

Worked Example. A car starts at the origin, drives due east 12 km, then turns and drives 5 km due north. Using a coordinate system with east as the positive $x$-axis and north as the positive $y$-axis, the initial position is $\vec{r}_1 = (0, 0)\ \text{km}$ and the final position is $\vec{r}_2 = (12, 5)\ \text{km}$. The displacement vector is

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_2 - \vec{r}_1 = (12\ \hat{x} + 5\ \hat{y})\ \text{km}, $$

with magnitude

$$ |\Delta \vec{r}| = \sqrt{(12)^2 + (5)^2} = \sqrt{169} = 13\ \text{km}. $$

The total distance driven is $12 + 5 = 17\ \text{km}$, confirming that distance and displacement are not equivalent for a non-straight path.

Key Theorem. For motion in any number of dimensions, displacement is path-independent: it is determined entirely by the endpoints $\vec{r}_1$ and $\vec{r}_2$. This follows directly from the definition $\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_2 - \vec{r}_1$, which contains no information about intermediate positions. As a consequence, if an object returns to its starting point, $\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{0}$ regardless of the path taken. This property makes displacement the natural variable for kinematic equations involving velocity: the average velocity $\bar{\vec{v}} = \Delta \vec{r}/\Delta t$ is well-defined and physically meaningful precisely because it is based on displacement rather than distance.

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor and a dynamics cart on a straight track, record the cart's position at $t = 0\ \text{s}$ and $t = 3\ \text{s}$ after it is given a brief push, bounces off a bumper, and returns partway toward the start. Compute $\Delta x$, noting its sign, and compare it to the total distance the cart traveled as reported by integrating the speed signal. Explain why the two values differ and identify which quantity appears in the kinematic equation $\Delta x = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$.
