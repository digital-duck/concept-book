🌱 One Dimensional Kinematics

🌱 One Dimensional Kinematics

Definition. One-dimensional kinematics is the systematic description of motion along a single straight-line axis without reference to the forces that produce that motion. The fundamental quantities are position $x(t)$, displacement $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$, velocity $v = dx/dt$, and acceleration $a = dv/dt$. For the special case of constant acceleration, these relationships yield the four kinematic equations:

$$ v = v_0 + at $$ $$ x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 $$ $$ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x $$ $$ \bar{v} = \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v) $$

The section focuses on displacement as the single new primitive: the signed change in position $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$, which distinguishes how far an object has moved from where it started without reference to the path taken.

Worked Example. A car starts from rest at a traffic light and accelerates uniformly at $a = 3.0\ \text{m/s}^2$ for $t = 4.0\ \text{s}$. Find its final speed and the displacement.

Step 1 — Final speed:

$$ v = v_0 + at = 0 + (3.0)(4.0) = 12\ \text{m/s} $$

Step 2 — Displacement:

$$ \Delta x = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 = 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(3.0)(4.0)^2 = 24\ \text{m} $$

Verification using the third equation: $v^2 = 0 + 2(3.0)(24) = 144$, so $v = 12\ \text{m/s}$. Consistent.

Key Theorem. Under constant acceleration, the displacement equals the area under the $v$–$t$ graph (a trapezoid with parallel sides $v_0$ and $v$):

$$ \Delta x = \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)\,\Delta t $$

This result follows directly from integrating $v(t) = v_0 + at$ over the interval $[0, t]$, and it remains valid even when $v_0 = 0$ or the object decelerates ($a < 0$).

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor and dynamics cart on a frictionless track, record position versus time as the cart accelerates under a hanging mass. Plot $x$ vs. $t$ and $v$ vs. $t$ from the sensor data. Extract the acceleration from the slope of the $v$–$t$ graph and compare it with the value predicted by $a = mg/(M+m)$, where $M$ is the cart mass and $m$ is the hanging mass. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify likely sources of systematic error, such as friction or string mass.
