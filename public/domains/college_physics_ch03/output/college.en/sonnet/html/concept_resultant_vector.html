🍃 Resultant Vector

🍃 Resultant Vector

Definition. Given a set of vectors $\vec{A}_1, \vec{A}_2, \ldots, \vec{A}_n$ acting on a system, the resultant vector $\vec{R}$ is defined as their vector sum:

$$ \vec{R} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \vec{A}_i = \vec{A}_1 + \vec{A}_2 + \cdots + \vec{A}_n. $$

Unlike scalar addition, vector addition accounts for both magnitude and direction. The standard analytical method decomposes each vector into orthogonal components along chosen coordinate axes. If $\vec{A}$ makes angle $\theta$ with the positive $x$-axis, then $A_x = A\cos\theta$ and $A_y = A\sin\theta$. The resultant components are then $R_x = \sum A_{ix}$ and $R_y = \sum A_{iy}$, yielding magnitude $R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}$ and direction $\phi = \arctan(R_y / R_x)$, with quadrant determined by the signs of $R_x$ and $R_y$.

Worked Example. A hiker walks $\vec{A} = 3.0\,\text{km}$ due east, then $\vec{B} = 4.0\,\text{km}$ due north. Find the resultant displacement.

Decomposing: $A_x = 3.0\,\text{km}$, $A_y = 0$; $B_x = 0$, $B_y = 4.0\,\text{km}$.

$$ R_x = 3.0 + 0 = 3.0\,\text{km}, \qquad R_y = 0 + 4.0 = 4.0\,\text{km}. $$ $$ R = \sqrt{(3.0)^2 + (4.0)^2} = \sqrt{9.0 + 16.0} = 5.0\,\text{km}. $$ $$ \phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{4.0}{3.0}\right) \approx 53.1^\circ \text{ north of east.} $$

The hiker ends up $5.0\,\text{km}$ from the starting point at $53.1^\circ$ north of east.

Key Theorem (Triangle Law of Vector Addition). For any two vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$, the resultant $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$ is the vector closing the triangle formed by placing $\vec{B}$ tip-to-tail after $\vec{A}$. By the law of cosines, $R^2 = A^2 + B^2 + 2AB\cos\alpha$, where $\alpha$ is the angle between $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$. Vector addition is both commutative ($\vec{A} + \vec{B} = \vec{B} + \vec{A}$) and associative.

Lab Exercise. Using a force table, hang masses over two pulleys to produce forces of $3.0\,\text{N}$ at $0^\circ$ and $4.0\,\text{N}$ at $90^\circ$. Compute the theoretical resultant analytically using the component method. Then experimentally determine the equilibrant—the single force that balances the system and is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to $\vec{R}$. Compare the magnitude and direction of the experimental equilibrant with $-\vec{R}$, and calculate the percent difference.
