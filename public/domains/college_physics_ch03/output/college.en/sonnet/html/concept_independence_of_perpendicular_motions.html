🍃 Independence Of Perpendicular Motions

🍃 Independence Of Perpendicular Motions

Definition. When an object undergoes two-dimensional motion, its displacement, velocity, and acceleration can be resolved into components along any two mutually perpendicular axes. The principle of independence of perpendicular motions states that the motion along one axis proceeds entirely independently of the motion along the perpendicular axis. Mathematically, if $\hat{x}$ and $\hat{y}$ denote orthogonal unit vectors, then the equations of motion decouple:

$$ x(t) = x_0 + v_{x0}\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}a_x t^2, \qquad y(t) = y_0 + v_{y0}\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}a_y t^2. $$

Each equation is solved independently; knowledge of $x(t)$ conveys no information about $y(t)$ beyond the shared time parameter $t$.

Worked Example. A ball is launched horizontally from a cliff of height $H = 45\ \text{m}$ with initial speed $v_{x0} = 20\ \text{m/s}$. Taking downward as positive $y$ with $a_y = g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$ and $a_x = 0$:

  • Vertical: $H = \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2 \Rightarrow t = \sqrt{2H/g} = \sqrt{90/9.8} \approx 3.03\ \text{s}$.
  • Horizontal: $x = v_{x0}\,t = 20 \times 3.03 \approx 60.6\ \text{m}$.

The horizontal component does not affect the time of flight; only gravity (acting vertically) determines when the ball lands. This clean separation is a direct consequence of the independence principle.

Key Theorem. Newton's second law in vector form, $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$, projects onto orthogonal axes without cross-coupling when the coordinate axes are chosen to align with the principal force directions. Consequently, if the net force has no $x$-component ($F_x = 0$), then $a_x = 0$ and $v_x$ remains constant for all time, regardless of how $v_y$ evolves. This result holds rigorously for any inertial reference frame and any choice of orthogonal axes.

Lab Exercise. Set up a simultaneous drop-and-shoot apparatus: one ball is released from rest and falls vertically; a second ball is projected horizontally from the same height. Using the independence principle, predict that both balls strike the floor at the same instant — a consequence of the vertical motions being identical in both cases. Record impact times with a photosensor and compute the percentage discrepancy between the two measured flight times. Discuss whether observed deviations, such as those caused by air resistance or launch-height calibration error, genuinely violate the independence principle or merely represent small perturbations on an otherwise exact result.
