🌸 River Crossing Relative Velocity

🌸 River Crossing Relative Velocity

Definition. When an object moves within a medium that is itself in motion, its velocity relative to a stationary observer is the vector sum of its velocity relative to the medium and the medium's velocity relative to the observer. Formally, if a boat moves with velocity $\vec{v}_{b/w}$ relative to the water, and the water flows with velocity $\vec{v}_{w/g}$ relative to the ground, then the boat's velocity relative to the ground is

$$ \vec{v}_{b/g} = \vec{v}_{b/w} + \vec{v}_{w/g}. $$

This vector addition governs both the boat's speed over ground and its actual direction of travel, which generally differs from the heading it steers.

Worked Example. A river flows due east at $3.0\ \text{m/s}$. A rower can propel her boat at $4.0\ \text{m/s}$ relative to the water. She wishes to cross a river $120\ \text{m}$ wide.

Case 1 — Straight across. She aims her bow directly north. Then $\vec{v}_{b/w} = 4.0\,\hat{j}\ \text{m/s}$ and $\vec{v}_{w/g} = 3.0\,\hat{i}\ \text{m/s}$, giving

$$ \vec{v}_{b/g} = 3.0\,\hat{i} + 4.0\,\hat{j}\ \text{m/s}, \qquad |\vec{v}_{b/g}| = \sqrt{3.0^2 + 4.0^2} = 5.0\ \text{m/s}. $$

Crossing time: $t = 120/4.0 = 30\ \text{s}$. Downstream drift: $x = 3.0 \times 30 = 90\ \text{m}$.

Case 2 — Land directly opposite. She must angle upstream so that the eastward component of $\vec{v}_{b/w}$ cancels the current. Setting $v_{b/w}\sin\theta = 3.0\ \text{m/s}$ gives $\theta = \arcsin(3/4) \approx 48.6°$ west of north. Her northward speed is then $v_N = \sqrt{4.0^2 - 3.0^2} = \sqrt{7} \approx 2.65\ \text{m/s}$, and crossing time is $t = 120/2.65 \approx 45.3\ \text{s}$.

Key Theorem (Minimum Crossing Time). The crossing time $t = d/v_\perp$, where $d$ is the river width and $v_\perp$ is the component of $\vec{v}_{b/w}$ perpendicular to the current. This is minimized when the boat is aimed directly across ($v_\perp = v_{b/w}$), regardless of the current speed.

Lab Exercise. A river of width $80\ \text{m}$ flows at $2.0\ \text{m/s}$. A swimmer's speed relative to water is $1.5\ \text{m/s}$. (a) Can the swimmer reach the opposite bank directly? Justify your answer. (b) Find the angle that minimizes downstream drift given the constraint that direct crossing is impossible. (c) Compute the minimum crossing time and the corresponding drift distance.
