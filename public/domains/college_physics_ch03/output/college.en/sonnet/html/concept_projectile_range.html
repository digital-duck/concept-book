🍃 Projectile Range

🍃 Projectile Range

Definition. The range $R$ of a projectile is the horizontal distance traveled from the launch point to the point at which the projectile returns to its original vertical height. For a projectile launched from level ground with initial speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, the horizontal and vertical motions are independent. Taking the launch point as the origin with $+x$ rightward and $+y$ upward, the equations of motion are

$$ x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t, \qquad y(t) = v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2, $$

where $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$. Setting $y(t) = 0$ and discarding the trivial solution $t = 0$ yields the total flight time

$$ T = \frac{2v_0 \sin\theta}{g}. $$

Substituting into $x(T)$ and applying the double-angle identity $2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin 2\theta$ gives the range formula:

$$ \boxed{R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}.} $$

Worked Example. A soccer ball is kicked at $v_0 = 20.0\ \text{m/s}$ and $\theta = 35.0°$. Find $R$.

$$ R = \frac{(20.0)^2 \sin(70.0°)}{9.80} = \frac{400 \times 0.9397}{9.80} \approx \frac{375.9}{9.80} \approx 38.4\ \text{m}. $$

The ball lands approximately $38.4\ \text{m}$ from the kick point.

Key Theorem — Maximum Range. Because $\sin 2\theta$ is maximized when $2\theta = 90°$, the range $R$ is greatest at $\theta = 45°$ for a fixed launch speed. Moreover, the range function is symmetric about $45°$: launch angles $\theta$ and $(90° - \theta)$ produce equal ranges. For example, kicks at $35°$ and $55°$ both yield $R \approx 38.4\ \text{m}$, yet have different maximum heights and flight times. This symmetry follows directly from $\sin 2\theta = \sin(180° - 2\theta)$.

Lab Exercise. Set up a projectile launcher on a table at a fixed height equal to the ground level (use a ramp that returns the ball to the tabletop height at landing). Fire the launcher at five angles between $20°$ and $70°$ in $10°$ increments, measuring $R$ each time. Plot $R$ versus $\theta$ and overlay the theoretical curve $R(\theta) = v_0^2 \sin 2\theta / g$, estimating $v_0$ from your $45°$ trial. Identify which pair of angles produces equal ranges and compare with the prediction $\theta$ and $90° - \theta$. Discuss sources of deviation such as air resistance and finite ball size.
