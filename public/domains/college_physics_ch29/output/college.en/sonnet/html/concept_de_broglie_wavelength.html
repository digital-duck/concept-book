🍃 De Broglie Wavelength

🍃 De Broglie Wavelength

Definition. In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that matter, like light, exhibits wave-particle duality. For any particle with momentum $p = mv$, the associated de Broglie wavelength is

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}, $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant. This relation unifies the wave and particle descriptions: photons obey $p = h/\lambda$, and de Broglie simply inverted this to apply to massive particles. The wavelength governs interference and diffraction behavior, while the particle picture governs discrete scattering events.

Worked Example. An electron is accelerated through a potential difference of $V = 54\ \text{V}$. Find its de Broglie wavelength.

The electron (mass $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$) gains kinetic energy

$$ K = eV = (1.602 \times 10^{-19})(54) = 8.65 \times 10^{-18}\ \text{J}. $$

Its momentum follows from $K = p^2/(2m_e)$:

$$ p = \sqrt{2m_e K} = \sqrt{2(9.109 \times 10^{-31})(8.65 \times 10^{-18})} = 3.97 \times 10^{-24}\ \text{kg·m/s}. $$

Therefore,

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{3.97 \times 10^{-24}} \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{m} = 0.167\ \text{nm}. $$

This wavelength is comparable to interatomic spacings in crystals (~0.2 nm), which is precisely why Davisson and Germer (1927) observed electron diffraction from a nickel crystal, providing direct experimental confirmation of de Broglie's hypothesis.

Key Theorem. For a non-relativistic particle accelerated through potential $V$, the de Broglie wavelength simplifies to

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m_e eV}}. $$

This shows $\lambda \propto V^{-1/2}$: doubling the accelerating voltage reduces the wavelength by a factor of $\sqrt{2}$, enabling tunable-wavelength electron beams in electron microscopy.

Lab Exercise. A proton ($m_p = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$) moves with kinetic energy $K = 1.00\ \text{keV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-16}\ \text{J}$. Calculate its de Broglie wavelength using $\lambda = h/\sqrt{2m_p K}$ and compare it to the 0.167 nm result for the 54 eV electron above. Because $\lambda \propto (mK)^{-1/2}$, a particle with larger mass $m$ at the same kinetic energy carries greater momentum and therefore a shorter wavelength. Quantify the ratio $\lambda_\text{proton}/\lambda_\text{electron}$ at equal kinetic energy and discuss what this implies for the resolving power of proton versus electron probes in diffraction experiments.
