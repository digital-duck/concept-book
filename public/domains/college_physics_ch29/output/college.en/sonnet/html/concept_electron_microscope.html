🌸 Electron Microscope

🌸 Electron Microscope

Definition. An electron microscope is an imaging instrument that uses a focused beam of electrons rather than visible light to form magnified images of specimens. The fundamental principle rests on the de Broglie wavelength: a particle of momentum $p$ possesses an associated wavelength

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}, $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant and $m$, $v$ are the electron's mass and speed. This is the one new primitive this section introduces. Because diffraction limits a microscope's resolution to features no smaller than approximately $\lambda$, and electrons can be accelerated to wavelengths far below visible light ($400$–$700\ \text{nm}$), electron microscopes achieve resolution at the nanometer or even sub-nanometer scale.

Worked Example. An electron is accelerated through a potential difference of $V = 10.0\ \text{kV}$. Determine its de Broglie wavelength, assuming non-relativistic motion.

The electron acquires kinetic energy equal to the work done by the field:

$$ K = eV = (1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C})(1.00 \times 10^4\ \text{V}) = 1.602 \times 10^{-15}\ \text{J}. $$

Solving for momentum via $K = p^2/(2m_e)$:

$$ p = \sqrt{2m_e K} = \sqrt{2(9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg})(1.602 \times 10^{-15}\ \text{J})} = 1.708 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{kg·m/s}. $$

Therefore:

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{1.708 \times 10^{-23}} \approx 3.88 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{m} = 0.0388\ \text{nm}, $$

roughly ten thousand times shorter than visible light, explaining the dramatically superior resolving power.

Key Theorem. For an electron accelerated through potential $V$, the de Broglie wavelength is

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m_e eV}}. $$

A transmission electron microscope (TEM) operating at $100\ \text{kV}$ achieves $\lambda \approx 3.7\ \text{pm}$—sufficient to resolve individual atomic columns in crystalline materials, a direct consequence of this wavelength-voltage relationship.

Lab Exercise. Using $\lambda = h/\sqrt{2m_e eV}$ as your working formula, construct a table of $\lambda$ values for $V \in \{1, 10, 50, 100, 200\}\ \text{kV}$. For each voltage, verify the non-relativistic assumption by checking that the kinetic energy $eV$ remains small compared to the electron rest energy $m_e c^2 = 0.511\ \text{MeV}$. Identify the threshold voltage beyond which the non-relativistic formula becomes unreliable, and discuss the practical implication for high-voltage TEM design.
