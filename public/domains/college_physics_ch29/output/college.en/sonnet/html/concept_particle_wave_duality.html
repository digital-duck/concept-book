🍃 Particle Wave Duality

🍃 Particle Wave Duality

Definition. Particle-wave duality is the principle that quantum objects — electrons, photons, and all matter — exhibit both particle-like and wave-like behaviors depending on how they are observed. This is not a deficiency of our instruments but a fundamental feature of nature. The de Broglie hypothesis (1924) quantifies the wave character of any particle with momentum $p$: the associated wavelength is

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}, $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant and $mv$ is the nonrelativistic momentum. Conversely, photons carry momentum $p = h/\lambda = E/c$, making them particle-like despite being waves. The two descriptions are complementary: a single experiment reveals either particle or wave behavior, never both simultaneously.

Worked Example. An electron is accelerated through a potential difference of $V = 54\ \text{V}$. The kinetic energy gained is $K = eV = (1.602\times10^{-19})(54) \approx 8.65\times10^{-18}\ \text{J}$. Using $K = p^2/2m_e$,

$$ p = \sqrt{2m_e K} = \sqrt{2(9.109\times10^{-31})(8.65\times10^{-18})} \approx 3.97\times10^{-24}\ \text{kg·m/s}. $$

The de Broglie wavelength is therefore

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{3.97\times10^{-24}} \approx 1.67\times10^{-10}\ \text{m} = 0.167\ \text{nm}. $$

This is comparable to the interatomic spacing of crystalline nickel ($\sim 0.215\ \text{nm}$), which is why Davisson and Germer (1927) observed electron diffraction from a nickel crystal — direct experimental confirmation of matter waves.

Key Theorem. For both photons and material particles, the momentum–wavelength relation takes the single unified form $p = h/\lambda$. For photons this follows from $E = hf$ and $E = pc$; de Broglie's hypothesis extends the same relation to all matter. The Davisson–Germer experiment confirmed this quantitatively: the diffraction peak appeared at precisely the angle predicted by the Bragg condition $2d\sin\theta = n\lambda$ when $\lambda = h/p$ is substituted, with no free parameters. The universality of $p = h/\lambda$ is the mathematical content of particle-wave duality.

Lab Exercise. Using a laser pointer ($\lambda = 650\ \text{nm}$) and a diffraction grating (500 lines/mm), measure the first-order diffraction angle $\theta_1$ and verify the grating equation $d\sin\theta = m\lambda$. Then calculate the momentum of a single photon from that beam and estimate what speed a dust particle (mass $\sim 10^{-9}\ \text{g}$) would need to have the same de Broglie wavelength. Discuss why macroscopic objects appear entirely particle-like in everyday experience.
