🌱 Energy

🌱 Energy

Definition. In the context of College Physics 2E, energy is a scalar quantity representing the capacity of a physical system to do work. In electromagnetic and quantum contexts (Chapter 29, pertaining to atomic spectra and the Bohr model), energy is most precisely characterized by the total mechanical energy of a bound system. For an electron in a hydrogen-like atom, the total energy is the sum of kinetic and potential contributions:

$$ E = K + U = \frac{1}{2}m_e v^2 - \frac{ke^2}{r} $$

where $k = 8.99 \times 10^9\ \mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C^2}$ is the Coulomb constant, $e$ the elementary charge, $m_e$ the electron mass, and $r$ the orbital radius. Applying the Bohr quantization condition $m_e v r = n\hbar$, one derives the quantized energy levels:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \mathrm{eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

The negative sign indicates a bound (confined) state; energy must be supplied to the electron to free it. The integer $n$ is the principal quantum number, the single new primitive introduced here: it indexes the discrete allowed orbits and fully determines the electron's energy in the Bohr model.

Worked Example. Determine the energy of a photon emitted when a hydrogen electron transitions from $n = 4$ to $n = 2$.

$$ \Delta E = E_4 - E_2 = -\frac{13.6}{16}\ \mathrm{eV} - \left(-\frac{13.6}{4}\ \mathrm{eV}\right) = -0.85\ \mathrm{eV} + 3.40\ \mathrm{eV} = 2.55\ \mathrm{eV} $$

The emitted photon carries energy $E_\gamma = 2.55\ \mathrm{eV}$, corresponding to wavelength $\lambda = hc/E_\gamma \approx 486\ \mathrm{nm}$, the blue-green line of the Balmer series.

Key Theorem (Bohr Energy Quantization). The total energy of an electron in the $n$-th Bohr orbit is $E_n = -m_e k^2 e^4 / (2\hbar^2 n^2)$. This result follows from simultaneously satisfying Newton's second law for circular motion and the quantization condition on angular momentum, and it predicts the observed hydrogen spectrum with remarkable precision.

🌱 Frequency

🌱 Frequency

Definition. The frequency of a periodic phenomenon is the number of complete cycles that occur per unit time. For a wave or oscillating system, if the period $T$ is the time required to complete one full cycle, then the frequency is defined as

$$ f = \frac{1}{T} $$

The SI unit of frequency is the hertz (Hz), where $1\ \text{Hz} = 1\ \text{s}^{-1}$. For electromagnetic waves propagating in vacuum, frequency is related to wavelength $\lambda$ and the speed of light $c$ by

$$ f = \frac{c}{\lambda}, \quad c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}. $$

For mechanical waves in a medium with wave speed $v$, the analogous relation is $f = v/\lambda$. The angular frequency $\omega$ (in rad/s) relates to $f$ by

$$ \omega = 2\pi f. $$

Worked Example. A radio station broadcasts at a wavelength of $\lambda = 3.00\ \text{m}$ (in the UHF band). Find the broadcast frequency.

$$ f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}{3.00\ \text{m}} = 1.00 \times 10^8\ \text{Hz} = 100\ \text{MHz}. $$

The corresponding period is $T = 1/f = 1.00 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{s} = 10.0\ \text{ns}$, and the angular frequency is $\omega = 2\pi(1.00 \times 10^8) \approx 6.28 \times 10^8\ \text{rad/s}$.

Key Theorem (Dispersion Relation). In a non-dispersive medium, frequency and wave number $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ satisfy the linear dispersion relation

$$ \omega = vk, $$

which implies $f = v/\lambda$. In dispersive media (e.g., light in glass), $v$ itself depends on $f$, causing different frequencies to travel at different speeds — the physical basis of prismatic separation of white light.

🍃 Quantization

🍃 Quantization

Definition. Quantization is the principle that certain physical quantities — most notably energy — cannot vary continuously in a bound system but instead take only discrete, integer-multiple values. For the hydrogen atom, the Bohr model yields

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\text{ eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $n$ is the principal quantum number. The ground state ($n=1$) has energy $-13.6$ eV; no intermediate energies between adjacent levels are permitted. A transition between levels proceeds by the emission or absorption of a photon carrying exactly $\Delta E = hf$, where $h$ is Planck's constant and $f$ is the photon frequency.

Worked Example. An electron in hydrogen transitions from the $n=3$ state to the $n=1$ state. Calculate the wavelength of the emitted photon.

The energy released is

$$ \Delta E = E_1 - E_3 = -13.6\left(1 - \frac{1}{9}\right)\text{ eV} = -13.6 \times \frac{8}{9}\text{ eV} \approx -12.09\text{ eV}. $$

The photon energy equals $|\Delta E| = 12.09\text{ eV} = 1.936 \times 10^{-18}$ J. Using $E = hc/\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{hc}{E} = \frac{(6.626\times10^{-34})(3.00\times10^{8})}{1.936\times10^{-18}} \approx 1.027\times10^{-7}\text{ m} = 102.7\text{ nm}. $$

This falls in the ultraviolet Lyman series, confirming that only specific wavelengths are emitted by hydrogen.

Key Theorem (Bohr Quantization Condition). The discrete energy spectrum follows directly from requiring that only certain electron orbits are stable. For a circular orbit of radius $r$, the angular momentum must satisfy

$$ L = m_e v r = n\hbar, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

where $\hbar = h/2\pi$. Combining this restriction with the condition that Coulomb attraction provides the centripetal force uniquely determines both $r_n = n^2 a_0$ (with $a_0 = 0.0529$ nm) and the energy formula above, showing that energy quantization is a consequence of this single orbital stability condition.

Verify that only integer values of $n_i$ produce agreement with your measured wavelengths, providing direct experimental evidence for energy quantization.

🌱 Electromagnetic Radiation

🌱 Electromagnetic Radiation

Definition. Electromagnetic radiation is self-propagating, coupled oscillations of electric and magnetic fields that travel through vacuum at the speed of light $c = 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$. This is the section's single new primitive: unlike mechanical waves, electromagnetic radiation requires no medium and carries energy through empty space. A plane wave propagating in the $+x$-direction takes the form

$$ E_y(x,t) = E_0 \sin(kx - \omega t), \qquad B_z(x,t) = B_0 \sin(kx - \omega t), $$

where $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ and $\omega = 2\pi f$ are the already-familiar wave number and angular frequency. The amplitudes satisfy $E_0 = cB_0$, both fields are mutually perpendicular, and both are perpendicular to the propagation direction, making this a transverse wave. The time-averaged intensity follows from energy-transport analysis as $I = E_0^2/(2\mu_0 c)$.

Worked Example. A radio transmitter broadcasts at $f = 100\ \text{MHz}$ with a peak electric field $E_0 = 5.0\ \text{V/m}$ at a receiver. Find $\lambda$, $B_0$, and $I$.

  • Wavelength: $\lambda = c/f = (3.0 \times 10^8)/(1.0 \times 10^8) = 3.0\ \text{m}$.
  • Peak magnetic field: $B_0 = E_0/c = 5.0/(3.0 \times 10^8) \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{T}$.
  • Intensity: $I = (5.0)^2/[2(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(3.0 \times 10^8)] \approx 3.32 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{W/m}^2$.

Key Theorem (Maxwell's Prediction of $c$). Applying Faraday's law and the Ampère–Maxwell law to a propagating wave yields the constraint

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}}. $$

Substituting $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{C}^2/(\text{N·m}^2)$ gives $c \approx 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$, matching the measured speed of light and establishing that light itself is electromagnetic radiation.

🍃 Planck Constant

🍃 Planck Constant

Definition. The Planck constant, denoted $h$, is a fundamental physical constant that sets the scale of quantum phenomena. Its accepted value is

$$ h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \ \text{J·s}. $$

The Planck constant first appeared in Max Planck's 1900 resolution of the ultraviolet catastrophe. Planck postulated that electromagnetic energy is not emitted or absorbed continuously but in discrete packets called quanta, each carrying energy

$$ E = hf, $$

where $f$ is the frequency of the radiation. This single hypothesis reproduced the observed blackbody spectrum exactly and marked the birth of quantum mechanics.

Worked Example. A photon has a wavelength of $\lambda = 500 \ \text{nm}$ (green light). Determine its energy.

First, convert wavelength to frequency using $c = f\lambda$:

$$ f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8 \ \text{m/s}}{500 \times 10^{-9} \ \text{m}} = 6.00 \times 10^{14} \ \text{Hz}. $$

Then apply the Planck relation:

$$ E = hf = (6.626 \times 10^{-34} \ \text{J·s})(6.00 \times 10^{14} \ \text{Hz}) = 3.98 \times 10^{-19} \ \text{J} \approx 2.49 \ \text{eV}. $$

This result illustrates why visible photons (energies of order 1–3 eV) are capable of driving photochemical reactions but cannot ionize most atoms unaided.

Key Theorem — Planck–Einstein Relation. For any photon propagating in vacuum, the energy $E$ and frequency $f$ satisfy

$$ E = hf, $$

with momentum given by

$$ p = \frac{h}{\lambda}, $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength. These relations bridge the classical wave description (frequency, wavelength) and the particle description (energy, momentum) of light, forming the foundation for the photoelectric effect and the de Broglie hypothesis. The reduced Planck constant $\hbar = h/2\pi$ appears naturally when the same relations are written in terms of angular frequency $\omega = 2\pi f$ and wave number $k = 2\pi/\lambda$, but the underlying physics is identical.

Lab Exercise. Using a photoelectric-effect apparatus, measure the stopping potential $V_s$ for light of several known frequencies $f$. The slope of the resulting straight line equals $h/e$; multiplying by the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \ \text{C}$ yields an experimental value of $h$. Compare your result with the accepted value $6.626 \times 10^{-34} \ \text{J·s}$ and discuss sources of systematic uncertainty, such as contact potentials and stray light.

🍃 Photon

🍃 Photon

Definition. A photon is the quantum of electromagnetic radiation — the smallest discrete unit of light energy. Proposed by Einstein in 1905 to explain the photoelectric effect, a photon carries energy proportional to its frequency:

$$ E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda} $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant, $f$ is frequency, $c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$ is the speed of light in vacuum, and $\lambda$ is wavelength. A photon has zero rest mass and travels at speed $c$ in vacuum. Its momentum follows directly from relativistic energy–momentum relations:

$$ p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda} $$

Despite having no rest mass, the photon carries both energy and momentum, making it a genuine relativistic particle.

Worked Example. A green laser emits light at $\lambda = 532\ \text{nm}$. Find the energy and momentum of a single photon.

$$ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{532 \times 10^{-9}} = 3.74 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J} $$

Converting to electron-volts: $E = 3.74 \times 10^{-19} / 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \approx 2.33\ \text{eV}$.

$$ p = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{532 \times 10^{-9}} = 1.25 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg·m/s} $$

Key Theorem: Compton Scattering. When a photon of wavelength $\lambda$ scatters off a free electron at angle $\theta$, its wavelength shifts by:

$$ \Delta\lambda = \lambda' - \lambda = \frac{h}{m_e c}(1 - \cos\theta) $$

The quantity $\lambda_C = h/(m_e c) = 2.426 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{m}$ is the Compton wavelength of the electron — the one new primitive introduced here, characterizing the scale at which photon–electron scattering produces measurable wavelength shifts. This result confirms that photons carry momentum and obey relativistic collision mechanics — behavior impossible to reconcile with a purely wave model of light.

🌱 Wavelength

🌱 Wavelength

Definition. The wavelength of a periodic wave is the spatial distance over which the wave pattern repeats exactly once. For a sinusoidal wave propagating in one dimension, the displacement field takes the form

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi_0), $$

where $A$ is the amplitude, $\omega$ the angular frequency, and $k$ the wave number. The wavelength $\lambda$ is related to the wave number by

$$ \lambda = \frac{2\pi}{k}. $$

Equivalently, $\lambda$ is the distance between any two adjacent points that are in phase — for instance, crest to crest or trough to trough. The wave speed $v$, frequency $f$, and wavelength are connected by the fundamental relation

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Worked Example. A sinusoidal electromagnetic wave in vacuum has a frequency of $f = 6.00 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}$ (visible green light). Find its wavelength.

Using $v = c = 3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}{6.00 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}} = 5.00 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m} = 500\ \text{nm}. $$

This places the wave squarely in the green portion of the visible spectrum, consistent with the wavelength range $\approx 495$–$570\ \text{nm}$.

Key Theorem — Bragg's Law. When waves of wavelength $\lambda$ reflect from parallel crystal planes separated by spacing $d$, constructive interference occurs when the path-length difference is an integer multiple of the wavelength:

$$ 2d\sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = 1, 2, 3, \ldots $$

This result, known as Bragg's Law, demonstrates that wavelength is the critical parameter governing interference phenomena. It underlies X-ray crystallography, neutron diffraction, and the operation of diffraction gratings.

🌱 Electron

🌱 Electron

Definition. The electron is a fundamental subatomic particle carrying a charge of $q_e = -e = -1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, a rest mass of $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, and an intrinsic spin of $\hbar/2$ (spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ fermion). Electrons are leptons—they do not participate in the strong nuclear interaction—and are regarded as point-like and structureless to the precision of current experiment. In an atom, electrons occupy quantized energy levels described by the principal quantum number $n$ and three additional quantum numbers $(\ell, m_\ell, m_s)$, governed by the Pauli exclusion principle, which prohibits any two electrons from sharing an identical set of quantum numbers. In conducting materials, a fraction of electrons are delocalized, giving rise to electrical current when an electric field is applied.

Worked Example. An electron is accelerated from rest through a potential difference of $V = 500\ \text{V}$. Find its final kinetic energy and speed.

By conservation of energy, the work done by the electric field equals the gain in kinetic energy:

$$ K = |q_e| V = (1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C})(500\ \text{V}) = 8.01 \times 10^{-17}\ \text{J}. $$

Setting $K = \frac{1}{2}m_e v^2$ and solving for $v$:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{2K}{m_e}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(8.01 \times 10^{-17})}{9.109 \times 10^{-31}}} \approx 1.33 \times 10^{7}\ \text{m/s}. $$

This speed is approximately $4.4\%$ of $c$, so the classical formula is valid to within $\sim 0.1\%$ error.

Key Theorem (de Broglie Relation). Every electron with momentum $p = m_e v$ has an associated de Broglie wavelength

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{m_e v}, $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J\cdot s}$. For the electron above, $\lambda \approx 5.49 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{m}$, comparable to atomic spacings and directly responsible for electron diffraction phenomena observed in crystal lattices.

🍃 Photon Energy

🍃 Photon Energy

Definition. Electromagnetic radiation is quantized into discrete packets called photons. The energy of a single photon is directly proportional to the frequency of the associated electromagnetic wave:

$$ E = hf $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant and $f$ is the frequency in hertz. Since frequency and wavelength are related by $c = f\lambda$, this expression may equivalently be written as

$$ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} $$

where $c = 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$ is the speed of light in vacuum and $\lambda$ is the wavelength. In atomic physics it is often convenient to express photon energies in electron-volts, using the conversion $1\ \text{eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J}$.

Worked Example. A sodium vapor lamp emits yellow light at a wavelength of $\lambda = 589\ \text{nm}$. Find the energy of a single photon in both joules and electron-volts.

Converting units: $\lambda = 589 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{m}$. Applying the wavelength form:

$$ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s})(2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s})}{589 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{m}} $$ $$ E = \frac{1.986 \times 10^{-25}\ \text{J·m}}{5.89 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}} = 3.37 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J} $$

Converting to electron-volts:

$$ E = \frac{3.37 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J/eV}} \approx 2.11\ \text{eV} $$

Key Theorem (Planck–Einstein Relation). The quantization condition $E = hf$ asserts that energy exchange between radiation and matter occurs only in integer multiples of the fundamental quantum $hf$. This result, first postulated by Max Planck (1900) to resolve the ultraviolet catastrophe and reformulated by Einstein (1905) to explain the photoelectric effect, establishes that the classical wave picture of light is incomplete: radiation carries both wave-like and particle-like properties. The photon momentum $p = h/\lambda$ follows directly from combining $E = hf$ with $c = f\lambda$ and the relativistic energy–momentum relation $E = pc$ for a massless particle, requiring no additional postulates beyond $E = hf$ itself.

🌸 X Ray Production

🌸 X Ray Production

Definition

X-rays are high-energy electromagnetic radiation (wavelengths roughly $0.01$–$10\,\text{nm}$, energies $\sim 0.1$–$100\,\text{keV}$) produced when energetic electrons decelerate rapidly inside a target material. The dominant production mechanism in an X-ray tube is Bremsstrahlung ("braking radiation"): a high-speed electron with kinetic energy $K = eV_0$ (where $V_0$ is the accelerating potential) undergoes Coulomb deflection by a target nucleus. The lost kinetic energy is emitted as a photon. Because the energy loss per collision is continuous and variable, a smooth, broad spectrum results. The total kinetic energy of the electron places a strict upper bound on any single photon's energy, which translates to a lower bound on wavelength known as the Duane–Hunt limit:

$$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV_0} = \frac{1.24 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{V·m}}{V_0} $$

Superimposed on this continuous Bremsstrahlung spectrum are sharp, material-specific peaks arising from inner-shell electron transitions in the target atoms; these characteristic lines are discussed separately in the context of atomic energy levels.

Worked Example

An X-ray tube is operated at $V_0 = 50.0\,\text{kV}$. Find the minimum wavelength of emitted X-rays.

$$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV_0} = \frac{(6.626\times10^{-34})(3.00\times10^{8})}{(1.602\times10^{-19})(5.00\times10^{4})} $$ $$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{1.988\times10^{-25}}{8.01\times10^{-15}} = 2.48\times10^{-11}\,\text{m} = 0.0248\,\text{nm} $$

This corresponds to the maximum photon energy $E_{\max} = 50.0\,\text{keV}$, confirming energy conservation: the entire electron kinetic energy converts to a single photon in the limiting case.

Key Theorem

The Duane–Hunt Law states that the short-wavelength cutoff of the Bremsstrahlung spectrum depends solely on the accelerating voltage and not on the target material:

$$ eV_0 = \frac{hc}{\lambda_{\min}} \implies h = \frac{e\,V_0\,\lambda_{\min}}{c} $$

This relation provided one of the earliest precision measurements of Planck's constant, predating the Compton effect, and constitutes direct evidence for the photon picture of radiation.

Payoff 🎯

X-ray production stands as the natural culmination of Chapter 29 because it unites the quantum mechanical principles developed throughout the chapter—discrete energy levels, characteristic emission spectra, and the wave-particle duality of electromagnetic radiation—into a physically realized, technologically consequential phenomenon. To understand how X-rays are produced is to understand how high-energy electrons interact with matter at the atomic scale, converting kinetic energy into photons through two distinct mechanisms: bremsstrahlung (braking radiation) and characteristic emission.

Definition and Mechanism. When an electron of kinetic energy $K = eV$ (accelerated through potential difference $V$) decelerates in the Coulomb field of a target nucleus, it emits a continuous spectrum of photons with maximum frequency given by the Duane–Hunt relation,

$$ \nu_{\max} = \frac{eV}{h}, $$

corresponding to complete conversion of kinetic energy into a single photon. Superimposed on this continuous bremsstrahlung spectrum are sharp characteristic lines produced when an incident electron ejects an inner-shell electron from a target atom, and an outer-shell electron transitions downward to fill the vacancy, emitting a photon of energy $\Delta E = E_{\text{upper}} - E_{\text{lower}}$.

Worked Example. A tungsten target X-ray tube operates at $V = 80\text{ kV}$. Find the minimum wavelength of the emitted X-rays:

$$ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{hc}{eV} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^{8})}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})(8.00 \times 10^{4})} \approx 1.55 \times 10^{-11}\text{ m} = 0.0155\text{ nm}. $$

This wavelength falls squarely in the diagnostic X-ray regime, confirming that tube voltage directly controls penetrating power.

Key Theorem. Moseley's law establishes that the frequency of characteristic $K_\alpha$ lines scales as $\sqrt{\nu} \propto (Z - 1)$, where $Z$ is the atomic number of the target. This empirical regularity, now understood through quantum mechanical selection rules and shielding, provided some of the earliest direct experimental evidence that atomic number—not atomic mass—is the fundamental ordering principle of the periodic table.

Lab Exercise. Using a simulated X-ray tube interface, vary the accelerating voltage from $20\text{ kV}$ to $100\text{ kV}$ in steps of $10\text{ kV}$. For each setting, record $\lambda_{\min}$, plot $1/\lambda_{\min}$ versus $V$, and extract Planck's constant $h$ from the slope. Then switch target materials (aluminum, copper, molybdenum) and identify which characteristic lines shift and which remain fixed as $V$ changes.

The phenomenon of X-ray production opens immediate pathways into medical radiography, crystallographic diffraction, and fluorescence spectroscopy. Of these, X-ray crystallography is perhaps the most profound: the same bremsstrahlung continuum and characteristic lines derived here, when diffracted by atomic planes separated by distances of order $0.1\text{ nm}$, allow the direct determination of molecular and crystal structures—a technique that revealed the double helix of DNA and the active sites of enzymes. Exploring Bragg diffraction in depth is a natural and rewarding next step.
