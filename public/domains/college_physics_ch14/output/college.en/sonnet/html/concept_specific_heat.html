🍃 Specific Heat

🍃 Specific Heat

Definition. The specific heat of a substance is the amount of thermal energy required to raise the temperature of one kilogram of that substance by one degree Celsius (equivalently, one kelvin). It is a material-specific intensive property, denoted $c$, and defined formally through the heat transfer relation

$$ Q = mc\Delta T, $$

where $Q$ is the heat transferred (in joules), $m$ is the mass of the sample (in kilograms), and $\Delta T = T_f - T_i$ is the change in temperature. Rearranging gives the defining expression

$$ c = \frac{Q}{m\,\Delta T} \quad \left[\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}\right]. $$

A large specific heat indicates that a substance requires substantial energy input to change temperature — water, with $c \approx 4186\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$, is anomalously high due to extensive hydrogen bonding, which underpins its role as a biological and industrial coolant.

Worked Example. A 0.250 kg aluminum block ($c_\mathrm{Al} = 900\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$) is heated from $20.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$ to $80.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Find the heat transferred.

$$ Q = mc\Delta T = (0.250\ \mathrm{kg})(900\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}})(80.0 - 20.0)\ \mathrm{K} = 13{,}500\ \mathrm{J}. $$

The block absorbs $13.5\ \mathrm{kJ}$ of thermal energy. Depositing the same energy into an equal mass of water would yield only $\Delta T = Q/(mc_w) \approx 12.9^\circ\mathrm{C}$, illustrating how dramatically specific heat governs thermal response for a fixed energy input.

Key Theorem (Calorimetric Equilibrium). When two objects at different temperatures reach thermal equilibrium inside an isolated calorimeter, conservation of energy requires the heat lost by one to equal the heat gained by the other:

$$ m_1 c_1 \Delta T_1 + m_2 c_2 \Delta T_2 = 0. $$

Because specific heat is the only material-dependent quantity in this expression, the equation provides the operational basis for measuring $c$ experimentally: a reference substance of known specific heat and initial temperature is mixed with the unknown sample, and the measured equilibrium temperature determines $c$ of the unknown.

Lab Exercise. Place $0.150\ \mathrm{kg}$ of an unknown metal at $95.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$ into $0.200\ \mathrm{kg}$ of water initially at $20.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$; the equilibrium temperature is measured at $26.7^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Apply the calorimetric equation above to determine the specific heat of the metal, then identify the material by comparison with standard tabulated values.
