🌱 Temperature

🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar intensive property that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. Two systems are said to be in thermal equilibrium when they share the same temperature and no net energy transfer occurs between them upon contact. This observation is formalized by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, and system $B$ is also in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then $A$ and $B$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. The Zeroth Law establishes temperature as a well-defined, transitive property and underpins the logical foundation of thermometry.

Temperature is measured on several scales. The Celsius scale sets $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the ice point and $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the steam point of water at standard pressure. The Kelvin (absolute) scale shifts the origin to absolute zero, the theoretical minimum temperature at which molecular motion ceases:

$$ T(\text{K}) = T(^\circ\text{C}) + 273.15. $$

The Fahrenheit scale, common in everyday U.S. usage, relates to Celsius via $T_F = \frac{9}{5}T_C + 32$.

Worked Example. A mercury thermometer reads $98.6\,^\circ\text{F}$, the normal human body temperature. Convert to Celsius and Kelvin.

Step 1 — Fahrenheit to Celsius:

$$ T_C = \frac{5}{9}(T_F - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(98.6 - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(66.6) = 37.0\,^\circ\text{C}. $$

Step 2 — Celsius to Kelvin:

$$ T_K = 37.0 + 273.15 = 310.15\,\text{K} \approx 310\,\text{K}. $$

Key Theorem. From kinetic theory, the average translational kinetic energy of a monatomic ideal-gas molecule is directly proportional to the absolute temperature:

$$ \langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2}k_B T, $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\,\text{J/K}$ is the Boltzmann constant. This result bridges the macroscopic observable $T$ and the microscopic world, confirming that absolute zero ($T = 0\,\text{K}$) corresponds to complete cessation of thermal motion.

Lab Exercise. Immerse three identical thermometers in (a) an ice–water mixture, (b) boiling water at sea level, and (c) a room-temperature water bath. Record the readings, convert each to Kelvin, and verify that the ice-point and steam-point values match $273.15\,\text{K}$ and $373.15\,\text{K}$, respectively. Discuss sources of systematic error such as thermometer lag and impurity effects on the boiling point.

🍃 Heat

🍃 Heat

Definition. Heat is the energy transferred between a system and its surroundings due solely to a temperature difference. It is not a property possessed by a body but rather a process quantity—energy in transit. Formally, if a system undergoes a quasi-static process, the infinitesimal heat transfer is denoted $\delta Q$, with the sign convention that $Q > 0$ when energy flows into the system and $Q < 0$ when energy flows out. Heat is measured in joules (J) in SI, though the calorie (1 cal = 4.186 J) remains common in thermochemical contexts. Unlike internal energy $U$, heat has no meaning independent of a process; one cannot speak of "the heat contained in a body." The rate at which heat is transferred depends on the temperature difference, the material properties, and the geometry of the system—but the quantity $Q$ itself refers only to the total energy exchanged during a process.

Worked Example. A copper block of mass $m = 0.500\ \text{kg}$ is heated from $T_i = 20.0^\circ\text{C}$ to $T_f = 80.0^\circ\text{C}$. The specific heat capacity of copper is $c = 385\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$. The heat absorbed is

$$ Q = mc\Delta T = (0.500\ \text{kg})(385\ \text{J/(kg·K)})(80.0 - 20.0\ \text{K}) = 1.16 \times 10^4\ \text{J}. $$

Since $Q > 0$, energy flows into the copper, consistent with the temperature rise. Note that $\Delta T$ in kelvins equals $\Delta T$ in degrees Celsius for a difference, so no unit conversion is required beyond the subtraction.

Key Theorem — First Law Connection. The First Law of Thermodynamics relates heat to work and internal energy:

$$ \Delta U = Q - W, $$

where $W$ is the work done by the system on its surroundings. This equation codifies energy conservation: the change in internal energy equals the heat added minus the work extracted. For a constant-volume process ($W = 0$), all heat transferred goes directly into raising the internal energy, giving $\Delta U = Q_V = nC_V\Delta T$ for an ideal gas, where $C_V$ is the molar heat capacity at constant volume.

Lab Exercise. Fill a calorimeter with $200\ \text{g}$ of water at $25^\circ\text{C}$ and submerge a pre-heated $50\ \text{g}$ aluminum slug ($c_{Al} = 900\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$) initially at $95^\circ\text{C}$. Record the equilibrium temperature $T_{eq}$, then calculate $Q_{Al} = -Q_{water}$ to verify energy conservation and determine whether any heat was lost to the calorimeter walls.

🍃 Specific Heat

🍃 Specific Heat

Definition. The specific heat of a substance is the amount of thermal energy required to raise the temperature of one kilogram of that substance by one degree Celsius (equivalently, one kelvin). It is a material-specific intensive property, denoted $c$, and defined formally through the heat transfer relation

$$ Q = mc\Delta T, $$

where $Q$ is the heat transferred (in joules), $m$ is the mass of the sample (in kilograms), and $\Delta T = T_f - T_i$ is the change in temperature. Rearranging gives the defining expression

$$ c = \frac{Q}{m\,\Delta T} \quad \left[\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}\right]. $$

A large specific heat indicates that a substance requires substantial energy input to change temperature — water, with $c \approx 4186\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$, is anomalously high due to extensive hydrogen bonding, which underpins its role as a biological and industrial coolant.

Worked Example. A 0.250 kg aluminum block ($c_\mathrm{Al} = 900\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$) is heated from $20.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$ to $80.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Find the heat transferred.

$$ Q = mc\Delta T = (0.250\ \mathrm{kg})(900\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}})(80.0 - 20.0)\ \mathrm{K} = 13{,}500\ \mathrm{J}. $$

The block absorbs $13.5\ \mathrm{kJ}$ of thermal energy. Depositing the same energy into an equal mass of water would yield only $\Delta T = Q/(mc_w) \approx 12.9^\circ\mathrm{C}$, illustrating how dramatically specific heat governs thermal response for a fixed energy input.

Key Theorem (Calorimetric Equilibrium). When two objects at different temperatures reach thermal equilibrium inside an isolated calorimeter, conservation of energy requires the heat lost by one to equal the heat gained by the other:

$$ m_1 c_1 \Delta T_1 + m_2 c_2 \Delta T_2 = 0. $$

Because specific heat is the only material-dependent quantity in this expression, the equation provides the operational basis for measuring $c$ experimentally: a reference substance of known specific heat and initial temperature is mixed with the unknown sample, and the measured equilibrium temperature determines $c$ of the unknown.

Lab Exercise. Place $0.150\ \mathrm{kg}$ of an unknown metal at $95.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$ into $0.200\ \mathrm{kg}$ of water initially at $20.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$; the equilibrium temperature is measured at $26.7^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Apply the calorimetric equation above to determine the specific heat of the metal, then identify the material by comparison with standard tabulated values.

🍃 Heat Capacity Equation

🍃 Heat Capacity Equation

Definition. The heat capacity equation relates the heat $Q$ transferred to or from a substance to the resulting change in its temperature. For a substance of mass $m$ with specific heat capacity $c$, the relation is

$$ Q = mc\Delta T $$

where $\Delta T = T_f - T_i$ is the change in temperature. The specific heat capacity $c$ is an intrinsic material property, defined as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one kilogram of the substance by one kelvin (or equivalently, one degree Celsius). Its SI unit is $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$. When $Q > 0$, the substance absorbs heat and its temperature rises; when $Q < 0$, heat is released and the temperature falls. Because $c$ depends on the material alone and not on the sample size, it serves as a reliable fingerprint for identifying substances and designing thermal systems.

Worked Example. A copper calorimeter contains $0.500\,\mathrm{kg}$ of water initially at $20.0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. A $0.200\,\mathrm{kg}$ block of iron at $80.0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ is placed into the water. Taking $c_\mathrm{water} = 4186\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$ and $c_\mathrm{iron} = 449\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$, find the equilibrium temperature $T_f$.

Applying conservation of energy with no heat lost to the surroundings:

$$ Q_\mathrm{iron} + Q_\mathrm{water} = 0 $$ $$ m_\mathrm{Fe}\,c_\mathrm{Fe}(T_f - 80.0) + m_w\,c_w(T_f - 20.0) = 0 $$ $$ (0.200)(449)(T_f - 80.0) + (0.500)(4186)(T_f - 20.0) = 0 $$ $$ 89.8\,T_f - 7184 + 2093\,T_f - 41860 = 0 $$ $$ T_f = \frac{49044}{2182.8} \approx 22.5\,^\circ\mathrm{C} $$

The result lies close to the initial water temperature, as expected: water's specific heat capacity is nearly ten times that of iron, so the larger thermal mass of the water dominates.

Key Theorem. When two bodies at different temperatures reach thermal equilibrium in an isolated system, energy conservation requires $\sum_i m_i c_i \Delta T_i = 0$. This calorimetric principle, a direct consequence of the first law of thermodynamics, underpins all calorimetry measurements: the heat lost by hotter objects equals the heat gained by cooler ones.

Lab Exercise. Place $0.300\,\mathrm{kg}$ of water at $15.0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ in an insulated container. Add $0.150\,\mathrm{kg}$ of aluminum ($c = 900\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$) heated to $95.0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Record the equilibrium temperature, then calculate $T_f$ using the heat capacity equation and compare your measured value to the theoretical prediction. Quantify sources of discrepancy, such as heat exchange with the container walls.

🌱 Thermal Equilibrium

🌱 Thermal Equilibrium

Definition. Two systems are in thermal equilibrium when they are in thermal contact and no net heat flows between them. Operationally, this occurs when both systems reach the same temperature. The concept is formalized by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system A is in thermal equilibrium with system C, and system B is also in thermal equilibrium with system C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This law establishes temperature as a well-defined, transitive property and provides the logical foundation for thermometry.

Macroscopically, thermal equilibrium is the state in which all measurable state variables—pressure, volume, and temperature—remain constant in time once two systems are brought into contact and allowed to exchange heat freely.

Worked Example. A copper block of mass $m_1 = 0.200\ \text{kg}$ at temperature $T_1 = 80.0\ ^\circ\text{C}$ is placed in contact with an aluminum block of mass $m_2 = 0.400\ \text{kg}$ at temperature $T_2 = 20.0\ ^\circ\text{C}$. Using specific heats $c_{\text{Cu}} = 385\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$ and $c_{\text{Al}} = 900\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$, find the equilibrium temperature $T_f$.

Conservation of energy requires that heat lost by the copper equals heat gained by the aluminum:

$$ m_1 c_{\text{Cu}}(T_1 - T_f) = m_2 c_{\text{Al}}(T_f - T_2) $$

Substituting values:

$$ (0.200)(385)(80.0 - T_f) = (0.400)(900)(T_f - 20.0) $$ $$ 77(80.0 - T_f) = 360(T_f - 20.0) $$ $$ 6160 - 77T_f = 360T_f - 7200 $$ $$ T_f = \frac{13360}{437} \approx 30.6\ ^\circ\text{C} $$

Both blocks reach $T_f \approx 30.6\ ^\circ\text{C}$, confirming thermal equilibrium.

Key Theorem (Zeroth Law). The relation "is in thermal equilibrium with" is an equivalence relation on the set of all thermodynamic systems. It partitions systems into equivalence classes, each characterized by a unique temperature value $T$. This is the rigorous justification for assigning a single scalar temperature to a system and underlies every practical measurement made with a thermometer.

Lab Exercise. Place equal masses of water at $10\ ^\circ\text{C}$ and $90\ ^\circ\text{C}$ into an insulated calorimeter. Record temperature every 30 seconds until readings stabilize. Verify that the final temperature equals the predicted value of $T_f = 50.0\ ^\circ\text{C}$, and estimate the heat lost to the calorimeter by analyzing any deviation from the ideal prediction.

🌸 Calorimetry

🌸 Calorimetry

Definition. Calorimetry is the experimental science of measuring heat transfer between a system and its surroundings. The foundational principle is conservation of energy: in a thermally isolated system, the total heat gained by cooler objects equals the total heat lost by warmer objects. For a substance of mass $m$, specific heat capacity $c$, undergoing a temperature change $\Delta T$, the heat exchanged is

$$ Q = mc\Delta T. $$

When a phase transition occurs at constant temperature, the relevant quantity is instead the latent heat $L$, so that $Q = mL$. A calorimeter enforces thermal isolation so that the calorimeter contents form a closed system, giving the governing equation

$$ \sum_i Q_i = 0, $$

where each $Q_i$ carries a sign: positive for heat absorbed, negative for heat released.

Worked Example. A 0.250 kg aluminum block ($c_\text{Al} = 900\ \text{J}\cdot\text{kg}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}$) is heated to $150.0\ ^\circ\text{C}$ and dropped into a calorimeter containing 0.500 kg of water ($c_w = 4186\ \text{J}\cdot\text{kg}^{-1}\cdot\text{K}^{-1}$) initially at $20.0\ ^\circ\text{C}$. Assuming the calorimeter cup has negligible heat capacity, find the equilibrium temperature $T_f$.

Setting $Q_\text{Al} + Q_w = 0$:

$$ m_\text{Al}\,c_\text{Al}(T_f - 150.0) + m_w\,c_w(T_f - 20.0) = 0. $$

Substituting values:

$$ (0.250)(900)(T_f - 150.0) + (0.500)(4186)(T_f - 20.0) = 0 $$ $$ 225\,T_f - 33750 + 2093\,T_f - 41860 = 0 $$ $$ 2318\,T_f = 75610 $$ $$ T_f \approx 32.6\ ^\circ\text{C}. $$

The large heat capacity of water dominates, pulling the final temperature close to the initial water temperature.

Key Theorem. The method of mixtures is valid only when (1) no heat escapes to the environment, (2) no phase change occurs unexpectedly, and (3) the system reaches a single uniform equilibrium temperature. Violation of any condition introduces systematic error; in practice, a correction term $Q_\text{cal} = C_\text{cal}\,\Delta T$ accounts for the finite heat capacity $C_\text{cal}$ of the calorimeter itself.

Lab Exercise. Determine the specific heat of an unknown metal sample ($\approx 0.100\ \text{kg}$). Heat the sample to $100.0\ ^\circ\text{C}$ in boiling water, transfer it rapidly to a calorimeter containing 0.200 kg of water at a measured initial temperature, and record the equilibrium temperature. Use $\sum Q_i = 0$, including a calorimeter correction, to solve for $c_\text{metal}$. Repeat three times and report the mean with standard deviation.

Payoff 🎯

Calorimetry is the quantitative science of heat measurement, and it stands as the natural culmination of thermodynamic study precisely because it transforms abstract energy concepts into experimentally accessible quantities. Where earlier chapters established the theoretical framework — internal energy, heat capacity, and the first law of thermodynamics — calorimetry closes the loop by providing the operational method through which thermal phenomena are measured, verified, and applied. The governing relation is deceptively simple:

$$ Q = mc\Delta T $$

where $Q$ is the heat transferred, $m$ is the mass of the substance, $c$ is its specific heat capacity, and $\Delta T = T_f - T_i$ is the change in temperature. For phase transitions, this extends to $Q = mL$, where $L$ denotes the latent heat of fusion or vaporization. Together, these expressions constitute the quantitative engine of calorimetry.

Worked Example. A 0.250 kg aluminum block at $85.0^\circ\text{C}$ is submerged in 0.500 kg of water initially at $20.0^\circ\text{C}$ inside an insulated calorimeter. Using $c_\text{Al} = 900\ \text{J kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ and $c_\text{water} = 4186\ \text{J kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$, the equilibrium temperature $T_f$ follows from conservation of energy:

$$ m_\text{Al}\,c_\text{Al}(T_i^\text{Al} - T_f) = m_w\,c_w(T_f - T_i^w) $$

Substituting and solving gives $T_f \approx 23.7^\circ\text{C}$, a result verifiable to within experimental uncertainty in any undergraduate laboratory.

Key Theorem — Conservation of Thermal Energy. In a thermally isolated system, the total heat lost by hot bodies equals the total heat gained by cold bodies: $\sum Q_i = 0$. This is a direct consequence of the first law for a closed system with no work done, and it underpins every calorimetric calculation.

Lab Exercise. Using a coffee-cup calorimeter, dissolve a known mass of ammonium nitrate in deionized water, record the temperature change, and calculate the molar enthalpy of dissolution. Compare your result to the accepted value of $+25.7\ \text{kJ mol}^{-1}$; assess systematic error sources including heat loss to the cup itself.

Calorimetry's reach extends from nutritional science — where bomb calorimeters determine the caloric content of food — to materials engineering, where differential scanning calorimetry characterizes phase transitions in polymers and alloys. Each application reduces to the same disciplined measurement of $Q$. To pursue one thread further, consider exploring the thermochemistry of combustion reactions, where calorimetric data directly constrains the enthalpy changes that drive chemical bond-energy analysis.
