🍃 Radiation

🍃 Radiation

Definition. Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy through the emission and absorption of electromagnetic waves, requiring no intervening medium. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation propagates through vacuum. All objects with temperature $T > 0\,\text{K}$ emit thermal radiation, with the radiated power depending on the nature of the surface through a dimensionless material property called emissivity $\varepsilon \in [0,1]$. A surface with $\varepsilon = 1$ is an ideal blackbody — a perfect emitter and absorber of radiation. The power radiated by a surface is governed by the Stefan–Boltzmann Law:

$$ P = \sigma \varepsilon A T^4 $$

where $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\,\text{W}\,\text{m}^{-2}\,\text{K}^{-4}$ is the Stefan–Boltzmann constant, $A$ is the surface area in $\text{m}^2$, and $T$ is the absolute temperature in Kelvin. The net power radiated by an object at temperature $T$ surrounded by an environment at temperature $T_{\text{env}}$ is:

$$ P_{\text{net}} = \sigma \varepsilon A \left(T^4 - T_{\text{env}}^4\right) $$

A positive $P_{\text{net}}$ indicates net energy loss from the object to the surroundings.

Worked Example. A spherical blackbody ($\varepsilon = 1$) of radius $r = 0.10\,\text{m}$ is maintained at $T = 500\,\text{K}$ in a room at $T_{\text{env}} = 300\,\text{K}$. Calculate the net radiated power.

Surface area: $A = 4\pi r^2 = 4\pi(0.10)^2 \approx 0.1257\,\text{m}^2$.

$$ P_{\text{net}} = (5.67\times10^{-8})(1)(0.1257)\left(500^4 - 300^4\right) $$

Computing: $500^4 = 6.25\times10^{10}$, $300^4 = 8.10\times10^{9}$, so the difference is $5.44\times10^{10}\,\text{K}^4$.

$$ P_{\text{net}} \approx (5.67\times10^{-8})(0.1257)(5.44\times10^{10}) \approx 388\,\text{W} $$

Key Theorem. The Stefan–Boltzmann Law implies that radiated power scales as the fourth power of absolute temperature. Consequently, doubling $T$ increases $P$ by a factor of $2^4 = 16$, making radiation the dominant heat-transfer mechanism at high temperatures. At thermal equilibrium, $P_{\text{net}} = 0$ only when $T = T_{\text{env}}$, confirming that radiative exchange drives the system toward equilibrium.

Lab Exercise. Using a Stefan–Boltzmann sensor and a heated metal sphere of known area and emissivity, measure radiated power at three different sphere temperatures (e.g., 350 K, 450 K, 550 K). Plot $P$ versus $T^4$ and determine the slope. Compare the experimental slope $\sigma \varepsilon A$ to the theoretical value, and calculate the percent error in your measured $\varepsilon$.
