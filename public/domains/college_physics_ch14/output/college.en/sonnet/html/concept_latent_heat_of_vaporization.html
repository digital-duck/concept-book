🍃 Latent Heat Of Vaporization

🍃 Latent Heat Of Vaporization

Definition. The latent heat of vaporization, denoted $L_v$, is the amount of thermal energy per unit mass required to convert a substance from its liquid phase to its gaseous phase at constant temperature and pressure. During this phase transition, supplied heat does not raise the temperature of the substance; instead it overcomes intermolecular attractions and performs work against the surrounding pressure to expand the vapor. Formally,

$$ Q = m L_v $$

where $Q$ is the heat energy transferred (in joules), $m$ is the mass of the substance (in kilograms), and $L_v$ is the latent heat of vaporization (in $\text{J/kg}$). For water at $100^\circ\text{C}$ and standard atmospheric pressure, $L_v \approx 2.26 \times 10^6 \, \text{J/kg}$.

Worked Example. A kettle contains $0.500 \, \text{kg}$ of water already at its boiling point. How much heat must be supplied to completely vaporize the water?

Using $Q = m L_v$:

$$ Q = (0.500 \, \text{kg})(2.26 \times 10^6 \, \text{J/kg}) = 1.13 \times 10^6 \, \text{J} $$

Thus approximately $1.13 \, \text{MJ}$ of energy is required. This energy breaks intermolecular hydrogen bonds and expands the vapor against atmospheric pressure, increasing the internal energy of the system while leaving the temperature unchanged at $100^\circ\text{C}$.

Key Theorem — Energy Partition During Vaporization. The latent heat of vaporization partitions into two contributions: the increase in internal energy $\Delta U$ associated with separating molecules, and the work $P_{\text{atm}}\,\Delta V$ done by the expanding vapor against the atmosphere. Per unit mass,

$$ L_v = \frac{\Delta U}{m} + \frac{P_{\text{atm}}\,\Delta V}{m} $$

For water at $100^\circ\text{C}$, approximately $92\%$ of $L_v$ goes to $\Delta U$ and only $8\%$ to $P_{\text{atm}}\,\Delta V$, confirming that intermolecular bond-breaking dominates the energy requirement rather than mechanical expansion work.

Lab Exercise. Heat $200 \, \text{g}$ of water from $25^\circ\text{C}$ to complete vaporization using an immersion heater of known wattage $W$. Record the time $t_1$ required to reach $100^\circ\text{C}$ and the additional time $t_2$ to fully vaporize the sample. Use $c_w = 4186 \, \text{J/(kg·K)}$ and the measured $W \cdot t_2$ to experimentally determine $L_v$. Compare your result to the accepted value and compute the percent error, identifying possible sources of heat loss to the surroundings.
