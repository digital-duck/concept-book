🌸 Evaporative Cooling

🌸 Evaporative Cooling

Definition. Evaporative cooling is the process by which a liquid loses thermal energy when its highest-kinetic-energy molecules escape the liquid surface as vapor. Because intermolecular attractive forces must be overcome for a molecule to leave the liquid phase, only those molecules whose kinetic energy exceeds the energy barrier set by the latent heat of vaporization $L_v$ (the energy per unit mass required to convert liquid to vapor at constant temperature) can escape. Their departure removes energy disproportionately from the remaining liquid, lowering its average kinetic energy—and therefore its temperature. Quantitatively, if a mass $\Delta m$ evaporates from a liquid of mass $m$ and specific heat $c$, the temperature drop is

$$ \Delta T = -\frac{L_v \,\Delta m}{m c}, $$

where $c$ and $m$ are treated as approximately constant over the small temperature change involved.

Worked Example. A student places 200 g of water at 30 °C in an open container. Due to air currents, 2.0 g evaporates before equilibrium is re-established. Determine the temperature drop of the remaining water. Given: $L_v = 2.43 \times 10^6\ \text{J kg}^{-1}$, $c = 4186\ \text{J kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$.

$$ \Delta T = -\frac{L_v \,\Delta m}{m c} = -\frac{(2.43 \times 10^6)(0.0020)}{(0.200)(4186)} \approx -5.8\ \text{K}. $$

The water cools by approximately 5.8 °C, reaching about 24.2 °C. Note that the evaporated mass (1% of total) produces a temperature change nearly six times larger than would result from simply removing the same mass at the same temperature, because $L_v \operatorname{gg} c\,\Delta T$ for modest temperature changes.

Key Theorem (Maxwell–Boltzmann Escape Criterion). In a liquid at temperature $T$, the fraction of molecules with kinetic energy exceeding the per-molecule escape threshold $\varepsilon_0 = L_v / N_A$ is proportional to $e^{-\varepsilon_0 / k_B T}$. Consequently, the evaporation rate increases exponentially with temperature, consistent with the Clausius–Clapeyron equation

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L_v\, P}{R\, T^2}, $$

which describes how vapor pressure rises steeply with temperature and follows directly from applying the second law of thermodynamics to the liquid–vapor phase boundary.

Lab Exercise. Wrap a thermometer bulb in a cotton wick soaked in isopropyl alcohol ($L_v \approx 8.5 \times 10^5\ \text{J kg}^{-1}$, $c \approx 2600\ \text{J kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$). Record the temperature every 30 s as the alcohol evaporates in moving air. Using the measured temperature drop and the known mass loss (determined by weighing before and after), verify the expression for $\Delta T$ and compare your computed $L_v$ with the accepted value. Discuss sources of systematic error, including heat exchange with the surrounding air.
