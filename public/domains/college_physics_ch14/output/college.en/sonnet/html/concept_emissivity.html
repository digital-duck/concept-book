🍃 Emissivity

🍃 Emissivity

Definition. Emissivity, denoted $\varepsilon$, is a dimensionless material property that quantifies how effectively a surface emits thermal radiation relative to an ideal blackbody at the same temperature. Formally, it is defined as the ratio of the radiant power emitted per unit area by the surface to that emitted by a perfect blackbody at identical temperature:

$$ \varepsilon = \frac{P_{\text{surface}}}{P_{\text{blackbody}}} = \frac{P}{A\sigma T^4} $$

where $P$ is the total radiated power, $A$ is the surface area, $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}\,K^{-4}}$ is the Stefan–Boltzmann constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvins. By definition, $0 \leq \varepsilon \leq 1$, with $\varepsilon = 1$ corresponding to a perfect blackbody and $\varepsilon = 0$ to a perfect reflector. The Stefan–Boltzmann law for a real surface therefore takes the form:

$$ P = \varepsilon \sigma A T^4 $$

Worked Example. A tungsten filament at $T = 3000\ \mathrm{K}$ has emissivity $\varepsilon = 0.45$ and a surface area $A = 8.0 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{m^2}$. Find the total radiated power.

$$ P = \varepsilon \sigma A T^4 = (0.45)(5.67 \times 10^{-8})(8.0 \times 10^{-5})(3000)^4 $$

Computing $(3000)^4 = 8.1 \times 10^{13}\ \mathrm{K^4}$:

$$ P = (0.45)(5.67 \times 10^{-8})(8.0 \times 10^{-5})(8.1 \times 10^{13}) \approx 16.6\ \mathrm{W} $$

A blackbody of the same area and temperature would radiate approximately $36.9\ \mathrm{W}$; tungsten emits roughly $45\%$ of that maximum.

Key Theorem — Kirchhoff's Law of Thermal Radiation. For any surface in thermodynamic equilibrium, the spectral emissivity equals the spectral absorptivity at every wavelength:

$$ \varepsilon_\lambda = \alpha_\lambda $$

This fundamental result implies that a surface that is a poor emitter is equally a poor absorber. A blackbody ($\varepsilon = 1$) absorbs all incident radiation and emits the maximum possible power; polished metals ($\varepsilon \approx 0.02$–$0.10$) both reflect and emit poorly in the infrared.

Lab Exercise. Using a radiation sensor and two identical metal cans — one painted matte black and one polished — filled with hot water at $T = 353\ \mathrm{K}$, measure the power radiated from each surface over five minutes. Calculate the experimental emissivity of the polished surface using $\varepsilon = P_{\text{meas}}/(A\sigma T^4)$, and compare your result with the accepted value for aluminum ($\varepsilon \approx 0.05$). Discuss sources of systematic error, including reflected ambient radiation and non-uniform surface temperature.
