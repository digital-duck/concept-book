🍃 Phase Change

🍃 Phase Change

Definition. A phase change (or phase transition) is a thermodynamic process in which a substance transforms from one state of matter to another—solid, liquid, or gas—at constant temperature and pressure. During a phase change, energy is exchanged between the system and its surroundings without any change in temperature; this energy, called latent heat, is used entirely to alter the intermolecular structure rather than the kinetic energy of the molecules. The latent heat $L$ is defined by

$$ Q = mL, $$

where $Q$ is the heat absorbed or released, $m$ is the mass of the substance, and $L$ has SI units of $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$. Two values of $L$ are in common use: the specific latent heat of fusion $L_f$, which governs solid–liquid transitions, and the specific latent heat of vaporization $L_v$, which governs liquid–gas transitions.

Worked Example. Determine the total heat required to convert $0.500\,\mathrm{kg}$ of ice at $-10.0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ entirely into steam at $100.0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Use $c_\text{ice} = 2090\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}K^{-1}}$, $c_\text{water} = 4186\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}K^{-1}}$, $L_f = 3.34\times10^5\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$, and $L_v = 2.26\times10^6\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$.

$$ Q_1 = mc_\text{ice}\,\Delta T_1 = (0.500)(2090)(10.0) = 1.045\times10^4\,\mathrm{J} $$ $$ Q_2 = mL_f = (0.500)(3.34\times10^5) = 1.670\times10^5\,\mathrm{J} $$ $$ Q_3 = mc_\text{water}\,\Delta T_3 = (0.500)(4186)(100.0) = 2.093\times10^5\,\mathrm{J} $$ $$ Q_4 = mL_v = (0.500)(2.26\times10^6) = 1.130\times10^6\,\mathrm{J} $$ $$ Q_\text{total} = Q_1+Q_2+Q_3+Q_4 \approx 1.51\times10^6\,\mathrm{J}. $$

Vaporization accounts for roughly 75% of the total energy requirement, illustrating the dominant role of $L_v$ relative to all other contributions in this process.

Key Theorem. At thermodynamic equilibrium, the Clausius–Clapeyron equation describes how the saturation pressure $P$ varies with temperature $T$ along a phase boundary:

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L\,P}{R\,T^2}, $$

where $R$ is the specific gas constant. This relation predicts that the boiling point of a liquid decreases with decreasing ambient pressure, consistent with the observation that water boils below $100\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ at high altitudes.

Lab Exercise. Using a calorimeter containing $200\,\mathrm{g}$ of water at $20\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$, add a $50.0\,\mathrm{g}$ ice cube at $0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Measure the equilibrium temperature and use energy conservation—applying $Q = mL_f$ for the melting ice and $Q = mc\,\Delta T$ for both water masses—to estimate $L_f$, then compare your result to the accepted value and identify sources of systematic error.
