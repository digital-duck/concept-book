🍃 Latent Heat

🍃 Latent Heat

Definition. When a substance undergoes a phase transition—melting, freezing, vaporization, or condensation—energy is exchanged with the environment at constant temperature and pressure. This energy, called latent heat, does not raise or lower the thermometric temperature of the substance; instead, it drives the rearrangement of intermolecular bonds. The latent heat $L$ is defined as the energy per unit mass required (or released) during the transition:

$$ Q = mL $$

where $Q$ is the total heat transferred (in joules), $m$ is the mass of the substance (in kilograms), and $L$ is the specific latent heat (in $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$). For fusion (solid $\leftrightarrow$ liquid) the quantity is called the latent heat of fusion $L_f$; for vaporization (liquid $\leftrightarrow$ gas) it is the latent heat of vaporization $L_v$. For water at standard pressure, $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$ and $L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$.

Worked Example. A kettle contains $0.500\ \mathrm{kg}$ of water already at its boiling point ($100\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$, $1\ \mathrm{atm}$). How much energy must be supplied to convert it entirely to steam?

$$ Q = mL_v = (0.500\ \mathrm{kg})(2.26 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}) = 1.13 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{J} $$

Note that no temperature change occurs during this process; all $1.13\ \mathrm{MJ}$ goes into breaking hydrogen bonds between water molecules.

Key Theorem (Clausius–Clapeyron relation). The latent heat is connected to the slope of the phase boundary in the $P$–$T$ diagram by

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T\,\Delta v} $$

where $\Delta v$ is the specific volume change across the phase boundary. This result, derived from the condition that the chemical potential is equal on both sides of the boundary, shows that a substance with large $L$ has a steeply sloped liquid–vapor curve.

Lab Exercise. Place $200\ \mathrm{g}$ of ice at $0\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ into a thermally insulated calorimeter containing $500\ \mathrm{g}$ of water at $40\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Using $c_w = 4186\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}K^{-1}}$ and $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$, apply energy conservation to predict the equilibrium temperature, then measure it with a thermometer and compute the percentage error in your value of $L_f$.
