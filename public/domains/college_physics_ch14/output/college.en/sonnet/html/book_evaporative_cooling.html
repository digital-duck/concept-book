🌱 Temperature

🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar intensive property that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. Two systems are said to be in thermal equilibrium when they share the same temperature and no net energy transfer occurs between them upon contact. This observation is formalized by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, and system $B$ is also in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then $A$ and $B$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. The Zeroth Law establishes temperature as a well-defined, transitive property and underpins the logical foundation of thermometry.

Temperature is measured on several scales. The Celsius scale sets $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the ice point and $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the steam point of water at standard pressure. The Kelvin (absolute) scale shifts the origin to absolute zero, the theoretical minimum temperature at which molecular motion ceases:

$$ T(\text{K}) = T(^\circ\text{C}) + 273.15. $$

The Fahrenheit scale, common in everyday U.S. usage, relates to Celsius via $T_F = \frac{9}{5}T_C + 32$.

Worked Example. A mercury thermometer reads $98.6\,^\circ\text{F}$, the normal human body temperature. Convert to Celsius and Kelvin.

Step 1 — Fahrenheit to Celsius:

$$ T_C = \frac{5}{9}(T_F - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(98.6 - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(66.6) = 37.0\,^\circ\text{C}. $$

Step 2 — Celsius to Kelvin:

$$ T_K = 37.0 + 273.15 = 310.15\,\text{K} \approx 310\,\text{K}. $$

Key Theorem. From kinetic theory, the average translational kinetic energy of a monatomic ideal-gas molecule is directly proportional to the absolute temperature:

$$ \langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2}k_B T, $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\,\text{J/K}$ is the Boltzmann constant. This result bridges the macroscopic observable $T$ and the microscopic world, confirming that absolute zero ($T = 0\,\text{K}$) corresponds to complete cessation of thermal motion.

Record the readings, convert each to Kelvin, and verify that the ice-point and steam-point values match $273.15\,\text{K}$ and $373.15\,\text{K}$, respectively. 🍃 Heat

🍃 Heat

Definition. Heat is the energy transferred between a system and its surroundings due solely to a temperature difference. It is not a property possessed by a body but rather a process quantity—energy in transit. Formally, if a system undergoes a quasi-static process, the infinitesimal heat transfer is denoted $\delta Q$, with the sign convention that $Q > 0$ when energy flows into the system and $Q < 0$ when energy flows out. Heat is measured in joules (J) in SI, though the calorie (1 cal = 4.186 J) remains common in thermochemical contexts. Unlike internal energy $U$, heat has no meaning independent of a process; one cannot speak of "the heat contained in a body." The rate at which heat is transferred depends on the temperature difference, the material properties, and the geometry of the system—but the quantity $Q$ itself refers only to the total energy exchanged during a process.

Worked Example. A copper block of mass $m = 0.500\ \text{kg}$ is heated from $T_i = 20.0^\circ\text{C}$ to $T_f = 80.0^\circ\text{C}$. The specific heat capacity of copper is $c = 385\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$. The heat absorbed is

$$ Q = mc\Delta T = (0.500\ \text{kg})(385\ \text{J/(kg·K)})(80.0 - 20.0\ \text{K}) = 1.16 \times 10^4\ \text{J}. $$

Since $Q > 0$, energy flows into the copper, consistent with the temperature rise. Note that $\Delta T$ in kelvins equals $\Delta T$ in degrees Celsius for a difference, so no unit conversion is required beyond the subtraction.

Key Theorem — First Law Connection. The First Law of Thermodynamics relates heat to work and internal energy:

$$ \Delta U = Q - W, $$

where $W$ is the work done by the system on its surroundings. This equation codifies energy conservation: the change in internal energy equals the heat added minus the work extracted. For a constant-volume process ($W = 0$), all heat transferred goes directly into raising the internal energy, giving $\Delta U = Q_V = nC_V\Delta T$ for an ideal gas, where $C_V$ is the molar heat capacity at constant volume.

Lab Exercise. Fill a calorimeter with $200\ \text{g}$ of water at $25^\circ\text{C}$ and submerge a pre-heated $50\ \text{g}$ aluminum slug ($c_{Al} = 900\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$) initially at $95^\circ\text{C}$. Record the equilibrium temperature $T_{eq}$, then calculate $Q_{Al} = -Q_{water}$ to verify energy conservation and determine whether any heat was lost to the calorimeter walls.

🍃 Phase Change

🍃 Phase Change

Definition. A phase change (or phase transition) is a thermodynamic process in which a substance transforms from one state of matter to another—solid, liquid, or gas—at constant temperature and pressure. During a phase change, energy is exchanged between the system and its surroundings without any change in temperature; this energy, called latent heat, is used entirely to alter the intermolecular structure rather than the kinetic energy of the molecules. The latent heat $L$ is defined by

$$ Q = mL, $$

where $Q$ is the heat absorbed or released, $m$ is the mass of the substance, and $L$ has SI units of $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$. Two values of $L$ are in common use: the specific latent heat of fusion $L_f$, which governs solid–liquid transitions, and the specific latent heat of vaporization $L_v$, which governs liquid–gas transitions.

Worked Example. Determine the total heat required to convert $0.500\,\mathrm{kg}$ of ice at $-10.0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ entirely into steam at $100.0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Use $c_\text{ice} = 2090\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}K^{-1}}$, $c_\text{water} = 4186\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}K^{-1}}$, $L_f = 3.34\times10^5\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$, and $L_v = 2.26\times10^6\,\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$.

$$ Q_1 = mc_\text{ice}\,\Delta T_1 = (0.500)(2090)(10.0) = 1.045\times10^4\,\mathrm{J} $$ $$ Q_2 = mL_f = (0.500)(3.34\times10^5) = 1.670\times10^5\,\mathrm{J} $$ $$ Q_3 = mc_\text{water}\,\Delta T_3 = (0.500)(4186)(100.0) = 2.093\times10^5\,\mathrm{J} $$ $$ Q_4 = mL_v = (0.500)(2.26\times10^6) = 1.130\times10^6\,\mathrm{J} $$ $$ Q_\text{total} = Q_1+Q_2+Q_3+Q_4 \approx 1.51\times10^6\,\mathrm{J}. $$

Vaporization accounts for roughly 75% of the total energy requirement, illustrating the dominant role of $L_v$ relative to all other contributions in this process.

Key Theorem. At thermodynamic equilibrium, the Clausius–Clapeyron equation describes how the saturation pressure $P$ varies with temperature $T$ along a phase boundary:

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L\,P}{R\,T^2}, $$

where $R$ is the specific gas constant. This relation predicts that the boiling point of a liquid decreases with decreasing ambient pressure, consistent with the observation that water boils below $100\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ at high altitudes.

Lab Exercise. Using a calorimeter containing $200\,\mathrm{g}$ of water at $20\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$, add a $50.0\,\mathrm{g}$ ice cube at $0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Measure the equilibrium temperature and use energy conservation—applying $Q = mL_f$ for the melting ice and $Q = mc\,\Delta T$ for both water masses—to estimate $L_f$, then compare your result to the accepted value and identify sources of systematic error.

🍃 Latent Heat

🍃 Latent Heat

Definition. When a substance undergoes a phase transition—melting, freezing, vaporization, or condensation—energy is exchanged with the environment at constant temperature and pressure. This energy, called latent heat, does not raise or lower the thermometric temperature of the substance; instead, it drives the rearrangement of intermolecular bonds. The latent heat $L$ is defined as the energy per unit mass required (or released) during the transition:

$$ Q = mL $$

where $Q$ is the total heat transferred (in joules), $m$ is the mass of the substance (in kilograms), and $L$ is the specific latent heat (in $\mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$). For fusion (solid $\leftrightarrow$ liquid) the quantity is called the latent heat of fusion $L_f$; for vaporization (liquid $\leftrightarrow$ gas) it is the latent heat of vaporization $L_v$. For water at standard pressure, $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$ and $L_v = 2.26 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$.

Worked Example. A kettle contains $0.500\ \mathrm{kg}$ of water already at its boiling point ($100\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$, $1\ \mathrm{atm}$). How much energy must be supplied to convert it entirely to steam?

$$ Q = mL_v = (0.500\ \mathrm{kg})(2.26 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}) = 1.13 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{J} $$

Note that no temperature change occurs during this process; all $1.13\ \mathrm{MJ}$ goes into breaking hydrogen bonds between water molecules.

Key Theorem (Clausius–Clapeyron relation). The latent heat is connected to the slope of the phase boundary in the $P$–$T$ diagram by

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T\,\Delta v} $$

where $\Delta v$ is the specific volume change across the phase boundary. This result, derived from the condition that the chemical potential is equal on both sides of the boundary, shows that a substance with large $L$ has a steeply sloped liquid–vapor curve.

Lab Exercise. Place $200\ \mathrm{g}$ of ice at $0\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ into a thermally insulated calorimeter containing $500\ \mathrm{g}$ of water at $40\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$. Using $c_w = 4186\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}K^{-1}}$ and $L_f = 3.34 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{J\,kg^{-1}}$, apply energy conservation to predict the equilibrium temperature, then measure it with a thermometer and compute the percentage error in your value of $L_f$.

🍃 Latent Heat Of Vaporization

🍃 Latent Heat Of Vaporization

Definition. The latent heat of vaporization, denoted $L_v$, is the amount of thermal energy per unit mass required to convert a substance from its liquid phase to its gaseous phase at constant temperature and pressure. During this phase transition, supplied heat does not raise the temperature of the substance; instead it overcomes intermolecular attractions and performs work against the surrounding pressure to expand the vapor. Formally,

$$ Q = m L_v $$

where $Q$ is the heat energy transferred (in joules), $m$ is the mass of the substance (in kilograms), and $L_v$ is the latent heat of vaporization (in $\text{J/kg}$). For water at $100^\circ\text{C}$ and standard atmospheric pressure, $L_v \approx 2.26 \times 10^6 \, \text{J/kg}$.

Worked Example. A kettle contains $0.500 \, \text{kg}$ of water already at its boiling point. How much heat must be supplied to completely vaporize the water?

Using $Q = m L_v$:

$$ Q = (0.500 \, \text{kg})(2.26 \times 10^6 \, \text{J/kg}) = 1.13 \times 10^6 \, \text{J} $$

Thus approximately $1.13 \, \text{MJ}$ of energy is required. This energy breaks intermolecular hydrogen bonds and expands the vapor against atmospheric pressure, increasing the internal energy of the system while leaving the temperature unchanged at $100^\circ\text{C}$.

Key Theorem — Energy Partition During Vaporization. The latent heat of vaporization partitions into two contributions: the increase in internal energy $\Delta U$ associated with separating molecules, and the work $P_{\text{atm}}\,\Delta V$ done by the expanding vapor against the atmosphere. Per unit mass,

$$ L_v = \frac{\Delta U}{m} + \frac{P_{\text{atm}}\,\Delta V}{m} $$

For water at $100^\circ\text{C}$, approximately $92\%$ of $L_v$ goes to $\Delta U$ and only $8\%$ to $P_{\text{atm}}\,\Delta V$, confirming that intermolecular bond-breaking dominates the energy requirement rather than mechanical expansion work.

Lab Exercise. Heat $200 \, \text{g}$ of water from $25^\circ\text{C}$ to complete vaporization using an immersion heater of known wattage $W$. Record the time $t_1$ required to reach $100^\circ\text{C}$ and the additional time $t_2$ to fully vaporize the sample. Use $c_w = 4186 \, \text{J/(kg·K)}$ and the measured $W \cdot t_2$ to experimentally determine $L_v$. Compare your result to the accepted value and compute the percent error, identifying possible sources of heat loss to the surroundings.

🌸 Evaporative Cooling

🌸 Evaporative Cooling

Definition. Evaporative cooling is the process by which a liquid loses thermal energy when its highest-kinetic-energy molecules escape the liquid surface as vapor. Because intermolecular attractive forces must be overcome for a molecule to leave the liquid phase, only those molecules whose kinetic energy exceeds the energy barrier set by the latent heat of vaporization $L_v$ (the energy per unit mass required to convert liquid to vapor at constant temperature) can escape. Their departure removes energy disproportionately from the remaining liquid, lowering its average kinetic energy—and therefore its temperature. Quantitatively, if a mass $\Delta m$ evaporates from a liquid of mass $m$ and specific heat $c$, the temperature drop is

$$ \Delta T = -\frac{L_v \,\Delta m}{m c}, $$

where $c$ and $m$ are treated as approximately constant over the small temperature change involved.

Worked Example. A student places 200 g of water at 30 °C in an open container. Due to air currents, 2.0 g evaporates before equilibrium is re-established. Determine the temperature drop of the remaining water. Given: $L_v = 2.43 \times 10^6\ \text{J kg}^{-1}$, $c = 4186\ \text{J kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$.

$$ \Delta T = -\frac{L_v \,\Delta m}{m c} = -\frac{(2.43 \times 10^6)(0.0020)}{(0.200)(4186)} \approx -5.8\ \text{K}. $$

The water cools by approximately 5.8 °C, reaching about 24.2 °C. Note that the evaporated mass (1% of total) produces a temperature change nearly six times larger than would result from simply removing the same mass at the same temperature, because $L_v \operatorname{gg} c\,\Delta T$ for modest temperature changes.

Key Theorem (Maxwell–Boltzmann Escape Criterion). In a liquid at temperature $T$, the fraction of molecules with kinetic energy exceeding the per-molecule escape threshold $\varepsilon_0 = L_v / N_A$ is proportional to $e^{-\varepsilon_0 / k_B T}$. Consequently, the evaporation rate increases exponentially with temperature, consistent with the Clausius–Clapeyron equation

$$ \frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L_v\, P}{R\, T^2}, $$

which describes how vapor pressure rises steeply with temperature and follows directly from applying the second law of thermodynamics to the liquid–vapor phase boundary.

Lab Exercise. Wrap a thermometer bulb in a cotton wick soaked in isopropyl alcohol ($L_v \approx 8.5 \times 10^5\ \text{J kg}^{-1}$, $c \approx 2600\ \text{J kg}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$). Record the temperature every 30 s as the alcohol evaporates in moving air. Using the measured temperature drop and the known mass loss (determined by weighing before and after), verify the expression for $\Delta T$ and compare your computed $L_v$ with the accepted value. Discuss sources of systematic error, including heat exchange with the surrounding air.

Payoff 🎯

Evaporative cooling represents the culmination of thermodynamic reasoning developed throughout this chapter, unifying the kinetic theory of gases, the Maxwell–Boltzmann speed distribution, latent heat, and vapor pressure into a single coherent physical phenomenon. At its core, evaporative cooling is not merely a practical observation — it is a direct, macroscopic consequence of the statistical nature of molecular energy: the most energetic molecules preferentially escape a liquid surface, leaving behind a population with a lower mean kinetic energy and therefore a lower temperature.

To state this precisely: consider a liquid in thermal equilibrium at temperature $T$. The fraction of molecules with speed $v$ is governed by the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution,

$$ f(v) = 4\pi \left(\frac{m}{2\pi k_B T}\right)^{3/2} v^2 \exp\!\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2k_BT}\right). $$

A molecule escapes the surface only if its kinetic energy in the direction normal to the surface exceeds the cohesive potential energy $U_0$, i.e., $\frac{1}{2}mv_\perp^2 > U_0$. Because this condition selectively removes the high-energy tail of the distribution, the remaining liquid's mean kinetic energy — and hence its temperature — decreases. The energy extracted per mole of evaporated liquid is the latent heat of vaporization $L_v$, so the cooling rate satisfies

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = L_v \frac{dn}{dt}, $$

where $dn/dt$ is the molar evaporation rate, itself strongly dependent on temperature through the Clausius–Clapeyron relation.

Worked Example. Suppose $0.50\ \text{kg}$ of water at $37\ ^\circ\text{C}$ evaporates from human skin. With $L_v \approx 2.43 \times 10^6\ \text{J kg}^{-1}$, the heat removed is $Q = L_v m = (2.43\times10^6)(0.50) = 1.22\times10^6\ \text{J}$, demonstrating how a modest mass of evaporated sweat can offset substantial metabolic heat production.

Key Theorem (Equipartition and Selective Escape). Because the escaping molecules carry away above-average energy, the residual distribution is no longer Maxwell–Boltzmann at the original temperature; the system relaxes toward a new, lower equilibrium temperature through continued collisions — a self-sustaining cooling cycle as long as evaporation proceeds.

Lab Exercise. Measure the surface temperature of an ethanol-wetted thermocouple in still air and under forced airflow. Identify which variables — vapor pressure, surface area, or airflow — dominate the rate.

Evaporative cooling is the gateway to an expansive landscape of applications: from the biophysics of thermoregulation and the engineering of swamp coolers to cryogenic laser cooling of atomic gases near absolute zero. Each domain reuses exactly the same statistical argument at a different energy scale.
