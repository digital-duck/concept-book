🍃 Conduction

🍃 Conduction

Definition. Thermal conduction is the transfer of internal energy through a material by direct molecular interaction, without any bulk motion of the material itself. In a solid, more energetic (higher-temperature) atoms vibrate with greater amplitude and collide with adjacent atoms, transferring kinetic energy along the temperature gradient. The macroscopic rate of heat transfer by conduction is governed by Fourier's Law:

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = -kA\frac{dT}{dx} $$

where $\frac{dQ}{dt}$ is the rate of heat flow (in watts), $k$ is the thermal conductivity of the material (W/m·K), $A$ is the cross-sectional area perpendicular to heat flow (m²), and $\frac{dT}{dx}$ is the temperature gradient along the direction of flow. The negative sign indicates that heat flows from higher to lower temperature. Thermal conductivity $k$ is the single new material property introduced here; it characterizes how readily a substance transmits heat and varies widely across materials, from roughly $0.025\ \text{W/m·K}$ for air to over $400\ \text{W/m·K}$ for copper.

Worked Example. A steel rod ($k = 50\ \text{W/m·K}$) has a cross-sectional area of $A = 4.0 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2$ and a length of $L = 0.50\ \text{m}$. One end is maintained at $T_H = 200°\text{C}$ and the other at $T_C = 20°\text{C}$. Find the steady-state rate of heat conduction through the rod.

Assuming a uniform temperature gradient:

$$ \frac{dT}{dx} \approx \frac{T_C - T_H}{L} = \frac{20 - 200}{0.50} = -360\ \text{K/m} $$

Applying Fourier's Law:

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = -kA\frac{dT}{dx} = -(50)(4.0 \times 10^{-4})(-360) = 7.2\ \text{W} $$

The rod conducts heat at a rate of $7.2\ \text{W}$ from the hot end toward the cold end.

Key Theorem. In steady-state conduction through a composite slab (layers in series), the heat flux is identical through every layer, and the total thermal resistance is additive:

$$ R_{\text{total}} = \sum_i \frac{L_i}{k_i A} \implies \frac{dQ}{dt} = \frac{\Delta T}{R_{\text{total}}} $$

This result follows directly from requiring energy conservation at each interface under steady-state conditions. It is the thermal analogue of Ohm's law, with heat current corresponding to electric current and temperature difference to voltage.

Lab Exercise. Obtain two rods of equal dimensions but different materials (e.g., copper and aluminum). Apply the same temperature differential across each rod using thermal reservoirs and measure the equilibrium heat flow rate using a power meter. Calculate the experimental thermal conductivity $k$ for each material and compare your results to tabulated values. Quantify the percentage error and identify possible sources of systematic uncertainty such as lateral heat loss.
