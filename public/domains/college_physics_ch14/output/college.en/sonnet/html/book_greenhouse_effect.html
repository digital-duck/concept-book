🌱 Temperature

🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar intensive property that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. Two systems are said to be in thermal equilibrium when they share the same temperature and no net energy transfer occurs between them upon contact. This observation is formalized by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, and system $B$ is also in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then $A$ and $B$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. The Zeroth Law establishes temperature as a well-defined, transitive property and underpins the logical foundation of thermometry.

Temperature is measured on several scales. The Celsius scale sets $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the ice point and $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the steam point of water at standard pressure. The Kelvin (absolute) scale shifts the origin to absolute zero, the theoretical minimum temperature at which molecular motion ceases:

$$ T(\text{K}) = T(^\circ\text{C}) + 273.15. $$

The Fahrenheit scale, common in everyday U.S. usage, relates to Celsius via $T_F = \frac{9}{5}T_C + 32$.

Worked Example. A mercury thermometer reads $98.6\,^\circ\text{F}$, the normal human body temperature. Convert to Celsius and Kelvin.

Step 1 — Fahrenheit to Celsius:

$$ T_C = \frac{5}{9}(T_F - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(98.6 - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(66.6) = 37.0\,^\circ\text{C}. $$

Step 2 — Celsius to Kelvin:

$$ T_K = 37.0 + 273.15 = 310.15\,\text{K} \approx 310\,\text{K}. $$

Key Theorem. From kinetic theory, the average translational kinetic energy of a monatomic ideal-gas molecule is directly proportional to the absolute temperature:

$$ \langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2}k_B T, $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\,\text{J/K}$ is the Boltzmann constant. This result bridges the macroscopic observable $T$ and the microscopic world, confirming that absolute zero ($T = 0\,\text{K}$) corresponds to complete cessation of thermal motion.

🍃 Heat

🍃 Heat

Definition. Heat is the energy transferred between a system and its surroundings due solely to a temperature difference. It is not a property possessed by a body but rather a process quantity—energy in transit. Formally, if a system undergoes a quasi-static process, the infinitesimal heat transfer is denoted $\delta Q$, with the sign convention that $Q > 0$ when energy flows into the system and $Q < 0$ when energy flows out. Heat is measured in joules (J) in SI, though the calorie (1 cal = 4.186 J) remains common in thermochemical contexts. Unlike internal energy $U$, heat has no meaning independent of a process; one cannot speak of "the heat contained in a body." The rate at which heat is transferred depends on the temperature difference, the material properties, and the geometry of the system—but the quantity $Q$ itself refers only to the total energy exchanged during a process.

Worked Example. A copper block of mass $m = 0.500\ \text{kg}$ is heated from $T_i = 20.0^\circ\text{C}$ to $T_f = 80.0^\circ\text{C}$. The specific heat capacity of copper is $c = 385\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$. The heat absorbed is

$$ Q = mc\Delta T = (0.500\ \text{kg})(385\ \text{J/(kg·K)})(80.0 - 20.0\ \text{K}) = 1.16 \times 10^4\ \text{J}. $$

Since $Q > 0$, energy flows into the copper, consistent with the temperature rise. Note that $\Delta T$ in kelvins equals $\Delta T$ in degrees Celsius for a difference, so no unit conversion is required beyond the subtraction.

Key Theorem — First Law Connection. The First Law of Thermodynamics relates heat to work and internal energy:

$$ \Delta U = Q - W, $$

where $W$ is the work done by the system on its surroundings. This equation codifies energy conservation: the change in internal energy equals the heat added minus the work extracted. For a constant-volume process ($W = 0$), all heat transferred goes directly into raising the internal energy, giving $\Delta U = Q_V = nC_V\Delta T$ for an ideal gas, where $C_V$ is the molar heat capacity at constant volume.

Lab Exercise. Fill a calorimeter with $200\ \text{g}$ of water at $25^\circ\text{C}$ and submerge a pre-heated $50\ \text{g}$ aluminum slug ($c_{Al} = 900\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$) initially at $95^\circ\text{C}$. Record the equilibrium temperature $T_{eq}$, then calculate $Q_{Al} = -Q_{water}$ to verify energy conservation and determine whether any heat was lost to the calorimeter walls.

🍃 Radiation

🍃 Radiation

Definition. Radiation is the transfer of thermal energy through the emission and absorption of electromagnetic waves, requiring no intervening medium. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation propagates through vacuum. All objects with temperature $T > 0\,\text{K}$ emit thermal radiation, with the radiated power depending on the nature of the surface through a dimensionless material property called emissivity $\varepsilon \in [0,1]$. A surface with $\varepsilon = 1$ is an ideal blackbody — a perfect emitter and absorber of radiation. The power radiated by a surface is governed by the Stefan–Boltzmann Law:

$$ P = \sigma \varepsilon A T^4 $$

where $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\,\text{W}\,\text{m}^{-2}\,\text{K}^{-4}$ is the Stefan–Boltzmann constant, $A$ is the surface area in $\text{m}^2$, and $T$ is the absolute temperature in Kelvin. The net power radiated by an object at temperature $T$ surrounded by an environment at temperature $T_{\text{env}}$ is:

$$ P_{\text{net}} = \sigma \varepsilon A \left(T^4 - T_{\text{env}}^4\right) $$

A positive $P_{\text{net}}$ indicates net energy loss from the object to the surroundings.

Worked Example. A spherical blackbody ($\varepsilon = 1$) of radius $r = 0.10\,\text{m}$ is maintained at $T = 500\,\text{K}$ in a room at $T_{\text{env}} = 300\,\text{K}$. Calculate the net radiated power.

Surface area: $A = 4\pi r^2 = 4\pi(0.10)^2 \approx 0.1257\,\text{m}^2$.

$$ P_{\text{net}} = (5.67\times10^{-8})(1)(0.1257)\left(500^4 - 300^4\right) $$

Computing: $500^4 = 6.25\times10^{10}$, $300^4 = 8.10\times10^{9}$, so the difference is $5.44\times10^{10}\,\text{K}^4$.

$$ P_{\text{net}} \approx (5.67\times10^{-8})(0.1257)(5.44\times10^{10}) \approx 388\,\text{W} $$

Key Theorem. The Stefan–Boltzmann Law implies that radiated power scales as the fourth power of absolute temperature. Consequently, doubling $T$ increases $P$ by a factor of $2^4 = 16$, making radiation the dominant heat-transfer mechanism at high temperatures. At thermal equilibrium, $P_{\text{net}} = 0$ only when $T = T_{\text{env}}$, confirming that radiative exchange drives the system toward equilibrium.

Lab Exercise. Using a Stefan–Boltzmann sensor and a heated metal sphere of known area and emissivity, measure radiated power at three different sphere temperatures (e.g., 350 K, 450 K, 550 K). Plot $P$ versus $T^4$ and determine the slope. Compare the experimental slope $\sigma \varepsilon A$ to the theoretical value, and calculate the percent error in your measured $\varepsilon$.

🍃 Emissivity

🍃 Emissivity

Definition. Emissivity, denoted $\varepsilon$, is a dimensionless material property that quantifies how effectively a surface emits thermal radiation relative to an ideal blackbody at the same temperature. Formally, it is defined as the ratio of the radiant power emitted per unit area by the surface to that emitted by a perfect blackbody at identical temperature:

$$ \varepsilon = \frac{P_{\text{surface}}}{P_{\text{blackbody}}} = \frac{P}{A\sigma T^4} $$

where $P$ is the total radiated power, $A$ is the surface area, $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}\,K^{-4}}$ is the Stefan–Boltzmann constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvins. By definition, $0 \leq \varepsilon \leq 1$, with $\varepsilon = 1$ corresponding to a perfect blackbody and $\varepsilon = 0$ to a perfect reflector. The Stefan–Boltzmann law for a real surface therefore takes the form:

$$ P = \varepsilon \sigma A T^4 $$

Worked Example. A tungsten filament at $T = 3000\ \mathrm{K}$ has emissivity $\varepsilon = 0.45$ and a surface area $A = 8.0 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{m^2}$. Find the total radiated power.

$$ P = \varepsilon \sigma A T^4 = (0.45)(5.67 \times 10^{-8})(8.0 \times 10^{-5})(3000)^4 $$

Computing $(3000)^4 = 8.1 \times 10^{13}\ \mathrm{K^4}$:

$$ P = (0.45)(5.67 \times 10^{-8})(8.0 \times 10^{-5})(8.1 \times 10^{13}) \approx 16.6\ \mathrm{W} $$

A blackbody of the same area and temperature would radiate approximately $36.9\ \mathrm{W}$; tungsten emits roughly $45\%$ of that maximum.

Key Theorem — Kirchhoff's Law of Thermal Radiation. For any surface in thermodynamic equilibrium, the spectral emissivity equals the spectral absorptivity at every wavelength:

$$ \varepsilon_\lambda = \alpha_\lambda $$

This fundamental result implies that a surface that is a poor emitter is equally a poor absorber. A blackbody ($\varepsilon = 1$) absorbs all incident radiation and emits the maximum possible power; polished metals ($\varepsilon \approx 0.02$–$0.10$) both reflect and emit poorly in the infrared.

🌸 Greenhouse Effect

🌸 Greenhouse Effect

Definition. The greenhouse effect is the process by which certain atmospheric gases—collectively called greenhouse gases—absorb infrared radiation emitted by Earth's surface and re-radiate it in all directions, including back toward the surface. This trapping of outgoing longwave radiation elevates the equilibrium surface temperature above the value predicted by treating Earth as a bare blackbody radiating into space. The primary greenhouse gases are water vapor (H₂O), carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O); their molecules possess vibrational and rotational modes that couple strongly to infrared photons in the 5–100 µm range.

Radiative Equilibrium. Applying the Stefan–Boltzmann law and energy balance, the effective radiating temperature of a planet is

$$ T_{\text{eff}} = \left(\frac{S_0(1-\alpha)}{4\sigma}\right)^{1/4}, $$

where $S_0$ is the solar constant, $\alpha$ is the albedo, and $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}\,K^{-4}}$. For Earth, $S_0 \approx 1361\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}}$ and $\alpha \approx 0.30$, yielding $T_{\text{eff}} \approx 255\ \mathrm{K}$. Earth's observed mean surface temperature is approximately $288\ \mathrm{K}$; the $\approx 33\ \mathrm{K}$ difference quantifies the natural greenhouse contribution.

Worked Example. A planet has $\alpha = 0.25$ and $S_0 = 1400\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}}$. Compute its effective temperature:

$$ T_{\text{eff}} = \left(\frac{1400 \times 0.75}{4 \times 5.67\times10^{-8}}\right)^{1/4} = \left(\frac{1050}{2.268\times10^{-7}}\right)^{1/4} \approx (4.63\times10^{9})^{1/4} \approx 261\ \mathrm{K}. $$

If greenhouse gases reduce the net outgoing flux, the surface temperature must rise until radiative balance is restored.

Key Theorem (Kirchhoff's Law Applied to the Atmosphere). At thermal equilibrium, absorptivity equals emissivity at each wavelength. Greenhouse gases therefore absorb and re-emit at the same infrared frequencies, conserving energy across the atmospheric column while redirecting a portion of outgoing flux back toward the surface—the physical mechanism underlying the temperature enhancement.

Payoff 🎯

The greenhouse effect is the mechanism by which a planet's atmosphere intercepts outgoing longwave radiation emitted by the surface and re-radiates a portion of it back downward, elevating the equilibrium surface temperature above what blackbody balance with solar input alone would predict. It is the natural endpoint of Chapter 14 because it synthesizes every thermal-physics concept encountered along the way: blackbody radiation, Stefan–Boltzmann emission, radiative equilibrium, and the wavelength-selective absorption properties of molecular gases. Mastery of the greenhouse effect means mastery of the entire radiative-transfer framework.

Formal statement. For a planet with no atmosphere, the equilibrium surface temperature $T_s$ satisfies

$$ \frac{L_\odot (1-\alpha)}{4} = \sigma T_s^4, $$

where $L_\odot$ is the solar irradiance at the planet's orbit, $\alpha$ is the Bond albedo, and $\sigma = 5.67\times10^{-8}\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}\,K^{-4}}$ is the Stefan–Boltzmann constant. Introducing a single-layer atmosphere with longwave emissivity $\varepsilon$ modifies the surface energy balance to

$$ \sigma T_s^4 = \frac{S_0(1-\alpha)}{4}\cdot\frac{1}{1-\varepsilon/2}, $$

so that $T_s$ rises monotonically with $\varepsilon$. For Earth, $S_0 \approx 1361\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}}$, $\alpha \approx 0.30$, and $\varepsilon \approx 0.78$, yielding $T_s \approx 288\ \mathrm{K}$—consistent with observation and roughly $33\ \mathrm{K}$ warmer than the bare-planet prediction of $255\ \mathrm{K}$.

Worked example. Suppose atmospheric $\mathrm{CO_2}$ concentration doubles, increasing $\varepsilon$ by $0.02$. Differentiating the equilibrium relation gives $\Delta T_s \approx \frac{T_s}{2}\cdot\frac{\Delta\varepsilon}{1-\varepsilon/2} \approx 0.9\ \mathrm{K}$ before feedback amplification—a first-order estimate of the no-feedback climate sensitivity.

Key theorem. The Planck feedback parameter $\lambda_0 = -\partial(\sigma T_s^4)/\partial T_s = -4\sigma T_s^3 \approx -3.2\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}\,K^{-1}}$ is negative, guaranteeing that the bare radiative system is thermodynamically stable; external feedbacks (water vapour, ice-albedo) modify this restoring coefficient and determine net climate sensitivity.
