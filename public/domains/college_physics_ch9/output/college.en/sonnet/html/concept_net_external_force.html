🌱 Net External Force

🌱 Net External Force

Definition. The net external force acting on a system is the vector sum of all forces exerted on that system by agents outside its boundaries. For a system of particles with total mass $M$, Newton's second law states

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_{i}^{\text{ext}} = M\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}, $$

where $\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}$ is the acceleration of the system's center of mass. Internal forces—those between particles within the system—appear in equal and opposite pairs by Newton's third law and therefore cancel identically in the sum, contributing nothing to $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$. This cancellation reflects the physical principle that a system cannot accelerate itself through purely internal interactions.

Worked Example. Two blocks, $m_1 = 3\,\text{kg}$ and $m_2 = 5\,\text{kg}$, rest on a frictionless horizontal surface and are connected by a light string. A horizontal force $\vec{F} = 16\,\text{N}$ is applied to $m_2$. Treating both blocks together as the system (total mass $M = 8\,\text{kg}$), the tension $T$ in the string is an internal force and drops out of the net-force calculation. The only external horizontal force is $\vec{F}$, so

$$ a_{\text{cm}} = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{M} = \frac{16\,\text{N}}{8\,\text{kg}} = 2\,\text{m/s}^2. $$

To find $T$, isolate $m_1$ as a sub-system: the sole external horizontal force on it is $T$, giving $T = m_1 a = (3\,\text{kg})(2\,\text{m/s}^2) = 6\,\text{N}$.

Key Theorem — Momentum–Force Relation. The net external force equals the time rate of change of the system's total linear momentum $\vec{p} = M\vec{v}_{\text{cm}}$:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}. $$

This formulation is more general than $\vec{F} = M\vec{a}$ because it remains valid even when the system's mass changes with time, as in rocket propulsion. When $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{0}$, it follows immediately that $\vec{p}$ is constant—the conservation of linear momentum.

Lab Exercise. Place two carts of measured masses on an air track. Apply a known external force (a hanging weight via pulley) to the two-cart system and record the resulting acceleration using photogates. Confirm that the measured acceleration satisfies $a = F_{\text{net}}/(m_1 + m_2)$ to within 5%, verifying that only the external force—not any internal contact force between the carts—governs the system's center-of-mass motion.
