🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. In classical mechanics, a force is a vector quantity that describes an interaction capable of changing the momentum of an object. Formally, Newton's second law defines net force as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} = m\vec{a} $$

where $\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$ is the linear momentum, $m$ is the inertial mass (assumed constant in non-relativistic contexts), and $\vec{a}$ is the acceleration of the object. The SI unit of force is the newton: $1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Force is a free vector applied at a point of contact or acting at a distance (e.g., gravity, electrostatic force), and multiple forces superpose by vector addition: $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_i \vec{F}_i$.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 5.0\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\,\hat{x} + 6\,\hat{y}\,\text{N}$. Find the acceleration.

Solution. Compute the net force component-wise:

$$ F_x = 12 + (-4) = 8\,\text{N}, \qquad F_y = 0 + 6 = 6\,\text{N} $$ $$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = 8\,\hat{x} + 6\,\hat{y}\,\text{N}, \qquad |\vec{F}_{\text{net}}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 6^2} = 10\,\text{N} $$

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8\,\hat{x} + 6\,\hat{y}}{5.0} = 1.6\,\hat{x} + 1.2\,\hat{y}\;\text{m/s}^2 $$

The magnitude of acceleration is $|\vec{a}| = 2.0\,\text{m/s}^2$ directed at $\theta = \arctan(6/8) \approx 36.9°$ above the $x$-axis.

Key Theorem (Newton's Third Law). For every force $\vec{F}_{A \to B}$ exerted by object $A$ on object $B$, there exists a reaction force $\vec{F}_{B \to A} = -\vec{F}_{A \to B}$. These forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects, so they do not cancel when analyzing a single body's free-body diagram.

🍃 Pivot Point

🍃 Pivot Point

Definition. A pivot point (also called a fulcrum or axis of rotation) is the fixed point about which a rigid body rotates when subjected to a net torque. For a body in static equilibrium or undergoing pure rotation, the pivot point serves as the reference location from which all torques are calculated. The torque $\tau$ produced by a force $\vec{F}$ applied at position $\vec{r}$ relative to the pivot is defined as

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F} $$

with magnitude $\tau = rF\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$. The choice of pivot point is mathematically arbitrary for a system in complete static equilibrium, but a judicious choice eliminates unknown reaction forces from the torque equation, greatly simplifying analysis.

Worked Example. Consider a uniform horizontal beam of length $L = 4.0\ \text{m}$ and mass $m = 20\ \text{kg}$, supported at its left end by a hinge and held horizontal by a cable attached at the right end at angle $\theta = 30°$ above the beam. A box of mass $M = 50\ \text{kg}$ rests at $x = 3.0\ \text{m}$ from the hinge. Find the tension $T$ in the cable.

Choose the hinge as the pivot point. The hinge exerts an unknown reaction force, but by placing the pivot there, that force contributes zero torque (moment arm $= 0$). Applying $\sum \tau = 0$:

$$ T\sin(30°)\cdot L - Mg\cdot x - mg\cdot\frac{L}{2} = 0 $$ $$ T(0.500)(4.0) = (50)(9.8)(3.0) + (20)(9.8)(2.0) $$ $$ 2T = 1470 + 392 = 1862\ \text{N·m} $$ $$ T = 931\ \text{N} $$

Key Theorem. For a rigid body in static equilibrium, the sum of all torques about any pivot point must vanish:

$$ \sum_i \tau_i = 0 $$

This follows from Newton's second law for rotation: $\sum \tau = I\alpha$, which equals zero when $\alpha = 0$. The theorem guarantees that equilibrium is pivot-independent — a powerful consistency check.

Verify your result using the torque-balance equation with the fulcrum as pivot, and calculate the percent discrepancy between predicted and measured positions.

🍃 Perpendicular Lever Arm

🍃 Perpendicular Lever Arm

Definition. The perpendicular lever arm (also called the moment arm) of a force $\vec{F}$ with respect to a pivot point $O$ is the perpendicular distance $r_\perp$ from $O$ to the line of action of $\vec{F}$. Formally, if $\vec{r}$ is the position vector from $O$ to any point on the line of action, and $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$, then

$$ r_\perp = r\sin\theta, $$

where $r = |\vec{r}|$. The torque produced by the force about $O$ is then

$$ \tau = F\, r_\perp = rF\sin\theta, $$

which is the magnitude of the cross product $\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$. The sign of $\tau$ is positive for counterclockwise rotation and negative for clockwise rotation (using the right-hand rule in three dimensions).

Worked Example. A mechanic applies a force $F = 80\,\text{N}$ to a wrench handle at a point $r = 0.30\,\text{m}$ from the bolt center. The force is directed at $\theta = 60°$ from the handle axis. Find the torque about the bolt.

$$ r_\perp = r\sin\theta = (0.30\,\text{m})\sin 60° = (0.30)(0.866) \approx 0.260\,\text{m}. $$ $$ \tau = F\,r_\perp = (80\,\text{N})(0.260\,\text{m}) \approx 20.8\,\text{N·m}. $$

Equivalently, $\tau = rF\sin\theta = (0.30)(80)(0.866) \approx 20.8\,\text{N·m}$. Note that applying the force perpendicular to the handle ($\theta = 90°$) would maximize the torque to $\tau_{\max} = rF = 24\,\text{N·m}$, confirming that $r_\perp = r$ only when the force is perpendicular to the position vector.

Key Theorem. The torque of a force about a point depends only on its perpendicular lever arm, not on where along the line of action the force is applied. Any two forces sharing the same line of action produce identical torques about a given pivot — a consequence of the geometric definition of $r_\perp$ as a property of the line, not the specific point of application.

Lab Exercise. Mount a uniform meter stick horizontally on a pivot at the 50 cm mark. Hang a 200 g mass at the 20 cm mark. At what position must a 150 g mass be placed on the opposite side of the pivot to achieve rotational equilibrium? Apply $\sum \tau = 0$, computing each torque using $\tau = mg\,r_\perp$, and verify your predicted position experimentally by observing whether the stick remains level.

🌱 Net External Force

🌱 Net External Force

Definition. The net external force acting on a system is the vector sum of all forces exerted on that system by agents outside its boundaries. For a system of particles with total mass $M$, Newton's second law states

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_{i}^{\text{ext}} = M\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}, $$

where $\vec{a}_{\text{cm}}$ is the acceleration of the system's center of mass. Internal forces—those between particles within the system—appear in equal and opposite pairs by Newton's third law and therefore cancel identically in the sum, contributing nothing to $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$. This cancellation reflects the physical principle that a system cannot accelerate itself through purely internal interactions.

Worked Example. Two blocks, $m_1 = 3\,\text{kg}$ and $m_2 = 5\,\text{kg}$, rest on a frictionless horizontal surface and are connected by a light string. A horizontal force $\vec{F} = 16\,\text{N}$ is applied to $m_2$. Treating both blocks together as the system (total mass $M = 8\,\text{kg}$), the tension $T$ in the string is an internal force and drops out of the net-force calculation. The only external horizontal force is $\vec{F}$, so

$$ a_{\text{cm}} = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{M} = \frac{16\,\text{N}}{8\,\text{kg}} = 2\,\text{m/s}^2. $$

To find $T$, isolate $m_1$ as a sub-system: the sole external horizontal force on it is $T$, giving $T = m_1 a = (3\,\text{kg})(2\,\text{m/s}^2) = 6\,\text{N}$.

Key Theorem — Momentum–Force Relation. The net external force equals the time rate of change of the system's total linear momentum $\vec{p} = M\vec{v}_{\text{cm}}$:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}. $$

This formulation is more general than $\vec{F} = M\vec{a}$ because it remains valid even when the system's mass changes with time, as in rocket propulsion. When $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{0}$, it follows immediately that $\vec{p}$ is constant—the conservation of linear momentum.

Lab Exercise. Place two carts of measured masses on an air track. Apply a known external force (a hanging weight via pulley) to the two-cart system and record the resulting acceleration using photogates. Confirm that the measured acceleration satisfies $a = F_{\text{net}}/(m_1 + m_2)$ to within 5%, verifying that only the external force—not any internal contact force between the carts—governs the system's center-of-mass motion.

🍃 Torque

🍃 Torque

Definition. Torque is the rotational analog of force, quantifying the tendency of an applied force to produce angular acceleration about a specified pivot point. Given a force $\vec{F}$ applied at a position vector $\vec{r}$ measured from the pivot, the torque is defined as the cross product

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}. $$

The magnitude is $\tau = rF\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{F}$. The SI unit of torque is the newton-meter ($\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$). The perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force is called the moment arm $\ell = r\sin\theta$, so equivalently $\tau = F\ell$. The direction of $\vec{\tau}$ is given by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand from $\vec{r}$ toward $\vec{F}$; the extended thumb points in the direction of $\vec{\tau}$.

Worked Example. A mechanic applies a force of $F = 80\,\text{N}$ perpendicular to the end of a wrench of length $r = 0.35\,\text{m}$. Since $\theta = 90°$, the torque about the bolt is

$$ \tau = rF\sin 90° = (0.35\,\text{m})(80\,\text{N})(1) = 28\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}. $$

If the same force were applied at $\theta = 30°$ instead, the torque would be $\tau = (0.35)(80)\sin 30° = 14\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$, half the previous value, illustrating that only the component of force perpendicular to $\vec{r}$ contributes to rotation.

Key Theorem — Newton's Second Law for Rotation. For a rigid body, the net torque about a fixed axis equals the product of the moment of inertia $I$ and the angular acceleration $\alpha$:

$$ \sum \tau = I\alpha. $$

This relation is the rotational counterpart of $\sum F = ma$. Moment of inertia $I$ encodes how mass is distributed about the rotation axis and is the single new primitive introduced here; once $I$ is known, this equation governs how quickly an object's angular velocity changes under an applied net torque.

🍃 Equilibrium

🍃 Equilibrium

Definition. A rigid body is in mechanical equilibrium when both its translational and rotational motion remain constant—equivalently, when the net force and net torque acting on it simultaneously vanish:

$$ \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}, \qquad \sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}. $$

The first condition ensures zero linear acceleration ($\vec{a} = \vec{0}$), while the second ensures zero angular acceleration ($\alpha = 0$). An object satisfying both conditions may be at rest or moving at constant velocity and constant angular velocity; in most introductory contexts, the body is at rest and the analysis focuses on the static case.

Worked Example. A uniform horizontal beam of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ and length $L = 4\,\text{m}$ is pinned to a wall at its left end and supported by a cable attached at its right end, making an angle $\theta = 30°$ above horizontal. Find the tension $T$ in the cable and the reaction force at the pin.

Choose the pivot at the pin to eliminate the pin reaction from the torque equation. The beam's weight $W = mg = (20)(9.8) = 196\,\text{N}$ acts downward at the midpoint $L/2$. Setting net torque about the pin to zero:

$$ T \sin\theta \cdot L - W \cdot \frac{L}{2} = 0 \implies T = \frac{W}{2\sin\theta} = \frac{196}{2\sin 30°} = 196\,\text{N}. $$

Applying the force conditions: horizontal, $R_x = T\cos 30° \approx 169.7\,\text{N}$; vertical, $R_y = W - T\sin 30° = 196 - 98 = 98\,\text{N}$.

Key Theorem. The torque balance is pivot-independent for a body in complete equilibrium: if $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, then $\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$ computed about any point $O$ implies $\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$ about every other point $O'$. This follows because the additional torque from shifting the pivot equals $\vec{r}_{OO'} \times \sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$. Strategically placing the pivot at an unknown force's point of application eliminates that unknown from the torque equation, simplifying algebraic work.

🌸 Free Body Diagram

🌸 Free Body Diagram

Definition. A free body diagram (FBD) is a schematic representation of a single object isolated from its environment, showing all external forces acting upon it. Each force is drawn as a vector arrow originating at the point of application (or at the center of mass for translational analysis), labeled with its symbol and, where known, its magnitude and direction. The object itself is typically idealized as a point mass or a simple geometric shape. The FBD is the foundational tool for applying Newton's second law:

$$ \sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}. $$

Only forces exerted on the object by external agents appear in the diagram; internal forces between parts of the object cancel by Newton's third law and are excluded.

Worked Example. Consider a block of mass $m = 5.0\,\text{kg}$ resting on a rough inclined plane inclined at $\theta = 30°$ above the horizontal. To construct the FBD, isolate the block and identify every external force: (1) the gravitational force $\vec{W} = mg\,\hat{j}$ directed vertically downward with magnitude $mg = 49\,\text{N}$; (2) the normal force $\vec{N}$ perpendicular to the incline surface; and (3) the static friction force $\vec{f}_s$ directed up the incline, opposing impending motion. Decomposing along axes parallel ($x'$) and perpendicular ($y'$) to the incline:

$$ \sum F_{x'} = f_s - mg\sin\theta = 0 \implies f_s = (49)\sin 30° = 24.5\,\text{N}, $$ $$ \sum F_{y'} = N - mg\cos\theta = 0 \implies N = (49)\cos 30° \approx 42.4\,\text{N}. $$

Key Theorem. Newton's second law applied to the FBD is both necessary and sufficient for determining the motion of the isolated body, provided the force inventory is complete. Newton's third law guarantees that internal forces — action-reaction pairs between parts of the same body — appear in equal and opposite pairs and cancel identically, so they need not appear in the FBD. This cancellation is what makes the isolation step valid: every force crossing the system boundary is external and must be included; every force confined within is internal and must be excluded.

Lab Exercise. Hang two masses $m_1 = 2.0\,\text{kg}$ and $m_2 = 3.0\,\text{kg}$ over a frictionless pulley (Atwood machine). Draw complete FBDs for each mass separately, define a consistent positive direction, and apply Newton's second law to each FBD to derive expressions for the acceleration $a$ of the system and the tension $T$ in the string. Verify that $T = \frac{2m_1 m_2}{m_1+m_2}g$ and interpret why $T$ differs from both $m_1 g$ and $m_2 g$.

Payoff 🎯

A free body diagram (FBD) is a schematic representation of a single object in which every external force acting on that object is drawn as a vector arrow, labeled with its physical origin and anchored at the object's center of mass (or point of application when torque is relevant). Internal forces are deliberately omitted, and the surrounding environment is replaced entirely by the force vectors it exerts. This deliberate reduction transforms an arbitrarily complex physical scenario into a precise, auditable input for Newton's second law:

$$ \sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a} $$

The FBD is not merely a bookkeeping aid; it is the formal bridge between a physical situation and its mathematical model.

Worked example. A block of mass $m = 5.0\ \text{kg}$ rests on a plane inclined at $\theta = 30°$ to the horizontal. The FBD isolates the block and identifies three external forces: the gravitational force $\vec{W} = mg\hat{j}$ directed downward, the normal force $\vec{N}$ perpendicular to the incline surface, and kinetic friction $\vec{f}_k = \mu_k N$ directed up the slope opposing motion. Choosing axes parallel and perpendicular to the incline, the component equations become

$$ \sum F_\parallel: mg\sin\theta - f_k = ma, \qquad \sum F_\perp: N - mg\cos\theta = 0. $$

Substituting $\mu_k = 0.20$ yields $a = g(\sin 30° - 0.20\cos 30°) \approx 3.2\ \text{m/s}^2$ down the slope—a result that is unambiguous precisely because the diagram forced an explicit accounting of every force before any algebra began.

Key theorem (Newton's first law as a corollary). When $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, the FBD reduces to an equilibrium condition. For any rigid body in static equilibrium the net force and net torque both vanish simultaneously, a constraint that underlies the entire analysis of structures, trusses, and jointed systems. The FBD makes this constraint visually checkable: if the vector arrows drawn on the diagram do not close into a polygon, the model is incomplete.

The free body diagram is the natural endpoint of Chapter 9 because every quantitative force analysis in classical mechanics passes through it. Mastering this tool opens the door to the full landscape of dynamics: from the orbital mechanics of bound gravitational systems to the stress tensors of deformable solids. To see this power demonstrated concretely, follow the thread into the statics of equilibrium structures, where a single well-drawn FBD resolves forces in a bridge truss that no intuitive argument could untangle.
