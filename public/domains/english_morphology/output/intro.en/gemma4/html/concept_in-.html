🍃 In-

🍃 In-

Imagine you are looking at a flashlight beam. If you can see the light, something is visible. Now, if someone blocks that beam and you cannot see it, we say it is invisible. Notice how just adding those two little letters, in-, completely changes the meaning from "able to be seen" to "not able to be seen." This tiny addition is an example of a prefix—a morpheme (or smallest meaningful unit) attached to the beginning of a word to modify its core meaning.

Picture this: Prefixes are like little signaling flags placed at the start of words. When we add in- before a base word, it often acts as a signal for negation, meaning "not" or "the opposite of." Instead of rewriting the whole word, we simply attach the prefix to flip its original idea. For example, if complete means finished, adding in- creates incomplete, which signals that something is not finished.

The simple rule here is: When you see a single-syllable prefix like in-, pause and ask yourself, "Does this word mean the opposite of the base word?"
