🍃 In-

🍃 In-

Everyday example. You arrive at a party and someone says the venue is accessible. Great — easy to get in. But then you hear the backup location is inaccessible. Suddenly a single three-letter addition flips the meaning completely upside down.

Picture this. Imagine a light switch. The prefix in- acts like a finger that flicks the switch from ON to OFF. Attach it to the front of a word, and you reverse that word's meaning. Visible means you can see it; invisible means you cannot. Complete means finished; incomplete means not finished yet. The base word is still there — you can still see it inside the longer word — but in- signals "the opposite is true."

Simple rule. When in- appears at the start of an adjective or noun, it usually carries the meaning "not" or "the absence of":

$$ \text{in-} + \text{base word} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{"not" + base word} $$

So in- $+$ formal $=$ informal ("not formal"), and in- $+$ justice $=$ injustice ("absence of justice").

Try it yourself. Cover the list below, then guess the meaning of each word before peeking:

  • indecent
  • inequality
  • insecure

Check: did you read each one as "not decent," "lack of equality," and "not secure"? If yes, you have already mastered the core power of in-.
