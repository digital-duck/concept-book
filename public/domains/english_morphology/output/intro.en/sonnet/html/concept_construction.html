🍃 Construction

Imagine you want to build a bookshelf. You don't just pile wood together and hope for the best — you follow a precise plan, using specific tools in a specific order. Mathematics has the same idea, called a construction: a step-by-step process that builds a new mathematical object from objects you already have.

Picture this. You have two points, $A$ and $B$, drawn on paper. Using only a ruler (to draw straight lines) and a compass (to draw circles), you want to find the exact midpoint $M$ between them — the point exactly halfway. Each step you take must follow a rule; no guessing allowed.

Simple rule. A construction is valid only if every step uses allowed tools applied to already-known objects. In the classic compass-and-straightedge setting, the two allowed moves are:

  1. Draw a straight line through any two known points.
  2. Draw a circle centered at a known point, passing through another known point.

Starting from $A$ and $B$, you can reach $M = \left(\frac{x_A + x_B}{2},\, \frac{y_A + y_B}{2}\right)$ in just four steps — no arithmetic needed, only geometry.

Try it yourself. Place two dots on paper, label them $A$ and $B$. Open your compass to a width wider than half the distance between them. Draw an arc above and below the line from $A$, then repeat from $B$. Connect the two crossing points with a ruler. That line crosses $\overline{AB}$ exactly at $M$. You just performed your first construction!
