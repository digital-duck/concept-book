🌱 Stem

🌱 Stem

Imagine you collected the ages of everyone at a birthday party: 12, 15, 13, 21, 19, 14, 22, 17. You want to see the shape of the data at a glance — which ages cluster together, which stand alone — without losing any detail. A stem-and-leaf plot is the perfect tool for this.

Picture this. Split each number into two parts: the leading digit(s) form the stem, and the final digit is the leaf. For the number $14$, the stem is $1$ (meaning "tens") and the leaf is $4$. Line up the stems in a column, then attach every matching leaf to its right:

$$ \begin{array}{r|l} \text{Stem} & \text{Leaves} \\ \hline 1 & 2\; 3\; 4\; 5\; 7\; 9 \\ 2 & 1\; 2 \\ \end{array} $$

Each row is one "branch" of your data. You can instantly see that most guests are teenagers (stem $= 1$), while only two are in their twenties (stem $= 2$).

Simple rule. For a two-digit number $\overline{ab}$, the stem is $a$ and the leaf is $b$. For a three-digit number like $134$, the stem is $13$ and the leaf is $4$.

Try it yourself. Take the scores $82, 85, 91, 78, 84, 90, 77$. Sort them by stem and write out the leaves. Which group of scores appears most often?

🌱 Prefix

🌱 Prefix

Everyday example. Think about the word pre-heat. The part pre- comes before the main word and tells you something happens first. In mathematics and computer science, a prefix works the same way: it is the beginning piece of a sequence — everything from the very first element up to some stopping point.

Picture this. Suppose you have the word

$$ s = \texttt{BANANA} $$

You can peel off just the first few letters:

Length Prefix
1 $\texttt{B}$
2 $\texttt{BA}$
3 $\texttt{BAN}$
6 $\texttt{BANANA}$

Notice that the whole string counts as a prefix of itself, and so does the empty string $\varepsilon$ (length 0). Both are called trivial prefixes.

Simple rule. For a sequence $s = s_1\, s_2\, \ldots\, s_n$, a string $p$ is a prefix of $s$ whenever

$$ p = s_1\, s_2\, \ldots\, s_k \quad \text{for some } 0 \le k \le n. $$

In other words, $p$ matches the very start of $s$ without skipping anything.

Try it yourself. Write down the word $\texttt{DREAM}$. List all of its prefixes, including the empty one. How many did you find? You should count exactly $6$ — one for each possible stopping point from $0$ to $5$. Check your list against the simple rule above!

🌱 Suffix

🌱 Suffix

Everyday example. Imagine you have the word "sunshine". Now start reading it from different points toward the end: "sunshine", "unshine", "nshine", … all the way down to just "e". Each of those tail-pieces is called a suffix — a chunk that goes right to the very last character.

Picture this. Write the string $s = \texttt{"cake"}$ and imagine a pair of scissors that can only cut across the left side:

$$ s = \underbrace{\texttt{c}}_{\text{cut here}}\texttt{ake}, \quad \underbrace{\texttt{ca}}_{\text{cut here}}\texttt{ke}, \quad \ldots $$

Every piece that stays attached to the right end is a suffix. We also count the whole word and the empty string $\varepsilon$ as suffixes.

Simple rule. Formally, if $s$ has length $n$ and its characters are $s_1 s_2 \cdots s_n$, then for any index $i$ with $1 \le i \le n+1$, the string $s_i s_{i+1} \cdots s_n$ is a suffix of $s$. So $s$ has exactly $n + 1$ suffixes (including $\varepsilon$).

Try it yourself. Take $s = \texttt{"dog"}$ (length $3$). List all four suffixes:

$$ \texttt{"dog"}, \quad \texttt{"og"}, \quad \texttt{"g"}, \quad \varepsilon $$

Can you see the pattern? Each step just removes one letter from the front until nothing remains. Great job — you now know exactly what a suffix is!

🍃 -Ion

🍃 -Ion

Have you ever dissolved table salt in water and noticed it conducts electricity? That tiny miracle happens because of ions — atoms or molecules that carry an electric charge.

Everyday example: Imagine a perfectly balanced seesaw. Now add an extra weight to one side — suddenly it tips. Atoms work the same way. A neutral atom has equal numbers of positively charged protons (in its nucleus) and negatively charged electrons (orbiting around it). When an atom loses or gains an electron, the balance tips, and it becomes an ion.

Picture this: Sodium ($\text{Na}$) normally has 11 protons and 11 electrons, so its total charge is zero. When it loses one electron, it has 11 protons but only 10 electrons. The charge becomes:

$$ \text{charge} = +11 + (-10) = +1 $$

We write this as $\text{Na}^+$, a cation (positively charged ion). If an atom gains an electron instead, it becomes an anion (negatively charged ion), like chloride $\text{Cl}^-$.

Simple rule: Lose an electron → positive ion ($+$). Gain an electron → negative ion ($-$).

Try it yourself: Magnesium ($\text{Mg}$) has 12 protons and 12 electrons. If it loses 2 electrons, how many electrons remain? What is the total charge? Write the ion symbol.

(Answer: 10 electrons remain; charge $= +2$; symbol $\text{Mg}^{2+}$)

🍃 In-

🍃 In-

Everyday example. In everyday English, the prefix "in-" flips a word's meaning: visible becomes invisible, correct becomes incorrect. Mathematics borrows this same habit, and you will spot "in-" hiding at the front of dozens of important terms.

Picture this. Imagine a light switch. When the light is ON, it is finite — you can count what is lit. Turn the switch OFF and you have something that never ends: infinite. The prefix "in-" simply attached itself to "finite" and reversed it, giving us the symbol $\infty$ for a quantity without end.

Simple rule. In mathematics, "in-" most often signals negation — not or without. A few common examples:

Word Root meaning What "in-" adds
infinite $(\infty)$ finite (has an end) without end
inequality $(a \neq b)$ equal not equal
irrational $(\sqrt{2})$ rational (a ratio) not a ratio
injection to throw into a set

Notice that injection is the odd one out: here "in-" means into, not not. Context always tells you which sense applies.

Try it yourself. You encounter the word invertible. The root vert means "to turn." Can you guess what an invertible function does? It can be turned around — run backwards — to recover its input. Now scan your next math reading for "in-" and ask yourself: does it mean not, or into?

🍃 Port

🍃 Port

Imagine you live in a large apartment building. The building has one street address, but dozens of individual apartments inside. When a delivery arrives, the courier uses the building address to find the right building, then the apartment number to find the right door.

Picture this: Your computer is that apartment building. It has one IP address (like 192.168.1.5), but many programs run on it at once — a web browser, a chat app, a music server. A port is the apartment number that tells incoming data which program it belongs to. Ports are simply numbers, ranging from $0$ to $65535$.

Simple rule: Every network connection uses a pair — an IP address and a port number — written together like 192.168.1.5:8080. The part after the colon is the port. Some ports have well-known jobs by convention: port $80$ carries ordinary web traffic, port $443$ carries secure web traffic (HTTPS), and port $22$ handles remote login (SSH).

Try it yourself: Open a terminal and type:

curl http://localhost:8080

This asks your own computer (localhost, always address 127.0.0.1) to answer at port $8080$. If nothing is listening there, you will see a "connection refused" error — proof that the port exists but no program has opened that door yet. Start a simple server on port $8080$ and try again; this time, data flows through.

🍃 Spect

🍃 Spect

Everyday example. Imagine you are tuning a guitar string. Each string vibrates at its own special frequency — a number that captures how the string naturally moves. Matrices have their own version of these special numbers.

Picture this. A matrix $A$ is a rectangular grid of numbers that transforms vectors — it stretches, rotates, or reflects them. Most vectors get pushed in completely new directions. But a few special vectors, called eigenvectors, only get scaled — they keep pointing the same way, just longer or shorter. The scaling factor for each such vector is called an eigenvalue.

The spectrum of a matrix $A$, written $\operatorname{spec}(A)$, is simply the set of all its eigenvalues. Think of it as the collection of all those special scaling factors at once:

$$ \operatorname{spec}(A) = \{\, \lambda \in \mathbb{C} \mid A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v} \text{ for some } \mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0} \,\} $$

Simple rule. To find eigenvalues, solve the characteristic equation:

$$ \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 $$

Here $I$ is the identity matrix and $\det$ means determinant (a single number summarizing the matrix). Each solution $\lambda$ belongs to $\operatorname{spec}(A)$.

Try it yourself. Take $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$. Compute $\det(A - \lambda I) = (3-\lambda)(5-\lambda)$. Setting this to zero gives $\lambda = 3$ or $\lambda = 5$. So $\operatorname{spec}(A) = \{3, 5\}$. Those are your eigenvalues!

🍃 -Able

🍃 -Able

Everyday example. You pick up a shirt and check the label. It says washable. That one word tells you everything: this shirt can be washed. You didn't need a full sentence — the ending -able did all the work.

Picture this. Think of -able as a tiny stamp you press onto the end of a word. The stamp changes the word's job: it turns an action into a description. The action "read" becomes the description "readable" — something that can be read. The action "break" becomes "breakable" — something that can be broken.

$$ \text{verb} + \text{-able} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{adjective meaning "can be [verb]-ed"} $$

This suffix (a suffix is a word-ending added to change meaning) comes from Latin and has been part of English for centuries. You will spot it everywhere once you start looking.

Simple rule. Attach -able to a verb and you get an adjective that says "this is capable of being done to." A few spellings shift slightly — "love" drops its silent e to become "lovable" — but the meaning stays the same.

Try it yourself. What do these become?

  • adjust → ?
  • print → ?
  • manage → ?

Check your answers: adjustable, printable, manageable. Notice anything? Each new word tells you that something can be adjusted, printed, or managed. You just built three new adjectives — well done!

🍃 Con-

🍃 Con-

Everyday example. Think about the word "connect" — it means to join things together. Or "convene," which means a group comes together to meet. The little syllable "con-" (sometimes "co-" or "com-") is a Latin prefix meaning with or together.

Picture this. Mathematics borrows heavily from Latin, so "con-" shows up everywhere. When two shapes are congruent ($\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$), they fit together perfectly — same size, same angles. When a sequence converges, its terms come together toward a single value, like $\frac{1}{n} \to 0$. When two lines are concurrent, they all pass together through one point.

Simple rule. Whenever you see "con-" at the start of a math term, ask: what two (or more) things are being brought together? The answer is almost always built right into the rest of the word.

Term What comes together?
Congruent Two figures that match
Convergent Terms approaching one limit
Concurrent Lines meeting at one point
Conjugate A pair linked by a shared operation

Try it yourself. Look at the word concatenate — it means to chain strings end-to-end. Using the "con-" clue, can you guess what the "-caten-" part might mean? (Hint: think of a chain or links.) Write down your guess before looking it up!

🍃 Dict

🍃 Dict

Everyday example. Imagine a physical address book where you look up a person's phone number by their name. You don't flip through every page counting — you jump straight to "Maria" and find her number instantly. A Python dict (short for dictionary) works exactly the same way.

Picture this. A dict stores information as pairs: a key (the name you search by) and a value (the thing you want to find). You write it with curly braces:

phone_book = {"Maria": "555-1234", "Leo": "555-5678"}

To look up Maria's number, you write phone_book["Maria"], and Python returns "555-1234" immediately — no scanning required. Under the hood, Python uses a technique called hashing that lets it locate any key in roughly $O(1)$ time, meaning the lookup cost stays nearly constant no matter how many entries you have.

Simple rule. Every key in a dict must be unique and immutable (strings, numbers, and tuples all work; lists do not). Values, however, can be anything — numbers, strings, lists, even other dicts.

Try it yourself. Create a small dict mapping three fruits to their calorie counts per 100 g:

calories = {"apple": 52, "banana": 89, "grape": 67}
print(calories["banana"])   # What does this print?

Now add a new fruit with calories["mango"] = 60 and print the whole dict. Notice how the new pair appears alongside the others — Python dicts remember insertion order in Python $\geq 3.7$.

🍃 Inspect

🍃 Inspect

Everyday example. Imagine you borrow a mysterious gadget from a friend. Before you press any buttons, you flip it over, read the label, and count the inputs. That act of looking before touching is exactly what inspect means in programming.

Picture this. In Python, every function is an object you can peek inside. Suppose someone hands you a function called add_numbers but gives you no documentation. The built-in inspect module lets you ask: "What arguments does this function accept?" — without guessing or running it.

import inspect

def add_numbers(a, b, scale=1.0):
    return (a + b) * scale

sig = inspect.signature(add_numbers)
print(sig)   # (a, b, scale=1.0)

The call inspect.signature(f) returns a Signature object that lists every parameter — its name, its order, and its default value when one exists. Mathematically, if a function maps $f : A \times B \to C$, the signature tells you the shape of $A$ and $B$ before you ever supply a value.

Simple rule. inspect answers structural questions ("what does this thing look like?") rather than value questions ("what does this thing return?").

Try it yourself. Import any Python function you have used before — say, print — and call inspect.signature(print). Write down every parameter name you see. Which ones have defaults? Which ones are required?

🍃 Ject

🍃 Ject

Have you ever tossed a ball across a room? That simple act of throwing is hiding inside dozens of English words you already use every day.

Picture this. Imagine a tiny, invisible action packed inside a word — the action of throwing something somewhere. When you project a movie onto a wall, you are "throwing it forward" (pro = forward). When a doctor gives you an injection, medicine is "thrown into" your body (in = into). When you eject a disc from a player, you "throw it out" (e = out).

Simple rule. The root ject (also spelled -ject) comes from the Latin word jacere, meaning to throw. Whenever you see ject in a word, ask yourself: What is being thrown, and in which direction? The prefix attached to ject answers the direction:

$$ \text{prefix} + \text{ject} = \text{"to throw [something] in the direction of the prefix"} $$
Word Prefix meaning Full meaning
project forward throw forward
inject into throw into
eject out throw out
reject back throw back
subject under thrown under (authority)

Try it yourself. Look up the word trajectory — it contains ject in disguise (spelled -ject- inside traject-). What prefix is hiding there, and what does it suggest about the path of a thrown object?

🍃 Pre-

🍃 Pre-

Everyday example. Think of the word "preview" — you see something before the main show. Or "preheating" an oven — you warm it up before you bake. In everyday language, the prefix pre- simply means before or in front of.

Picture this. Imagine a machine (a function $f$) that takes numbers in, processes them, and spits numbers out. You drop the number $3$ in, and $9$ comes out — so $f(3) = 9$. Now someone hands you the output $9$ and asks: "What went in to produce this?" The answer — the number that came before the output — is called the pre-image. Formally, the pre-image of $9$ under $f(x) = x^2$ is $\{3,\, -3\}$, written $f^{-1}(\{9\})$.

Simple rule. Whenever you see pre- attached to a math word, ask yourself: what comes before? A pre-order is a ranking that comes before a full ordering. A pre-image is the input that comes before applying $f$. The prefix is always your hint that something lives upstream of the main operation.

Try it yourself. Let $g(x) = x + 4$. The output is $10$. What is the pre-image? Set up $x + 4 = 10$ and solve: $x = 6$. So the pre-image of $10$ under $g$ is $\{6\}$. You worked backwards — and that is exactly what pre- always asks you to do.

🍃 Re-

🍃 Re-

Everyday example. Think about the word redo. When you redo a drawing, you draw it again from the start. The tiny word "re-" carries all the meaning: it signals going back and repeating an action.

Picture this. In mathematics, "re-" shows up constantly. When you reflect a shape across a line, you flip it back to the other side. When a process is recursive, it calls itself again and again, like a mirror facing a mirror. Each use of "re-" quietly whispers: something is happening twice, or in reverse.

Simple rule. Whenever you see "re-" at the front of a mathematical word, ask yourself: What is being repeated, or what is being reversed?

Word What repeats or reverses
Recursion a function calling itself again
Reflection flipping a point back across a line
Reverse undoing an operation, e.g. $f^{-1}$ undoes $f$
$\text{Re}(z)$ the real part extracted back from a complex number $z = a + bi$

Notice that last one: $\text{Re}(z) = a$ simply pulls out the real component of a complex number, peeling away the imaginary layer.

Try it yourself. Write down two words from everyday life that start with "re-" and describe what action is being repeated or reversed. Then see if you can find a matching idea in math!

🍃 Struct

🍃 Struct

Everyday example. Imagine filling out a library card. One card holds your name, your address, and your membership number — all different pieces of information, but they belong together because they describe you. A struct (short for structure) does exactly the same thing in code: it bundles several related values under one roof.

Picture this. Say you want to track a point on a map. Without a struct, you'd juggle two separate numbers: one for how far east you are, one for how far north. With a struct, you create a single thing called Point that carries both at once:

$$ \text{Point} = \{ x \in \mathbb{R},\ y \in \mathbb{R} \} $$

Now Point is a new type you invented. Every time you need a location, you create one Point instead of two loose numbers.

Simple rule. A struct groups named fields of (possibly different) types into one named unit. Each field has its own type — $x$ could be a decimal, a label could be text — and you access any field by its name.

Try it yourself. Think of a recipe card. What fields would a Recipe struct need? You might choose: title (text), servings (whole number), and rating (a decimal from $0$ to $5$). Sketch that struct on paper — you've just designed your first custom data type!

🍃 Trans-

🍃 Trans-

You already know the word translate from everyday life — it means to carry a message across from one language to another. The prefix trans- always carries that same idea: across, through, or over to the other side.

Picture this. Imagine sliding a chess piece straight across the board without rotating it. Every square moves the same distance in the same direction. Mathematicians call this a translation — the whole shape crosses over to a new position. If you instead flip a matrix (a grid of numbers) by swapping its rows and columns, that operation is called the transpose, written $A^T$. You are carrying each entry across the diagonal of the grid.

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix} $$

Simple rule. Whenever you see trans- in a math word — transpose, translation, transformation, transitive — ask yourself: what is being carried across, and where does it land? That question will almost always point you straight to the meaning.

Try it yourself. Take the matrix $B = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ -1 & 7 \end{pmatrix}$ and write down $B^T$. Which entry moved the farthest from its starting position? (Hint: look at the off-diagonal entries.) Once you spot the swap, the idea of trans- will feel natural every time you meet it.

🍃 -Or

🍃 -Or

Everyday example. Imagine you are deciding whether to bring an umbrella. You check two conditions: "Is it raining?" and "Is the forecast stormy?" You grab the umbrella if at least one of those is true — you don't need both to be true. This everyday reasoning is exactly what the logical operator or captures.

Picture this. Think of two light switches wired to a single bulb. The bulb turns on whenever either switch is flipped up. Only if both switches are down does the light stay off. The switches are your conditions; the bulb is the result.

Simple rule. In logic, for two statements $P$ and $Q$, the expression $P \operatorname{lor} Q$ (read "P or Q") is true in every case except when both are false:

$P$ $Q$ $P \operatorname{lor} Q$
T T T
T F T
F T T
F F F

The symbol $\operatorname{lor}$ is the standard notation for logical or. Notice there are three ways to get T and only one way to get F.

Try it yourself. Let $P$ = "I have a library card" and $Q$ = "I have cash." Evaluate $P \operatorname{lor} Q$ for each combination. Which combination means you cannot borrow or buy a book?

🍃 Construct

🍃 Construct

Imagine you have a box of LEGO bricks. You follow a set of rules — snap this piece onto that one, only in certain ways — and step by step a spaceship appears. You didn't invent new bricks; you built something new from what was already there. In mathematics, we call that a construct: a new object built from existing pieces by following a precise recipe.

Picture this. You know the number $2$ and the number $3$. The addition operation is your rule. Apply it, and you construct the new number $2 + 3 = 5$. The number $5$ wasn't handed to you from outside; you assembled it. Geometry works the same way: given two points $A$ and $B$, drawing a straight line through them constructs the line segment $\overline{AB}$.

Simple rule. A construct has three ingredients:

  1. Starting materials — the objects you already have.
  2. Allowed operations — the moves you are permitted to make.
  3. The result — the new object you produce.

Every mathematical object you will ever meet was built this way, layer by layer.

Try it yourself. Start with the set $\{1, 2\}$. Using the rule "form all possible pairs," construct the set of ordered pairs: $\{(1,1),\,(1,2),\,(2,1),\,(2,2)\}$. Notice you used only the original elements and one clear rule — nothing more. That's a construct!

🍃 Predict

🍃 Predict

Every morning you glance at the sky and decide whether to grab an umbrella. You are not certain it will rain — you are making a prediction: your best guess about something you have not seen yet.

Picture this. Imagine you have been tracking the temperature each day for a week: $60°, 62°, 65°, 67°, 70°$. The numbers climb by roughly $2°$ or $3°$ each day. What would you guess for tomorrow? Probably around $72°$ or $73°$. You spotted a pattern and extended it forward — that is prediction in a nutshell.

Simple rule. In mathematics and machine learning, to predict means to use information you already have (called input or features) to estimate an unknown value (called the output or label). We often write this as:

$$ \hat{y} = f(x) $$

where $x$ is the input you know, $f$ is some rule or model, and $\hat{y}$ (read "y-hat") is your predicted output. The hat symbol $\hat{\phantom{y}}$ is a reminder that this is an estimate, not a guaranteed truth.

Try it yourself. Look at this tiny sequence: $3, 6, 9, 12, \ldots$ What comes next? Write down your prediction, then check: each number grows by $3$, so your $\hat{y} = 15$. You just used a pattern as your model $f$ — welcome to predicting!

🍃 Reject

🍃 Reject

Imagine a friend claims their lucky coin lands heads every time. You flip it ten times and get eight heads. Is that enough to call their bluff, or could it just be chance?

This is exactly what rejecting means in statistics. You start with a null hypothesis — a boring default claim like "this coin is fair" — and collect data to challenge it. If the data would be extremely unlikely under that default claim, you reject it.

Picture this: Imagine a dartboard labeled "all the results a fair coin could produce." Most darts land near the center (roughly five heads out of ten). Your result of eight heads lands near the edge — in the rejection region, the zone where results are so rare they make the null hypothesis hard to believe.

Simple rule: You pick a threshold called the significance level, written $\alpha$ (alpha). Commonly $\alpha = 0.05$, meaning "I'll only call this surprising if there's less than a $5\%$ chance of seeing it under the null hypothesis." When your result falls in that unlikely $5\%$ zone — that is, when the p-value satisfies $p < \alpha$ — you reject the null hypothesis.

Rejecting doesn't prove the null is false; it just says the evidence is too strong to keep trusting it.

Try it yourself: Flip a coin 20 times. If you get 16 or more heads, calculate: does that feel like ordinary luck or something worth questioning?

🍃 Transport

🍃 Transport

Everyday example. Imagine you have a pile of sand on the left side of a sandbox and you want to move it to the right side. You pick up each grain and carry it. The cost of your work depends on two things: how heavy each grain is, and how far you have to carry it.

Picture this. Now replace "sand" with any collection of stuff — factory goods, heat, probability, even data points. Transport is the math of moving one distribution of mass into another distribution while keeping track of the total effort. If you have a pile described by a density function $\mu$ and you want it to look like a target density $\nu$, you need a transport plan — a rule telling you where each unit of mass ends up.

Simple rule. The total cost of a transport plan $T$ is:

$$ \text{Cost}(T) = \int d(x,\, T(x))\, d\mu(x) $$

where $d(x, T(x))$ is the distance each grain of mass at position $x$ travels. Optimal transport finds the plan $T^*$ that makes this cost as small as possible.

Try it yourself. Place three pennies at positions $0$, $1$, and $2$ on a number line. Now move them so they land at positions $1$, $2$, and $3$. List the distance each penny travels and add them up — that total is your transport cost. Can you rearrange the moves to get a smaller total?

🍃 Construction

🍃 Construction

Imagine you want to build a bookshelf. You don't just pile wood together and hope for the best — you follow a precise plan, using specific tools in a specific order. Mathematics has the same idea, called a construction: a step-by-step process that builds a new mathematical object from objects you already have.

Picture this. You have two points, $A$ and $B$, drawn on paper. Using only a ruler (to draw straight lines) and a compass (to draw circles), you want to find the exact midpoint $M$ between them — the point exactly halfway. Each step you take must follow a rule; no guessing allowed.

Simple rule. A construction is valid only if every step uses allowed tools applied to already-known objects. In the classic compass-and-straightedge setting, the two allowed moves are:

  1. Draw a straight line through any two known points.
  2. Draw a circle centered at a known point, passing through another known point.

Starting from $A$ and $B$, you can reach $M = \left(\frac{x_A + x_B}{2},\, \frac{y_A + y_B}{2}\right)$ in just four steps — no arithmetic needed, only geometry.

Try it yourself. Place two dots on paper, label them $A$ and $B$. Open your compass to a width wider than half the distance between them. Draw an arc above and below the line from $A$, then repeat from $B$. Connect the two crossing points with a ruler. That line crosses $\overline{AB}$ exactly at $M$. You just performed your first construction!

🍃 Inspection

🍃 Inspection

Everyday example. Imagine you receive a mystery box. Before opening it, you pick it up, shake it, feel its weight, and listen to what rattles inside. You haven't changed the box at all — you're just looking closely to gather clues. That act of careful looking is inspection.

Picture this. In mathematics, inspection means examining an expression or equation to draw a conclusion without doing a full calculation. Suppose you see:

$$ x^2 = -9 $$

You can spot the answer immediately: no real number squared gives a negative result, so there is no real solution — no arithmetic needed. You just looked at the structure.

A slightly larger example: if someone asks whether the equation

$$ 2x + 4 = 2(x + 2) $$

is always true, you can expand the right side mentally — $2 \cdot x + 2 \cdot 2 = 2x + 4$ — and see both sides are identical for every value of $x$.

Simple rule. Inspection = read the structure first, calculate second. Ask: What does the form of this expression tell me directly?

Try it yourself. Without solving, decide: does $x^2 + 1 = 0$ have any real solutions? Write one sentence explaining what you noticed by inspection alone — no algebra required.

🍃 Inspector

🍃 Inspector

Imagine you have a piggy bank on your shelf. You can shake it to hear how many coins are inside — but shaking it doesn't add or remove any coins. You are simply inspecting it: looking at its state without changing anything.

Picture this. In programming, an inspector (sometimes called a getter or accessor) is a function whose only job is to read and return some piece of information about an object. If you have a circle object $C$ that stores its radius $r$, an inspector might look like:

$$ \text{get\_radius}(C) \;\longrightarrow\; r $$

The arrow means "returns." Notice nothing on the right-hand side alters $C$; the circle's data stays exactly as it was.

Simple rule. An inspector follows one constraint:

$$ \text{state of object before} = \text{state of object after} $$

If calling your function changes anything about the object, it is not an inspector — it is a mutator (a different kind of function entirely).

Try it yourself. Think of a bank account object with a balance $b$. Which of these would you call an inspector?

  • A function that returns $b$
  • A function that adds $10 to $b$ and then returns $b$

The first one is the inspector. The second changes $b$, so it breaks the rule. When you spot a function that only reads, you have found an inspector.

🍃 Portable

🍃 Portable

Everyday example. Imagine a cookie recipe written on an index card. You can bake those cookies in your kitchen, your grandmother's kitchen, or a kitchen in another country — the card travels with you and works everywhere. A mathematical expression can have the same quality: it means the same thing no matter where you use it.

Picture this. Think of the fraction $\dfrac{1}{2}$. Whether you are splitting a pizza, dividing money, or measuring time, $\dfrac{1}{2}$ always represents one out of two equal parts. The notation packs its full meaning inside itself, so you can carry it into any problem and it will not break.

Simple rule. We call a representation portable when its meaning is self-contained — it does not depend on hidden context or a specific setting to be understood. In contrast, a phrase like "half of it" is not portable, because you need outside information to know what "it" refers to. A portable form, such as $\dfrac{x}{2}$, carries everything a reader needs.

Try it yourself. Look at these two ways of saying the same thing:

  • "Take the number and cut it in half."
  • $y = \dfrac{x}{2}$

Which one is portable? Ask yourself: could a friend in another city read it and apply it correctly without asking you any questions? The second form wins — $x$ is an explicit placeholder, and the rule is fully stated.

🍃 Prediction

🍃 Prediction

You do this every day without realizing it. When you glance at the sky and see dark clouds, you grab an umbrella. You haven't checked the forecast — you made a prediction: a guess about the future based on information you already have.

Picture this. Imagine you track how many hours you study each week and your exam score that follows. After a few weeks you notice a pattern: more study hours tend to produce higher scores. You could draw a straight line through those points on a graph. That line is your prediction machine — given any number of study hours $x$, it estimates the score $\hat{y}$ you might get:

$$ \hat{y} = mx + b $$

Here $m$ (the slope) says how much the score rises per extra hour, and $b$ (the intercept) is the baseline score even at zero hours. The hat symbol $\hat{\ }$ is a reminder that $\hat{y}$ is an estimate, not a guaranteed fact.

Simple rule. A prediction takes known inputs, applies a learned pattern, and outputs a best guess. It will never be perfect — the world is noisy — but a good prediction is useful even when it is not exact.

Try it yourself. Write down today's temperature and tomorrow's temperature for five days in a row. Can you spot a trend? Try writing a rough rule: "If today is $T$ degrees, tomorrow will be about $T + \_\_$ degrees." You just built your first prediction model!

🍃 Rejection

🍃 Rejection

Imagine you order a bag of chips labeled "500 g." You weigh it and get 312 g. Something seems wrong — you reject the manufacturer's claim. This everyday instinct is exactly what statisticians formalize as rejection.

Picture this. You start with a working assumption called the null hypothesis $H_0$ — for example, "the average bag weighs 500 g." You then collect data and compute a single number called a test statistic $T$, which measures how far your sample is from that assumption. Think of $T$ as a surprise meter: small values mean "nothing unusual here," large values mean "this is hard to explain if $H_0$ were true."

Simple rule. Before you look at the data, you pick a threshold called the critical value $c$. If your surprise meter exceeds it — $T > c$ — you say the data fall in the rejection region and you reject $H_0$. If $T \leq c$, you don't reject it (you simply don't have enough evidence yet).

$$ \text{Reject } H_0 \iff T > c $$

The cutoff $c$ is chosen so that false alarms happen only $\alpha$ of the time — typically $\alpha = 0.05$, meaning a 5 % chance of wrongly rejecting a true hypothesis.

Try it yourself. Flip a coin 20 times and count heads $X$. Under $H_0$: "fair coin," you'd expect around 10. If $X \leq 4$ or $X \geq 16$, reject $H_0$ — that's your rejection region at roughly $\alpha = 0.05$.

🍃 Transportable

🍃 Transportable

Everyday example. Imagine you pack your favourite board game, carry it across town, and set it up at a friend's house. All the rules still work exactly the same — the game itself survived the move. A transportable property in mathematics works the same way: it is a property that survives whenever you carry a mathematical object across an isomorphism (a perfect, structure-preserving map between two objects).

Picture this. Say you have a number system $A = (\{0,1,2\}, +)$ and a second system $B$ that "looks identical" to $A$ under a renaming map $f : A \to B$. If $A$ happens to be commutative — meaning $a + b = b + a$ for all $a, b$ — then $B$ is automatically commutative too, because $f$ copies every equation faithfully. Commutativity is transportable.

Simple rule. A property $P$ is transportable when: if object $A$ has property $P$, and $f : A \operatorname{xrightarrow}{\;\sim\;} B$ is an isomorphism, then $B$ also has property $P$.

Properties that depend on the name of elements — like "contains exactly the element $\pi$" — are not transportable, because renaming can erase that identity.

Try it yourself. Consider "having exactly $3$ elements." Is this transportable? Pick any set $A$ with $|A| = 3$ and any isomorphism to a set $B$. Must $|B| = 3$? Yes! An isomorphism is a bijection, so element-count is preserved. Cardinality is transportable.

🍃 Suffix Derivation

🍃 Suffix Derivation

Think about autocomplete on your phone. You type "un" and the keyboard suggests "undo," "uncle," and "until." In effect, it is asking: given that I have already typed this prefix, what endings are still valid? That question is exactly what suffix derivation answers — but for any formal language, not just a keyboard dictionary.

Picture this. Imagine a language $L$ as a set of accepted strings: $L = \{\text{cat}, \text{car}, \text{cap}, \text{dog}\}$. Ask yourself: after committing to the prefix $\text{ca}$, which completions lead to a word in $L$? Scanning the list you find $\text{cat}$, $\text{car}$, $\text{cap}$ — so the valid endings are $\{t, r, p\}$.

Simple rule. Formally, given a language $L$ over an alphabet $\Sigma$ and a string $u$, the left quotient (or suffix derivation) of $L$ by $u$ is written $u^{-1}L$ and defined as:

$$ u^{-1}L \;=\; \{\, v \in \Sigma^{*} \mid uv \in L \,\} $$

Read this as: "every string $v$ such that prepending $u$ to $v$ lands inside $L$."

Try it yourself. Let $L = \{\text{abc},\, \text{abd},\, \text{xyz}\}$.

  • $(\text{ab})^{-1}L = \{c,\, d\}$ — both $\text{abc}$ and $\text{abd}$ begin with $\text{ab}$.
  • $(\text{xy})^{-1}L = \{z\}$ — only $\text{xyz}$ qualifies.
  • $(\text{q})^{-1}L = \emptyset$ — no word starts with $\text{q}$, so the result is the empty set.

Notice how suffix derivation shrinks the problem: each step strips away a prefix and leaves a smaller, simpler language to reason about.

🌸 Academic Vocabulary

🌸 Academic Vocabulary

When you walk into a bakery, you quickly learn words like crumb, proof, and glaze — terms that mean something very specific in that world. Academic writing works the same way: it has its own set of words that carry precise, agreed-upon meanings.

Picture this. Imagine two scientists both want to say "the result we found supports our guess." Instead, they write: "The data corroborate our hypothesis." Both sentences mean the same thing, but the second one is shorter, exact, and understood the same way by every reader in the field. That shared precision is the whole point of academic vocabulary.

Simple rule. Academic vocabulary falls into two buckets:

  1. General academic words — words that appear across many subjects, like analyze, evaluate, significant, or hypothesis.
  2. Domain-specific terms — words that belong to one field, like $\Delta$ (change) in physics, or metaphor in literature.

When you encounter an unfamiliar academic word, ask: Is this used everywhere, or only here? That one question tells you how broadly you can apply its meaning.

Try it yourself. Pick any paragraph from a textbook. Underline every word that feels "formal" or field-specific. For each underlined word, write a one-sentence definition in your own everyday language. If you cannot, that is your signal to look it up — you have just found a gap worth closing.
