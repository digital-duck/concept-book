🌱 Stem

🌱 Stem

Imagine you collected the ages of everyone at a birthday party: 12, 15, 13, 21, 19, 14, 22, 17. You want to see the shape of the data at a glance — which ages cluster together, which stand alone — without losing any detail. A stem-and-leaf plot is the perfect tool for this.

Picture this. Split each number into two parts: the leading digit(s) form the stem, and the final digit is the leaf. For the number $14$, the stem is $1$ (meaning "tens") and the leaf is $4$. Line up the stems in a column, then attach every matching leaf to its right:

$$ \begin{array}{r|l} \text{Stem} & \text{Leaves} \\ \hline 1 & 2\; 3\; 4\; 5\; 7\; 9 \\ 2 & 1\; 2 \\ \end{array} $$

Each row is one "branch" of your data. You can instantly see that most guests are teenagers (stem $= 1$), while only two are in their twenties (stem $= 2$).

Simple rule. For a two-digit number $\overline{ab}$, the stem is $a$ and the leaf is $b$. For a three-digit number like $134$, the stem is $13$ and the leaf is $4$.

Try it yourself. Take the scores $82, 85, 91, 78, 84, 90, 77$. Sort them by stem and write out the leaves. Which group of scores appears most often?
