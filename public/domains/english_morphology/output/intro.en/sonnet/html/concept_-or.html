🍃 -Or

🍃 -Or

Everyday example. Think about the word act. It names something you do — you perform on stage, you move, you do something. Now add two letters to the end: actor. Suddenly the word points to a person — someone who acts. That tiny ending, -or, did all the work.

Picture this. Imagine a little badge that says "person who ___." Whenever you pin that badge onto a verb (an action word), you get a noun (a thing word) naming the person who performs that action. The badge is -or.

Base verb + -or Meaning
edit editor one who edits
direct director one who directs
operate operator one who operates
invent inventor one who invents

Simple rule. When you see a word ending in -or, ask: "What action does the base describe?" The -or word names whoever carries out that action. In notation, we can write:

$$ \text{verb} + \textit{-or} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{noun (agent)} $$

An agent just means "the one doing the thing."

Try it yourself. Cover the right-hand column and guess each meaning:

  1. conduct → conductor = ?
  2. supervise → supervisor = ?
  3. govern → governor = ?

Check: one who conducts, one who supervises, one who governs. You already know the pattern — -or turns actions into people!
