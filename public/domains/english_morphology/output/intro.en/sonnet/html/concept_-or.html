🍃 -Or

🍃 -Or

Everyday example. Imagine you are deciding whether to bring an umbrella. You check two conditions: "Is it raining?" and "Is the forecast stormy?" You grab the umbrella if at least one of those is true — you don't need both to be true. This everyday reasoning is exactly what the logical operator or captures.

Picture this. Think of two light switches wired to a single bulb. The bulb turns on whenever either switch is flipped up. Only if both switches are down does the light stay off. The switches are your conditions; the bulb is the result.

Simple rule. In logic, for two statements $P$ and $Q$, the expression $P \operatorname{lor} Q$ (read "P or Q") is true in every case except when both are false:

$P$ $Q$ $P \operatorname{lor} Q$
T T T
T F T
F T T
F F F

The symbol $\operatorname{lor}$ is the standard notation for logical or. Notice there are three ways to get T and only one way to get F.

Try it yourself. Let $P$ = "I have a library card" and $Q$ = "I have cash." Evaluate $P \operatorname{lor} Q$ for each combination. Which combination means you cannot borrow or buy a book?
