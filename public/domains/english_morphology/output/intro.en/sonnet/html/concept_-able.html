🍃 -Able

🍃 -Able

Everyday example. You pick up a shirt and check the label. It says washable. That one word tells you everything: this shirt can be washed. You didn't need a full sentence — the ending -able did all the work.

Picture this. Think of -able as a tiny stamp you press onto the end of a word. The stamp changes the word's job: it turns an action into a description. The action "read" becomes the description "readable" — something that can be read. The action "break" becomes "breakable" — something that can be broken.

$$ \text{verb} + \text{-able} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{adjective meaning "can be [verb]-ed"} $$

This suffix (a suffix is a word-ending added to change meaning) comes from Latin and has been part of English for centuries. You will spot it everywhere once you start looking.

Simple rule. Attach -able to a verb and you get an adjective that says "this is capable of being done to." A few spellings shift slightly — "love" drops its silent e to become "lovable" — but the meaning stays the same.
