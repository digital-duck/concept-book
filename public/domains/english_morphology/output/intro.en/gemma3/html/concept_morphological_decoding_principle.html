🍃 Morphological Decoding Principle

🍃 Morphological Decoding Principle

Let’s talk about how computers “guess” what a word means, even if they've never seen it before. It sounds like magic, but it’s based on a clever idea called the morphological decoding principle. Don’t worry; we’ll break this down step by step.

Everyday Example: Think about the word "unbreakable." You probably know that “break” means to shatter something, and “able” means capable of doing something. “Un-” is a prefix meaning ‘not’. So, "unbreakable" clearly means "not able to be broken.” We understand this because we can break down the word into its smaller parts and combine our knowledge of those parts.

Picture This: Imagine you’re building with LEGOs. Each brick has a specific function – some are long, some are short, some have studs for connecting. You wouldn't just throw all the bricks together randomly; you build things by combining these basic pieces in different ways to create something new.

Simple Rule: The morphological decoding principle says that words are built from smaller meaningful parts called morphemes. A morpheme is like a LEGO brick – it carries a specific meaning, like “break” or “able.” When we see a word, we try to identify all the morphemes within it. Then, we combine our knowledge of those morphemes to figure out what the whole word means.

Try It Yourself: Let’s take the word "running." We can break it down into: “run” (the action) and "-ing" (a suffix that often indicates a continuous action). So, “running” likely means “the action of running.” This simple process – breaking words down into their morphemes – is at the heart of how computers start to understand language.
