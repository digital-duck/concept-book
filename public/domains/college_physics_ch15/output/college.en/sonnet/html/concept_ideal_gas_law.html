🌱 Ideal Gas Law

🌱 Ideal Gas Law

Definition. The ideal gas law is a fundamental equation of state that relates the macroscopic properties of a gas under conditions where intermolecular forces and molecular volume are negligible. It is expressed as

$$ PV = nRT $$

where $P$ is the absolute pressure (in pascals), $V$ is the volume (in cubic meters), $n$ is the amount of substance (in moles), $R = 8.314\ \mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$ is the universal gas constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature (in kelvin). This single relation synthesizes three independently discovered empirical laws: Boyle's law ($P \propto 1/V$ at constant $T$, $n$), Charles's law ($V \propto T$ at constant $P$, $n$), and Avogadro's law ($V \propto n$ at constant $P$, $T$). Each of these is recovered immediately by holding the appropriate variables fixed in $PV = nRT$.

Worked Example. A sealed container holds $2.00\ \mathrm{mol}$ of nitrogen gas at a temperature of $27.0^\circ\mathrm{C}$ and a volume of $0.0500\ \mathrm{m^3}$. Find the pressure.

Convert temperature: $T = 27.0 + 273.15 = 300.15\ \mathrm{K}$. Applying the ideal gas law:

$$ P = \frac{nRT}{V} = \frac{(2.00)(8.314)(300.15)}{0.0500} \approx 9.97 \times 10^4\ \mathrm{Pa} $$

This is approximately $0.984\ \mathrm{atm}$, consistent with near-atmospheric conditions where the ideal approximation holds well.

Key Theorem. For a fixed amount of gas undergoing any quasi-static process, the combined gas law follows directly:

$$ \frac{P_1 V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2 V_2}{T_2} = nR = \text{constant} $$

This result is derivable from kinetic theory by modeling gas molecules as non-interacting point particles undergoing elastic collisions. The key primitive introduced here is Boltzmann's constant $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \mathrm{J\,K^{-1}}$, which links the macroscopic temperature $T$ to the average translational kinetic energy of a single molecule: $\langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$. Through $k_B$, the molar form $PV = nRT$ and the molecular form $PV = Nk_BT$ are unified, since $R = N_A k_B$ where $N_A$ is Avogadro's number.

Lab Exercise. Using a sealed syringe connected to a pressure sensor, record $P$ and $V$ at five different compression settings while keeping temperature constant. Plot $P$ versus $1/V$ and confirm linearity. Then immerse the syringe in water baths at three different temperatures, measure the resulting pressure at fixed volume, and verify that $P/T$ remains constant. Quantify how closely your data match the predicted ideal behavior and identify conditions where deviations become measurable.
