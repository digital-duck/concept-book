🍃 Reversible Process

🍃 Reversible Process

Definition. A reversible process is a quasi-static thermodynamic process that can be reversed in direction without leaving any net change in either the system or its surroundings. Formally, a process taking a system from state $A$ to state $B$ is reversible if and only if the reverse path from $B$ to $A$ restores every thermodynamic variable—pressure $P$, volume $V$, temperature $T$, and internal energy $U$—to their original values while exchanging the same magnitudes of heat and work with the surroundings but with opposite signs. A necessary condition is that the process proceeds infinitely slowly (quasi-statically) through a continuous sequence of equilibrium states, so that at every instant the system can be described by well-defined state variables. Any process involving friction, finite temperature gradients, or unrestrained expansion is inherently irreversible.

Worked Example. Consider an ideal gas compressed isothermally and reversibly from volume $V_i = 2.00\,\text{L}$ to $V_f = 1.00\,\text{L}$ at constant temperature $T = 300\,\text{K}$, with $n = 0.100\,\text{mol}$.

The work done on the gas is:

$$ W_{\text{on}} = -nRT\ln\!\left(\frac{V_f}{V_i}\right) = -(0.100)(8.314)(300)\ln\!\left(\frac{1.00}{2.00}\right) \approx +172\,\text{J}. $$

Because $\Delta U = 0$ for an isothermal ideal-gas process, the heat released by the gas equals $Q = -172\,\text{J}$. Reversing the piston infinitely slowly returns the gas to $V_i$, and the surroundings receive back exactly $172\,\text{J}$ of work while absorbing $172\,\text{J}$ of heat—net change in both system and surroundings is zero.

Key Theorem. The Second Law of Thermodynamics requires that the total entropy change of the universe for any reversible process is exactly zero:

$$ \Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} = 0, $$

where entropy $S$ is the state function defined by $dS = \delta Q_{\text{rev}}/T$ for a reversible heat exchange at temperature $T$. Because the system gains $+\delta Q_{\text{rev}}/T$ while the surroundings lose $-\delta Q_{\text{rev}}/T$, the two contributions cancel exactly. Any irreversible process satisfies $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0$, making the reversible process an idealized upper bound on thermodynamic efficiency.

Lab Exercise. Using a syringe fitted with a pressure sensor, slowly compress a fixed mass of air while recording $P$ and $V$ at ten equally spaced intervals. Plot $P$ versus $V$ and compare the resulting curve to the theoretical isotherm $PV = nRT$. Estimate the work done by computing $\int P\,dV$ numerically via the trapezoidal rule, and discuss which aspects of your procedure introduced irreversibilities and how they might be minimized.
