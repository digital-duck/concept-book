🍃 Adiabatic Process

🍃 Adiabatic Process

Definition. An adiabatic process is a thermodynamic process in which no heat is exchanged between the system and its surroundings, so that $Q = 0$. By the first law of thermodynamics,

$$ \Delta U = Q - W = -W, $$

meaning any work done by the system comes entirely at the expense of its internal energy, and vice versa. For an ideal gas undergoing a quasi-static adiabatic process, the pressure and volume satisfy the adiabatic condition

$$ PV^{\gamma} = \text{constant}, $$

where $\gamma = C_P / C_V$ is the heat capacity ratio (approximately $1.4$ for diatomic gases such as $\mathrm{N_2}$ and $\mathrm{O_2}$ at room temperature, as established in the treatment of heat capacities). This relation follows from combining the ideal gas law with the condition $dU = -\delta W$, yielding $C_V \, dT = -P \, dV$, which integrates to the above after eliminating $T$ via $PV = nRT$.

Worked Example. A monatomic ideal gas ($\gamma = 5/3$) occupies a volume $V_1 = 2.00\,\mathrm{L}$ at pressure $P_1 = 3.00\,\mathrm{atm}$. It expands adiabatically to $V_2 = 6.00\,\mathrm{L}$. Find the final pressure.

Using $P_1 V_1^{\gamma} = P_2 V_2^{\gamma}$:

$$ P_2 = P_1 \left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right)^{\gamma} = 3.00\,\mathrm{atm}\times\left(\frac{2.00}{6.00}\right)^{5/3}. $$

Computing $(1/3)^{5/3} = 3^{-5/3} \approx 0.160$, so $P_2 \approx 0.481\,\mathrm{atm}$. The temperature drop follows from the ideal gas law: $T_2/T_1 = P_2 V_2 / (P_1 V_1) \approx 0.962$.

Key Theorem. For a reversible adiabatic process, the entropy of the system remains constant ($\Delta S = 0$), since $dS = \delta Q_{\text{rev}}/T = 0$ when $Q = 0$. Consequently, adiabatic curves in the $P$-$V$ diagram are steeper than isotherms at every point: the adiabatic slope is $-\gamma P/V$, compared with $-P/V$ for an isotherm, reflecting the additional pressure drop required to account for the falling temperature.

Lab Exercise. Using a bicycle pump fitted with a pressure gauge and thermometer, rapidly compress air by a factor of two. Record the initial and final temperatures, then calculate the theoretical final temperature using $T_2 = T_1 (V_1/V_2)^{\gamma - 1}$. Compare your measured result to the theoretical prediction and discuss sources of deviation from ideal adiabatic behavior, such as heat conduction through the pump walls.
