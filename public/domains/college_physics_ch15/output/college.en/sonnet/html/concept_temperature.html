🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar thermodynamic state variable that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. For an ideal monatomic gas, this relationship is made precise by the equipartition theorem:

$$ \frac{1}{2}m\langle v^2 \rangle = \frac{3}{2}k_B T $$

where $m$ is the molecular mass, $\langle v^2 \rangle$ is the mean-square speed, $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{J K}^{-1}$ is the Boltzmann constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvins. Temperature is thus not a measure of total thermal energy but of the energy per degree of freedom. The SI unit is the kelvin (K), defined since 2019 by fixing $k_B$ to its exact value, with absolute zero ($T = 0\ \text{K}$) representing the theoretical state of minimum thermal motion.

Worked Example. A sample of nitrogen gas ($M = 28.0\ \text{g mol}^{-1}$) is held at $T = 300\ \text{K}$. Find the root-mean-square speed of the molecules.

Using the kinetic-molecular result $v_\text{rms} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M}}$, where $R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$:

$$ v_\text{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{3(8.314)(300)}{0.0280}} = \sqrt{267{,}000\ \text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2}} \approx 517\ \text{m s}^{-1} $$

This speed is comparable to the speed of sound in air, consistent with the molecular basis of acoustic propagation.

Key Theorem — Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics. If system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, and system $B$ is separately in thermal equilibrium with $C$, then $A$ and $B$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This statement is non-trivial: it guarantees that temperature is a well-defined, transitive equivalence relation among systems, and it is the logical foundation that makes thermometry possible. Without the zeroth law, the concept of a universal temperature scale would be operationally undefined.

Lab Exercise. Using a constant-volume gas thermometer containing a fixed amount of helium, record the pressure $P$ at two known reference temperatures: the ice point ($273.15\ \text{K}$) and the steam point ($373.15\ \text{K}$). Plot $P$ versus $T$ and extrapolate the linear fit to $P = 0$. Verify that the intercept coincides with absolute zero, confirming that pressure is directly proportional to absolute temperature at constant volume.
