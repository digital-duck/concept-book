🌱 Kinetic Energy

🌱 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is defined as

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

This scalar quantity carries SI units of joules ($\text{J} = \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-2}$) and is always non-negative. Kinetic energy is a state function: it depends only on the instantaneous speed of the object, not on its direction of travel or the path taken to reach that speed. For systems composed of multiple particles, the total kinetic energy is the algebraic sum of the individual contributions $K_\text{tot} = \sum_i \frac{1}{2}m_i v_i^2$.

Worked Example. A 1200 kg automobile accelerates from rest to $v = 27\ \text{m/s}$ (approximately 97 km/h) on a level road. Find its kinetic energy at full speed and the net work done on it during acceleration.

By definition,

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(1200\ \text{kg})(27\ \text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1200)(729)\ \text{J} = 437{,}400\ \text{J} \approx 4.37 \times 10^5\ \text{J}. $$

Because the car starts from rest ($K_i = 0$), the work–energy theorem gives

$$ W_\text{net} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i = 4.37 \times 10^5\ \text{J} - 0 = 4.37 \times 10^5\ \text{J}. $$

The Work–Energy Theorem. The central result connecting kinematics and energy is:

The net work done on a particle equals the change in its kinetic energy: $$ W_\text{net} = \Delta K = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2. $$

Proof sketch. Starting from Newton's second law, $F_\text{net} = ma$, multiply both sides by an infinitesimal displacement $dx$ and integrate along the particle's path. Using $a\,dx = v\,dv$ (from the chain rule), one obtains $\int F_\text{net}\,dx = \int_{v_i}^{v_f} mv\,dv = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2$, which is the theorem.

🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the normal force exerted per unit area on a surface. For a uniform force $F$ acting perpendicularly over a flat area $A$, the pressure is

$$ P = \frac{F}{A}. $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal ($\text{Pa}$), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude but no directional dependence at a point in a static fluid. In a fluid at rest, pressure at a given depth $h$ below the surface is given by

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface, $\rho$ is the fluid density, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. This relation, known as the hydrostatic pressure equation, follows directly from the requirement that each horizontal fluid layer be in mechanical equilibrium under the weight of the fluid above it.

Worked Example. A rectangular tank contains water ($\rho = 1000\,\text{kg/m}^3$) to a depth of $3.0\,\text{m}$. The surface is open to the atmosphere, so $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Find the absolute pressure at the bottom of the tank.

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1000)(9.81)(3.0) $$ $$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 2.943 \times 10^4 = 1.307 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}. $$

The gauge pressure — the excess above atmospheric — is simply $\rho g h = 2.94 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa}$.

Pascal's Principle. A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point within the fluid and to the walls of its container. Formally, if the external pressure on a confined fluid increases by $\Delta P$, then the pressure at every interior point increases by the same $\Delta P$. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small area can produce a large force over a larger area, with $F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2$.

🌱 Temperature

🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar thermodynamic state variable that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. For an ideal monatomic gas, this relationship is made precise by the equipartition theorem:

$$ \frac{1}{2}m\langle v^2 \rangle = \frac{3}{2}k_B T $$

where $m$ is the molecular mass, $\langle v^2 \rangle$ is the mean-square speed, $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \text{J K}^{-1}$ is the Boltzmann constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvins. Temperature is thus not a measure of total thermal energy but of the energy per degree of freedom. The SI unit is the kelvin (K), defined since 2019 by fixing $k_B$ to its exact value, with absolute zero ($T = 0\ \text{K}$) representing the theoretical state of minimum thermal motion.

Worked Example. A sample of nitrogen gas ($M = 28.0\ \text{g mol}^{-1}$) is held at $T = 300\ \text{K}$. Find the root-mean-square speed of the molecules.

Using the kinetic-molecular result $v_\text{rms} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M}}$, where $R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$:

$$ v_\text{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{3(8.314)(300)}{0.0280}} = \sqrt{267{,}000\ \text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2}} \approx 517\ \text{m s}^{-1} $$

This speed is comparable to the speed of sound in air, consistent with the molecular basis of acoustic propagation.

Key Theorem — Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics. If system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, and system $B$ is separately in thermal equilibrium with $C$, then $A$ and $B$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This statement is non-trivial: it guarantees that temperature is a well-defined, transitive equivalence relation among systems, and it is the logical foundation that makes thermometry possible. Without the zeroth law, the concept of a universal temperature scale would be operationally undefined.

🌱 Volume

🌱 Volume

Definition. Volume is a scalar quantity that measures the three-dimensional extent of space occupied by an object or enclosed within a boundary. In the SI system, the standard unit of volume is the cubic meter ($\text{m}^3$), though the liter ($1\ \text{L} = 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3$) is commonly used in laboratory contexts. For a continuous medium, the volume element $dV$ represents an infinitesimal region of space, and the total volume is obtained by integration over the region $\mathcal{R}$:

$$ V = \iiint_{\mathcal{R}} dV $$

In thermodynamics, volume is a fundamental state variable. For an ideal gas, volume is directly related to pressure and temperature through the ideal gas law:

$$ PV = nRT $$

where $P$ is absolute pressure, $n$ is the number of moles, $R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ is the universal gas constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvin.

Worked Example. A sealed cylinder contains $2.00\ \text{mol}$ of an ideal gas at $T = 300\ \text{K}$ and $P = 1.50 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$. Find the volume.

Solving the ideal gas law for $V$:

$$ V = \frac{nRT}{P} = \frac{(2.00)(8.314)(300)}{1.50 \times 10^5} $$ $$ V = \frac{4988.4}{1.50 \times 10^5} \approx 3.33 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{m}^3 = 33.3\ \text{L} $$

Key Theorem. For a quasi-static thermodynamic process, the work done by a gas is related to its volume change by:

$$ W = \int_{V_i}^{V_f} P\, dV $$

This result follows from the definition of mechanical work applied to a piston of cross-sectional area $A$: a displacement $dx$ produces work $\delta W = PA\, dx = P\, dV$. It demonstrates that volume change is the mechanism by which a gas exchanges mechanical energy with its surroundings. For an isobaric (constant-pressure) process, this simplifies to $W = P\Delta V$.

🍃 Heat Transfer

🍃 Heat Transfer

Definition. Heat transfer is the process by which thermal energy moves from a region of higher temperature to one of lower temperature, driven by a temperature difference $\Delta T$. Three distinct mechanisms govern this transport: conduction, the diffusion of thermal energy through direct molecular collisions; convection, the bulk movement of a fluid carrying thermal energy; and radiation, the emission and absorption of electromagnetic waves. Each mechanism obeys its own quantitative law, yet all share the fundamental constraint that spontaneous heat flow proceeds only from hot to cold, consistent with the second law of thermodynamics.

For conduction through a slab of material, Fourier's Law states

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = -kA\frac{dT}{dx}, $$

where $k$ is the thermal conductivity of the material $(\text{W m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1})$, $A$ is the cross-sectional area, and $dT/dx$ is the temperature gradient. For convection from a surface to a fluid, Newton's Law of Cooling gives

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = hA(T_s - T_f), $$

with $h$ the convective heat-transfer coefficient. Thermal radiation from a surface follows the Stefan–Boltzmann Law:

$$ P = \varepsilon \sigma A T^4, $$

where $\varepsilon$ is the emissivity, $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{W m}^{-2}\text{K}^{-4}$, and $T$ is the absolute temperature.

Worked Example. A glass window pane of thickness $L = 5.0\ \text{mm}$, area $A = 1.2\ \text{m}^2$, and thermal conductivity $k = 0.96\ \text{W m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ separates an interior at $21^\circ\text{C}$ from an exterior at $-5^\circ\text{C}$. The conductive heat-loss rate is

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = kA\frac{\Delta T}{L} = (0.96)(1.2)\frac{26}{0.005} = 5990\ \text{W} \approx 6.0\ \text{kW}. $$

This illustrates why thin, single-pane windows produce substantial heating loads.

Key Theorem (Thermal Resistance). Conduction through a composite wall of $n$ layers in series obeys

$$ R_\text{total} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{L_i}{k_i A},\quad \frac{dQ}{dt} = \frac{\Delta T}{R_\text{total}}, $$

in direct analogy with Ohm's Law for electrical circuits. Adding low-$k$ insulating layers increases $R_\text{total}$ and reduces heat flow proportionally.

🍃 Thermodynamic Work

🍃 Thermodynamic Work

Definition. In thermodynamics, work is the energy transferred between a system and its surroundings through a macroscopic mechanical interaction. For a gas confined in a cylinder with a movable piston, the infinitesimal work done by the system during a quasi-static process is

$$ dW = P\,dV, $$

where $P$ is the pressure of the gas and $dV$ is the infinitesimal change in volume. For a finite process taking the system from volume $V_i$ to $V_f$, the total work done by the gas is

$$ W = \int_{V_i}^{V_f} P\,dV. $$

Because $P$ may depend on $V$ along the path, thermodynamic work is a path function, not a state function: its value depends on how the process is carried out, not merely on the initial and final states. Work done on the system carries the opposite sign, $W_{\text{on}} = -W$.

Worked Example. An ideal gas undergoes an isobaric (constant-pressure) expansion at $P = 2.00 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$ from $V_i = 0.500\,\text{m}^3$ to $V_f = 1.20\,\text{m}^3$. Find the work done by the gas.

Since pressure is constant, it factors out of the integral:

$$ W = P\int_{V_i}^{V_f}dV = P(V_f - V_i) = (2.00\times10^5\,\text{Pa})(1.20 - 0.500)\,\text{m}^3 = 1.40\times10^5\,\text{J}. $$

The gas does $140\,\text{kJ}$ of positive work on the piston, consistent with expansion pushing outward.

Key Theorem — Work as Area Under a $P$–$V$ Curve. For any quasi-static process, $W = \int_{V_i}^{V_f} P\,dV$ equals the area enclosed between the process path and the volume axis on a $P$–$V$ diagram. Consequently, a cyclic process that traces a closed loop on the $P$–$V$ diagram performs net work equal to the area enclosed by that loop. Clockwise traversal yields net positive work output (a heat engine), while counterclockwise traversal requires net work input (a refrigerator or heat pump).

Lab Exercise. Using a hand-operated syringe connected to a pressure sensor, slowly compress $50\,\text{mL}$ of air to $25\,\text{mL}$ while recording $P$ versus $V$ at ten evenly spaced intervals. Plot the data on a $P$–$V$ diagram and numerically integrate the curve using the trapezoidal rule to estimate the work done on the gas. Compare your result to the prediction from Boyle's Law, $W = nRT\ln(V_i/V_f)$, assuming an isothermal process, and discuss sources of discrepancy.

🌱 Potential Energy

🌱 Potential Energy

Definition. Potential energy is the stored energy a system possesses by virtue of the configuration or position of its constituent parts. For a conservative force $\mathbf{F}$, the potential energy function $U$ is defined by the relation

$$ \Delta U = -\int_{x_i}^{x_f} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{x}, $$

so that the work done by the conservative force equals the negative change in potential energy: $W_\text{cons} = -\Delta U$. This definition is meaningful only for conservative forces — those for which the work done is path-independent and the associated energy can be fully recovered.

Two forms appear throughout College Physics 2E Ch15. Gravitational potential energy near Earth's surface is

$$ U_g = mgh, $$

where $m$ is the object's mass, $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$, and $h$ is the height measured from a chosen reference level. Elastic (spring) potential energy stored in an ideal spring compressed or stretched by displacement $x$ from equilibrium is

$$ U_s = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2, $$

where $k$ is the spring constant.

Worked example. A 2.0 kg block is released from rest at the top of a frictionless ramp 5.0 m high. Find its speed at the bottom.

Taking the ground as the reference level, the initial potential energy is $U_i = mgh = (2.0)(9.8)(5.0) = 98\ \text{J}$, with $K_i = 0$. At the bottom, $U_f = 0$. By conservation of mechanical energy:

$$ K_f = U_i \implies \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 = 98\ \text{J} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{2(98)}{2.0}} = 9.9\ \text{m/s}. $$

Key theorem — Conservation of mechanical energy. For a system subject only to conservative forces,

$$ E_\text{mech} = K + U = \text{constant}. $$

Any decrease in potential energy produces an equal increase in kinetic energy, and vice versa.

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

Definition. The law of conservation of energy states that the total mechanical energy of an isolated system—the sum of kinetic energy $K$ and potential energy $U$—remains constant over time, provided no non-conservative forces (such as friction or air resistance) do work on the system. Formally,

$$ E_{\text{mech}} = K + U = \text{constant}, $$

or equivalently,

$$ K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f. $$

When non-conservative forces are present, the work-energy theorem generalizes to

$$ W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta E_{\text{mech}}, $$

where $W_{\text{nc}}$ represents the net work done by non-conservative forces, which typically manifest as thermal energy dissipated into the environment.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 2.0\ \text{kg}$ is released from rest at the top of a frictionless incline of height $h = 5.0\ \text{m}$. Find the speed of the block at the bottom.

Taking the base of the incline as the reference level ($U = 0$), the initial state has $K_i = 0$ and $U_i = mgh$. At the bottom, $U_f = 0$. Applying conservation of energy:

$$ K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f $$ $$ 0 + mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + 0 $$ $$ v_f = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8\ \text{m/s}^2)(5.0\ \text{m})} \approx 9.9\ \text{m/s}. $$

Note that $m$ cancels, confirming that the result is independent of the object's mass.

Key Theorem. For a conservative system, the mechanical energy $E_{\text{mech}}$ is a constant of the motion. This follows directly from Newton's second law and the definition of a conservative force: the work done by such a force around any closed path is zero, ensuring that energy stored as potential can be fully recovered as kinetic energy and vice versa. This theorem underpins the analysis of oscillatory systems, orbital mechanics, and collision dynamics.

🍃 Internal Energy

🍃 Internal Energy

Definition. The internal energy $U$ of a thermodynamic system is the total energy contained within the system, comprising the kinetic energies of all constituent particles (translational, rotational, and vibrational) and the potential energies arising from intermolecular interactions. Unlike macroscopic kinetic or potential energy associated with bulk motion or position, $U$ depends solely on the current thermodynamic state of the system, not on the path by which that state was reached. For an ideal monatomic gas of $N$ particles, the equipartition theorem (each translational degree of freedom contributes $\frac{1}{2}k_B T$ per particle) yields the particularly clean form

$$ U = \frac{3}{2} N k_B T = \frac{3}{2} n R T, $$

where $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant, $T$ is absolute temperature, $n$ is the number of moles, and $R = 8.314\ \mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$ is the universal gas constant.

Worked Example. Consider $2.00\ \mathrm{mol}$ of an ideal monatomic gas at $T = 300\ \mathrm{K}$. Compute the internal energy.

$$ U = \frac{3}{2} n R T = \frac{3}{2}(2.00\ \mathrm{mol})(8.314\ \mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}\,K^{-1}})(300\ \mathrm{K}) = 7.48 \times 10^{3}\ \mathrm{J}. $$

If the gas is heated at constant volume to $T' = 450\ \mathrm{K}$, the change in internal energy is

$$ \Delta U = \frac{3}{2} n R \,\Delta T = \frac{3}{2}(2.00)(8.314)(150) = 3.74 \times 10^{3}\ \mathrm{J}. $$

No work is done at constant volume, so the first law gives $\Delta U = Q$: all heat added goes directly into raising internal energy.

Key Theorem — Path Independence of $\Delta U$. Because $U$ is determined entirely by the thermodynamic state, $\Delta U$ between any two equilibrium states is the same regardless of process. Applied to the first law, $\Delta U = Q - W$, this means that while $Q$ and $W$ individually depend on the path taken, their difference does not. For the constant-volume case, $W = 0$ identically, so the full heat input appears as a change in $U$ — a direct, measurable consequence of this path independence.

🍃 First Law Of Thermodynamics

🍃 First Law Of Thermodynamics

Definition. The first law of thermodynamics is a statement of energy conservation applied to thermodynamic systems. It asserts that the change in the internal energy $\Delta U$ of a system equals the heat $Q$ added to the system minus the work $W$ done by the system on its surroundings:

$$ \Delta U = Q - W $$

Here, $Q > 0$ when heat flows into the system, $W > 0$ when the system does positive work on the environment, and $\Delta U$ represents the net change in all microscopic kinetic and potential energies of the constituent particles. This law rules out perpetual motion machines of the first kind — devices that produce work without an energy source.

Worked Example. An ideal gas enclosed in a cylinder absorbs $Q = 500\ \text{J}$ of heat from a reservoir while expanding quasi-statically against a constant external pressure of $P = 1.0 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$, increasing its volume by $\Delta V = 3.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3$. The work done by the gas is:

$$ W = P\,\Delta V = (1.0 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa})(3.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3) = 300\ \text{J} $$

Applying the first law:

$$ \Delta U = Q - W = 500\ \text{J} - 300\ \text{J} = 200\ \text{J} $$

The internal energy of the gas increases by $200\ \text{J}$, consistent with a temperature rise in the gas.

Key Theorem. For any cyclic process, the system returns to its initial state, so $\Delta U = 0$. Consequently:

$$ Q_{\text{net}} = W_{\text{net}} $$

The net heat absorbed equals the net work done. This result underlies the analysis of heat engines and refrigerators, where one evaluates thermal efficiency $\eta = W_{\text{net}}/Q_{\text{in}}$ over a complete cycle — the single new quantity introduced here to characterize how effectively a cyclic device converts heat into useful work.

Repeat the measurement at two or three different values of supplied energy to confirm that $\Delta U$ scales linearly with $Q - W$ and that the efficiency $\eta$ remains consistent across trials.

🍃 Cyclical Process

🍃 Cyclical Process

Definition. A cyclical process (or thermodynamic cycle) is a sequence of state changes that returns a system to its exact initial thermodynamic state — the same temperature, pressure, volume, and internal energy from which it began. Because the internal energy $U$ is a state function, completing one full cycle requires $\Delta U_{\text{cycle}} = 0$. Applying the first law of thermodynamics,

$$ \Delta U = Q - W = 0 \implies Q_{\text{net}} = W_{\text{net}}, $$

where $Q_{\text{net}}$ is the net heat exchanged with the surroundings and $W_{\text{net}}$ is the net work done by the system over one cycle. On a $P$–$V$ diagram, a cyclical process forms a closed curve; the enclosed area equals $W_{\text{net}}$, with a clockwise orientation indicating positive (engine) work and counterclockwise indicating negative (refrigerator) work.

Worked Example. Consider a monatomic ideal gas taken through a three-step cycle: (1) isothermal expansion at $T_H = 500\ \text{K}$ from $V_1 = 1.0\ \text{L}$ to $V_2 = 2.0\ \text{L}$; (2) isochoric (constant-volume) cooling back to $T_C = 250\ \text{K}$; (3) isobaric compression back to $V_1$. For step (1), the work done by the gas is

$$ W_1 = nRT_H \ln\!\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right) = (1\ \text{mol})(8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1})(500\ \text{K})\ln 2 \approx 2880\ \text{J}. $$

Tracking $Q$ and $W$ for each leg and summing confirms $Q_{\text{net}} = W_{\text{net}}$, consistent with $\Delta U_{\text{cycle}} = 0$.

Key Theorem. The defining constraint $\Delta U_{\text{cycle}} = 0$ carries a fundamental consequence: no cyclical engine operating between reservoirs at $T_H$ and $T_C$ can convert heat entirely into work. The Carnot result, derived elsewhere, sets the upper bound on efficiency at $\eta \leq 1 - T_C/T_H$. For the cycle described above, $\eta_{\text{max}} = 1 - 250/500 = 0.50$, meaning at most half the heat absorbed at $T_H$ can appear as net work; the remainder must be rejected to the cold reservoir.

🍃 Reversible Process

🍃 Reversible Process

Definition. A reversible process is a quasi-static thermodynamic process that can be reversed in direction without leaving any net change in either the system or its surroundings. Formally, a process taking a system from state $A$ to state $B$ is reversible if and only if the reverse path from $B$ to $A$ restores every thermodynamic variable—pressure $P$, volume $V$, temperature $T$, and internal energy $U$—to their original values while exchanging the same magnitudes of heat and work with the surroundings but with opposite signs. A necessary condition is that the process proceeds infinitely slowly (quasi-statically) through a continuous sequence of equilibrium states, so that at every instant the system can be described by well-defined state variables. Any process involving friction, finite temperature gradients, or unrestrained expansion is inherently irreversible.

Worked Example. Consider an ideal gas compressed isothermally and reversibly from volume $V_i = 2.00\,\text{L}$ to $V_f = 1.00\,\text{L}$ at constant temperature $T = 300\,\text{K}$, with $n = 0.100\,\text{mol}$.

The work done on the gas is:

$$ W_{\text{on}} = -nRT\ln\!\left(\frac{V_f}{V_i}\right) = -(0.100)(8.314)(300)\ln\!\left(\frac{1.00}{2.00}\right) \approx +172\,\text{J}. $$

Because $\Delta U = 0$ for an isothermal ideal-gas process, the heat released by the gas equals $Q = -172\,\text{J}$. Reversing the piston infinitely slowly returns the gas to $V_i$, and the surroundings receive back exactly $172\,\text{J}$ of work while absorbing $172\,\text{J}$ of heat—net change in both system and surroundings is zero.

Key Theorem. The Second Law of Thermodynamics requires that the total entropy change of the universe for any reversible process is exactly zero:

$$ \Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} = 0, $$

where entropy $S$ is the state function defined by $dS = \delta Q_{\text{rev}}/T$ for a reversible heat exchange at temperature $T$. Because the system gains $+\delta Q_{\text{rev}}/T$ while the surroundings lose $-\delta Q_{\text{rev}}/T$, the two contributions cancel exactly. Any irreversible process satisfies $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0$, making the reversible process an idealized upper bound on thermodynamic efficiency.

🍃 Heat Engine

🍃 Heat Engine

Definition. A heat engine is a device that converts thermal energy into mechanical work by operating cyclically between two thermal reservoirs: a high-temperature source at temperature $T_H$ and a low-temperature sink at temperature $T_C$. In each cycle, the engine absorbs heat $Q_H$ from the hot reservoir, performs net work $W$, and expels waste heat $Q_C$ to the cold reservoir. By the first law of thermodynamics applied to a complete cycle (where the internal energy change is zero),

$$ W = Q_H - Q_C. $$

The thermal efficiency of the engine is defined as the fraction of absorbed heat converted to useful work:

$$ e = \frac{W}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H}. $$

Worked Example. A steam engine absorbs $Q_H = 2000\ \text{J}$ of heat per cycle from a boiler and exhausts $Q_C = 1400\ \text{J}$ to a condenser. Find the net work output per cycle and the thermal efficiency.

Solution:

$$ W = Q_H - Q_C = 2000\ \text{J} - 1400\ \text{J} = 600\ \text{J}. $$ $$ e = \frac{W}{Q_H} = \frac{600}{2000} = 0.30 = 30\%. $$

Thus only 30% of the heat extracted from the boiler is converted to mechanical work; the remaining 70% is expelled as waste.

Key Theorem — Carnot Efficiency. No heat engine operating between reservoirs at $T_H$ and $T_C$ can have an efficiency greater than that of the Carnot engine:

$$ e_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}, $$

where temperatures are in kelvins. This upper bound follows directly from the second law of thermodynamics: any irreversible process (friction, heat conduction across finite temperature differences) necessarily reduces efficiency below $e_{\text{Carnot}}$. For the example above, if $T_H = 500\ \text{K}$ and $T_C = 300\ \text{K}$, the Carnot efficiency would be $e_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - 300/500 = 40\%$, confirming that the engine's actual 30% is physically permissible but not ideal.

🍃 Irreversible Process

🍃 Irreversible Process

Definition. An irreversible process is a thermodynamic process that cannot be reversed by an infinitesimal change in an external parameter, and whose spontaneous reversal would violate the second law of thermodynamics. Formally, a process is irreversible if the total entropy of the universe (system plus surroundings) increases:

$$ \Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} > 0 $$

Common sources of irreversibility include friction, unrestrained expansion, heat transfer across a finite temperature difference, and mixing of distinct substances. Unlike a quasi-static (reversible) process, an irreversible process drives the system through non-equilibrium states, making it impossible to retrace the exact path in both the system and surroundings simultaneously.

Worked Example. Consider $n = 1.00\,\text{mol}$ of an ideal gas at $T = 300\,\text{K}$ confined to one side of an insulated container of total volume $V_f = 2V_i$. When a partition is removed, the gas undergoes a free expansion into the vacuum.

Since no work is done ($W = 0$) and the walls are adiabatic ($Q = 0$), the internal energy and hence the temperature remain unchanged. The entropy change of the system is calculated using the equivalent reversible isothermal path:

$$ \Delta S_{\text{system}} = nR\ln\!\left(\frac{V_f}{V_i}\right) = (1.00)(8.314)\ln(2) \approx +5.76\,\text{J/K} $$

The surroundings receive no heat, so $\Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} = 0$. Therefore:

$$ \Delta S_{\text{universe}} = 5.76\,\text{J/K} > 0 $$

confirming the process is irreversible.

Key Theorem (Clausius Inequality). For any cyclic process, the Clausius inequality states:

$$ \oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} \leq 0 $$

with equality holding only for a reversible cycle. For any process between states 1 and 2, this implies $\Delta S \geq \int_1^2 \frac{\delta Q}{T}$, where the inequality corresponds to an irreversible path. This theorem provides the quantitative criterion distinguishing irreversible from reversible processes.

🍃 Second Law Of Thermodynamics

🍃 Second Law Of Thermodynamics

Definition. The second law of thermodynamics asserts that the total entropy of an isolated system never decreases over time. Formally, for any spontaneous process occurring in an isolated system,

$$ \Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} \geq 0, $$

where equality holds only for reversible (quasi-static) processes. An equivalent formulation due to Kelvin and Planck states that no heat engine operating in a cycle can convert heat entirely into work without rejecting some heat to a cold reservoir. The Clausius statement provides yet another equivalent: heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter body without external work input. These statements are logically equivalent; violation of one implies violation of all.

Worked Example. A Carnot engine operates between a hot reservoir at $T_H = 600\ \text{K}$ and a cold reservoir at $T_C = 300\ \text{K}$. It absorbs $Q_H = 1200\ \text{J}$ per cycle. Determine the work output, heat rejected, and entropy change of the universe per cycle.

The Carnot efficiency is

$$ e_C = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H} = 1 - \frac{300}{600} = 0.50. $$

Work output: $W = e_C Q_H = 0.50 \times 1200 = 600\ \text{J}$.

Heat rejected: $Q_C = Q_H - W = 600\ \text{J}$.

Entropy change: $\Delta S_H = -Q_H/T_H = -2.00\ \text{J/K}$ and $\Delta S_C = +Q_C/T_C = +2.00\ \text{J/K}$, giving $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = 0$, consistent with the Carnot cycle being reversible.

Key Theorem. The Carnot theorem establishes that no heat engine operating between two thermal reservoirs can be more efficient than a Carnot engine operating between the same reservoirs. Any real (irreversible) engine satisfies $e < e_C$, and $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0$ for each cycle. This places a fundamental upper bound on the efficiency of all heat engines and underlies the concept of thermodynamic irreversibility.

Payoff 🎯

The heat death of the universe represents the ultimate consequence of the second law of thermodynamics applied at cosmological scale. It is not merely a distant curiosity but the logical terminus of every thermodynamic process studied throughout this course. Understanding it consolidates the entire arc of entropy, energy dispersal, and irreversibility into a single, unified conclusion about the fate of all physical systems.

What the concept achieves. The second law asserts that in any isolated system, the total entropy $S$ never decreases: $\Delta S \geq 0$. Applied to the universe as a whole — itself the largest conceivable isolated system — this inequality drives all spontaneous processes relentlessly toward states of greater disorder. Stars convert gravitational potential energy into radiation, engines dissipate work into thermal noise, and every irreversible process increases the global entropy budget. The heat death hypothesis, formalized by Kelvin and Clausius in the nineteenth century, proposes that this progression terminates when the universe reaches a state of maximum entropy $S_{\max}$, at which point no thermodynamic gradient remains to drive further work:

$$ \lim_{t \to \infty} \Delta S_{\text{universe}} = 0, \quad T_{\text{everywhere}} = T_{\text{eq}}. $$

At this equilibrium temperature $T_{\text{eq}}$, every region of space shares the same energy density. No heat engine can operate without a temperature difference $\Delta T = T_H - T_C > 0$; when $\Delta T \to 0$, the maximum efficiency $\eta = 1 - T_C/T_H \to 0$ and no useful work can be extracted from any process.

Why it is the natural endpoint. Every concept introduced in this chapter — entropy, the Carnot cycle, free energy, and the statistical interpretation of disorder — points here. Boltzmann's statistical definition, $S = k_B \ln \Omega$, makes the conclusion quantitative: the number of accessible microstates $\Omega$ grows monotonically until the macrostate of uniform energy distribution overwhelms all others by an astronomical combinatorial margin, making any spontaneous return to order effectively impossible.

A worked illustration. Consider a Carnot engine operating between a star at $T_H = 6000\ \text{K}$ and deep space at $T_C = 3\ \text{K}$. Its efficiency is $\eta = 1 - 3/6000 \approx 99.95\%$. As the star radiates and cools toward $T_C$, efficiency collapses to zero — a miniature rehearsal of the universe's final state compressed into stellar lifetime.

Key theorem. The Clausius inequality guarantees that for any cyclic process in an isolated universe, $\oint \delta Q / T \leq 0$, with equality only for reversible processes. Since all real processes are irreversible, the universal entropy increases without bound until no reversible pathway remains — the formal proof that heat death is asymptotically inevitable.
