🍃 PV Diagram

A PV diagram (pressure-volume diagram) is a graphical representation of the thermodynamic state of a system, where pressure $P$ is plotted on the vertical axis and volume $V$ on the horizontal axis. Each point on the diagram corresponds to a unique equilibrium state of the system, and any quasi-static process — one proceeding slowly enough that the system remains in thermal equilibrium at each step — traces a continuous curve. The enclosed area of any closed path on a PV diagram equals the net work done by the system over one complete thermodynamic cycle:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \oint P \, dV $$

For a process from state $A$ to state $B$, the work done by the gas is:

$$ W_{A \to B} = \int_{V_A}^{V_B} P \, dV $$

This integral equals the area under the process curve, making PV diagrams an invaluable tool for computing work without explicit integration when the process shape is standard (e.g., isobaric, isothermal, adiabatic).

Worked Example. Consider 1.00 mol of an ideal monatomic gas undergoing a two-step cycle: (1) an isobaric expansion at $P_1 = 2.00 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$ from $V_1 = 0.010\ \text{m}^3$ to $V_2 = 0.020\ \text{m}^3$, then (2) an isochoric (constant-volume) return to the original pressure at $V = V_2$. Work is done only during the isobaric step:

$$ W = P_1 (V_2 - V_1) = (2.00 \times 10^5)(0.020 - 0.010) = 2.00 \times 10^3\ \text{J} $$

On the PV diagram, this equals the rectangular area beneath the horizontal segment at $P_1$.

Key Theorem. For a closed cycle traversed clockwise on a PV diagram, the net work $W_{\text{net}} > 0$, meaning the system acts as a heat engine delivering net work to the surroundings. A counterclockwise cycle yields $W_{\text{net}} < 0$, characteristic of a refrigerator or heat pump. This sign convention follows directly from the orientation of the line integral.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a bicycle pump fitted with a pressure gauge and a volume scale. Compress the gas inside slowly from $V_i$ to $V_f$ while recording $(P, V)$ pairs at five intermediate states. Plot your data on a PV diagram, estimate the area under the curve using the trapezoidal rule, and compare this numerical result to the work you calculate from $W = \int P \, dV$ assuming an isothermal process ($PV = nRT = \text{const}$). Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify sources of non-ideality.
