🌱 Kinetic Energy

🌱 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is defined as

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

This scalar quantity carries SI units of joules ($\text{J} = \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-2}$) and is always non-negative. Kinetic energy is a state function: it depends only on the instantaneous speed of the object, not on its direction of travel or the path taken to reach that speed. For systems composed of multiple particles, the total kinetic energy is the algebraic sum of the individual contributions $K_\text{tot} = \sum_i \frac{1}{2}m_i v_i^2$.

Worked Example. A 1200 kg automobile accelerates from rest to $v = 27\ \text{m/s}$ (approximately 97 km/h) on a level road. Find its kinetic energy at full speed and the net work done on it during acceleration.

By definition,

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(1200\ \text{kg})(27\ \text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1200)(729)\ \text{J} = 437{,}400\ \text{J} \approx 4.37 \times 10^5\ \text{J}. $$

Because the car starts from rest ($K_i = 0$), the work–energy theorem gives

$$ W_\text{net} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i = 4.37 \times 10^5\ \text{J} - 0 = 4.37 \times 10^5\ \text{J}. $$

The Work–Energy Theorem. The central result connecting kinematics and energy is:

The net work done on a particle equals the change in its kinetic energy: $$ W_\text{net} = \Delta K = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2. $$

Proof sketch. Starting from Newton's second law, $F_\text{net} = ma$, multiply both sides by an infinitesimal displacement $dx$ and integrate along the particle's path. Using $a\,dx = v\,dv$ (from the chain rule), one obtains $\int F_\text{net}\,dx = \int_{v_i}^{v_f} mv\,dv = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2$, which is the theorem.

Lab Exercise. Using a dynamics cart of known mass $m$ on a frictionless air track, apply a constant horizontal force $F$ over a measured distance $d$ starting from rest. (1) Compute the predicted final speed $v_f = \sqrt{2Fd/m}$ from the work–energy theorem. (2) Measure $v_f$ with a photogate timer. (3) Calculate the percentage discrepancy between the theoretical and measured values and identify the dominant source of error.
