🍃 Heat Engine

🍃 Heat Engine

Definition. A heat engine is a device that converts thermal energy into mechanical work by operating cyclically between two thermal reservoirs: a high-temperature source at temperature $T_H$ and a low-temperature sink at temperature $T_C$. In each cycle, the engine absorbs heat $Q_H$ from the hot reservoir, performs net work $W$, and expels waste heat $Q_C$ to the cold reservoir. By the first law of thermodynamics applied to a complete cycle (where the internal energy change is zero),

$$ W = Q_H - Q_C. $$

The thermal efficiency of the engine is defined as the fraction of absorbed heat converted to useful work:

$$ e = \frac{W}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H}. $$

Worked Example. A steam engine absorbs $Q_H = 2000\ \text{J}$ of heat per cycle from a boiler and exhausts $Q_C = 1400\ \text{J}$ to a condenser. Find the net work output per cycle and the thermal efficiency.

Solution:

$$ W = Q_H - Q_C = 2000\ \text{J} - 1400\ \text{J} = 600\ \text{J}. $$ $$ e = \frac{W}{Q_H} = \frac{600}{2000} = 0.30 = 30\%. $$

Thus only 30% of the heat extracted from the boiler is converted to mechanical work; the remaining 70% is expelled as waste.

Key Theorem — Carnot Efficiency. No heat engine operating between reservoirs at $T_H$ and $T_C$ can have an efficiency greater than that of the Carnot engine:

$$ e_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}, $$

where temperatures are in kelvins. This upper bound follows directly from the second law of thermodynamics: any irreversible process (friction, heat conduction across finite temperature differences) necessarily reduces efficiency below $e_{\text{Carnot}}$. For the example above, if $T_H = 500\ \text{K}$ and $T_C = 300\ \text{K}$, the Carnot efficiency would be $e_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - 300/500 = 40\%$, confirming that the engine's actual 30% is physically permissible but not ideal.

Lab Exercise. Using a $P$-$V$ diagram, sketch one complete cycle of an idealized engine consisting of two isothermal and two adiabatic processes (a Carnot cycle). Label each process, identify where heat is absorbed and expelled, and calculate $e_{\text{Carnot}}$ for $T_H = 600\ \text{K}$ and $T_C = 300\ \text{K}$. Compare your result to an engine with $e = 0.45$ and determine whether that engine violates the second law.
