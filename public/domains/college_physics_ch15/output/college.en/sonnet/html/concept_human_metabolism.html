🌸 Human Metabolism

🌸 Human Metabolism

Definition. Human metabolism refers to the totality of chemical reactions occurring within the body that convert food into usable energy and structural materials. Thermodynamically, metabolism is modeled as an open system exchanging both energy and matter with its environment. The metabolic rate $P$ is defined as the rate at which the body converts chemical potential energy (from food) into work and heat:

$$ P = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} $$

where $\Delta E$ is the energy extracted from metabolic processes over time interval $\Delta t$. The basal metabolic rate (BMR) represents the minimum power required to sustain life at rest, typically ranging from 60–100 W for an adult human. Total daily energy expenditure (TDEE) incorporates physical activity and is measured in kilocalories (kcal), where $1\ \text{kcal} = 4186\ \text{J}$.

Worked Example. A 70 kg adult has a BMR of 1700 kcal/day. During a 30-minute moderate cycling session, they expend an additional 400 kcal. Assuming the body operates at a mechanical efficiency of $\eta = 25\%$, determine the useful mechanical work output during cycling.

The additional energy input during exercise is:

$$ Q = 400\ \text{kcal} \times 4186\ \frac{\text{J}}{\text{kcal}} = 1.674 \times 10^6\ \text{J} $$

The useful mechanical work output is:

$$ W = \eta \cdot Q = 0.25 \times 1.674 \times 10^6\ \text{J} = 4.19 \times 10^5\ \text{J} $$

The remaining $75\%$ of the energy is dissipated as metabolic heat, which the body must remove through perspiration, respiration, and radiation.

Key Theorem — First Law Applied to Metabolism. For a living system, the first law of thermodynamics gives:

$$ \Delta U = Q_{\text{food}} - W_{\text{mech}} - Q_{\text{heat}} $$

where $\Delta U$ is the change in internal (chemical) energy stored in the body, $Q_{\text{food}}$ is the energy input from nutrients, $W_{\text{mech}}$ is mechanical work performed, and $Q_{\text{heat}}$ is heat lost to the environment. At steady state ($\Delta U = 0$), caloric intake exactly balances energy expenditure.

Lab Exercise. Using a calorimeter, measure the heat released by combustion of a 1 g sample of glucose ($C_6H_{12}O_6$). Compare the result with the accepted value of $15.7\ \text{kJ/g}$, and calculate the percent efficiency of energy transfer if a resting subject oxidizes 1 g of glucose over 10 minutes while dissipating 12.5 kJ as heat.
