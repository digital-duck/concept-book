🍃 Isochoric Process

🍃 Isochoric Process

Definition. An isochoric process (also called an isovolumetric or constant-volume process) is a thermodynamic process in which the volume of the system remains fixed throughout: $\Delta V = 0$. Because no expansion or compression occurs, the system performs no pressure–volume work on its surroundings. Formally,

$$ W = \int_{V_i}^{V_f} P \, dV = 0 \quad \text{(since } V_f = V_i\text{)}. $$

Applying the first law of thermodynamics, $\Delta U = Q - W$, the absence of work reduces to

$$ \Delta U = Q_V, $$

where $Q_V$ denotes the heat exchanged at constant volume. Every joule of heat added goes entirely into changing the internal energy of the system. For an ideal gas, this heat is related to the molar heat capacity at constant volume $C_V$ by

$$ Q_V = n C_V \Delta T, $$

so $\Delta U = n C_V \Delta T$. The quantity $C_V$ is the one new primitive introduced here: it measures how much energy a mole of gas stores per kelvin of temperature rise at fixed volume.

Worked Example. Suppose $2.00\ \text{mol}$ of a monatomic ideal gas (with $C_V = \tfrac{3}{2}R$) is heated isochorically from $T_i = 300\ \text{K}$ to $T_f = 450\ \text{K}$. Calculate the heat added and the change in internal energy.

$$ \Delta T = 450 - 300 = 150\ \text{K} $$ $$ Q_V = n C_V \Delta T = (2.00)\!\left(\tfrac{3}{2}(8.314)\right)(150) = 3741\ \text{J} $$ $$ \Delta U = Q_V = 3741\ \text{J}, \quad W = 0. $$

On a $P$–$V$ diagram, this process appears as a vertical line segment — pressure changes while volume stays constant.

Key Theorem. For any ideal gas undergoing an isochoric process, the pressure and absolute temperature are directly proportional:

$$ \frac{P_i}{T_i} = \frac{P_f}{T_f} \quad \text{(Gay-Lussac's Law)}. $$

This follows immediately from the ideal gas law $PV = nRT$ with $V$ and $n$ held fixed: since $nR/V$ is constant, $P/T$ is constant.

Lab Exercise. Seal a fixed quantity of air inside a rigid steel canister fitted with a pressure gauge and a thermometer. Record $P$ and $T$ at room temperature ($\approx 295\ \text{K}$), then submerge the canister in boiling water ($373\ \text{K}$). Predict the new pressure using Gay-Lussac's Law and compare with the gauge reading. Calculate the percentage discrepancy and discuss whether treating air as an ideal gas is justified at these conditions.
