🍃 Centripetal Force

🍃 Centripetal Force

Definition. When an object moves along a circular path at constant speed, its velocity vector continuously changes direction, requiring a net acceleration directed toward the center of the circle. By Newton's second law, this centripetal acceleration must be produced by a net force of equal direction. The centripetal force is defined as the net force acting on an object undergoing uniform circular motion, directed radially inward toward the center of curvature. For an object of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ along a circle of radius $r$, the magnitude of the centripetal force is

$$ F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}, $$

where $v$ is the linear speed. Centripetal force is not a new fundamental force; it is always provided by one or more existing physical forces — tension, gravity, normal force, friction, or their combinations.

Worked Example. A car of mass $1200\ \text{kg}$ rounds a flat, unbanked curve of radius $r = 80\ \text{m}$ at a speed of $v = 20\ \text{m/s}$. Determine whether the car can complete the turn if the coefficient of static friction between the tires and road is $\mu_s = 0.60$.

The only horizontal force available on a flat road is static friction, so $f_s \leq \mu_s mg$. The required centripetal force is

$$ F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{1200 \times (20)^2}{80} = 6000\ \text{N}. $$

The maximum static friction force is

$$ f_{s,\text{max}} = \mu_s mg = 0.60 \times 1200 \times 9.8 = 7056\ \text{N}. $$

Since $F_c = 6000\ \text{N} < 7056\ \text{N}$, friction is sufficient and the car completes the turn safely.

Key Theorem. For uniform circular motion, the centripetal acceleration satisfies $a_c = v^2/r$, derivable from the limit of the change in velocity vector over an infinitesimal arc. Because the speed is constant, the tangential acceleration is zero and the centripetal force does no work on the object — it changes direction only, not kinetic energy.

Lab Exercise. Attach a known mass $m$ to a string of length $L$ and swing it in a horizontal circle on a frictionless table. Measure the period $T$ at several radii. Plot the measured string tension (read from a spring scale) against $mv^2/r$, where $v = 2\pi L/T$, and verify that the two quantities agree across trials, confirming that tension alone supplies the centripetal force.
