🌱 Radius Of Curvature

🌱 Radius Of Curvature

Definition. When a particle moves along a curved path, the trajectory at any point can be locally approximated by a circular arc called the osculating circle. The radius of curvature $R$ at that point is the radius of this best-fitting circle. For a path described by $y = f(x)$, the radius of curvature is given by

$$ R = \frac{\left[1 + \left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)^2\right]^{3/2}}{\left|\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}\right|}. $$

Because centripetal acceleration (a concept established in the chapter) satisfies $a_c = v^2/R$, the radius of curvature is the single geometric quantity linking any curved trajectory to the Newtonian force analysis: a smaller $R$ (tighter curve) demands a larger centripetal force for the same speed.

Worked Example. A car travels over a hill whose profile is approximated by $y = -0.01x^2$ (in meters, with the crest at $x = 0$). Find $R$ at the crest and determine the maximum speed at which a 1200 kg car maintains contact with the road.

At $x = 0$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$ and $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -0.02\ \text{m}^{-1}$. Substituting:

$$ R = \frac{(1 + 0)^{3/2}}{|-0.02|} = \frac{1}{0.02} = 50\ \text{m}. $$

At the crest, the normal force $N$ and weight $mg$ provide the net centripetal force directed downward (toward the center of curvature). Newton's second law gives

$$ mg - N = \frac{mv^2}{R}. $$

Contact is lost when $N = 0$, so $v_{\max} = \sqrt{gR} = \sqrt{9.8 \times 50} \approx 22.1\ \text{m/s}$ ($\approx 79.5\ \text{km/h}$).

Key Theorem. For projectile motion, the radius of curvature at the apex (where $v_y = 0$) reduces to $R = v_{x}^{2}/g$, since only horizontal velocity persists and gravity acts as the centripetal acceleration. This result follows immediately from $a_c = v^2/R$ with $v = v_x$ and $a_c = g$.

Compare the centripetal acceleration $v^2/R$ with the value inferred from force sensors attached to the ball, and quantify the percent discrepancy.

🍃 Radian

🍃 Radian

Definition. The radian is the SI unit of plane angle defined as the ratio of arc length to radius. Formally, if a central angle $\theta$ subtends an arc of length $s$ on a circle of radius $r$, then

$$ \theta = \frac{s}{r}. $$

Because both $s$ and $r$ carry units of length, the radian is dimensionless—a pure number. A full revolution corresponds to an arc length equal to the full circumference $2\pi r$, giving $\theta = 2\pi r / r = 2\pi$ rad. The conversion between degrees and radians therefore satisfies

$$ 180^\circ = \pi \text{ rad}, \qquad 1^\circ = \frac{\pi}{180} \text{ rad}. $$

Worked Example. A wheel of radius $r = 0.40$ m rotates so that a point on its rim travels a distance $s = 1.20$ m along the arc. Find the angle swept in radians and in degrees.

$$ \theta = \frac{s}{r} = \frac{1.20 \text{ m}}{0.40 \text{ m}} = 3.0 \text{ rad}. $$

Converting to degrees:

$$ \theta = 3.0 \times \frac{180^\circ}{\pi} \approx 171.9^\circ. $$

Key Theorem — Small-Angle Approximation. For angles expressed in radians, the trigonometric functions satisfy

$$ \sin\theta \approx \theta, \qquad \tan\theta \approx \theta, \qquad \cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2}, $$

whenever $|\theta| \operatorname{ll} 1$. This result follows directly from the Taylor series expansions of $\sin\theta$ and $\cos\theta$ about $\theta = 0$. The approximation is indispensable in classical mechanics (pendulum dynamics), optics (paraxial ray tracing), and wave theory, and it is valid to within 1% for $|\theta| \lesssim 0.14$ rad ($\approx 8^\circ$). Crucially, the approximation is only correct when $\theta$ is measured in radians; inserting a value in degrees produces a numerically incorrect result.

🍃 Rotation Angle

🍃 Rotation Angle

Definition. When a rigid body rotates about a fixed axis, the rotation angle $\Delta\theta$ is the ratio of the arc length $\Delta s$ traced by a reference point to the radial distance $r$ of that point from the axis:

$$ \Delta\theta = \frac{\Delta s}{r} $$

The SI unit is the radian (rad), a dimensionless ratio of two lengths. One full revolution corresponds to an arc length equal to the full circumference $2\pi r$, giving $\Delta\theta = 2\pi$ rad. The conversion between common units is

$$ 1 \text{ revolution} = 2\pi \text{ rad} = 360^\circ, \qquad 1 \text{ rad} \approx 57.3^\circ. $$

Because $\Delta\theta$ is defined as an arc-length ratio, it is independent of the choice of radius: every point on a rotating rigid body sweeps the same angle in the same time interval, regardless of its distance from the axis.

Worked Example. A ceiling fan blade of length $r = 0.60\ \text{m}$ completes $3.5$ revolutions. Find (a) the rotation angle in radians and (b) the arc length traced by the blade tip.

(a) Converting revolutions to radians:

$$ \Delta\theta = 3.5 \times 2\pi = 7\pi \approx 21.99\ \text{rad}. $$

(b) Solving the defining relation for arc length:

$$ \Delta s = r\,\Delta\theta = (0.60\ \text{m})(7\pi) \approx 13.2\ \text{m}. $$

The blade tip travels over thirteen metres even though the fan itself is stationary — a direct consequence of the lever-arm amplification built into rotational kinematics.

Key Theorem (Arc–Angle Proportionality). For a rigid body rotating through angle $\Delta\theta$, every radial position $r$ yields a proportional arc length $\Delta s = r\,\Delta\theta$. Differentiating with respect to time at constant $r$ gives the relationship between the tangential speed $v_t$ and the angular velocity $\omega$:

$$ v_t = r\omega, $$

which links the rotational and translational descriptions of motion.

Record percent error and identify the dominant source of measurement uncertainty.

🍃 Angular Velocity

🍃 Angular Velocity

Definition. Consider a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis. As a reference point on the body sweeps through an arc, the rotation angle $\Delta\theta$ (measured in radians) changes with time. The average angular velocity over an interval $\Delta t$ is

$$ \bar{\omega} = \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta t}, $$

and the instantaneous angular velocity is the limit

$$ \omega = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\theta}{dt}. $$

The SI unit is radians per second ($\text{rad/s}$). Angular velocity is a signed scalar for planar rotation: positive values conventionally denote counterclockwise rotation when viewed from the positive $z$-axis, and negative values denote clockwise rotation. In three dimensions, $\omega$ generalizes to a vector $\vec{\omega}$ aligned with the rotation axis via the right-hand rule.

Worked Example. A centrifuge rotor completes 12,000 revolutions per minute (rpm). Convert this to $\text{rad/s}$ and find the instantaneous linear speed of a cell sample located $r = 8.0\ \text{cm}$ from the axis.

Step 1 — Convert to rad/s:

$$ \omega = 12{,}000\ \frac{\text{rev}}{\text{min}} \times \frac{2\pi\ \text{rad}}{1\ \text{rev}} \times \frac{1\ \text{min}}{60\ \text{s}} = \frac{12{,}000 \times 2\pi}{60}\ \text{rad/s} \approx 1{,}257\ \text{rad/s}. $$

Step 2 — Find linear speed using the relation $v = r\omega$:

$$ v = (0.080\ \text{m})(1{,}257\ \text{rad/s}) \approx 100.5\ \text{m/s}. $$

This is roughly $362\ \text{km/h}$, illustrating why centrifuge rotors must be precision-balanced.

Key Theorem — Relation Between Angular and Linear Kinematics. For a point at radius $r$ from the rotation axis, the magnitudes of the tangential velocity, tangential acceleration, and centripetal acceleration are related to the angular quantities by

$$ v = r\omega, \qquad a_t = r\alpha, \qquad a_c = r\omega^2, $$

where $\alpha = d\omega/dt$ is the angular acceleration. These three identities form the bridge between rotational and translational dynamics.

Lab Exercise. Mount a bicycle wheel on a fixed axle and affix a radial marker at $r = 0.30\ \text{m}$. Using a stroboscope, freeze the wheel at a known flicker frequency $f$. Measure the apparent angular displacement $\Delta\theta$ between successive flashes and compute $\omega = \Delta\theta / (1/f)$. Vary the wheel speed and verify that the linear speed of the rim, measured independently with a contact tachometer, satisfies $v = r\omega$ to within 5%.

🌱 Linear Velocity

🌱 Linear Velocity

Definition. When a rigid body undergoes circular motion, every point on the body traces an arc. The linear velocity (also called tangential velocity) of a point at radius $r$ from the axis of rotation is defined as the time rate of change of arc length:

$$ v = \frac{ds}{dt} $$

where $s$ is the arc length measured along the circular path. Because $s = r\theta$ (with $\theta$ in radians), differentiating with respect to time yields the fundamental relationship

$$ v = r\omega $$

where $\omega = d\theta/dt$ is the angular velocity in radians per second. This equation reveals that, for a fixed $\omega$, points farther from the axis move faster along their paths—a fact with immediate practical consequences in rotating machinery and celestial mechanics.

Worked Example. A vinyl record spins at $33\tfrac{1}{3}$ rpm. Find the linear velocity of a point located $12\text{ cm}$ from the center.

First, convert angular velocity to SI units:

$$ \omega = 33.\overline{3}\ \frac{\text{rev}}{\text{min}} \times \frac{2\pi\ \text{rad}}{\text{rev}} \times \frac{1\ \text{min}}{60\ \text{s}} = \frac{10\pi}{9}\ \text{rad/s} \approx 3.49\ \text{rad/s} $$

Then apply $v = r\omega$:

$$ v = (0.12\ \text{m})(3.49\ \text{rad/s}) \approx 0.419\ \text{m/s} $$

A point on the outer edge of a 30 cm record ($r = 0.15\ \text{m}$) would travel at $\approx 0.524\ \text{m/s}$—noticeably faster despite the identical angular velocity.

Key Theorem. For any two points on a rotating rigid body, their angular velocities are equal, but their linear velocities are proportional to their respective radii:

$$ \frac{v_1}{v_2} = \frac{r_1}{r_2} $$

This follows directly from $v = r\omega$ with $\omega$ constant across the body. The theorem underlies gear-ratio design: meshed gears share a common linear velocity at their contact point, so $r_1\omega_1 = r_2\omega_2$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a motorized turntable and attach reflective tape at radii $r = 5\ \text{cm}$, $10\ \text{cm}$, and $15\ \text{cm}$. Using a stroboscope or photogate, measure the period $T$ at each position and compute $v = 2\pi r/T$. Verify that all three points yield the same $\omega = 2\pi/T$, and confirm that the ratio $v_1 : v_2 : v_3 = 1 : 2 : 3$ as predicted by theory.

🌱 Acceleration

🌱 Acceleration

Definition. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time. For a particle whose velocity vector is $\mathbf{v}(t)$, the instantaneous acceleration is defined as

$$ \mathbf{a}(t) = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = \frac{d^2\mathbf{x}}{dt^2}, $$

where $\mathbf{x}(t)$ is the position vector. In one dimension, acceleration $a = dv/dt$ carries SI units of $\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$. Acceleration is a vector quantity: it possesses both magnitude and direction, and a body accelerates whenever its speed, direction of motion, or both change.

Worked Example. A car starts from rest and moves along a straight road. Its position is given by $x(t) = 3t^2 + 2t$ (in meters, with $t$ in seconds). Find the acceleration at $t = 4\,\mathrm{s}$.

First, differentiate to obtain the velocity:

$$ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t + 2. $$

Differentiating again gives the acceleration:

$$ a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = 6\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}. $$

Because the coefficient of $t^2$ is constant, the acceleration is uniform and equals $6\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$ at every instant, including $t = 4\,\mathrm{s}$. Note that the initial velocity is $v(0) = 2\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, so the car was not at rest despite $x(0) = 0$; always distinguish initial position from initial velocity.

Key Theorem (Kinematic Equations for Constant Acceleration). When acceleration $a$ is constant, integrating $a = dv/dt$ once yields $v = v_0 + at$, and integrating again gives $x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2$. Eliminating $t$ between these two relations produces the velocity–displacement relation

$$ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,(x - x_0). $$

These three equations are valid only when $a$ is strictly constant; for variable acceleration, the full calculus definition must be applied.

Assess how well the constant-acceleration model holds and identify sources of systematic error.

🍃 Tangential Speed

🍃 Tangential Speed

Definition. When a particle moves along a circular path of radius $r$, its tangential speed $v_t$ is the magnitude of the linear velocity vector at any instant, directed tangent to the circle at that point. Because the arc length $s$ traversed in time $t$ satisfies $s = r\theta$ (where $\theta$ is the rotation angle in radians), differentiating with respect to time yields

$$ v_t = \frac{ds}{dt} = r\frac{d\theta}{dt} = r\omega, $$

where $\omega$ is the angular velocity in radians per second. This relation shows that, for a rigid body rotating at a fixed $\omega$, points farther from the axis move faster in the linear sense, even though every point shares the same angular velocity.

Worked Example. A CD spins at $\omega = 22.4\ \text{rad/s}$. Find the tangential speed of a point on the outer rim at $r = 0.060\ \text{m}$ and of a point near the hub at $r = 0.025\ \text{m}$.

Outer rim:

$$ v_t = r\omega = (0.060\ \text{m})(22.4\ \text{rad/s}) = 1.34\ \text{m/s}. $$

Hub region:

$$ v_t = (0.025\ \text{m})(22.4\ \text{rad/s}) = 0.560\ \text{m/s}. $$

The outer point moves 2.4 times faster despite identical $\omega$, illustrating the linear dependence on radius.

Key Theorem. For uniform circular motion (constant $\omega$), tangential speed is constant in magnitude, yet the velocity vector continuously changes direction. The centripetal acceleration required to sustain this circular path is

$$ a_c = \frac{v_t^2}{r} = r\omega^2, $$

directed radially inward. This acceleration changes the direction of motion without altering $v_t$, which is why tangential speed remains constant even as the velocity vector rotates.

Lab Exercise. Attach a rubber stopper to a string and swing it in a horizontal circle. Measure the period $T$ for several string lengths $r$. Compute $\omega = 2\pi/T$ and then $v_t = r\omega$ for each trial. Plot $v_t$ versus $r$ at constant $T$ and verify the predicted linear relationship. Estimate the uncertainty in $r$ and $T$, and propagate those uncertainties to $v_t$.

🍃 Uniform Circular Motion

🍃 Uniform Circular Motion

Definition. Uniform circular motion describes the motion of an object traveling along a circular path at constant speed. Although the speed $v$ remains unchanged, the direction of the velocity vector continuously rotates, so the object undergoes nonzero acceleration. For an object moving in a circle of radius $r$, the velocity vector is always tangent to the path, and the acceleration vector points radially inward toward the center — this is called centripetal acceleration:

$$ a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} $$

The period $T$ is the time required to complete one full revolution. Since the circumference of the circle is $2\pi r$, the speed and period are related by:

$$ v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} $$

The centripetal force — the net inward force required to maintain circular motion — follows directly from Newton's second law, $F = ma$:

$$ F_c = m a_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} $$

This force is not a new type of force; it is always provided by some physical agent (tension, friction, gravity, normal force) directed toward the center.

Worked Example. A 1,200 kg car rounds a flat, horizontal curve of radius $r = 50\ \text{m}$ at a speed of $v = 15\ \text{m/s}$. Determine the minimum coefficient of static friction $\mu_s$ required to prevent skidding.

The centripetal force is supplied entirely by static friction on a flat road:

$$ f_s = \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{(1200)(15)^2}{50} = 5{,}400\ \text{N} $$

The maximum static friction force is $f_{s,\max} = \mu_s m g$. Setting $f_{s,\max} \geq f_s$:

$$ \mu_s \geq \frac{v^2}{rg} = \frac{225}{(50)(9.8)} \approx 0.46 $$

Key Theorem. For uniform circular motion, the work done by the centripetal force is identically zero. Because the centripetal acceleration is always perpendicular to the velocity, $\mathbf{F}_c \cdot d\mathbf{s} = 0$ at every instant. Therefore the kinetic energy — and hence the speed — remains constant, consistent with the definition.

Lab Exercise. Attach a rubber stopper of known mass $m$ to a string of length $r$ and swing it in a horizontal circle. Measure the period $T$ using a stopwatch over ten revolutions. Compute the experimental centripetal force $F_c = 4\pi^2 mr/T^2$ and compare it to the weight of the hanging counterweight that supplies the tension. Investigate how $F_c$ changes as $r$ is varied while keeping $T$ fixed.

🍃 Centripetal Acceleration

🍃 Centripetal Acceleration

Definition. When a particle moves along a circular path of radius $r$ with speed $v$, its velocity vector continuously changes direction even if its magnitude remains constant. This change in direction constitutes an acceleration directed toward the center of the circle. The centripetal acceleration is defined as

$$ a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} $$

Equivalently, since the angular velocity $\omega$ relates to linear speed by $v = r\omega$, one obtains

$$ a_c = r\omega^2 $$

The direction of $\vec{a}_c$ is always radially inward — perpendicular to the velocity vector and pointing toward the center of curvature. Because it is perpendicular to $\vec{v}$, centripetal acceleration changes the direction of motion without altering the speed.

Worked Example. A car of mass $1{,}200\ \text{kg}$ rounds a horizontal circular curve of radius $r = 80\ \text{m}$ at a constant speed of $v = 20\ \text{m/s}$. Find the centripetal acceleration and the net horizontal force required to maintain circular motion.

Applying the definition:

$$ a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{(20\ \text{m/s})^2}{80\ \text{m}} = \frac{400}{80}\ \text{m/s}^2 = 5.0\ \text{m/s}^2 $$

By Newton's second law, the net horizontal force (supplied here by static friction) is

$$ F_c = ma_c = (1{,}200\ \text{kg})(5.0\ \text{m/s}^2) = 6{,}000\ \text{N} $$

This force points toward the center of the curve at every instant.

Key Theorem. For uniform circular motion, the centripetal acceleration can be derived rigorously from kinematics. Consider position vectors $\vec{r}(t)$ on a circle; differentiating twice and using $|\vec{r}| = r$ yields $\vec{a} = -\omega^2 \vec{r}$, confirming both the magnitude $a_c = \omega^2 r = v^2/r$ and the inward direction. The result is exact for any uniform circular motion, regardless of the physical agent supplying the force.

Lab Exercise. Attach a rubber stopper of known mass $m$ to a string of length $L$ and whirl it in a horizontal circle above your head at measured frequency $f$. Record $f$ using a stroboscope, then compute $\omega = 2\pi f$, $v = L\omega$, and $a_c = v^2/L$. Compare the predicted centripetal force $ma_c$ against the tension measured by a spring scale threaded through a glass tube at the bottom of the string. Tabulate results for three different frequencies and assess the agreement.

🌸 Centrifuge

🌸 Centrifuge

Definition. A centrifuge is a device that rotates a sample at high angular velocity $\omega$ to exploit the effective separation force arising from circular motion. When an object of mass $m$ moves in a circle of radius $r$, it requires a centripetal force $F_c = m\omega^2 r$ directed inward. Inside the rotating frame, this manifests as an apparent outward (centrifugal) force of equal magnitude. Denser components of a mixture experience a greater net outward force per unit volume and therefore migrate radially outward faster than less dense components, achieving separation that gravity alone would require prohibitively long timescales to accomplish.

The effectiveness of a centrifuge is characterized by its relative centrifugal force (RCF), expressed as a multiple of gravitational acceleration $g$:

$$ \text{RCF} = \frac{\omega^2 r}{g} $$

where $\omega$ is in rad/s and $r$ is the radial distance of the sample from the rotation axis. RCF is also reported in units of $\times g$.

Worked Example. A microcentrifuge spins at $12{,}000\ \text{rpm}$ with samples held at $r = 0.080\ \text{m}$ from the axis. Find the RCF.

Convert rpm to rad/s:

$$ \omega = 12{,}000\ \frac{\text{rev}}{\text{min}} \times \frac{2\pi\ \text{rad}}{\text{rev}} \times \frac{1\ \text{min}}{60\ \text{s}} = 1257\ \text{rad/s} $$

Compute RCF:

$$ \text{RCF} = \frac{(1257)^2 (0.080)}{9.81} \approx \frac{1.580 \times 10^6 \times 0.080}{9.81} \approx 1.29 \times 10^4 $$

The sample experiences approximately $12{,}900\,g$, compressing sedimentation time from hours (under gravity alone) to seconds.

Key Theorem. The sedimentation velocity $v_s$ of a spherical particle is given by Stokes' law modified for centrifugal acceleration:

$$ v_s = \frac{2r_p^2 (\rho_p - \rho_f)\,\omega^2 r}{9\eta} $$

where $r_p$ is the particle radius, $\rho_p$ and $\rho_f$ are particle and fluid densities, and $\eta$ is fluid viscosity. Separation efficiency scales as $\omega^2$, making rotational speed the dominant design parameter.

Lab Exercise. Set up a centrifuge at two speeds, $3{,}000\ \text{rpm}$ and $6{,}000\ \text{rpm}$, using the same rotor radius. Calculate the RCF at each setting, determine the ratio of sedimentation velocities, and verify experimentally that doubling $\omega$ increases $v_s$ by a factor of four.

Payoff 🎯

The centrifuge stands as the natural culmination of uniform circular motion because it transforms the abstract mathematics of centripetal acceleration into a precise, controllable tool for separating matter by density. Throughout Chapter 6, each concept—rotation angle, angular velocity, centripetal acceleration, and the dynamics of circular motion—has built toward a single, unifying question: what happens when we engineer rotation to do useful work? The centrifuge answers that question with elegant economy.

What the Centrifuge Achieves

When a sample rotates at angular velocity $\omega$ at radius $r$, every particle of mass $m$ experiences an effective centrifugal force $F = m\omega^2 r$ in the rotating frame, or equivalently, requires a centripetal force of the same magnitude directed inward. Particles of different densities $\rho$ experience the same $\omega^2 r$, but their net motion through a viscous medium depends on the balance between this inertial effect and fluid drag. Denser particles migrate outward faster, achieving separation that gravity alone—at $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$—could not accomplish on any practical timescale. A modern ultracentrifuge operating at $\omega = 2\pi \times 10^5\ \text{rad/s}$ generates accelerations exceeding $10^6 g$, compressing hours of gravitational settling into seconds.

Connections to Application Domains

This mechanism unlocks a remarkable breadth of practice. In clinical medicine, blood centrifugation at roughly $3000\ \text{rpm}$ separates plasma, platelets, and red cells by density for transfusion and diagnostics. In molecular biology, ultracentrifugation resolves macromolecules such as DNA and ribosomes whose sedimentation coefficients are measured in Svedberg units $(\text{S} = 10^{-13}\ \text{s})$. In materials science, centrifugal casting distributes molten metal or composite fiber under controlled $\omega^2 r$ to produce graded-density structures impossible by static pouring. In environmental engineering, centrifuges dewater municipal sludge, reducing mass for landfill disposal. Each application is a direct implementation of $a_c = \omega^2 r$, scaled and tuned to a specific separation problem.
