🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. Mass is a fundamental scalar quantity that characterizes two distinct but experimentally equivalent properties of matter: inertial mass and gravitational mass. Inertial mass $m_i$ measures a body's resistance to changes in its state of motion, formalized through Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m_i \vec{a} $$

Gravitational mass $m_g$ quantifies the strength of a body's coupling to a gravitational field, appearing in Newton's law of universal gravitation:

$$ F_g = G \frac{m_{g,1} \, m_{g,2}}{r^2} $$

The equivalence principle asserts $m_i = m_g$ for all bodies, a result confirmed experimentally to better than one part in $10^{12}$ (Eötvös experiments). In the SI system, mass is measured in kilograms (kg) and is an intrinsic property independent of location, distinguishing it from weight, which is the gravitational force $W = mg$ acting on a body and varies with local gravitational acceleration $g$.

Worked Example. A block of unknown mass rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A horizontal net force of $F = 12.0\ \text{N}$ produces an acceleration of $a = 3.00\ \text{m/s}^2$. Find the mass.

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ m = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{a} = \frac{12.0\ \text{N}}{3.00\ \text{m/s}^2} = 4.00\ \text{kg} $$

Note that this result is independent of any gravitational field; the measurement isolates inertial mass through kinematics alone.

Key Theorem — Equivalence of Inertial and Gravitational Mass. For any body, $m_i = m_g$. A direct consequence is that all bodies in a uniform gravitational field experience the same free-fall acceleration $g$, regardless of composition or size:

$$ a = \frac{F_g}{m_i} = \frac{m_g \, g}{m_i} = g $$

This universality underlies Galileo's inclined-plane experiments and is the empirical cornerstone of general relativity.

🍃 Gravitational Constant

🍃 Gravitational Constant

Definition

The gravitational constant, denoted $G$, is the universal proportionality factor appearing in Newton's law of universal gravitation. For two point masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ separated by a distance $r$, the magnitude of the attractive gravitational force between them is

$$ F = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} $$

The accepted value of $G$ is

$$ G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \ \text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{kg}^{-2} $$

Unlike fundamental constants tied to the structure of quantum mechanics or electromagnetism, $G$ is purely empirical — it cannot be derived from other known constants and must be measured experimentally. Its extraordinarily small numerical value reflects the intrinsic weakness of gravity relative to the other fundamental forces.

Worked Example

Calculate the gravitational force between Earth ($M_E = 5.972 \times 10^{24}\ \text{kg}$) and a $70\ \text{kg}$ person standing on its surface, where Earth's radius is $R_E = 6.371 \times 10^6\ \text{m}$.

$$ F = G\frac{M_E \cdot m}{R_E^2} = (6.674 \times 10^{-11})\frac{(5.972 \times 10^{24})(70)}{(6.371 \times 10^6)^2} $$ $$ F = \frac{6.674 \times 10^{-11} \times 4.180 \times 10^{26}}{4.059 \times 10^{13}} \approx 686 \ \text{N} $$

This matches the familiar result $F = mg$ with $g \approx 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$, confirming internal consistency: surface gravitational acceleration is $g = GM_E/R_E^2$.

Key Theorem

From the equivalence $g = GM/R^2$, one can determine $G$ experimentally by measuring $g$, $M$, and $R$ independently. Henry Cavendish first measured $G$ in 1798 using a torsion balance, suspending two small lead spheres near two large ones and measuring the minute angular deflection caused by gravitational attraction. The experiment yielded a value within 1% of the modern accepted value, and the method remains the conceptual basis for precision measurements of $G$ today.

🌱 Radius Of Curvature

🌱 Radius Of Curvature

Definition. When a particle moves along a curved path, the trajectory at any point can be locally approximated by a circular arc called the osculating circle. The radius of curvature $R$ at that point is the radius of this best-fitting circle. For a path described by $y = f(x)$, the radius of curvature is given by

$$ R = \frac{\left[1 + \left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)^2\right]^{3/2}}{\left|\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}\right|}. $$

Because centripetal acceleration (a concept established in the chapter) satisfies $a_c = v^2/R$, the radius of curvature is the single geometric quantity linking any curved trajectory to the Newtonian force analysis: a smaller $R$ (tighter curve) demands a larger centripetal force for the same speed.

Worked Example. A car travels over a hill whose profile is approximated by $y = -0.01x^2$ (in meters, with the crest at $x = 0$). Find $R$ at the crest and determine the maximum speed at which a 1200 kg car maintains contact with the road.

At $x = 0$: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$ and $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -0.02\ \text{m}^{-1}$. Substituting:

$$ R = \frac{(1 + 0)^{3/2}}{|-0.02|} = \frac{1}{0.02} = 50\ \text{m}. $$

At the crest, the normal force $N$ and weight $mg$ provide the net centripetal force directed downward (toward the center of curvature). Newton's second law gives

$$ mg - N = \frac{mv^2}{R}. $$

Contact is lost when $N = 0$, so $v_{\max} = \sqrt{gR} = \sqrt{9.8 \times 50} \approx 22.1\ \text{m/s}$ ($\approx 79.5\ \text{km/h}$).

Key Theorem. For projectile motion, the radius of curvature at the apex (where $v_y = 0$) reduces to $R = v_{x}^{2}/g$, since only horizontal velocity persists and gravity acts as the centripetal acceleration. This result follows immediately from $a_c = v^2/R$ with $v = v_x$ and $a_c = g$.

🍃 Center Of Mass

🍃 Center Of Mass

Definition. The center of mass (COM) of a system of particles is the unique point $\mathbf{r}_{cm}$ at which the entire mass of the system may be considered concentrated for the purpose of analyzing translational motion. For a discrete system of $n$ particles with masses $m_i$ located at positions $\mathbf{r}_i$, the center of mass is defined by

$$ \mathbf{r}_{cm} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i \mathbf{r}_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i \mathbf{r}_i, $$

where $M = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i$ is the total mass. For a continuous mass distribution with linear, areal, or volume mass density, the sum becomes an integral:

$$ \mathbf{r}_{cm} = \frac{1}{M}\int \mathbf{r}\, dm. $$

In component form, the $x$-coordinate of the COM is $x_{cm} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_i m_i x_i$, with analogous expressions for $y_{cm}$ and $z_{cm}$.

Worked Example. Consider two blocks on a frictionless surface: block A has mass $m_A = 3.0\ \text{kg}$ at $x_A = 1.0\ \text{m}$, and block B has mass $m_B = 7.0\ \text{kg}$ at $x_B = 5.0\ \text{m}$. The total mass is $M = 10.0\ \text{kg}$. Applying the definition,

$$ x_{cm} = \frac{m_A x_A + m_B x_B}{M} = \frac{(3.0)(1.0) + (7.0)(5.0)}{10.0} = \frac{3.0 + 35.0}{10.0} = 3.8\ \text{m}. $$

The COM lies closer to the heavier block, as expected from the mass-weighted average.

Key Theorem. Newton's second law applied to the center of mass yields

$$ \mathbf{F}_{net,ext} = M\mathbf{a}_{cm}, $$

where $\mathbf{F}_{net,ext}$ is the vector sum of all external forces acting on the system. Internal forces between particles cancel in pairs by Newton's third law and do not affect the COM motion. Consequently, if $\mathbf{F}_{net,ext} = \mathbf{0}$, then $\mathbf{a}_{cm} = \mathbf{0}$ and the center of mass moves with constant velocity — or remains at rest — regardless of how the individual particles interact internally.

🍃 Newtons Law Of Gravitation

🍃 Newtons Law Of Gravitation

Definition. Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation states that every particle of mass $m_1$ attracts every other particle of mass $m_2$ with a force directed along the line joining them, with magnitude

$$ F = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} $$

where $r$ is the distance between the centers of mass and $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\ \mathrm{N \cdot m^2 \cdot kg^{-2}}$ is the universal gravitational constant. The force is always attractive and obeys Newton's third law: if body 1 exerts force $\vec{F}_{12}$ on body 2, then body 2 exerts an equal and opposite force $\vec{F}_{21} = -\vec{F}_{12}$ on body 1.

Worked Example. Determine the gravitational force between Earth ($M_E = 5.97 \times 10^{24}\ \mathrm{kg}$) and a $70\ \mathrm{kg}$ student standing on its surface ($R_E = 6.37 \times 10^{6}\ \mathrm{m}$).

$$ F = G\frac{M_E m}{R_E^2} = (6.674 \times 10^{-11})\frac{(5.97 \times 10^{24})(70)}{(6.37 \times 10^{6})^2} $$ $$ F = \frac{6.674 \times 10^{-11} \times 4.179 \times 10^{26}}{4.058 \times 10^{13}} \approx 686\ \mathrm{N} $$

This matches $mg = 70 \times 9.8 = 686\ \mathrm{N}$, confirming that the surface gravitational acceleration $g = GM_E/R_E^2$ is derived directly from Newton's law.

Key Theorem — Shell Theorem. For a spherically symmetric mass distribution, the gravitational force on an external particle is identical to that produced by concentrating all the mass at the center. Formally, integrating the force contributions from infinitesimal shells:

$$ F = G\frac{Mm}{r^2}, \quad r > R $$

and for a particle inside a uniform shell, the net gravitational force is zero, because contributions from opposite sides cancel exactly. These results, originally proved by Newton using geometric arguments and rederived cleanly via integration, justify treating planets and stars as point masses in orbital mechanics.

🌸 Tides

🌸 Tides

Definition. Tides are the periodic rise and fall of sea levels caused by the differential gravitational forces exerted by the Moon and, to a lesser extent, the Sun on the rotating Earth. Newton's law of universal gravitation states that the gravitational force between two masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ separated by distance $r$ is

$$ F = \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2} $$

Because the Moon's gravitational field is nonuniform across Earth's diameter, the side of Earth nearest the Moon experiences a stronger pull than the center, which in turn is pulled more strongly than the far side. This differential, or tidal force, stretches Earth's oceans into a prolate (elongated) shape, producing two tidal bulges simultaneously — one facing the Moon and one on the opposite side.

Worked Example. Estimate the tidal acceleration difference between Earth's near and far sides due to the Moon. Let $M_M = 7.35 \times 10^{22}$ kg be the lunar mass, $d = 3.84 \times 10^8$ m the Earth–Moon center-to-center distance, and $R_E = 6.37 \times 10^6$ m Earth's radius. The gravitational acceleration toward the Moon at Earth's center is

$$ g_c = \frac{GM_M}{d^2} $$

At the near side, the distance is $d - R_E$, so the differential tidal acceleration is

$$ \Delta g = \frac{GM_M}{(d-R_E)^2} - \frac{GM_M}{d^2} \approx \frac{2GM_M R_E}{d^3} $$

Substituting values: $\Delta g \approx \frac{2(6.674\times10^{-11})(7.35\times10^{22})(6.37\times10^6)}{(3.84\times10^8)^3} \approx 1.1 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{m/s}^2$, roughly one ten-millionth of surface gravity.

Key Theorem. The tidal force scales as $r^{-3}$ rather than $r^{-2}$, because it arises from the gradient of the gravitational field:

$$ F_{\text{tidal}} \approx \frac{2GM_{\text{source}} m R}{r^3} $$

where $R$ is the size of the body being deformed. This cubic dependence explains why the Moon dominates over the more massive but far more distant Sun: the Sun produces only about 46% of the Moon's tidal effect.

Payoff 🎯

What Tides Achieves

Tides represent the culminating synthesis of Chapter 6's central theme: that Newton's law of universal gravitation, $F = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$, operates not merely between point masses in isolation, but across extended bodies experiencing spatially varying gravitational fields. A tide arises precisely because the gravitational acceleration due to the Moon (or Sun) is not uniform across Earth's diameter. The near side of Earth is pulled more strongly than the center, and the center more strongly than the far side. These differential accelerations — the tidal forces — stretch Earth and its oceans along the Earth–Moon axis and compress them perpendicular to it.

Defining the tidal acceleration formally: if the Moon of mass $M$ is at distance $d$ from Earth's center, the tidal acceleration across a baseline $\Delta r$ aligned radially is

$$ a_\text{tidal} \approx \frac{2GM\,\Delta r}{d^3}. $$

This $d^{-3}$ dependence distinguishes tidal forces from the simple $d^{-2}$ gravitational force and explains why proximity matters far more for tidal effects than raw mass — the Sun exerts a stronger gravitational pull on Earth than the Moon does, yet the Moon drives larger ocean tides.

Worked Example

Consider a water column of height $h = 1.0\,\text{m}$ on Earth's surface at the sub-lunar point. Taking $M_\text{Moon} = 7.34 \times 10^{22}\,\text{kg}$, $d = 3.84 \times 10^8\,\text{m}$, and $\Delta r = h$:

$$ a_\text{tidal} = \frac{2(6.674\times10^{-11})(7.34\times10^{22})(1.0)}{(3.84\times10^8)^3} \approx 1.7\times10^{-6}\,\text{m/s}^2. $$

Multiplying by Earth's radius $R_\oplus = 6.37\times10^6\,\text{m}$ gives the effective tidal height scale — accounting for the full diameter baseline — of roughly 0.54 m, consistent with observed open-ocean tidal amplitudes before coastal resonance amplification.

Key Theorem

The Roche limit follows directly from tidal force analysis: a fluid satellite orbiting a body of mass $M$ and density $\rho_M$ will be tidally disrupted when its orbital radius $r$ satisfies

$$ r \lesssim 2.44\, R_M \left(\frac{\rho_M}{\rho_\text{sat}}\right)^{1/3}. $$

This result, derived by equating tidal forces to the satellite's self-gravity, predicts the inner boundary of Saturn's ring system with remarkable accuracy and unifies orbital mechanics with gravitational field theory.
