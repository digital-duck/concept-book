🍃 Rotation Angle

🍃 Rotation Angle

Definition. When a rigid body rotates about a fixed axis, the rotation angle $\Delta\theta$ is the ratio of the arc length $\Delta s$ traced by a reference point to the radial distance $r$ of that point from the axis:

$$ \Delta\theta = \frac{\Delta s}{r} $$

The SI unit is the radian (rad), a dimensionless ratio of two lengths. One full revolution corresponds to an arc length equal to the full circumference $2\pi r$, giving $\Delta\theta = 2\pi$ rad. The conversion between common units is

$$ 1 \text{ revolution} = 2\pi \text{ rad} = 360^\circ, \qquad 1 \text{ rad} \approx 57.3^\circ. $$

Because $\Delta\theta$ is defined as an arc-length ratio, it is independent of the choice of radius: every point on a rotating rigid body sweeps the same angle in the same time interval, regardless of its distance from the axis.

Worked Example. A ceiling fan blade of length $r = 0.60\ \text{m}$ completes $3.5$ revolutions. Find (a) the rotation angle in radians and (b) the arc length traced by the blade tip.

(a) Converting revolutions to radians:

$$ \Delta\theta = 3.5 \times 2\pi = 7\pi \approx 21.99\ \text{rad}. $$

(b) Solving the defining relation for arc length:

$$ \Delta s = r\,\Delta\theta = (0.60\ \text{m})(7\pi) \approx 13.2\ \text{m}. $$

The blade tip travels over thirteen metres even though the fan itself is stationary — a direct consequence of the lever-arm amplification built into rotational kinematics.

Key Theorem (Arc–Angle Proportionality). For a rigid body rotating through angle $\Delta\theta$, every radial position $r$ yields a proportional arc length $\Delta s = r\,\Delta\theta$. Differentiating with respect to time at constant $r$ gives the relationship between the tangential speed $v_t$ and the angular velocity $\omega$:

$$ v_t = r\omega, $$

which links the rotational and translational descriptions of motion.

Lab Exercise. Mount a protractor at the hub of a bicycle wheel. Mark two points on the rim at radii $r_1 = 0.20\ \text{m}$ and $r_2 = 0.30\ \text{m}$ from the axle. Rotate the wheel until the protractor reads $\Delta\theta = 1.50\ \text{rad}$. Measure the arc lengths $\Delta s_1$ and $\Delta s_2$ with a flexible tape, then verify that $\Delta s / r$ equals $1.50\ \text{rad}$ in both cases. Record percent error and identify the dominant source of measurement uncertainty.
