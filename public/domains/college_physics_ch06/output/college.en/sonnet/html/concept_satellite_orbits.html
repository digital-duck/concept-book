🌸 Satellite Orbits

A satellite in a circular orbit is an object moving under the sole influence of gravitational attraction toward a central body. For a satellite of mass $m$ orbiting a planet of mass $M$ at orbital radius $r$ (measured from the planet's center), Newton's second law equates the net centripetal force to gravitational attraction:

$$ \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2} $$

Solving for the orbital speed yields

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}} $$

This result is independent of the satellite's mass — a hallmark of gravitational orbits. The corresponding orbital period $T$ follows from $v = 2\pi r / T$:

$$ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{r^3}{GM}} $$

This is Kepler's Third Law for circular orbits: $T^2 \propto r^3$.

Worked Example. Determine the orbital speed and period of the International Space Station, which orbits Earth at an altitude of $h = 408\ \text{km}$ above the surface. Take $M_E = 5.97 \times 10^{24}\ \text{kg}$, $R_E = 6.371 \times 10^6\ \text{m}$, and $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/kg}^2$.

The orbital radius is $r = R_E + h = 6.779 \times 10^6\ \text{m}$. Then:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{(6.674\times10^{-11})(5.97\times10^{24})}{6.779\times10^6}} \approx 7.66\ \text{km/s} $$ $$ T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = \frac{2\pi(6.779\times10^6)}{7660} \approx 5560\ \text{s} \approx 92.7\ \text{min} $$

This matches the observed ISS orbital period closely.

Key Theorem — Vis-viva Equation. For an elliptical orbit with semi-major axis $a$, the speed at any point a distance $r$ from the central body satisfies:

$$ v^2 = GM\left(\frac{2}{r} - \frac{1}{a}\right) $$

For a circular orbit, $r = a$ everywhere, recovering $v^2 = GM/r$. This equation underlies all orbital transfer calculations, including the Hohmann transfer maneuver used to move satellites between orbits.

Lab Exercise. Using publicly available two-line element (TLE) data for any Earth satellite, extract the mean motion $n$ (in revolutions per day) and compute the semi-major axis via $a = \left(GM_E / n^2\right)^{1/3}$, accounting for unit conversions. Compare your result to the published orbital altitude and assess the percent discrepancy. Identify one physical effect neglected by the purely Newtonian model that explains any residual error.
