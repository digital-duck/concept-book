🍃 Converging Lens

🍃 Converging Lens

Definition. A converging lens (also called a convex lens) is a refracting optical element—typically made of glass or polycarbonate—whose surfaces curve outward such that parallel incident rays are refracted to meet at a single point on the far side. The fundamental quantitative relationship governing image formation is the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} $$

where $d_o > 0$ is the object distance (measured from the lens to the object), $d_i$ is the image distance (positive for a real image on the far side, negative for a virtual image on the same side as the object), and $f > 0$ is the focal length—the distance from the lens to the point where parallel rays converge, called the principal focus $F$. The lateral magnification is defined as:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} $$

A negative $m$ indicates an inverted image; $|m| > 1$ indicates magnification.

Worked Example. An object stands $30\,\text{cm}$ in front of a converging lens with focal length $f = 10\,\text{cm}$. Find the image distance and magnification.

Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{10} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3-1}{30} = \frac{2}{30} $$ $$ d_i = 15\,\text{cm} $$

The image forms $15\,\text{cm}$ beyond the lens (real and inverted). The magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{15}{30} = -0.5 $$

The image is inverted and reduced to half the object's size.

Key Theorem (Lensmaker's Equation). For a thin lens with radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$ and refractive index $n$:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) $$

By convention, $R > 0$ if the center of curvature lies on the transmission side. This equation establishes that lens geometry and material together determine $f$, providing the physical basis for the thin-lens equation.

Lab Exercise. Place a converging lens ($f \approx 15\,\text{cm}$) on an optical bench. Position a luminous object at $d_o = 45\,\text{cm}$, $30\,\text{cm}$, and $20\,\text{cm}$ in turn. For each position, locate the sharpest image on a screen and record $d_i$. Compute $1/d_o + 1/d_i$ for each trial and verify that the sum remains constant and equal to $1/f$. Note how image orientation and size change as $d_o$ approaches $f$.
