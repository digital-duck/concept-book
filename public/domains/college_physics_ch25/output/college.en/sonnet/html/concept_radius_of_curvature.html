🍃 Radius Of Curvature

🍃 Radius Of Curvature

Definition

The radius of curvature at a point on a curved path is the radius of the circle that best approximates the curve at that point — the so-called osculating circle. For a ray of light or a wavefront bending through an optical system, or for a particle moving along a curved trajectory, the radius of curvature $R$ quantifies how sharply the path bends. Formally, if a curve is described by $y = f(x)$, then

$$ R = \frac{\left(1 + \left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)^2\right)^{3/2}}{\left|\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}\right|}. $$

In optics, every refracting or reflecting surface possesses a radius of curvature that appears directly in the lensmaker's equation and the mirror equation, governing how surfaces focus or diverge light.

Worked Example

Consider a concave spherical mirror with a radius of curvature $R = 40\,\text{cm}$. The focal length is

$$ f = \frac{R}{2} = 20\,\text{cm}. $$

An object is placed $30\,\text{cm}$ in front of the mirror. Using the mirror equation,

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}, $$ $$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{20} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3-2}{60} = \frac{1}{60}, $$

so $d_i = 60\,\text{cm}$. The image forms $60\,\text{cm}$ in front of the mirror (real and inverted), with magnification $m = -d_i/d_o = -2$.

Key Theorem

For a thin lens with surfaces of radii $R_1$ and $R_2$ and refractive index $n$, the lensmaker's equation states:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n-1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right). $$

The sign convention (positive $R$ if the center of curvature lies to the right of the surface) is essential: it encodes whether each surface converges or diverges the incident wavefront. This theorem connects the geometry of the lens to its optical power $P = 1/f$.

Lab Exercise

Mount a concave mirror on an optical bench. Locate the center of curvature by finding the object distance at which the reflected image coincides with the object itself (no magnification, $d_i = d_o = R$). Measure this distance and verify that $f = R/2$ using the mirror equation with a second object distance. Estimate the uncertainty in $R$ and propagate it to $f$.
