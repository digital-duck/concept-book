🍃 Total Internal Reflection

🍃 Total Internal Reflection

Definition. When light travels from a medium with higher refractive index $n_1$ to one with lower refractive index $n_2 < n_1$, Snell's law requires the refracted ray to bend away from the normal. As the angle of incidence $\theta_i$ increases, the refracted angle $\theta_r$ approaches $90°$. The critical angle $\theta_c$ is defined as the angle of incidence for which $\theta_r = 90°$. Applying Snell's law at this boundary condition:

$$ n_1 \sin\theta_c = n_2 \sin 90° = n_2 $$ $$ \theta_c = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right) $$

For any $\theta_i > \theta_c$, no refracted ray exists and all incident light is reflected back into the denser medium — a phenomenon called total internal reflection (TIR). This is not partial reflection but complete reflection; the reflectance equals unity, and no energy is transmitted across the interface.

Worked Example. A ray of light travels through crown glass ($n_1 = 1.52$) toward a glass–air interface ($n_2 = 1.00$). Find the critical angle and determine whether a ray incident at $\theta_i = 45°$ undergoes TIR.

$$ \theta_c = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{1.00}{1.52}\right) = \arcsin(0.6579) \approx 41.1° $$

Since $\theta_i = 45° > \theta_c \approx 41.1°$, the ray undergoes total internal reflection. No light enters the air; the reflected ray obeys $\theta_{\text{reflected}} = 45°$ measured from the normal on the glass side.

Key Theorem. TIR requires two conditions simultaneously: (1) the ray must propagate from the optically denser medium ($n_1 > n_2$), and (2) $\theta_i > \theta_c$. The phenomenon is exploited in optical fibers, where a glass core ($n \approx 1.48$) is clad with material of lower index, trapping light by successive TIR along the fiber's length regardless of bends, provided the bend radius does not reduce the local angle of incidence below $\theta_c$.

Lab Exercise. Fill a semicircular acrylic block ($n = 1.49$) tank with water ($n = 1.33$) on the flat side. Aim a laser at the curved face (so it enters without refraction) and slowly increase $\theta_i$ on the flat face. Record the angle at which the transmitted beam disappears. Compare your measured critical angle with the theoretical value $\theta_c = \arcsin(1.33/1.49) \approx 63.2°$, and discuss any discrepancy in terms of surface imperfections and beam divergence.
