🍃 Focal Point

🍃 Focal Point

Definition. A focal point (or focus) of a spherical mirror or thin lens is the point on the optical axis at which rays parallel to that axis converge—or from which they appear to diverge—after reflection or refraction. For a converging (concave) mirror or a converging (convex) lens, the focal point $F$ is real: parallel incident rays actually pass through it. For a diverging (convex) mirror or a diverging (concave) lens, the focal point is virtual: the reflected or refracted rays diverge as though they originated from $F$, but no light physically passes through it. The distance from the optical center (or mirror vertex) to the focal point is the focal length $f$, defined as positive for converging optics and negative for diverging optics.

For a spherical mirror of radius of curvature $R$, the paraxial approximation yields

$$ f = \frac{R}{2}. $$

For a thin lens with surfaces of radii $R_1$ and $R_2$ immersed in a medium of refractive index $n_1$, surrounded by a lens of index $n_2$, the lensmaker's equation gives

$$ \frac{1}{f} = \left(\frac{n_2}{n_1} - 1\right)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right). $$

Worked Example. A biconvex glass lens ($n = 1.50$) has radii $R_1 = +20.0\,\text{cm}$ and $R_2 = -20.0\,\text{cm}$. Find $f$.

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (1.50 - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{20.0} - \frac{1}{-20.0}\right) = 0.50 \times \frac{2}{20.0} = 0.050\,\text{cm}^{-1}. $$

Therefore $f = 20.0\,\text{cm}$. Parallel rays striking this lens will converge at a real focal point $20.0\,\text{cm}$ beyond the lens.

Key Theorem (Mirror and Thin-Lens Equation). For an object at distance $d_o$ from the optic, the image forms at distance $d_i$ satisfying

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}. $$

As $d_o \to \infty$, $d_i \to f$, confirming that parallel rays (from an infinitely distant source) converge precisely at the focal point.

Lab Exercise. Mount a converging lens on an optical bench. Direct a distant light source along the optical axis and slide a white screen until the sharpest point of light appears. Measure the screen-to-lens distance; this is $f$. Repeat using the lensmaker's equation with calipers to measure $R_1$ and $R_2$, then compare the predicted and measured focal lengths. Record the percent discrepancy and identify sources of systematic error such as lens thickness and paraxial-approximation breakdown at large aperture.
