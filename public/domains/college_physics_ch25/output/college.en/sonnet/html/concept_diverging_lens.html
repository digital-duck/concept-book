🍃 Diverging Lens

🍃 Diverging Lens

Definition. A diverging lens (also called a concave lens) is an optical element, typically made of glass or transparent polymer, that is thinner at its center than at its edges. By refraction, it causes an incident parallel beam of light to spread outward as if the rays originated from a single point on the same side as the incoming light. This point is the lens's focal point $F$, located at a negative focal length $f < 0$ from the lens. The governing equation relating object distance $d_o$, image distance $d_i$, and focal length is the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} $$

For a diverging lens, $f < 0$ by convention. The lateral magnification is $m = -d_i / d_o$.

Worked Example. A diverging lens has focal length $f = -15\,\text{cm}$. An object is placed $30\,\text{cm}$ in front of the lens. Find the image distance and magnification.

Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{-15} - \frac{1}{30} = -\frac{2}{30} - \frac{1}{30} = -\frac{3}{30} = -\frac{1}{10} $$

Thus $d_i = -10\,\text{cm}$. The negative sign confirms the image forms on the same side as the object — it is virtual. The magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{-10}{30} = +\frac{1}{3} $$

The image is upright ($m > 0$) and reduced to one-third of the object's size.

Key Theorem. A diverging lens always produces a virtual, upright, and diminished image for any real object. This follows directly from the thin-lens equation: since $f < 0$ and $d_o > 0$, the equation $1/d_i = 1/f - 1/d_o$ yields $d_i < 0$ for all finite positive $d_o$, and one can show that $|d_i| < d_o$, hence $|m| < 1$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a diverging lens of unknown focal length on an optical bench. Place a luminous object at $d_o = 40\,\text{cm}$. Use a second converging lens ($f_c = +10\,\text{cm}$) to intercept the diverging rays and form a real image on a screen. Trace the diverging rays back geometrically to locate the virtual image, then apply the thin-lens equation to calculate $f$. Compare your result to the manufacturer's specification and compute the percent error.
