🌸 Concave Mirror Heater

🌸 Concave Mirror Heater

Definition. A concave mirror heater is a practical optical device that exploits the focusing property of a concave (converging) mirror to concentrate parallel rays of incoming radiation—typically sunlight—onto a small focal region, producing elevated temperatures sufficient for heating or ignition. A concave mirror with radius of curvature $R$ has a focal length

$$ f = \frac{R}{2}, $$

and by the mirror equation

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}, $$

parallel rays from a distant source (effectively $d_o \to \infty$) converge at $d_i = f$. The concentrated irradiance at the focal point follows from energy conservation: all radiation intercepted by the mirror's aperture area $A_{\text{mirror}}$ is deposited onto the much smaller focal spot of area $A_{\text{spot}}$, yielding an intensity amplification

$$ G = \frac{A_{\text{mirror}}}{A_{\text{spot}}}. $$

Worked Example. A parabolic concave mirror has diameter $D = 0.80\ \text{m}$ and focal length $f = 0.50\ \text{m}$. Solar irradiance is $I_0 = 1000\ \text{W/m}^2$. Find the power delivered to the focal spot and the intensity amplification if the spot diameter is $d = 2.0\ \text{cm}$.

Power intercepted:

$$ P = I_0 \cdot A_{\text{mirror}} = 1000 \times \frac{\pi (0.40)^2}{1} \approx 503\ \text{W}. $$

Spot area:

$$ A_{\text{spot}} = \pi (0.010)^2 \approx 3.14 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2. $$

Amplification:

$$ G = \frac{\pi(0.40)^2}{\pi(0.010)^2} = \frac{0.1600}{1.00\times10^{-4}} = 1600. $$

The focal intensity reaches $G \cdot I_0 = 1.6 \times 10^6\ \text{W/m}^2$, well above the threshold for igniting paper ($\approx 10^4\ \text{W/m}^2$).

Key Theorem (Focusing Condition). For a parabolic mirror, all rays traveling parallel to the optical axis reflect through a single focal point $F$ without spherical aberration. Spherical mirrors achieve this only paraxially (small angle approximation $\sin\theta \approx \theta$); parabolic geometry satisfies the condition exactly for all ray heights.

Lab Exercise. Using a concave mirror of known $f$, aim it at the sun and locate the focal spot on a white card. Measure the spot diameter $d$ at minimum size, record the mirror diameter $D$, and compute the amplification factor $G$. Compare your measured $G$ with the theoretical value $G = (D/d)^2$. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including mirror surface imperfections, non-parallel solar rays (solar angular diameter $\approx 0.53°$), and absorption losses.
