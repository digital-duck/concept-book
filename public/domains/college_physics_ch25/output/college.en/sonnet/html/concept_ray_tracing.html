🍃 Ray Tracing

🍃 Ray Tracing

Definition. Ray tracing is a geometric method for analyzing the propagation of light through optical systems by representing light as rays—idealized one-dimensional lines that travel in straight paths through homogeneous media and change direction only at interfaces according to the law of reflection ($\theta_i = \theta_r$) or Snell's law of refraction ($n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$). For a thin converging lens, three principal rays are sufficient to locate any image: (1) the ray parallel to the optical axis, which refracts through the far focal point $F'$; (2) the ray directed through the near focal point $F$, which emerges parallel to the axis; and (3) the ray passing through the optical center, which continues undeviated. The intersection of any two of these rays determines the image position.

Worked Example. An object of height $h_o = 4\text{ cm}$ is placed $d_o = 30\text{ cm}$ in front of a thin converging lens with focal length $f = 10\text{ cm}$. To locate the image, apply the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} $$ $$ \frac{1}{30} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{10} \implies d_i = 15\text{ cm} $$

The image forms $15\text{ cm}$ beyond the lens (real, on the transmission side). The lateral magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{15}{30} = -0.5 $$

The negative sign confirms the image is inverted; its height is $h_i = m\, h_o = -2\text{ cm}$.

Key Theorem (Conjugate Points). For any thin lens of focal length $f$, every object point at distance $d_o$ maps to a unique conjugate image point at $d_i$ satisfying the thin-lens equation. When $d_o > f$, the image is real and inverted; when $d_o < f$, the image is virtual, upright, and magnified. At $d_o = 2f$, image and object are equidistant from the lens and the magnification equals $-1$.

Lab Exercise. Using an optical bench, a converging lens ($f \approx 15\text{ cm}$), a backlit object slide, and a projection screen, place the object at $d_o = 40\text{ cm}$. Predict $d_i$ and $m$ analytically. Move the screen until a sharp image forms; measure $d_i$ and $h_i$. Compare your measurements to predictions and compute the percent error. Repeat for $d_o = 20\text{ cm}$ and $d_o = 12\text{ cm}$, recording whether each image is real or virtual.
