🍃 Image Distance

🍃 Image Distance

Definition. In geometric optics, the image distance $d_i$ is the perpendicular distance from the refracting or reflecting surface (or the principal plane of a lens) to the point where light rays converge to form a real image, or from which they appear to diverge to form a virtual image. By the standard sign convention, $d_i$ is positive when the image forms on the transmission side of a lens (or the reflection side of a mirror), and negative when the image is virtual—located on the same side as the incoming light.

The image distance is governed by the thin-lens equation (equivalently, the mirror equation):

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} $$

where $d_o$ is the object distance and $f$ is the focal length of the optical element. Solving explicitly for the image distance:

$$ d_i = \frac{f \, d_o}{d_o - f} $$

Worked Example. A converging lens has focal length $f = 15\,\text{cm}$. An object is placed $d_o = 45\,\text{cm}$ in front of the lens. Find $d_i$.

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{15} - \frac{1}{45} = \frac{3}{45} - \frac{1}{45} = \frac{2}{45} $$ $$ d_i = \frac{45}{2} = 22.5\,\text{cm} $$

The positive value confirms a real image forms $22.5\,\text{cm}$ beyond the lens on the far side. The lateral magnification is $m = -d_i/d_o = -22.5/45 = -0.5$, indicating an inverted image half the size of the object.

Key Theorem. When $d_o > f$ for a converging lens, $d_i > 0$ (real image); when $d_o < f$, $d_i < 0$ (virtual image). As $d_o \to f^+$, $d_i \to +\infty$, corresponding to parallel rays emerging from the lens—the basis of collimation in optical instruments.

Lab Exercise. Mount a converging lens ($f \approx 10\,\text{cm}$) on an optical bench. Place a luminous object at $d_o = 30\,\text{cm}$, $20\,\text{cm}$, and $12\,\text{cm}$. For each position, locate the sharpest image on a screen and record $d_i$. Verify each measurement against the thin-lens equation, and note how image distance and image orientation change as $d_o$ approaches $f$.
