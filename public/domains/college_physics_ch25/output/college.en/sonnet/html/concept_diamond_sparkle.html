🌸 Diamond Sparkle

🌸 Diamond Sparkle

The characteristic brilliance and spectral display of a diamond arises from two optical phenomena acting in concert: total internal reflection and dispersion. Together, these effects trap light within the stone and then separate it into its constituent wavelengths, producing the flashes of color — termed fire — that make diamonds visually distinctive.

Definition. Total internal reflection occurs when light traveling in a medium of refractive index $n_1$ strikes an interface with a medium of lower refractive index $n_2 < n_1$ at an angle of incidence $\theta_i$ exceeding the critical angle $\theta_c$, defined by

$$ \theta_c = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right). $$

For diamond in air, $n_{\text{diamond}} \approx 2.42$ and $n_{\text{air}} = 1.00$, giving

$$ \theta_c = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{1.00}{2.42}\right) \approx 24.4°. $$

This exceptionally small critical angle means that virtually any ray entering the crown facets is internally reflected multiple times before exiting, maximizing luminosity. Dispersion further separates colors because $n$ varies with wavelength: for diamond, $n$ ranges from approximately $2.410$ (red, $\lambda = 700\,\text{nm}$) to $2.450$ (violet, $\lambda = 400\,\text{nm}$), spreading refracted rays by several degrees.

Worked Example. A ray of white light enters a diamond facet at an internal angle of $\theta_i = 30°$. Compare this to $\theta_c \approx 24.4°$. Because $30° > 24.4°$, total internal reflection occurs for all visible wavelengths — no light escapes at this surface, and the ray continues to the next facet. When it eventually reaches a face at $\theta_i = 15° < \theta_c$, the ray refracts out. Using Snell's law for red light: $2.410\sin(15°) = \sin\theta_r$, so $\theta_r \approx 38.6°$. For violet: $2.450\sin(15°) = \sin\theta_v$, giving $\theta_v \approx 39.3°$. The angular spread of $\Delta\theta \approx 0.7°$ is visible as spectral color separation (fire).

Key Theorem (Snell's Law at the Critical Angle). At $\theta_i = \theta_c$, the refracted ray grazes the interface ($\theta_t = 90°$), and for $\theta_i > \theta_c$, no transmitted ray exists. The reflectance is then unity for both polarizations.

Lab Exercise. Using a semicircular acrylic block ($n = 1.49$), direct a laser beam from the flat face toward the curved surface. Increase $\theta_i$ in $2°$ increments and record transmitted intensity. Plot intensity versus angle to experimentally locate $\theta_c$ and compare with the theoretical value $\arcsin(1/1.49) \approx 42.2°$.
