🌸 Corrective Eyeglasses

🌸 Corrective Eyeglasses

Definition. Corrective eyeglasses compensate for refractive errors of the eye by placing a thin lens in front of the eye such that the combined optical system—lens plus eye—forms a clear image on the retina. The human eye focuses light using a converging lens whose focal length is adjusted by the ciliary muscles; when this adjustment range is insufficient, the far point or near point falls outside the comfortable viewing range. The eyeglass lens shifts the object's virtual image to a position the defective eye can focus upon. The lens power is expressed in diopters: $P = \frac{1}{f}$, where $f$ is the focal length in meters.

Worked Example. A nearsighted (myopic) person has a far point of $d_\text{far} = 0.50\ \text{m}$, meaning they cannot focus on objects beyond 50 cm. To correct for distant vision, the eyeglass lens must form a virtual image of an infinitely distant object at the far point.

Using the thin-lens equation,

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}, $$

set $d_o = \infty$ and $d_i = -0.50\ \text{m}$ (negative because the virtual image is on the same side as the object):

$$ \frac{1}{\infty} + \frac{1}{-0.50} = \frac{1}{f} \implies f = -0.50\ \text{m}. $$

The required lens power is $P = \frac{1}{-0.50} = -2.0\ \text{D}$, a diverging lens.

Key Theorem. For farsightedness (hyperopia), the near point $d_\text{near}$ exceeds the standard 25 cm near point. The corrective lens must be converging, forming a virtual image at $d_i = -d_\text{near}$ when the object is at $d_o = 0.25\ \text{m}$:

$$ P = \frac{1}{0.25} + \frac{1}{-d_\text{near}} = 4.0 - \frac{1}{d_\text{near}}\ [\text{D}]. $$

This expression shows that stronger positive power is needed as $d_\text{near}$ increases.

Lab Exercise. Using a ray-tracing setup with a converging lens (the model eye) and a screen (the retina), introduce a second lens to simulate myopia by moving the screen back. Measure the far point, then compute the required corrective lens power. Insert a diverging lens of that power and verify the screen again receives a sharp image from a distant source. Record how image sharpness varies with small deviations from the calculated power, quantifying the tolerance in diopters.
