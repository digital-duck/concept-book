🍃 Thin Lens Equations

🍃 Thin Lens Equations

Definition. A thin lens is an optical element whose thickness is negligible compared to its focal length, allowing both refracting surfaces to be treated as coincident at the lens plane. For a thin lens in air, the relationship between object distance $d_o$, image distance $d_i$, and focal length $f$ is given by the thin lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} $$

The focal length $f$ is determined by the lensmaker's equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) $$

where $n$ is the index of refraction of the lens material, and $R_1$, $R_2$ are the radii of curvature of the two surfaces (positive if the center of curvature lies to the right of the surface). The lateral magnification $m$ relates image height $h_i$ to object height $h_o$:

$$ m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} $$

A negative magnification indicates an inverted image; $|m| > 1$ denotes magnification, $|m| < 1$ denotes reduction.

Worked Example. A converging lens has focal length $f = 20\ \text{cm}$. An object stands $d_o = 30\ \text{cm}$ to the left of the lens. Find the image distance and magnification.

Applying the thin lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{20} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3 - 2}{60} = \frac{1}{60} $$

Thus $d_i = 60\ \text{cm}$. The image forms $60\ \text{cm}$ to the right of the lens — a real, inverted image. The magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{60}{30} = -2 $$

The image is inverted and twice the height of the object.

Key Theorem. For a thin converging lens ($f > 0$), a real image ($d_i > 0$) forms when $d_o > f$, a virtual image ($d_i < 0$) forms when $d_o < f$, and no image forms when $d_o = f$ (parallel outgoing rays). For a diverging lens ($f < 0$), all images are virtual, upright, and reduced.

Lab Exercise. Place a converging lens of unknown focal length on an optical bench. Position a luminous object at three distances ($d_o = 15, 25, 40\ \text{cm}$), locate the sharpest image on a screen, record $d_i$ for each trial, and compute $f$ from each measurement. Average the results and compare to the manufacturer's specification, assessing the primary source of uncertainty.
