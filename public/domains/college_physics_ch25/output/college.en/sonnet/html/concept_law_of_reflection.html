🍃 Law Of Reflection

🍃 Law Of Reflection

Definition. When a ray of light strikes a smooth, flat surface, it reflects in a predictable, deterministic manner described by the law of reflection: the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, where both angles are measured from the normal to the surface at the point of incidence. Formally, if $\theta_i$ denotes the angle between the incident ray and the normal, and $\theta_r$ denotes the angle between the reflected ray and the normal, then

$$ \theta_i = \theta_r. $$

Both rays and the normal lie in the same plane, called the plane of incidence. This law applies universally to specular (mirror-like) reflection and forms the geometric foundation of optical instrument design, including mirrors, periscopes, and laser cavities.

Worked Example. A light ray traveling in air strikes a flat mirror, making an angle of $35°$ with the mirror's surface. Find the angle of reflection.

The angle with the surface is the grazing angle, not the angle of incidence. The normal is perpendicular to the surface, so

$$ \theta_i = 90° - 35° = 55°. $$

By the law of reflection,

$$ \theta_r = \theta_i = 55°. $$

The reflected ray therefore makes an angle of $55°$ with the normal, or equivalently $35°$ with the mirror surface. This symmetry means that if you tilt the mirror by an angle $\alpha$, the reflected beam rotates by $2\alpha$ — a fact exploited in galvanometer mirrors and laser scanning systems.

Key Theorem — Rotation of Reflected Ray. If a plane mirror rotates by angle $\alpha$ about an axis in its surface while the incident ray remains fixed, the reflected ray rotates by $2\alpha$. Proof sketch: rotating the mirror rotates the normal by $\alpha$, which shifts both $\theta_i$ and $\theta_r$ by $\alpha$ on opposite sides of the new normal, giving a net $2\alpha$ rotation of the reflected ray relative to the original direction.

Lab Exercise. Place a plane mirror on a protractor and direct a laser pointer at the mirror's center at several measured incidence angles ($20°$, $40°$, $60°$, $80°$). Record the position of the reflected spot on a distant screen and compute $\theta_r$ for each trial. Tabulate $\theta_i$ vs. $\theta_r$ and verify the linear relationship $\theta_r = \theta_i$ using a least-squares fit. Determine the slope of the best-fit line and assess how closely it approaches unity.
