🌸 Endoscope

🌸 Endoscope

Definition. An endoscope is an optical instrument that transmits images through a flexible bundle of optical fibers by exploiting the principle of total internal reflection. Each fiber is a thin glass or plastic strand with a high-index core (refractive index $n_1$) surrounded by a low-index cladding ($n_2 < n_1$). When light strikes the core–cladding interface at an angle of incidence $\theta_i$ greater than or equal to the critical angle $\theta_c$, no light escapes into the cladding and the ray propagates along the fiber with negligible loss. The critical angle is given by

$$ \theta_c = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right). $$

A clinical endoscope contains two fiber bundles: an illumination bundle that carries light from an external source into a body cavity, and a coherent imaging bundle whose fibers maintain their relative spatial arrangement end-to-end, thereby faithfully reconstructing a two-dimensional image at the eyepiece or camera.

Worked Example. A glass fiber has core index $n_1 = 1.62$ and cladding index $n_2 = 1.48$. Find the critical angle and determine whether a ray striking the interface at $\theta_i = 65°$ undergoes total internal reflection.

$$ \theta_c = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{1.48}{1.62}\right) = \arcsin(0.9136) \approx 66.0°. $$

Because $\theta_i = 65° < \theta_c = 66°$, the condition for total internal reflection is not satisfied; the ray partially refracts into the cladding and signal is lost. A ray at $70°$ would satisfy $\theta_i > \theta_c$ and be fully guided.

Key Theorem (Numerical Aperture). The maximum half-angle $\theta_{\max}$ of the acceptance cone — the range of entry angles for which a ray will undergo total internal reflection inside the fiber — satisfies

$$ \mathrm{NA} = \sin\theta_{\max} = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}. $$

A larger NA captures a wider cone of input light, improving brightness at the cost of increased ray dispersion and reduced image sharpness over long fibers.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a plastic optical fiber and a laser pointer. Measure the output intensity as a function of bend radius by looping the fiber into circles of decreasing diameter. Record the bend radius at which transmitted intensity drops by 50%, and use the NA formula to estimate whether macrobending or evanescent leakage is the dominant loss mechanism.
