🍃 Virtual Image

🍃 Virtual Image

Definition. A virtual image is formed at a location from which light rays appear to diverge, but do not actually pass through. Unlike a real image, which is produced by the convergence of actual light rays at a point, a virtual image cannot be projected onto a screen. It is perceived by an observer because the eye traces the diverging rays backward along straight lines to a common apparent source. Mathematically, a virtual image corresponds to a negative image distance $d_i$ in the thin-lens or mirror equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} $$

where $d_o$ is the object distance (positive when the object is on the incoming side), $f$ is the focal length, and a negative $d_i$ indicates that the image forms on the same side as the object (for a lens) or in front of the mirror (for a concave or convex mirror).

Worked Example. A diverging lens with focal length $f = -20\ \text{cm}$ has an object placed $30\ \text{cm}$ in front of it. Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{-20} - \frac{1}{30} = -\frac{3}{60} - \frac{2}{60} = -\frac{5}{60} $$ $$ d_i = -12\ \text{cm} $$

The negative sign confirms a virtual image located $12\ \text{cm}$ on the same side as the object. The lateral magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{-12}{30} = +0.4 $$

The image is upright (positive $m$) and reduced in size, both characteristic of virtual images formed by diverging lenses.

Key Theorem. For a single refracting surface or thin lens, any object placed inside the focal length of a converging lens produces a virtual, upright, and magnified image. For a diverging lens or convex mirror, all real objects produce virtual images regardless of object position.

Lab Exercise. Using a converging lens of known focal length ($f \approx 15\ \text{cm}$), place a luminous object at a distance $d_o < f$. Attempt to project the image onto a screen placed on the far side of the lens, then on the same side as the object. Record your observations and compute $d_i$ from the lens equation. Measure the apparent image height using a ruler held at the image location as viewed through the lens, and compare with the predicted magnification $m = -d_i/d_o$.
