🍃 Real Image

🍃 Real Image

Definition. A real image is formed at a point where light rays actually converge after reflection or refraction. Unlike a virtual image, a real image can be projected onto a screen placed at the image location, because the rays themselves—not merely their backward extensions—intersect at that point. For a converging (convex) lens or concave mirror, the thin-lens (mirror) equation governs image formation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} $$

where $d_o$ is the object distance, $d_i$ is the image distance, and $f$ is the focal length. A real image corresponds to a positive image distance $d_i > 0$ (using the standard sign convention in which distances are measured from the optical element along the direction of incident light).

Worked Example. A converging lens has focal length $f = 20\,\text{cm}$. An object is placed $d_o = 60\,\text{cm}$ in front of the lens. Find the image distance and lateral magnification.

Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{20} - \frac{1}{60} = \frac{3}{60} - \frac{1}{60} = \frac{2}{60} $$ $$ d_i = 30\,\text{cm} $$

Since $d_i > 0$, the image is real and forms on the opposite side of the lens from the object. The lateral magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{30}{60} = -0.5 $$

The image is inverted ($m < 0$) and reduced to half the object's size.

Key Theorem. For a thin converging lens, a real image forms if and only if the object is placed beyond the focal point ($d_o > f$). As $d_o \to f^+$, the image recedes to infinity; as $d_o \to \infty$, the image converges to the focal plane ($d_i \to f$). This follows directly from the monotonic behavior of the thin-lens equation over the domain $d_o \in (f, \infty)$.

Lab Exercise. Place a candle $45\,\text{cm}$ from a converging lens of focal length $15\,\text{cm}$. Predict $d_i$ and $m$ analytically, then slide a white screen along the optical bench until a sharp image appears. Measure the image distance and compare with your prediction. Record whether the image is upright or inverted and verify the sign of $m$.
