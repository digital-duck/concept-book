🌸 Magnifying Glass

🌸 Magnifying Glass

Definition. A magnifying glass is a converging (convex) lens used to produce an enlarged virtual image of a nearby object. When an object is placed within the focal length $f$ of the lens, the lens refracts diverging rays such that they appear to originate from a larger, upright virtual image located on the same side as the object. The angular magnification $M$ quantifies the enlargement and is defined as the ratio of the angle $\theta'$ subtended by the image when using the lens to the angle $\theta_0$ subtended by the object at the near point $N$ (conventionally $N = 25\,\text{cm}$) without the lens:

$$ M = \frac{\theta'}{\theta_0}. $$

When the image is formed at infinity (the most comfortable viewing condition), the angular magnification simplifies to:

$$ M = \frac{N}{f}, $$

where $f$ is the focal length of the lens in centimeters. When the image is formed at the near point, the magnification increases slightly to $M = \frac{N}{f} + 1$.

Worked Example. A jeweler's loupe has a focal length of $5.0\,\text{cm}$. An engraving is held just inside the focal point. Find the angular magnification when the image forms at infinity.

Solution. Using $N = 25\,\text{cm}$ and $f = 5.0\,\text{cm}$:

$$ M = \frac{N}{f} = \frac{25\,\text{cm}}{5.0\,\text{cm}} = 5.0\times. $$

The engraving appears five times larger in angular size than it would to the unaided eye at the near point. Using the thin-lens equation $\frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}$, one can verify that placing the object at $d_o = f$ sends $d_i \to -\infty$, confirming a virtual image at infinity.

Key Theorem (Thin-Lens Equation). For a thin lens with radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$ and refractive index $n$, the lensmaker's equation gives:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right). $$

This establishes that a shorter focal length, achieved by increasing curvature or refractive index, yields higher magnification.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a biconvex lens of unknown focal length. Place a ruler at arm's length ($\approx 50\,\text{cm}$) and, using the lens, find the object distance $d_o$ at which the image appears at your near point ($d_i = -25\,\text{cm}$). Apply the thin-lens equation to determine $f$, then compute $M = N/f + 1$ and compare it with direct visual estimation of the magnification.
