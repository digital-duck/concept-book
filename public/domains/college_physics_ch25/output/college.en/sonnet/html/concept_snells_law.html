🍃 Snells Law

🍃 Snells Law

Definition. When a ray of light passes from one transparent medium into another, it changes direction at the interface. This phenomenon, called refraction, is governed by Snell's Law (also known as the Law of Refraction):

$$ n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2 $$

where $n_1$ and $n_2$ are the indices of refraction of the first and second media, respectively, and $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are the angles of incidence and refraction measured from the normal to the interface. The index of refraction of a medium is defined as $n = c/v$, where $c$ is the speed of light in vacuum and $v$ is the phase speed in the medium. Since $v \leq c$, we always have $n \geq 1$.

Worked Example. A ray of light in air ($n_1 = 1.00$) strikes a flat glass surface ($n_2 = 1.52$) at an angle of incidence $\theta_1 = 30.0°$. Find the angle of refraction.

Applying Snell's Law:

$$ \sin\theta_2 = \frac{n_1}{n_2}\sin\theta_1 = \frac{1.00}{1.52}\sin(30.0°) = \frac{1.00}{1.52}(0.500) \approx 0.329 $$ $$ \theta_2 = \arcsin(0.329) \approx 19.2° $$

The ray bends toward the normal upon entering the denser medium, consistent with $n_2 > n_1$.

Key Theorem — Total Internal Reflection. When light travels from a denser medium ($n_1$) into a less dense medium ($n_2 < n_1$), Snell's Law predicts that $\sin\theta_2 = (n_1/n_2)\sin\theta_1$. At a critical angle $\theta_c$ defined by

$$ \sin\theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1} $$

the refracted ray emerges at $\theta_2 = 90°$ (grazing the surface). For $\theta_1 > \theta_c$, no transmitted ray exists; all light is reflected back into the denser medium. This total internal reflection underpins fiber-optic light transmission and prismatic reflectors.

Lab Exercise. Place a semicircular acrylic block (flat side facing you) on polar graph paper. Direct a laser beam through the curved face so it strikes the flat face at varying angles. Record $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ for five different angles of incidence in the range $10°$–$50°$. Plot $\sin\theta_1$ versus $\sin\theta_2$ and determine the slope; compare your measured $n_{\text{acrylic}}$ against the accepted value of $1.49$, and calculate the percent error.
