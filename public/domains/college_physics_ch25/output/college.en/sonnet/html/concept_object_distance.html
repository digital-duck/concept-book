🍃 Object Distance

🍃 Object Distance

Definition. In geometrical optics, the object distance, conventionally denoted $d_o$ (or sometimes $s$ or $u$), is the perpendicular distance measured along the principal axis from the object to the optical element under consideration—whether a thin lens or a spherical mirror. By the standard sign convention (real-is-positive), $d_o$ is positive when the object is located on the same side as the incoming light (a real object) and negative when it lies on the opposite side (a virtual object, arising in compound optical systems). Object distance is one of the three fundamental quantities in the thin-lens equation and the mirror equation, both of which share the form

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}, $$

where $d_i$ is the image distance and $f$ is the focal length of the element.

Worked Example. A converging lens has a focal length of $f = 15\text{ cm}$. An object is placed $d_o = 45\text{ cm}$ in front of the lens. Find the image distance.

Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{15} - \frac{1}{45} = \frac{3}{45} - \frac{1}{45} = \frac{2}{45}. $$

Therefore $d_i = 22.5\text{ cm}$. Because $d_i > 0$, the image is real and forms on the opposite side of the lens from the object. The lateral magnification is $m = -d_i/d_o = -22.5/45 = -0.5$, indicating a real, inverted image reduced to half the object's size.

Key Theorem. As $d_o \to f^+$ (object approaches the focal point from outside), $d_i \to +\infty$: the refracted rays emerge parallel and no finite real image is formed. Conversely, when $d_o < f$, the thin-lens equation yields $d_i < 0$, signifying a virtual image on the same side as the object—the operating principle of a simple magnifier.

Lab Exercise. Mount a converging lens of known focal length on an optical bench. Place a luminous object at five different measured distances $d_o$ ranging from $1.5f$ to $4f$. For each position, locate the sharpest image on a screen and record $d_i$. Plot $1/d_i$ versus $1/d_o$ and verify that the slope equals $-1$ with a $y$-intercept of $1/f$, confirming the thin-lens equation experimentally.
