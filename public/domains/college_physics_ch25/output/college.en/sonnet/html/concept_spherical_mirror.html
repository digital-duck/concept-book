🍃 Spherical Mirror

🍃 Spherical Mirror

A spherical mirror is a reflecting surface whose geometry is a portion of a sphere of radius $R$. The center of that sphere is the center of curvature $C$, and the geometric midpoint of the mirror's reflecting surface is the vertex $V$. The straight line passing through $C$ and $V$ is the principal axis. Spherical mirrors are classified as concave (reflecting surface on the inner face of the sphere) or convex (reflecting surface on the outer face). For paraxial rays — rays that make small angles with the principal axis — a concave mirror converges reflected rays to a real focal point $F$, while a convex mirror diverges rays such that they appear to originate from a virtual focal point behind the mirror. In both cases the focal length satisfies

$$ f = \frac{R}{2}. $$

Image location is governed by the mirror equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}, $$

where $d_o$ is the object distance (positive in front of the mirror) and $d_i$ is the image distance (positive for real images in front, negative for virtual images behind). The lateral magnification is

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o}. $$

A negative $m$ indicates an inverted image; $|m| > 1$ indicates magnification.

Worked Example. A concave mirror has a radius of curvature $R = 40\,\text{cm}$, giving $f = 20\,\text{cm}$. An object is placed $d_o = 30\,\text{cm}$ in front of the mirror. Applying the mirror equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{20} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3 - 2}{60} = \frac{1}{60} \implies d_i = 60\,\text{cm}. $$

Since $d_i > 0$, the image is real and forms $60\,\text{cm}$ in front of the mirror. The magnification is $m = -60/30 = -2$, so the image is inverted and twice the height of the object.

Key Theorem (Focal-Length Derivation). For a ray parallel to the principal axis striking the mirror at height $h \operatorname{ll} R$, the law of reflection applied to the normal at the point of incidence (which passes through $C$) shows geometrically that the reflected ray crosses the principal axis at $f = R/2$. This paraxial approximation breaks down for large $h$, producing spherical aberration.

Lab Exercise. Using a concave mirror with known $f$, place a luminous object at five different distances ($d_o > f$, $d_o = 2f$, $d_o < f$, etc.) and locate the image on a screen. Record $d_i$ for each trial, compute $1/d_o + 1/d_i$, and verify that the sum remains constant and equal to $1/f$ within experimental uncertainty. Sketch the ray diagrams for each configuration and identify whether each image is real or virtual, upright or inverted.
