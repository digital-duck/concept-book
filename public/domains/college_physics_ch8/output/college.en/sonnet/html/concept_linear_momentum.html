🍃 Linear Momentum

Definition. The linear momentum of a particle is the product of its mass and velocity. For a particle of mass $m$ moving with velocity $\vec{v}$, the linear momentum is defined as

$$ \vec{p} = m\vec{v}. $$

Momentum is a vector quantity sharing the direction of $\vec{v}$, with SI units of kilogram-meters per second ($\text{kg·m/s}$). For a system of $N$ particles, the total momentum is the vector sum $\vec{P} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \vec{v}_i$. Newton's second law may be restated in its most general form as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}, $$

which reduces to $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}$ only when mass is constant. This formulation is more fundamental and remains valid for variable-mass systems such as rockets.

Worked Example. A 0.145 kg baseball moving horizontally at $40.0\ \text{m/s}$ is struck by a bat and leaves in the opposite direction at $55.0\ \text{m/s}$. Taking the initial direction as positive, the initial momentum is

$$ p_i = (0.145\ \text{kg})(+40.0\ \text{m/s}) = +5.80\ \text{kg·m/s}, $$

and the final momentum is

$$ p_f = (0.145\ \text{kg})(-55.0\ \text{m/s}) = -7.975\ \text{kg·m/s}. $$

The change in momentum (impulse delivered by the bat) is

$$ \Delta\vec{p} = p_f - p_i = -7.975 - 5.80 = -13.8\ \text{kg·m/s}. $$

If the contact time is $\Delta t = 1.20\ \text{ms} = 1.20 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{s}$, the average force exerted on the ball is $\bar{F} = \Delta p / \Delta t = -13.8 / (1.20\times10^{-3}) \approx -1.15\times10^{4}\ \text{N}$, directed opposite to the initial motion.

Key Theorem (Impulse–Momentum Theorem). The net impulse acting on a particle equals its change in momentum:

$$ \vec{J} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{F}_{\text{net}}\, dt = \Delta\vec{p}. $$

This theorem is a direct consequence of Newton's second law and provides a powerful tool when forces vary with time, since only the integrated effect—not the instantaneous force profile—is needed to determine the momentum change.

Lab Exercise. Using a dynamics cart on a frictionless track, attach a force sensor and a motion detector. Give the cart a measured push, record the force-versus-time curve, and numerically integrate to find $\vec{J}$. Compare this to $\Delta\vec{p}$ computed from the velocity measurements before and after the push. Quantify any discrepancy and identify sources of systematic error, such as track tilt or sensor calibration drift.
