🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. In classical mechanics, mass is the scalar quantity that measures an object's resistance to changes in its state of motion — a property called inertia — and simultaneously determines the strength of its gravitational interaction with other masses. Operationally, the inertial mass $m_i$ of an object is defined through Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m_i \vec{a}, $$

so that $m_i = F_{\text{net}} / a$ when a known net force produces a measurable acceleration. The gravitational mass $m_g$ appears in Newton's law of universal gravitation:

$$ F_g = G \frac{m_g M}{r^2}. $$

One of the foundational empirical results of classical physics — confirmed to one part in $10^{12}$ by Eötvös-type experiments — is the equivalence principle: $m_i = m_g$ for all objects. This equivalence is elevated to a postulate in Einstein's general theory of relativity. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg), defined since 2019 by fixing the Planck constant to $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$.

Worked Example. A hockey puck of mass $m = 0.170\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless ice surface. A player applies a constant horizontal force $F = 8.50\ \text{N}$ for $t = 2.00\ \text{s}$. Find the puck's final speed.

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{8.50\ \text{N}}{0.170\ \text{kg}} = 50.0\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

Using kinematics with $v_0 = 0$:

$$ v = v_0 + at = 0 + (50.0)(2.00) = 100\ \text{m/s}. $$

The result illustrates that a smaller mass produces a larger acceleration under the same force — a direct expression of inertia.

Key Theorem: Conservation of Mass (Classical Limit). In an isolated system with no relativistic effects, the total mass is conserved:

$$ \sum_i m_i = \text{constant}. $$

This follows from Newton's third law and the additive nature of inertia, and underpins all classical collision analyses.

🌱 Net External Force

🌱 Net External Force

Definition. The net external force acting on a system is the vector sum of all forces exerted on that system by agents outside the system boundary. If a system is subject to external forces $\vec{F}_1, \vec{F}_2, \ldots, \vec{F}_k$, then

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \vec{F}_i. $$

Internal forces—those between constituents within the system—cancel in pairs by Newton's third law and therefore contribute nothing to $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$. Newton's second law for the system then reads

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = M\vec{a}, $$

where $M$ is the total mass and $\vec{a}$ is the acceleration of the system.

Worked Example. A 3.0 kg block rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A horizontal applied force $\vec{F}_A = 12\,\hat{i}\ \text{N}$ acts to the right, while a second horizontal force $\vec{F}_B = -4.0\,\hat{i}\ \text{N}$ acts to the left. The normal force $\vec{N} = 29.4\,\hat{j}\ \text{N}$ and gravity $\vec{W} = -29.4\,\hat{j}\ \text{N}$ act vertically. Summing all external forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (12 - 4.0)\,\hat{i} + (29.4 - 29.4)\,\hat{j} = 8.0\,\hat{i}\ \text{N}. $$

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{M} = \frac{8.0\ \text{N}}{3.0\ \text{kg}} = 2.7\ \text{m/s}^2\ (\text{to the right}). $$

Key Theorem. If and only if $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{0}$, the system moves with constant velocity (Newton's first law as a corollary). Equivalently, the total linear momentum $\vec{p} = M\vec{v}$ is conserved whenever the net external force vanishes:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} = \vec{0} \implies \vec{p} = \text{constant}. $$

This result forms the foundation of momentum conservation in collision analysis.

🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the vector quantity that describes the rate of change of an object's position with respect to time. For a particle whose position vector is $\vec{r}(t)$, the instantaneous velocity is defined as the limit

$$ \vec{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt}. $$

In one dimension this reduces to $v = dx/dt$, while in two or three dimensions the velocity vector has components $\vec{v} = \dot{x}\,\hat{i} + \dot{y}\,\hat{j} + \dot{z}\,\hat{k}$. The magnitude $|\vec{v}|$ is the speed, a scalar quantity. Velocity carries both magnitude and direction; two objects moving at the same speed in different directions possess distinct velocities.

Worked Example. A particle moves along the $x$-axis according to $x(t) = 3t^2 - 2t + 5$ (SI units). Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 2\,\text{s}$ and the average velocity over the interval $[1,\,3]\,\text{s}$.

Instantaneous velocity:

$$ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 2 \implies v(2) = 6(2) - 2 = 10\,\text{m/s}. $$

Average velocity:

$$ \bar{v} = \frac{x(3) - x(1)}{3 - 1} = \frac{(27 - 6 + 5) - (3 - 2 + 5)}{2} = \frac{26 - 6}{2} = 10\,\text{m/s}. $$

The numerical equality here is not coincidental—it holds whenever $x(t)$ is a polynomial of degree two (uniform acceleration), a consequence of the mean value theorem applied to quadratic functions.

Key Theorem — Mean Value Theorem for Motion. If $x(t)$ is differentiable on $[t_1, t_2]$, then there exists at least one instant $t^* \in (t_1, t_2)$ such that

$$ v(t^*) = \bar{v} = \frac{x(t_2) - x(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1}. $$

This guarantees that the instantaneous velocity equals the average velocity at some moment in every smooth motion—a result with direct implications for analyzing experimental position data.

Fit a linear model to the resulting points, identify the slope, and compare it against the accepted gravitational acceleration component along the track.

🍃 Isolated System

🍃 Isolated System

Definition. A mechanical system is called isolated if it experiences no net external force—that is, every force acting on any constituent particle originates from within the system itself. Under this condition, Newton's second law applied to the entire system gives

$$ \sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}} = \frac{d\vec{p}_{\text{sys}}}{dt} = \vec{0}, $$

which immediately implies that the total linear momentum of the system is constant:

$$ \vec{p}_{\text{sys}} = \sum_{i} m_i \vec{v}_i = \text{const.} $$

This result is the law of conservation of momentum: whenever the net external force on a system is zero, the total linear momentum cannot change, regardless of how large the internal forces between parts may be.

Worked Example. Two ice skaters, A (mass $m_A = 60\,\text{kg}$) and B (mass $m_B = 80\,\text{kg}$), stand at rest on a frictionless rink and push off each other. After the push, skater A moves at $\vec{v}_A = +3.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}$. Find skater B's velocity.

The rink is horizontal and frictionless; gravity and the normal force cancel vertically; no horizontal external forces act. The system is therefore isolated in the horizontal direction.

Initial momentum: $\vec{p}_i = \vec{0}$.

Conservation gives

$$ m_A \vec{v}_A + m_B \vec{v}_B = \vec{0} $$ $$ \vec{v}_B = -\frac{m_A}{m_B}\vec{v}_A = -\frac{60}{80}(3.0\,\hat{x}) = -2.25\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}. $$

Skater B moves in the $-x$ direction at $2.25\,\text{m/s}$, consistent with Newton's third law.

Key Theorem. If $\sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}} = \vec{0}$ over an interval $[t_1, t_2]$, then

$$ \vec{p}_{\text{sys}}(t_2) - \vec{p}_{\text{sys}}(t_1) = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}}\, dt = \vec{0}. $$

Conservation of momentum holds component-by-component: a system may be isolated in one direction (e.g., horizontal) while non-isolated in another (e.g., vertical), permitting partial application of the law.

🍃 Linear Momentum

🍃 Linear Momentum

Definition. The linear momentum of a particle is the product of its mass and velocity. For a particle of mass $m$ moving with velocity $\vec{v}$, the linear momentum is defined as

$$ \vec{p} = m\vec{v}. $$

Momentum is a vector quantity sharing the direction of $\vec{v}$, with SI units of kilogram-meters per second ($\text{kg·m/s}$). For a system of $N$ particles, the total momentum is the vector sum $\vec{P} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \vec{v}_i$. Newton's second law may be restated in its most general form as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}, $$

which reduces to $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}$ only when mass is constant. This formulation is more fundamental and remains valid for variable-mass systems such as rockets.

Worked Example. A 0.145 kg baseball moving horizontally at $40.0\ \text{m/s}$ is struck by a bat and leaves in the opposite direction at $55.0\ \text{m/s}$. Taking the initial direction as positive, the initial momentum is

$$ p_i = (0.145\ \text{kg})(+40.0\ \text{m/s}) = +5.80\ \text{kg·m/s}, $$

and the final momentum is

$$ p_f = (0.145\ \text{kg})(-55.0\ \text{m/s}) = -7.975\ \text{kg·m/s}. $$

The change in momentum (impulse delivered by the bat) is

$$ \Delta\vec{p} = p_f - p_i = -7.975 - 5.80 = -13.8\ \text{kg·m/s}. $$

If the contact time is $\Delta t = 1.20\ \text{ms} = 1.20 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{s}$, the average force exerted on the ball is $\bar{F} = \Delta p / \Delta t = -13.8 / (1.20\times10^{-3}) \approx -1.15\times10^{4}\ \text{N}$, directed opposite to the initial motion.

Key Theorem (Impulse–Momentum Theorem). The net impulse acting on a particle equals its change in momentum:

$$ \vec{J} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{F}_{\text{net}}\, dt = \Delta\vec{p}. $$

This theorem is a direct consequence of Newton's second law and provides a powerful tool when forces vary with time, since only the integrated effect—not the instantaneous force profile—is needed to determine the momentum change.

🍃 Conservation Of Momentum

🍃 Conservation Of Momentum

Definition. The law of conservation of momentum states that the total linear momentum of an isolated system remains constant in time. For a system of $n$ particles with masses $m_i$ and velocities $\mathbf{v}_i$, the total momentum is

$$ \mathbf{p}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i \mathbf{v}_i. $$

If the net external force on the system is zero, $\sum \mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}} = \mathbf{0}$, then by Newton's second law in its momentum form, $\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}} = d\mathbf{p}_{\text{total}}/dt = \mathbf{0}$, so $\mathbf{p}_{\text{total}}$ is constant. Internal forces between particles always occur in action-reaction pairs (Newton's third law) and cancel when summed over the entire system, leaving only external forces capable of changing total momentum.

Worked Example. A 1500 kg car traveling east at $20\ \text{m/s}$ collides with a stationary 2000 kg truck on a frictionless surface. The vehicles lock together after impact. Find the velocity of the combined wreckage.

Taking east as positive, the initial momentum of the system is

$$ p_i = (1500\ \text{kg})(20\ \text{m/s}) + (2000\ \text{kg})(0) = 3.00 \times 10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}. $$

Since no external horizontal force acts, $p_f = p_i$. With the combined mass $M = 3500\ \text{kg}$,

$$ v_f = \frac{p_i}{M} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^4\ \text{kg·m/s}}{3500\ \text{kg}} \approx 8.57\ \text{m/s (east)}. $$

Because the vehicles merge into a single body, kinetic energy is not conserved; some is converted to deformation and heat. Momentum conservation, however, holds regardless of what happens internally.

Key Theorem. For any two-body collision, conservation of momentum gives

$$ m_1 \mathbf{v}_{1i} + m_2 \mathbf{v}_{2i} = m_1 \mathbf{v}_{1f} + m_2 \mathbf{v}_{2f}. $$

This relation is exact whenever the net external impulse during the collision is negligible — a condition well approximated when collision forces are large and the interaction time is short. It holds for all collision types and constrains the final momenta even when internal energy changes are unknown.

🌸 Recoil

🌸 Recoil

Definition. Recoil is the backward motion experienced by an object when it expels mass in the forward direction, a direct consequence of the conservation of linear momentum. For an isolated system (net external force equal to zero), total momentum is conserved. If two objects are initially at rest, the total initial momentum is zero, so after one object expels mass, the remaining system must acquire an equal and opposite momentum so that the vector sum remains zero:

$$ m_1 \vec{v}_1 + m_2 \vec{v}_2 = 0 $$

More precisely, if object 1 has mass $m_1$ and recoils with velocity $\vec{v}_1$, while the expelled mass $m_2$ moves with velocity $\vec{v}_2$, then:

$$ m_1 \vec{v}_1 = -m_2 \vec{v}_2 \implies \vec{v}_1 = -\frac{m_2}{m_1}\vec{v}_2 $$

Worked Example. A rifle of mass $M = 4.0\ \text{kg}$ fires a bullet of mass $m = 0.010\ \text{kg}$ at a muzzle velocity of $v_b = 600\ \text{m/s}$ horizontally. Find the recoil speed of the rifle.

The system (rifle + bullet) is initially at rest, so $p_i = 0$. Applying conservation of momentum:

$$ 0 = M v_r + m v_b $$ $$ v_r = -\frac{m v_b}{M} = -\frac{(0.010)(600)}{4.0} = -1.5\ \text{m/s} $$

The rifle recoils at $1.5\ \text{m/s}$ opposite to the bullet's direction. The negative sign confirms the backward motion.

Key Theorem. The internal forces that propel the bullet forward act equally and oppositely on the rifle, by Newton's third law. Over the brief firing interval $\Delta t$, these internal forces contribute no net impulse to the system as a whole, so total momentum is preserved. Formally, since $\sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}} = 0$ for the isolated system, $\Delta \vec{p}_{\text{total}} = 0$, regardless of the magnitude or time profile of the internal forces. This makes recoil independent of the details of the propulsion mechanism.

Lab Exercise. Place a dynamics cart on a frictionless track. Load it with a spring-launched projectile. Release the spring and measure the cart's recoil velocity with a motion sensor. Payoff 🎯

Recoil represents the culminating synthesis of Chapter 8's treatment of linear momentum and its conservation. Every concept encountered along the way—momentum as $\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}$, impulse as $\mathbf{J} = \int \mathbf{F}\,dt$, the center-of-mass frame, elastic and inelastic collisions—has been preparation for understanding what happens when an isolated system's internal forces cause its constituent parts to fly apart. Recoil is not a special case; it is the general case of momentum conservation made viscerally visible.

Definition. When an initially stationary system of total mass $M$ separates into fragments, conservation of momentum demands

$$ \sum_{i} m_i \mathbf{v}_i = \mathbf{0}, $$

since the initial momentum is zero. For the two-body case, this reduces to $m_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + m_2 \mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{0}$, which immediately gives the recoil relation $\mathbf{v}_2 = -\dfrac{m_1}{m_2}\mathbf{v}_1$.

Worked example. A rifle of mass $M = 3.50\,\text{kg}$ fires a bullet of mass $m = 0.0120\,\text{kg}$ with muzzle velocity $v_b = 890\,\text{m/s}$. Taking the system (rifle + bullet) as isolated during firing, initial momentum is zero. Therefore the rifle recoils at

$$ v_R = -\frac{m}{M}v_b = -\frac{0.0120}{3.50}(890) \approx -3.05\,\text{m/s}. $$

The negative sign confirms the rifle moves opposite to the bullet. The kinetic energy released, $\Delta K = \tfrac{1}{2}mv_b^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}Mv_R^2 \approx 4790\,\text{J}$, originates entirely from the chemical potential energy of the propellant—an internal energy source that does not violate momentum conservation.

Key theorem. The center of mass of an isolated system moves at constant velocity regardless of internal interactions. Recoil is the observable consequence of this invariance: however violent the internal event, $\mathbf{v}_{\text{cm}} = \text{const}$.

Lab exercise. Place two carts of measured masses on a frictionless air track with a compressed spring between them. Release the spring and record both velocities with motion sensors. Verify that $m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = 0$ to within experimental uncertainty, and compute the fraction of stored spring potential energy converted to kinetic energy.

Recoil governs rocket propulsion, nuclear alpha decay, jet thrust, and the kick felt by anyone who has ever fired a weapon. Each domain applies the same conservation law to a different physical scale and energy source. To see how recoil scales from a rifle range to the vacuum of space—where there is no ground to push against and momentum exchange with ejected mass is the only mechanism of propulsion—follow the thread into rocket dynamics, where the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation awaits.
