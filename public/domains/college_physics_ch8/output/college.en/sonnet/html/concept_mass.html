🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. In classical mechanics, mass is the scalar quantity that measures an object's resistance to changes in its state of motion — a property called inertia — and simultaneously determines the strength of its gravitational interaction with other masses. Operationally, the inertial mass $m_i$ of an object is defined through Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m_i \vec{a}, $$

so that $m_i = F_{\text{net}} / a$ when a known net force produces a measurable acceleration. The gravitational mass $m_g$ appears in Newton's law of universal gravitation:

$$ F_g = G \frac{m_g M}{r^2}. $$

One of the foundational empirical results of classical physics — confirmed to one part in $10^{12}$ by Eötvös-type experiments — is the equivalence principle: $m_i = m_g$ for all objects. This equivalence is elevated to a postulate in Einstein's general theory of relativity. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg), defined since 2019 by fixing the Planck constant to $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$.

Worked Example. A hockey puck of mass $m = 0.170\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless ice surface. A player applies a constant horizontal force $F = 8.50\ \text{N}$ for $t = 2.00\ \text{s}$. Find the puck's final speed.

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{8.50\ \text{N}}{0.170\ \text{kg}} = 50.0\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

Using kinematics with $v_0 = 0$:

$$ v = v_0 + at = 0 + (50.0)(2.00) = 100\ \text{m/s}. $$

The result illustrates that a smaller mass produces a larger acceleration under the same force — a direct expression of inertia.

Key Theorem: Conservation of Mass (Classical Limit). In an isolated system with no relativistic effects, the total mass is conserved:

$$ \sum_i m_i = \text{constant}. $$

This follows from Newton's third law and the additive nature of inertia, and underpins all classical collision analyses.

Lab Exercise. Using a dynamics cart on a low-friction track, measure the acceleration produced by three different hanging masses (e.g., 50 g, 100 g, 150 g) while keeping the cart mass fixed. Plot $a$ versus $F = m_{\text{hang}} g$ and extract $m_{\text{cart}}$ from the slope. Assess whether your result matches the value obtained by a digital balance, and comment on sources of systematic error such as track inclination and string mass.
