🌱 Receptor

A receptor ($R$) is defined as a macromolecular entity capable of recognizing and binding an external signaling molecule, termed the ligand ($L$). The interaction is highly specific and reversible, forming a complex $RL$. Operationally, receptors mediate cellular responses by converting the energy of molecular recognition into an intracellular signal. Mathematically, the initial binding event is modeled as:

$$ R + L \rightleftharpoons RL $$

The strength of this association is quantified by the dissociation constant, $K_{D} = [R][L]/[RL]$, which establishes the equilibrium affinity and governs the stoichiometry of the interaction (e.g., one-to-one, or cooperative).

\textbf{Theorem.} Receptor-mediated signal transduction requires ligand binding to induce a conformational change in the receptor complex that alters its ability to interact with downstream effector proteins ($E$). This activation process is characterized by an allosteric shift ($\Delta G_{act}$).

\textbf{Proof.} Consider the initial equilibrium $R + L \rightleftharpoons RL$. The subsequent signal transduction step requires the binding of a second macromolecule, $E$, which stabilizes an active conformation $R^*L$. Therefore, the rate-limiting step for signaling is not merely achieving occupancy, but overcoming the activation energy barrier $\Delta G_{act}$ required to transition from the unbound state $R$ to the functional effector site $R^*$.
