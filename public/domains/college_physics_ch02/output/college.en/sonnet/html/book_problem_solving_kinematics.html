🌱 Position

🌱 Position

Definition. In classical mechanics, the position of a particle is a vector quantity that specifies the particle's location relative to a chosen reference point called the origin. In one dimension, position is represented by a scalar coordinate $x$ (with an associated sign indicating direction), measured in meters (SI). In two or three dimensions, position is the vector

$$ \vec{r} = x\,\hat{i} + y\,\hat{j} + z\,\hat{k}, $$

where $x$, $y$, and $z$ are the Cartesian components along the respective coordinate axes. Position is defined only with respect to a specified coordinate system; changing the origin or orientation of the axes changes the numerical values of position, even though the physical location of the particle is unaltered.

Worked Example. A cyclist starts at a point $2.0\ \text{m}$ east of a lamppost, rides $5.0\ \text{m}$ further east, then turns and rides $3.0\ \text{m}$ west. Taking the lamppost as the origin and east as the positive $x$-direction:

  • Initial position: $x_0 = +2.0\ \text{m}$
  • After riding east: $x_1 = 2.0 + 5.0 = +7.0\ \text{m}$
  • Final position: $x_f = 7.0 - 3.0 = +4.0\ \text{m}$

The final position is $x_f = +4.0\ \text{m}$, meaning the cyclist is $4.0\ \text{m}$ east of the lamppost. Note that position ($+4.0\ \text{m}$) differs from the total distance traveled ($8.0\ \text{m}$); this distinction will be made precise when displacement is introduced in the next section.

Key Theorem (Frame Independence of Relative Separation). Although the position vector $\vec{r}$ of a single particle depends on the choice of origin, the relative position of two particles, $\Delta\vec{r} = \vec{r}_2 - \vec{r}_1$, is invariant under translations of the coordinate origin. Formally, if the origin is shifted by $\vec{d}$, then $\vec{r}_i' = \vec{r}_i - \vec{d}$, so $\vec{r}_2' - \vec{r}_1' = \vec{r}_2 - \vec{r}_1$. This guarantees that physically meaningful separations are independent of the arbitrary choice of reference point.

🌱 Time

🌱 Time

Definition. In classical mechanics, time is a scalar quantity that parameterizes the ordered sequence of events in a reference frame. Within the International System of Units (SI), the base unit of time is the second (s), defined as exactly 9,192,631,770 oscillation periods of radiation corresponding to the hyperfine transition of the ground state of cesium-133. Time is typically denoted $t$, and a duration (time interval) between two instants $t_1$ and $t_2$ is written

$$ \Delta t = t_2 - t_1. $$

Unlike position, time in Newtonian mechanics is absolute — all inertial observers agree on the duration $\Delta t$ between two events (a symmetry broken only in special relativity).

Worked Example. A sprinter crosses the starting line at $t_1 = 1.40\ \text{s}$ and the finish line at $t_2 = 11.65\ \text{s}$ on a race clock. Determine the elapsed time of the run.

$$ \Delta t = t_2 - t_1 = 11.65\ \text{s} - 1.40\ \text{s} = 10.25\ \text{s}. $$

Note that only the interval $\Delta t$ carries physical meaning; the choice of origin ($t = 0$) is a convention imposed by the observer. Had the clock been reset so that $t_1 = 0$, the result would be identical: $\Delta t = 10.25\ \text{s}$.

Key Theorem — Galilean Time Invariance. In Newtonian mechanics, under a Galilean transformation between two inertial frames $S$ and $S'$ moving with constant relative velocity $v$, positions transform as $x' = x - vt$ while time satisfies

$$ t' = t. $$

This invariance implies that the time interval $\Delta t$ is the same in every inertial reference frame, making it a universal parameter for describing motion. Consequently, kinematic equations such as $x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$ hold without modification in any inertial frame. Here $a$ denotes the (constant) acceleration — the one new quantity this section introduces as a preview of kinematics, defined formally in the next section.

🌱 Vector

🌱 Vector

Definition. A vector is a mathematical object possessing both magnitude and direction, in contrast to a scalar, which possesses magnitude alone. In two or three dimensions, a vector $\vec{A}$ is written in component form as

$$ \vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j} + A_z\,\hat{k}, $$

where $A_x$, $A_y$, $A_z$ are the scalar components and $\hat{i}$, $\hat{j}$, $\hat{k}$ are the standard orthonormal basis vectors. The magnitude (length) of $\vec{A}$ is

$$ |\vec{A}| = A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2 + A_z^2}. $$

The direction in the $xy$-plane is specified by the angle $\theta$ measured counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis:

$$ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{A_y}{A_x}\right). $$

Worked Example. A displacement vector points $30.0\text{ m}$ east and $40.0\text{ m}$ north. Express it in component form and find its magnitude and direction.

Taking east as $+x$ and north as $+y$:

$$ \vec{d} = 30.0\,\hat{i} + 40.0\,\hat{j}\ \text{m}. $$

The magnitude is

$$ d = \sqrt{(30.0)^2 + (40.0)^2} = \sqrt{900 + 1600} = \sqrt{2500} = 50.0\text{ m}. $$

The direction is

$$ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{40.0}{30.0}\right) = \arctan(1.333) \approx 53.1°\ \text{north of east.} $$

Key Theorem — Vector Addition is Commutative and Associative. For any vectors $\vec{A}$, $\vec{B}$, $\vec{C}$:

$$ \vec{A} + \vec{B} = \vec{B} + \vec{A} \qquad \text{(commutative)} $$ $$ (\vec{A} + \vec{B}) + \vec{C} = \vec{A} + (\vec{B} + \vec{C}) \qquad \text{(associative).} $$

These follow directly from the commutativity and associativity of real-number addition applied component-wise. Geometrically, addition obeys the parallelogram law: place $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ tail-to-tail; the diagonal of the resulting parallelogram is $\vec{A}+\vec{B}$.

🍃 Displacement

🍃 Displacement

Definition. Displacement is the vector quantity that describes the change in position of an object. If a particle moves from initial position $\vec{r}_i$ to final position $\vec{r}_f$, its displacement is defined as

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i. $$

In one dimension, this reduces to $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$, where the sign encodes direction. Critically, displacement depends only on the endpoints of the motion, not on the path taken between them. This distinguishes displacement from distance traveled, which accumulates the total arc length regardless of direction.

Worked Example. A jogger starts at position $x_i = 3.0\ \text{m}$ east of a reference point, runs to $x = 10.0\ \text{m}$ east, then turns and returns to $x_f = 5.0\ \text{m}$ east. Compute the displacement and the total distance traveled.

Displacement:

$$ \Delta x = x_f - x_i = 5.0\ \text{m} - 3.0\ \text{m} = +2.0\ \text{m}. $$

The positive sign indicates the net motion is 2.0 m in the positive (east) direction.

Distance traveled:

$$ d = (10.0 - 3.0)\ \text{m} + (10.0 - 5.0)\ \text{m} = 7.0\ \text{m} + 5.0\ \text{m} = 12.0\ \text{m}. $$

The jogger covered 12.0 m in total, yet the displacement is only 2.0 m—illustrating that distance and magnitude of displacement are equal only for straight-line motion in a single direction.

Key Theorem. For any motion in two or three dimensions, displacement is independent of coordinate origin: if all position vectors are shifted by a constant $\vec{c}$, the displacement $\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i$ is unchanged. Furthermore, displacement is additive over sequential segments: $\Delta \vec{r}_{AC} = \Delta \vec{r}_{AB} + \Delta \vec{r}_{BC}$, a direct consequence of vector addition.

🍃 Elapsed Time

🍃 Elapsed Time

Definition. Elapsed time is the total duration of a process or event, defined as the difference between the final instant and the initial instant of that process. If an event begins at clock reading $t_0$ (the initial time) and ends at clock reading $t$ (the final time), the elapsed time is

$$ \Delta t = t - t_0. $$

The SI unit of time is the second (s). Elapsed time is always a non-negative scalar quantity: $\Delta t \geq 0$, since $t \geq t_0$ by convention. It is important to distinguish elapsed time $\Delta t$ from the time coordinate $t$ itself; the former measures a duration, while the latter locates an instant on a chosen timeline.

Worked Example. A sprinter leaves the starting blocks at $t_0 = 1.50\ \text{s}$ after the master clock is started and crosses the finish line at $t = 11.24\ \text{s}$. Determine the elapsed time for the sprint.

Applying the definition directly:

$$ \Delta t = t - t_0 = 11.24\ \text{s} - 1.50\ \text{s} = 9.74\ \text{s}. $$

Note that although the master clock read $11.24\ \text{s}$ at the finish, the race itself lasted only $9.74\ \text{s}$. The choice of when to start the master clock is arbitrary; elapsed time is invariant under a shift of the time origin.

Key Theorem (Time-Origin Invariance). Let $t' = t - t_{\text{ref}}$ be a shifted time coordinate for any constant $t_{\text{ref}}$. Then

$$ \Delta t' = t' - t_0' = (t - t_{\text{ref}}) - (t_0 - t_{\text{ref}}) = t - t_0 = \Delta t. $$

This confirms that $\Delta t$ depends only on the separation between two instants, not on the choice of zero point for the clock. This invariance is foundational: physical laws expressed in terms of $\Delta t$ hold regardless of when an experimenter starts their stopwatch.

🍃 Average Velocity

🍃 Average Velocity

Definition. Average velocity is the ratio of displacement to the elapsed time over which that displacement occurs. Because displacement $\Delta x = x_2 - x_1$ is a signed quantity encoding both magnitude and direction, average velocity is likewise a vector quantity—in contrast to average speed, which uses total path length and carries no directional information. For one-dimensional motion, if a particle occupies position $x_1$ at time $t_1$ and position $x_2$ at time $t_2$, the average velocity over that interval is

$$ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1}. $$

The SI unit is meters per second ($\text{m/s}$). The sign of $\bar{v}$ encodes direction: a positive value indicates net displacement in the positive $x$-direction; a negative value indicates the opposite.

Worked Example. A cyclist is at position $x_1 = 4\,\text{m}$ at time $t_1 = 1\,\text{s}$ and at position $x_2 = -8\,\text{m}$ at time $t_2 = 5\,\text{s}$. Applying the definition:

$$ \bar{v} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1} = \frac{-8\,\text{m} - 4\,\text{m}}{5\,\text{s} - 1\,\text{s}} = \frac{-12\,\text{m}}{4\,\text{s}} = -3\,\text{m/s}. $$

The magnitude is $3\,\text{m/s}$ and the negative sign indicates net displacement in the negative $x$-direction, regardless of whatever path the cyclist traced between those two instants.

Key Theorem (Mean Value Theorem for Motion). If a particle's position function $x(t)$ is continuous on $[t_1, t_2]$ and differentiable on $(t_1, t_2)$, then there exists at least one instant $t^* \in (t_1, t_2)$ such that the instantaneous velocity equals the average velocity:

$$ v(t^*) = \frac{dx}{dt}\bigg|_{t=t^*} = \bar{v}. $$

This result guarantees that $\bar{v}$ is not merely a computational abstraction—it is an actual instantaneous velocity attained at some moment within the interval, provided $x(t)$ is smooth.

🍃 Average Acceleration

🍃 Average Acceleration

Definition. Average acceleration is the rate of change of velocity over a finite time interval. If an object's velocity changes from $\vec{v}_1$ at time $t_1$ to $\vec{v}_2$ at time $t_2$, the average acceleration is defined as

$$ \bar{\vec{a}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{v}_2 - \vec{v}_1}{t_2 - t_1}. $$

Because velocity is a vector quantity, average acceleration is likewise a vector — it carries both a magnitude (in $\text{m/s}^2$) and a direction. A change in speed, direction, or both produces a nonzero average acceleration. This distinguishes acceleration from speed change alone: a car rounding a curve at constant speed is still accelerating because its velocity direction changes.

Worked Example. A commuter train traveling east at $v_1 = 20\ \text{m/s}$ applies its brakes and slows to $v_2 = 8\ \text{m/s}$ (still eastward) over $\Delta t = 6\ \text{s}$. Taking east as the positive direction,

$$ \bar{a} = \frac{v_2 - v_1}{\Delta t} = \frac{8\ \text{m/s} - 20\ \text{m/s}}{6\ \text{s}} = \frac{-12\ \text{m/s}}{6\ \text{s}} = -2\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

The negative sign indicates the acceleration vector points west — opposing the direction of motion — consistent with the train decelerating. The magnitude $|\bar{a}| = 2\ \text{m/s}^2$ quantifies how rapidly the speed diminishes per second.

Key Theorem. The sign of $\bar{\vec{a}}$ encodes direction relative to the chosen positive axis, not whether the object is speeding up or slowing down. Specifically, if $\bar{\vec{a}}$ and $\vec{v}$ share the same sign, the object is speeding up; if they have opposite signs, it is slowing down. This follows directly from the definition: $\Delta \vec{v} = \vec{v}_2 - \vec{v}_1$ can be negative while both velocities remain positive, as in the braking example above. The theorem guards against the common error of equating "negative acceleration" with "decelerating" in all coordinate choices.

🍃 Kinematic Equations Constant Acceleration

🍃 Kinematic Equations Constant Acceleration

Definition. When a particle moves along a straight line under constant acceleration $a$, its position $x$, velocity $v$, and time $t$ are related by a set of four kinematic equations. Let $x_0$ and $v_0$ denote the initial position and velocity at $t = 0$. The equations are:

$$ v = v_0 + at \tag{1} $$ $$ x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 \tag{2} $$ $$ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0) \tag{3} $$ $$ x = x_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)t \tag{4} $$

These relations are derived by integrating the constant acceleration $a$ twice with respect to time. Equation (1) follows directly from $v(t) = \int a\, dt = v_0 + at$. Substituting into $x(t) = \int v(t)\, dt$ yields equation (2). Eliminating $t$ between equations (1) and (2) produces equation (3), while averaging the initial and final velocities gives equation (4).

Worked Example. A car starts from rest ($v_0 = 0$) and accelerates uniformly at $a = 3.0\ \text{m/s}^2$. Determine the car's speed and distance traveled after $t = 4.0\ \text{s}$.

Using equation (1):

$$ v = 0 + (3.0)(4.0) = 12\ \text{m/s} $$

Using equation (2) with $x_0 = 0$:

$$ x = 0 + 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(3.0)(4.0)^2 = 24\ \text{m} $$

The car reaches $12\ \text{m/s}$ and has covered $24\ \text{m}$.

Key Theorem. The four kinematic equations are not independent: any one can be derived from the remaining three. However, each equation omits one of the five kinematic variables $\{x_0, x, v_0, v, t\}$, making it strategically useful when that variable is unknown. Specifically, equation (3) is the time-independent relation, eliminating $t$ entirely.

🌸 Problem Solving Kinematics

🌸 Problem Solving Kinematics

Definition. Kinematics is the branch of classical mechanics that describes the motion of objects without reference to the forces causing that motion. A systematic problem-solving strategy applies the four kinematic equations for constant acceleration:

$$ v = v_0 + at $$ $$ x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 $$ $$ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0) $$ $$ x = x_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)t $$

Here $x_0$ and $v_0$ denote initial position and velocity, $a$ is constant acceleration, $t$ is elapsed time, and $v$ is instantaneous velocity at time $t$. The strategy consists of four steps: (1) identify all known and unknown quantities and assign a positive direction; (2) choose the equation(s) that relate knowns to the single unknown; (3) solve algebraically before substituting numbers; (4) check dimensional consistency and physical reasonableness of the answer.

Worked Example. A car initially traveling at $v_0 = 15.0\ \text{m/s}$ decelerates uniformly at $a = -3.00\ \text{m/s}^2$. Determine the distance traveled before the car comes to rest.

Known: $v_0 = 15.0\ \text{m/s}$, $a = -3.00\ \text{m/s}^2$, $v = 0\ \text{m/s}$, $x_0 = 0$.

Unknown: $x$.

Selecting $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2ax$ (contains only the unknown $x$):

$$ 0 = (15.0)^2 + 2(-3.00)x \implies x = \frac{225}{6.00} = 37.5\ \text{m} $$

The car travels 37.5 m before stopping. Dimensional check: $\text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2} / (\text{m}\,\text{s}^{-2}) = \text{m}$. ✓

Key Theorem. For motion under constant acceleration, specifying any three of the five quantities $\{x_0,\, x,\, v_0,\, v,\, a,\, t\}$ (with $x_0$ often set to zero) uniquely determines the remaining two. This follows from the fact that the kinematic equations form a closed, linearly independent system derived by integrating $a = \text{const}$ twice with respect to time.

Payoff 🎯

What Problem Solving Kinematics Achieves

Kinematics is the branch of classical mechanics concerned exclusively with the description of motion—position, velocity, and acceleration as functions of time—without reference to the forces that produce that motion. The capstone skill of this chapter, problem solving in kinematics, synthesizes every preceding concept: displacement $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$, elapsed time $\Delta t$, average velocity $\bar{v} = \Delta x / \Delta t$, average acceleration $\bar{a} = \Delta v / \Delta t$, and the kinematic equations valid under constant acceleration. Mastery of this problem-solving framework transforms those definitions from isolated facts into a coherent analytical toolkit capable of predicting the future state of any uniformly accelerating system from a minimal set of known quantities.

The Constant-Acceleration Kinematic Equations

For motion along a single axis with constant acceleration $a$, four equations relate the five kinematic variables $\{x_0,\, x,\, v_0,\, v,\, a,\, t\}$:

$$ v = v_0 + at, \qquad x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 $$ $$ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0), \qquad x = x_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)t $$

Each equation omits exactly one variable, so the strategy is to identify the three knowns and the one unknown, then select the equation that contains all four.

Worked Example

A stone is dropped from rest at the edge of a cliff of height $H = 45\,\text{m}$. Find the speed at impact and the time of flight. Taking downward as positive with $x_0 = 0$, $v_0 = 0$, $a = g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$:

$$ v^2 = 2gH = 2(9.8)(45) = 882\,\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2 \implies v \approx 29.7\,\text{m/s} $$ $$ t = v/g \approx 3.03\,\text{s} $$

Fundamental Theorem of Constant-Acceleration Motion

Theorem. Under constant acceleration, displacement is a quadratic function of elapsed time, and velocity is a linear function of elapsed time. Consequently, the position–time graph is a parabola and the velocity–time graph is a line whose slope equals $a$; the area beneath that line over any interval equals the displacement in that interval.

Laboratory Exercise

Problem-solving kinematics is the natural endpoint of Chapter 2 because it unifies every kinematic quantity into a single decision procedure applicable to any constant-acceleration scenario. The framework scales directly into two-dimensional projectile motion—where horizontal and vertical axes are treated as independent kinematic problems—and into rotational kinematics, where angular displacement, angular velocity, and angular acceleration obey an identical mathematical structure. For a deeper investigation, consider exploring projectile motion: a thrown ball is nothing more than two simultaneous one-dimensional kinematic problems, and the elegance of that decomposition reveals just how powerful the tools you have built here truly are.
