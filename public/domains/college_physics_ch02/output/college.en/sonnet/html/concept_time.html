🌱 Time

🌱 Time

Definition. In classical mechanics, time is a scalar quantity that parameterizes the ordered sequence of events in a reference frame. Within the International System of Units (SI), the base unit of time is the second (s), defined as exactly 9,192,631,770 oscillation periods of radiation corresponding to the hyperfine transition of the ground state of cesium-133. Time is typically denoted $t$, and a duration (time interval) between two instants $t_1$ and $t_2$ is written

$$ \Delta t = t_2 - t_1. $$

Unlike position, time in Newtonian mechanics is absolute — all inertial observers agree on the duration $\Delta t$ between two events (a symmetry broken only in special relativity).

Worked Example. A sprinter crosses the starting line at $t_1 = 1.40\ \text{s}$ and the finish line at $t_2 = 11.65\ \text{s}$ on a race clock. Determine the elapsed time of the run.

$$ \Delta t = t_2 - t_1 = 11.65\ \text{s} - 1.40\ \text{s} = 10.25\ \text{s}. $$

Note that only the interval $\Delta t$ carries physical meaning; the choice of origin ($t = 0$) is a convention imposed by the observer. Had the clock been reset so that $t_1 = 0$, the result would be identical: $\Delta t = 10.25\ \text{s}$.

Key Theorem — Galilean Time Invariance. In Newtonian mechanics, under a Galilean transformation between two inertial frames $S$ and $S'$ moving with constant relative velocity $v$, positions transform as $x' = x - vt$ while time satisfies

$$ t' = t. $$

This invariance implies that the time interval $\Delta t$ is the same in every inertial reference frame, making it a universal parameter for describing motion. Consequently, kinematic equations such as $x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$ hold without modification in any inertial frame. Here $a$ denotes the (constant) acceleration — the one new quantity this section introduces as a preview of kinematics, defined formally in the next section.

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor and a freely falling ball, record the position $y$ of the ball at intervals of $\Delta t = 0.05\ \text{s}$. Plot $y$ versus $t^2$ and determine the free-fall acceleration $g$ from the slope of that graph. Assess whether your result agrees with the standard value $9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$ to within 5%, and discuss how the precision of your time measurements limits the accuracy of $g$.
