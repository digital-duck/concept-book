🍃 Average Acceleration

🍃 Average Acceleration

Imagine you are riding a roller coaster. For most of the ride, your acceleration is constantly changing—sometimes rapidly positive when dropping, sometimes negative when braking. However, if we only care about what happened between the moment you left the platform and the moment you reached the first curve, we can calculate one single value: the average acceleration. This concept allows us to quantify the overall change in motion over a specific time interval, providing a stable metric even when the forces applied are variable.

Mathematically, average acceleration ($\bar{a}$) is defined as the total change in an object's velocity ($\Delta v$) divided by the total elapsed time ($\Delta t$). It measures the rate at which the velocity changes from an initial value ($v_i$) to a final value ($v_f$). The formula we use is:

$$ \bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t_f - t_i} $$

Consider a car that starts from rest at an intersection and accelerates uniformly to $30 \, \text{m/s}$ over a period of $6.0$ seconds. We can calculate the average acceleration it experienced:

$$ \bar{a} = \frac{30 \, \text{m/s} - 0 \, \text{m/s}}{6.0 \, \text{s}} = 5.0 \, \text{m/s}^2 $$

The positive sign confirms that the velocity increased over time.
