🌱 Inertial Reference Frame

🌱 Inertial Reference Frame

Definition. An inertial reference frame is any frame of reference in which Newton's first law holds: a body subject to no net force moves with constant velocity (or remains at rest). Equivalently, an inertial frame is one that is either at rest or moving with constant velocity relative to other inertial frames. No accelerating or rotating frame is inertial, because fictitious forces (such as the Coriolis or centrifugal force) appear in such frames, violating Newton's first law in its standard form.

Special relativity elevates this concept to a foundational postulate. Einstein's first postulate asserts that the laws of physics take the same mathematical form in every inertial frame. There is no preferred or "absolute" inertial frame; all inertial frames are physically equivalent. The second postulate states that the speed of light in vacuum, $c = 2.998 \times 10^8\ \mathrm{m/s}$, is the same in all inertial frames regardless of the motion of the source or observer.

Worked Example. A laboratory on Earth is, to excellent approximation, an inertial frame for most dynamics experiments. An observer $S$ on a stationary platform measures a photon traveling in the $+x$ direction with speed $c$. A second observer $S'$ rides a rocket moving at $v = 0.60\,c$ in the $+x$ direction relative to $S$. Galilean mechanics would predict that $S'$ measures the photon's speed as $c - 0.60c = 0.40c$. However, special relativity requires the velocity addition formula:

$$ u' = \frac{u - v}{1 - uv/c^2} = \frac{c - 0.60c}{1 - (c)(0.60c)/c^2} = \frac{0.40c}{0.40} = c. $$

Both frames measure the photon speed as exactly $c$, consistent with Einstein's second postulate.

Key Theorem (Equivalence of Inertial Frames). If frame $S$ is inertial and $S'$ moves with constant velocity $\mathbf{v}$ relative to $S$, then $S'$ is also inertial, and the Lorentz transformation connects the two: $x' = \gamma(x - vt)$, $t' = \gamma(t - vx/c^2)$, where $\gamma = (1 - v^2/c^2)^{-1/2}$.

Lab Exercise. Using a stationary source and a light-speed timing apparatus, design a thought experiment to verify that an observer moving at $v = 0.80\,c$ relative to the source would still record photon speed $c$. Compute $\gamma$ for this frame and identify which measured quantity — length, time interval, or photon speed — remains invariant.
