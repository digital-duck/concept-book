🌸 Gps Relativistic Corrections

🌸 Gps Relativistic Corrections

Definition. The Global Positioning System (GPS) relies on a constellation of satellites carrying atomic clocks that transmit precise timing signals. Because these satellites orbit at high altitude and high velocity, two relativistic effects—predicted by special and general relativity, respectively—cause their onboard clocks to run at rates measurably different from clocks on Earth's surface. Special relativity predicts that a moving clock runs slow relative to a stationary observer (time dilation), while general relativity predicts that a clock in a weaker gravitational field runs fast relative to one deeper in a gravitational well (gravitational blueshift). The GPS system must account for both corrections simultaneously to maintain the sub-meter positional accuracy users expect.

Worked Example. GPS satellites orbit at altitude $h \approx 20{,}200\ \text{km}$ with orbital speed $v \approx 3.87\ \text{km/s}$.

Special-relativistic correction (time dilation): The fractional rate change is

$$ \frac{\Delta f_{\text{SR}}}{f} = -\frac{v^2}{2c^2} \approx -\frac{(3.87\times10^3)^2}{2(3\times10^8)^2} \approx -8.35\times10^{-11}, $$

causing satellite clocks to lose roughly $7.2\ \mu\text{s/day}$.

General-relativistic correction (gravitational blueshift): Using $\Delta\Phi = g_{\text{eff}}\,h$ approximated with $g \approx 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$,

$$ \frac{\Delta f_{\text{GR}}}{f} = \frac{\Delta\Phi}{c^2} \approx \frac{(9.8)(20.2\times10^6)}{(3\times10^8)^2} \approx +5.28\times10^{-10}, $$

gaining about $45.6\ \mu\text{s/day}$. The net effect is $+38.4\ \mu\text{s/day}$; uncorrected, this would produce position errors exceeding $10\ \text{km/day}$.

Key Theorem. The combined fractional frequency offset between a satellite clock and a ground clock is

$$ \frac{\Delta f}{f} = \frac{\Delta\Phi}{c^2} - \frac{v^2}{2c^2}, $$

where $\Delta\Phi$ is the gravitational potential difference and $v$ is the satellite's orbital speed. GPS satellites are pre-adjusted to broadcast at a frequency slightly lower than their nominal $10.23\ \text{MHz}$ oscillator frequency, so that after relativistic effects the received signal matches the ground standard.
