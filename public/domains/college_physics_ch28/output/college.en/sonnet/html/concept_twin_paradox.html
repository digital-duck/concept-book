🌸 Twin Paradox

Definition. The twin paradox is a thought experiment in special relativity that exposes an apparent contradiction in time dilation. Two identical twins separate: one remains on Earth (an inertial frame), while the other travels at relativistic speed to a distant star and returns. Special relativity predicts that the traveling twin ages less than the stationary twin—a result that seems paradoxical because each twin, from their own frame, observes the other moving. The resolution lies in the asymmetry of the two worldlines: the traveling twin undergoes acceleration when reversing direction, which breaks the equivalence of the two frames.

The time dilation formula governs the effect. If the traveling twin moves at speed $v$ relative to Earth, the proper time $\tau$ elapsed on the traveler's clock is related to the coordinate time $t$ measured on Earth by:

$$ \tau = \frac{t}{\gamma}, \qquad \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} $$

Since $\gamma \geq 1$, the traveler's elapsed time is always less than or equal to the Earth twin's elapsed time.

Worked Example. Suppose twin B travels to a star $d = 4$ light-years away at $v = 0.8c$, then returns at the same speed. From Earth (twin A's frame), the round-trip coordinate time is:

$$ t = \frac{2d}{v} = \frac{2 \times 4 \text{ ly}}{0.8c} = 10 \text{ years} $$

The Lorentz factor is $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1 - 0.64} = 1/0.6 \approx 1.667$. Twin B's proper elapsed time is:

$$ \tau = \frac{t}{\gamma} = \frac{10}{1.667} \approx 6 \text{ years} $$

Twin A ages 10 years; twin B ages only 6 years. There is no paradox because twin B's frame is non-inertial—twin B must decelerate and accelerate at the turnaround, producing a genuine asymmetry.

Key Theorem. The proper time $\tau = \int \sqrt{1 - v(t)^2/c^2}\, dt$ is a Lorentz-invariant quantity: all inertial observers agree on the elapsed time recorded by a given clock along its worldline. The twin with the longer worldline in spacetime (the inertial twin) accumulates the greater coordinate time, while the traveler's curved worldline yields the lesser proper time.

Lab Exercise. Using muon lifetime data from cosmic-ray experiments, calculate the fraction of muons created at an altitude of $h = 15$ km that survive to sea level if they travel at $v = 0.998c$ and have a rest-frame mean lifetime of $\tau_0 = 2.2\;\mu\text{s}$. Compare the result with and without relativistic time dilation to demonstrate that the dilated lifetime is required to match observed muon flux.
