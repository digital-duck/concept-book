🌸 Muon Lifetime

🌸 Muon Lifetime

Definition. The muon is an unstable elementary particle with rest mass $m_\mu = 105.7\ \text{MeV}/c^2$, approximately 207 times the electron mass. In its own rest frame, the muon decays via the weak interaction with a characteristic mean lifetime of

$$ \tau_0 = 2.197\ \mu\text{s}. $$

This is the proper lifetime—the decay time measured in the frame co-moving with the muon. For a muon traveling at relativistic speed $v$ relative to a laboratory observer, special relativity predicts that the observed mean lifetime is dilated to

$$ \tau = \gamma\,\tau_0, \qquad \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}, $$

where $\gamma \geq 1$ is the Lorentz factor. Consequently, fast-moving muons survive far longer in the lab frame than their proper lifetime would naively suggest.

Worked Example. Cosmic-ray interactions in the upper atmosphere produce muons at an altitude of approximately $h = 15\ \text{km}$, moving downward at $v = 0.998\,c$.

Compute $\gamma$:

$$ \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-(0.998)^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.996004}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.003996}} \approx 15.8. $$

The dilated lifetime in the lab frame is:

$$ \tau = 15.8 \times 2.197\ \mu\text{s} \approx 34.7\ \mu\text{s}. $$

The distance a muon can travel before decay:

$$ d = v\tau = (0.998)(3\times10^8\ \text{m/s})(34.7\times10^{-6}\ \text{s}) \approx 10.4\ \text{km}. $$

Without time dilation ($d_0 = v\tau_0 \approx 0.66\ \text{km}$), virtually no muons would reach sea level. Experimentally, significant muon flux is detected at Earth's surface, confirming the time-dilation prediction.

Key Theorem. The number of muons surviving after laboratory time $t$ follows an exponential decay law modified by time dilation:

$$ N(t) = N_0\,e^{-t/(\gamma\tau_0)}. $$

This result is a direct consequence of the invariance of the spacetime interval: the proper time elapsed in the muon's frame is $\Delta\tau = t/\gamma$, and decay is governed by proper time, not coordinate time.

Lab Exercise. A beam of muons is measured to have speed $v = 0.950\,c$ in the laboratory. A detector records their flux at two positions separated by $L = 3.00\ \text{km}$ along the beam direction. Compute (a) the Lorentz factor $\gamma$, (b) the expected ratio $N_2/N_1$ of muon counts at the two detector positions, and (c) the contracted distance $L' = L/\gamma$ as measured in the muon rest frame. Verify that the same decay fraction is obtained using $L'$ and $\tau_0$ directly.
