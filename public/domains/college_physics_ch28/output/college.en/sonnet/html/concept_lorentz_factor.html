🍃 Lorentz Factor

🍃 Lorentz Factor

Definition. The Lorentz factor $\gamma$ is a dimensionless quantity that appears throughout special relativity, encoding how dramatically space and time measurements differ between inertial reference frames in relative motion. For an object moving at speed $v$ relative to an observer, it is defined as

$$ \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \dfrac{v^2}{c^2}}} $$

where $c \approx 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$ is the speed of light in vacuum. The ratio $\beta = v/c$ is often introduced for compactness, so $\gamma = (1-\beta^2)^{-1/2}$. By inspection, $\gamma \geq 1$ for all physically attainable speeds ($v < c$), with $\gamma \to 1$ as $v \to 0$ (recovering Newtonian mechanics) and $\gamma \to \infty$ as $v \to c$.

Worked Example. A muon produced in the upper atmosphere travels toward Earth at $v = 0.990\,c$. Calculate its Lorentz factor.

$$ \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - (0.990)^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.9801}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.0199}} \approx \frac{1}{0.1411} \approx 7.09 $$

This value directly explains the muon's apparent longevity: its proper half-life of $\tau_0 \approx 1.52\ \mu\text{s}$ is dilated in the Earth frame to $\tau = \gamma\,\tau_0 \approx 10.8\ \mu\text{s}$, allowing it to traverse roughly $3.2\ \text{km}$ before decaying — far more than the $\sim 450\ \text{m}$ Newtonian mechanics would predict.

Key Theorem. The Lorentz factor enters every kinematic transformation of special relativity. Time dilation and length contraction are given, respectively, by

$$ \Delta t = \gamma\,\Delta t_0, \qquad L = \frac{L_0}{\gamma} $$

where subscript-zero quantities are measured in the rest frame of the object. Relativistic momentum and total energy follow as $p = \gamma m v$ and $E = \gamma m c^2$, the latter yielding the rest energy $E_0 = mc^2$ when $v = 0$.

Lab Exercise. An electron is accelerated through a potential difference of $V = 5.11 \times 10^5\ \text{V}$ (equal to its rest-mass energy in electron-volts). Using energy conservation, show that its kinetic energy equals $m_e c^2$, then determine $\gamma$ and compute $v/c$. Verify that a naïve non-relativistic calculation would predict $v > c$, illustrating why the Lorentz factor is indispensable at high energies.
