🍃 Proper Length

🍃 Proper Length

Definition. The proper length $L_0$ of an object is the length measured in the reference frame in which the object is at rest. It represents the maximum length that can be measured for the object; any observer in relative motion with respect to the object will measure a contracted length. Proper length is an invariant quantity associated with the object itself, independent of any particular observer's state of motion.

When an observer moves at velocity $v$ relative to an object along the axis of its extent, the measured length $L$ is related to the proper length by the length contraction formula:

$$ L = \frac{L_0}{\gamma} = L_0\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}} $$

where $\gamma = \left(1 - v^2/c^2\right)^{-1/2}$ is the Lorentz factor and $c$ is the speed of light in vacuum. Since $\gamma \geq 1$, we always have $L \leq L_0$, with equality only when $v = 0$.

Worked Example. A muon traveling at $v = 0.990\,c$ relative to Earth approaches from an altitude of $h_0 = 4.00\ \text{km}$ above sea level (measured in Earth's frame). What is the proper length of the atmospheric path the muon must traverse—that is, what length does the muon measure?

In the muon's rest frame, it is the atmosphere that moves toward it. The Lorentz factor is:

$$ \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - (0.990)^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.0199}} \approx 7.09 $$

The proper length of the path in the muon's frame is:

$$ L = \frac{L_0}{\gamma} = \frac{4000\ \text{m}}{7.09} \approx 564\ \text{m} $$

The muon measures a path of only $564\ \text{m}$, explaining why relativistic muons—despite their short mean lifetime of $2.20\ \mu\text{s}$—routinely reach Earth's surface.

Key Theorem. The spacetime interval $\Delta s^2 = c^2\Delta t^2 - \Delta x^2$ is Lorentz-invariant. For a purely spatial separation ($\Delta t = 0$ in the rest frame), $|\Delta s| = L_0$, confirming that proper length corresponds to the invariant magnitude of a spacelike interval measured simultaneously in the object's rest frame.

Lab Exercise. Using a muon detector at two altitudes separated by $L_0 = 1.00\ \text{km}$, a student measures the muon flux ratio and infers an effective path length of $L = 141\ \text{m}$ in the muon frame. Calculate the muon speed $v$ as a fraction of $c$ and determine the corresponding Lorentz factor $\gamma$.
