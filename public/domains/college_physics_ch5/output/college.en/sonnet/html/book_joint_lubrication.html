🌱 Normal Force

🌱 Normal Force

Definition. The normal force $\vec{N}$ is the contact force exerted by a surface on an object perpendicular to the surface at the point of contact. It is a reactive force arising from the electromagnetic interactions between the atoms of the object and those of the surface. Crucially, the normal force is not universally equal to the object's weight; its magnitude is determined by Newton's second law applied to the direction perpendicular to the surface.

For an object of mass $m$ resting on a horizontal surface in equilibrium, the net force in the vertical direction is zero:

$$ \sum F_y = N - mg = 0 \implies N = mg $$

When the surface is inclined at angle $\theta$ from the horizontal, resolving forces along the axis perpendicular to the surface gives:

$$ N = mg\cos\theta $$

More generally, whenever an external force $F_{\text{ext}}$ has a component pressing the object into or away from the surface, the normal force adjusts accordingly.

Worked Example. A 10.0 kg box rests on a frictionless incline of $30^\circ$. Find the normal force.

Taking axes parallel and perpendicular to the incline, the weight component perpendicular to the surface is $mg\cos 30^\circ$. Since there is no acceleration perpendicular to the surface:

$$ N = mg\cos 30^\circ = (10.0\,\text{kg})(9.80\,\text{m/s}^2)\cos 30^\circ = (98.0\,\text{N})(0.866) \approx 84.9\,\text{N} $$

Note that $N < mg = 98.0\,\text{N}$, consistent with the incline supporting only a fraction of the full weight.

Key Theorem (Newton's Third Law Pair). The normal force $\vec{N}$ exerted by the surface on the object and the reaction force $\vec{N}'$ exerted by the object on the surface form a Newton's third law action–reaction pair: $\vec{N}' = -\vec{N}$. These forces act on different bodies and therefore do not cancel in a free-body diagram.

Lab Exercise. Place a bathroom scale on a flat surface and stand on it while holding a 5.0 kg mass. Record the reading $W_1$. Now push down on the mass with a measured force $F = 20\,\text{N}$ and record $W_2$. Predict both readings using $N = mg + F_{\text{applied}}$ and compare your predictions with the measured values, commenting on any discrepancy.

🌱 Coefficient Of Kinetic Friction

🌱 Coefficient Of Kinetic Friction

Definition. When two surfaces are in relative motion with respect to each other, the resistive force opposing that motion is called kinetic friction. The coefficient of kinetic friction, $\mu_k$, is a dimensionless scalar that characterizes the frictional interaction between a specific pair of surfaces. It is defined through the relation

$$ f_k = \mu_k N, $$

where $f_k$ is the magnitude of the kinetic friction force and $N$ is the magnitude of the normal force exerted by one surface on the other. The coefficient $\mu_k$ depends on the materials and surface conditions of the two objects in contact, but is independent of the contact area and, to a good approximation, independent of the relative speed of the surfaces.

Worked Example. A 5.0 kg wooden block slides across a horizontal concrete floor. The coefficient of kinetic friction between wood and concrete is $\mu_k = 0.30$. Find the kinetic friction force acting on the block.

Since the floor is horizontal and no vertical acceleration occurs, the normal force equals the gravitational force:

$$ N = mg = (5.0\,\text{kg})(9.8\,\text{m/s}^2) = 49\,\text{N}. $$

Applying the definition:

$$ f_k = \mu_k N = (0.30)(49\,\text{N}) = 14.7\,\text{N}. $$

This force acts opposite to the direction of the block's motion, decelerating it at $a = f_k/m = 2.94\,\text{m/s}^2$.

Key Theorem (Amontons–Coulomb Law). For macroscopic dry surfaces under moderate loads, kinetic friction satisfies $f_k = \mu_k N$ with $\mu_k$ constant. This result is independent of apparent contact area and, within a broad range, independent of sliding speed. Empirically, $\mu_k < \mu_s$, where $\mu_s$ is the coefficient of static friction for the same pair of surfaces — that is, a greater force is required to initiate sliding than to sustain it. While microscopic models attribute friction to adhesion and asperity deformation at real contact junctions, the macroscopic law provides excellent predictive accuracy for introductory mechanics problems.

🍃 Friction

🍃 Friction

Definition. Friction is a contact force that opposes relative motion (or the tendency of motion) between two surfaces in contact. It arises from microscopic interactions—adhesion and asperity interlocking—at the interface of the surfaces. Two distinct regimes are recognized. Static friction $f_s$ prevents the onset of motion and satisfies

$$ f_s \leq \mu_s N, $$

where $\mu_s$ is the coefficient of static friction and $N$ is the normal force. Kinetic friction $f_k$ acts on surfaces already sliding relative to each other and is modeled as

$$ f_k = \mu_k N, $$

with $\mu_k < \mu_s$ in virtually all material pairs. Both coefficients are dimensionless empirical constants that depend on the materials and surface conditions, not on the contact area or sliding speed (within the classical regime).

Worked Example. A 12.0 kg crate rests on a horizontal floor with $\mu_s = 0.55$ and $\mu_k = 0.40$. A horizontal applied force $F$ is gradually increased from zero.

  1. Maximum static friction: $N = mg = (12.0)(9.80) = 117.6\ \text{N}$, so $f_{s,\max} = \mu_s N = (0.55)(117.6) = 64.7\ \text{N}$. The crate does not move for $F \leq 64.7\ \text{N}$.
  2. Kinetic friction once sliding: $f_k = \mu_k N = (0.40)(117.6) = 47.0\ \text{N}$. If $F = 70.0\ \text{N}$ is maintained after the crate begins to slide, the net force is $70.0 - 47.0 = 23.0\ \text{N}$, giving acceleration $a = 23.0/12.0 \approx 1.92\ \text{m/s}^2$.

Key Theorem (Amontons–Coulomb Laws). The classical friction model rests on two empirical laws: (i) the friction force is proportional to the normal force, and (ii) the friction force is independent of the apparent contact area. A consequence is that on an incline of angle $\theta$, an object is on the verge of sliding when $\tan\theta = \mu_s$, providing a clean experimental method to measure $\mu_s$ directly.

🍃 Kinetic Friction

🍃 Kinetic Friction

Definition. Kinetic friction is the contact force that opposes the relative motion of two surfaces sliding against each other. When an object moves across a surface, the frictional force acts parallel to the interface and antiparallel to the velocity of the moving object. Quantitatively, the magnitude of the kinetic friction force is given by

$$ f_k = \mu_k N $$

where $\mu_k$ is the coefficient of kinetic friction (a dimensionless, empirically determined constant dependent on the materials in contact) and $N$ is the magnitude of the normal force pressing the surfaces together. Unlike static friction, kinetic friction does not vary with the applied force — once sliding occurs, $f_k$ remains essentially constant regardless of speed (for most everyday conditions).

Worked Example. A 10 kg wooden crate is pushed across a concrete floor with a horizontal applied force of 50 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction between wood and concrete is $\mu_k = 0.30$. Determine the crate's acceleration.

First, identify the normal force. Since the floor is horizontal and there is no vertical acceleration:

$$ N = mg = (10\,\text{kg})(9.8\,\text{m/s}^2) = 98\,\text{N} $$

The kinetic friction force is therefore:

$$ f_k = \mu_k N = (0.30)(98\,\text{N}) = 29.4\,\text{N} $$

Applying Newton's second law in the horizontal direction:

$$ \sum F_x = F_{\text{applied}} - f_k = ma $$ $$ 50\,\text{N} - 29.4\,\text{N} = (10\,\text{kg})\,a $$ $$ a = \frac{20.6\,\text{N}}{10\,\text{kg}} = 2.06\,\text{m/s}^2 $$

Key Theorem. For a body undergoing kinetic friction, the work done by friction over a displacement $d$ is

$$ W_f = -f_k\, d = -\mu_k N\, d $$

This quantity is always negative, confirming that kinetic friction removes mechanical energy from the system and converts it to thermal energy. The total thermal energy generated is $\Delta E_{\text{th}} = \mu_k N\, d$.

Place a block of known mass on an inclined board and increase the tilt angle $\theta$ until the block slides at constant velocity (net force zero, so $f_k = mg\sin\theta$ and $N = mg\cos\theta$). Compute $\mu_k = \tan\theta$. Repeat with different masses and verify that $\mu_k$ is independent of the normal force magnitude.

🌸 Joint Lubrication

🌸 Joint Lubrication

Definition. Joint lubrication refers to the physical and biochemical mechanisms by which synovial fluid reduces friction between articulating cartilage surfaces in diarthrodial joints. From a physics perspective, lubrication falls under the study of tribology and fluid mechanics. The coefficient of kinetic friction $\mu_k$ between two surfaces separated by a lubricating film is governed by the viscosity $\eta$ of the fluid and the relative velocity $v$ of the surfaces. In hydrodynamic (fluid-film) lubrication, a pressure gradient develops within the fluid wedge, generating a normal force that prevents direct surface contact. The Stribeck curve characterizes lubrication regimes by the dimensionless Hersey number:

$$ H = \frac{\eta v}{P} $$

where $P$ is the contact pressure. At high $H$, full fluid-film lubrication dominates; at low $H$, boundary lubrication (surface-adsorbed molecules) governs.

Worked Example. Consider the hip joint under static standing load. Suppose the applied compressive force across the joint is $F = 1800\ \text{N}$, the contact area is $A = 20\ \text{cm}^2 = 20 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2$, and the synovial fluid has viscosity $\eta = 0.005\ \text{Pa·s}$. The average contact pressure is:

$$ P = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{1800}{20 \times 10^{-4}} = 9.0 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa} $$

During slow walking, the relative surface velocity is approximately $v = 0.02\ \text{m/s}$. The Hersey number becomes:

$$ H = \frac{(0.005)(0.02)}{9.0 \times 10^5} \approx 1.1 \times 10^{-10} $$

This extremely low value indicates boundary lubrication dominates, consistent with the role of lubricin (a surface-active glycoprotein) in protecting cartilage at low speeds and high loads.

Key Theorem (Reynolds Equation). For a thin viscous film between converging surfaces, the pressure distribution satisfies the Reynolds equation:

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(h^3 \frac{\partial p}{\partial x}\right) = 6\eta U \frac{\partial h}{\partial x} $$

where $h(x)$ is the film thickness and $U$ is the relative sliding speed. This result predicts that a converging geometry is necessary to generate load-supporting pressure in hydrodynamic lubrication.

Payoff 🎯

Joint lubrication is the process by which a thin fluid film—synovial fluid in biological joints, or engineered lubricants in mechanical bearings—reduces friction and wear between two articulating surfaces. Formally, the phenomenon is governed by the Reynolds lubrication equation, a simplification of the Navier–Stokes equations under the assumption of thin-film geometry:

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(h^3 \frac{\partial p}{\partial x}\right) = 6\mu U \frac{\partial h}{\partial x} $$

where $h(x)$ is the film thickness, $p$ is the pressure distribution, $\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity of the fluid, and $U$ is the relative surface velocity. The pressure gradient generated by the converging geometry supports a normal load $W = \int p \, dA$, preventing direct solid-to-solid contact.

Worked Example. Consider a simplified slider bearing of length $L = 0.10\ \text{m}$, with inlet film thickness $h_1 = 0.20\ \text{mm}$ and outlet $h_2 = 0.10\ \text{mm}$, lubricant viscosity $\mu = 0.03\ \text{Pa·s}$, and surface speed $U = 2.0\ \text{m/s}$. Applying the integrated Reynolds equation yields a peak pressure on the order of $p_{\max} \approx 6\mu U L / h_2^2 \approx 3.6 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$, sufficient to support meaningful loads with zero mechanical contact.

Key Theorem (Sommerfeld Similarity). The load-carrying capacity of a hydrodynamic bearing scales as $W \propto \mu U L^2 / h^2$. This result implies that reducing film thickness by half quadruples the sustainable load—explaining why precision engineering demands tight tolerances and why cartilage degradation in arthritic joints catastrophically reduces load support.

Lab Exercise. Assemble a parallel-plate viscometer using two glass slides separated by calibrated spacers. Fill the gap with fluids of known viscosity ($\mu = 0.001$ to $0.1\ \text{Pa·s}$), apply a measured lateral force, and record the velocity of the upper plate. Plot $F/A$ versus $U/h$ to extract $\mu$ from the slope, and compare measured values to published data. Observe how reducing $h$ below $50\ \mu\text{m}$ transitions the system toward boundary lubrication, where the linear Couette model breaks down.
