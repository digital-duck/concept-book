🌱 Time

🌱 Time

Definition. In classical mechanics, time is treated as an absolute, universal parameter $t \in \mathbb{R}$, independent of the observer or the physical system under study. Following Newton's Principia Mathematica, absolute time "flows equably without relation to anything external." Formally, the state of a mechanical system is described by a trajectory $\mathbf{q}(t)$ in configuration space, where $t$ parametrizes the sequence of states. The instantaneous rate of change of any observable $f(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}}, t)$ with respect to $t$ defines the dynamical evolution of the system. Time is measured in SI units of seconds (s), defined operationally by atomic transition frequencies.

Worked Example. Consider a particle undergoing uniform acceleration from rest. Its position is given by

$$ x(t) = \frac{1}{2}a t^2, $$

with $a = 2\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$. To find the time at which the particle has traveled $x = 9\ \mathrm{m}$, solve

$$ 9 = \frac{1}{2}(2)t^2 \implies t^2 = 9 \implies t = 3\ \mathrm{s}. $$

Note that only the positive root is retained, since $t \geq 0$ by convention when measuring elapsed time from an initial event. This illustrates how $t$ serves as a monotonically increasing index of physical states.

Key Theorem (Time-Translation Symmetry). If the Lagrangian $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}})$ of a closed system has no explicit dependence on $t$, then by Noether's theorem the total energy $E$ is conserved:

$$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial t} = 0 \implies \frac{dE}{dt} = 0. $$

Proof sketch. Compute the total time derivative of $\mathcal{L}$ along a solution trajectory, apply the Euler–Lagrange equations to eliminate $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial \mathbf{q}$, and identify the conserved Hamiltonian $H = \dot{\mathbf{q}}\cdot\nabla_{\dot{\mathbf{q}}}\mathcal{L} - \mathcal{L} = E$.

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor and a cart on a frictionless air track, record position $x$ at uniform time intervals $\Delta t = 0.05\ \mathrm{s}$. Compute the finite-difference approximation to velocity,

$$ v_i \approx \frac{x_{i+1} - x_{i-1}}{2\Delta t}, $$

and plot $v$ versus $t$. Determine whether the slope $dv/dt$ is constant, and identify whether energy is conserved over the interval. Discuss how measurement precision in $t$ propagates into uncertainty in $v$.
