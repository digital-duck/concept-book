🍃 Kinetic Energy

🍃 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ is the scalar quantity

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}m\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \frac{1}{2}m v^2, $$

where $v = \|\mathbf{v}\|$ denotes the speed. Kinetic energy is measured in joules ($\text{J} = \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-2}$) and is always non-negative.

Worked Example. A hockey puck of mass $m = 0.170\ \text{kg}$ slides across frictionless ice with speed $v = 12.0\ \text{m/s}$. Its kinetic energy is

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(0.170\ \text{kg})(12.0\ \text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.170)(144) = 12.24\ \text{J}. $$

If the puck decelerates uniformly to rest over a distance $d = 3.00\ \text{m}$ due to a resistive force $f$, the work–energy theorem gives $-f\,d = \Delta K = 0 - 12.24\ \text{J}$, so $f = 4.08\ \text{N}$.

Key Theorem (Work–Energy Theorem). For a particle subject to a net force $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}$, the net work done as the particle moves from position $\mathbf{r}_1$ to $\mathbf{r}_2$ equals the change in kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \int_{\mathbf{r}_1}^{\mathbf{r}_2} \mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \Delta K = K_2 - K_1. $$

Proof sketch. Apply Newton's second law $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = m\dot{\mathbf{v}}$ and use the identity $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = m\dot{\mathbf{v}}\cdot\mathbf{v}\,dt = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{1}{2}mv^2\right)dt$. Integrating both sides over the time interval yields $W_{\text{net}} = K_2 - K_1$. $\square$

This theorem is coordinate-independent and holds whether or not the forces are conservative, making it one of the most broadly applicable results in mechanics.

Lab Exercise. Mount a dynamics cart ($m = 0.500\ \text{kg}$) on a frictionless track. Attach a hanging mass $M = 0.050\ \text{kg}$ via a string over a pulley.
