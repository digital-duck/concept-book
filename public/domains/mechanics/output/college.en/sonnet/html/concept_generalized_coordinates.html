🍃 Generalized Coordinates

🍃 Generalized Coordinates

Definition. In a mechanical system subject to $k$ holonomic constraints, the $3N$ Cartesian coordinates $\{x_i\}$ of $N$ particles are not all independent. Generalized coordinates $\{q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_n\}$, where $n = 3N - k$, are any set of $n$ independent parameters that uniquely specify the configuration of the system. Formally, there exist smooth invertible maps

$$ x_i = x_i(q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_n, t), \qquad i = 1, \ldots, 3N, $$

such that every constraint is satisfied identically. The number $n$ is called the number of degrees of freedom of the system.

Worked Example: The Simple Pendulum. Consider a point mass $m$ attached to a massless rod of length $\ell$, constrained to move in a vertical plane. In Cartesian coordinates one writes $(x, y)$ subject to the constraint $x^2 + y^2 = \ell^2$. This single holonomic constraint reduces $3(1) - 1 = 2$ to $n = 1$ degree of freedom. Choosing the angle $\theta$ measured from the downward vertical as the single generalized coordinate, the Cartesian positions are recovered by

$$ x = \ell\sin\theta, \qquad y = -\ell\cos\theta. $$

The kinetic energy expressed in terms of $q_1 = \theta$ becomes $T = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot{\theta}^2$, a scalar function of one variable, free of any constraint multipliers.

Key Theorem (Kinetic Energy in Generalized Coordinates). Differentiating the transformation equations with respect to time gives

$$ \dot{x}_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{\partial x_i}{\partial q_j}\dot{q}_j + \frac{\partial x_i}{\partial t}, $$

so the kinetic energy $T = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i m_i \dot{x}_i^2$ becomes a function of $(q_1,\ldots,q_n,\dot{q}_1,\ldots,\dot{q}_n,t)$ alone. For time-independent (scleronomic) constraints, $T$ is a homogeneous quadratic form in the generalized velocities $\dot{q}_j$, and no constraint forces enter the expression. This reduction from constrained Cartesian velocities to unconstrained generalized velocities is the central result of the formalism and the starting point for deriving the equations of motion in subsequent sections.

Lab Exercise. A double pendulum consists of two rods of lengths $\ell_1$, $\ell_2$ and masses $m_1$, $m_2$. Identify two suitable generalized coordinates $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$, write down the transformation equations $\mathbf{r}_1(\theta_1)$ and $\mathbf{r}_2(\theta_1, \theta_2)$, and verify that the number of degrees of freedom equals two. Then express the kinetic energy $T$ and potential energy $V$ explicitly in terms of $\theta_1$, $\theta_2$, and their time derivatives, confirming that no constraint forces appear in the result.
