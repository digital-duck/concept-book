🍃 Euler Lagrange Equations

🍃 Euler Lagrange Equations

Definition. Let a mechanical system be described by $n$ generalized coordinates $q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_n$ and their time derivatives $\dot{q}_1, \dot{q}_2, \ldots, \dot{q}_n$. The Lagrangian of the system is the scalar function

$$ L(q_i, \dot{q}_i, t) = T - V, $$

where $T$ denotes the kinetic energy and $V$ the potential energy. Hamilton's principle asserts that the actual trajectory of the system is the one that makes the action functional

$$ S[q] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q_i, \dot{q}_i, t)\, dt $$

stationary under variations $\delta q_i$ that vanish at the endpoints. Applying the calculus of variations yields, for each coordinate $q_i$, the Euler–Lagrange equation:

$$ \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i} = 0, \qquad i = 1, 2, \ldots, n. $$

Worked Example: Simple Pendulum. Consider a pendulum of length $\ell$ and bob mass $m$ swinging in a vertical plane. Choose the generalized coordinate $q = \theta$, the angle from the vertical. The kinetic and potential energies are

$$ T = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot{\theta}^2, \qquad V = -mg\ell\cos\theta, $$

so $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot{\theta}^2 + mg\ell\cos\theta$. Computing the required partial derivatives:

$$ \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\theta}} = m\ell^2\dot{\theta}, \qquad \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\theta}}\right) = m\ell^2\ddot{\theta}, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta} = -mg\ell\sin\theta. $$

Substituting into the Euler–Lagrange equation gives

$$ m\ell^2\ddot{\theta} + mg\ell\sin\theta = 0 \implies \ddot{\theta} + \frac{g}{\ell}\sin\theta = 0, $$

which is the familiar equation of motion for the pendulum.

Key Theorem (Noether's Theorem — special case). If $L$ does not depend explicitly on a generalized coordinate $q_i$ (i.e., $\partial L/\partial q_i = 0$), then $q_i$ is called cyclic, and the corresponding generalized momentum $p_i = \partial L/\partial \dot{q}_i$ is a conserved quantity.

Using polar coordinates $(r, \phi)$ as generalized coordinates, write down the Lagrangian, derive both Euler–Lagrange equations, identify the cyclic coordinate, and state the conserved quantity explicitly.
