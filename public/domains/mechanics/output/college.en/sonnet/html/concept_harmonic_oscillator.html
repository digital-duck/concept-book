🍃 Harmonic Oscillator

🍃 Harmonic Oscillator

Definition. A simple harmonic oscillator (SHO) is a mechanical system in which the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from equilibrium and directed opposite to it. For a particle of mass $m$ displaced by $x$ from equilibrium, Newton's second law yields

$$ m\ddot{x} = -kx, \qquad k > 0, $$

where $k$ is the spring constant. The single new primitive this section introduces is the natural angular frequency

$$ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}, $$

which recasts the equation of motion as

$$ \ddot{x} + \omega_0^2\, x = 0. $$

The general solution is $x(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t) + B\sin(\omega_0 t)$, where the constants $A$ and $B$ are determined by the initial conditions $x(0)$ and $\dot{x}(0)$.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 0.5\,\text{kg}$ is attached to a spring with $k = 8\,\text{N/m}$. At $t = 0$ the block is displaced $x_0 = 0.1\,\text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest.

$$ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{8}{0.5}} = 4\,\text{rad/s}, \qquad T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} = \frac{\pi}{2}\,\text{s} \approx 1.57\,\text{s}. $$

Applying initial conditions $x(0)=x_0$ and $\dot{x}(0)=0$ gives $A = 0.1\,\text{m}$ and $B = 0$, so

$$ x(t) = 0.1\cos(4t)\,\text{m}. $$

The total mechanical energy is conserved: $E = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(8)(0.01) = 0.04\,\text{J}$.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Energy). For an undamped SHO, the total energy

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2 $$

is constant in time. Proof sketch: Differentiate $E$ with respect to $t$:

$$ \dot{E} = m\dot{x}\ddot{x} + kx\dot{x} = \dot{x}(m\ddot{x} + kx) = 0, $$

where the last equality follows from the equation of motion.

Lab Exercise. A spring–mass system has $m = 0.2\,\text{kg}$ and $k = 5\,\text{N/m}$. The mass is released from rest at $x_0 = 0.05\,\text{m}$.

  1. Compute $\omega_0$, the period $T$, and the total energy $E$.
  2. Find $x(t)$ and determine the speed of the mass when $x = 0.025\,\text{m}$.
  3. Sketch one complete cycle of $x(t)$ and label the positions of maximum kinetic and potential energy.
