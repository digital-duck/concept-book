🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. In classical mechanics, mass is an intrinsic scalar property of a body that quantifies its resistance to acceleration. Newton's second law encodes this precisely:

$$ \mathbf{F} = m\,\mathbf{a}. $$

A larger mass requires a proportionally larger net force to produce the same acceleration. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg). It is worth noting that mass also governs a body's gravitational coupling—the force on a body near Earth's surface is $\mathbf{F}_g = m\mathbf{g}$—and experiment confirms to one part in $10^{12}$ that the same scalar $m$ governs both roles, a result elevated to a postulate in general relativity.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 4.0\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A net horizontal force $F = 12\ \text{N}$ is applied. Find the acceleration.

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{12\ \text{N}}{4.0\ \text{kg}} = 3.0\ \text{m s}^{-2}. $$

The direction of $\mathbf{a}$ is parallel to $\mathbf{F}$. Note that doubling the mass while holding $F$ constant would halve the acceleration, illustrating the inverse proportionality $a \propto m^{-1}$.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Mass in Newtonian Mechanics). For a closed system of $n$ particles with no relativistic effects, the total mass

$$ M = \sum_{k=1}^{n} m_k $$

is constant in time: $\dfrac{dM}{dt} = 0$. This follows from the assumption that classical particles neither appear nor disappear and that mass is additive. Combined with Newton's third law, this guarantees conservation of linear momentum $\mathbf{p} = M\mathbf{v}_{\text{cm}}$ in the absence of external forces.

Lab Exercise. Using a dynamics cart, track, and calibrated weights, design an experiment to verify the relationship $a = F/m$:

  1. Fix $F$ by attaching a hanging mass via a string over a pulley; vary the cart mass $m$ in five steps from $0.20\ \text{kg}$ to $1.00\ \text{kg}$.
  2. Record $a$ from a motion sensor for each trial.
  3. Plot $a$ versus $m^{-1}$ and perform a linear regression; confirm that the slope equals $F$ to within $5\%$.
  4. Identify and quantify at least two sources of systematic error.
