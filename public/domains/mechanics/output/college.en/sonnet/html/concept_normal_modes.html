🍃 Normal Modes

🍃 Normal Modes

Definition. Consider a conservative mechanical system with $n$ degrees of freedom described by generalized coordinates $\mathbf{q} = (q_1, \dots, q_n)^T$. Near a stable equilibrium $\mathbf{q}_0$, the equations of motion take the linearized form

$$ M\ddot{\mathbf{q}} + K\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{0}, $$

where $M$ is the symmetric positive-definite mass matrix and $K$ is the symmetric positive-semidefinite stiffness matrix. A normal mode is a solution of the form $\mathbf{q}(t) = \mathbf{a}\cos(\omega t + \phi)$, where $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the mode shape and $\omega > 0$ is the natural frequency. Substituting this ansatz yields the generalized eigenvalue problem

$$ K\mathbf{a} = \omega^2 M\mathbf{a}, $$

so each normal mode corresponds to an eigenpair $(\omega^2, \mathbf{a})$ of this system.

Worked Example. Consider two identical masses $m$ connected in series by three springs of stiffness $k$, with the outer ends fixed. The mass and stiffness matrices are

$$ M = m\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad K = k\begin{pmatrix}2 & -1\\ -1 & 2\end{pmatrix}. $$

Since $M = mI$, the eigenvalue problem reduces to $K\mathbf{a} = \lambda m\mathbf{a}$ with $\lambda = \omega^2$. The characteristic equation $\det(K - \lambda m I) = 0$ gives

$$ (2k - \lambda m)^2 - k^2 = 0 \implies \lambda_1 = \frac{k}{m},\quad \lambda_2 = \frac{3k}{m}. $$

The natural frequencies are $\omega_1 = \sqrt{k/m}$ and $\omega_2 = \sqrt{3k/m}$. The corresponding mode shapes are $\mathbf{a}_1 = (1,\,1)^T$ (symmetric: both masses move in phase) and $\mathbf{a}_2 = (1,\,-1)^T$ (antisymmetric: masses move out of phase). These two motions are the only purely harmonic solutions of the system.

Key Theorem (Orthogonality of Normal Modes). If $\omega_i \neq \omega_j$, then the mode shapes satisfy

$$ \mathbf{a}_i^T M\, \mathbf{a}_j = 0 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \mathbf{a}_i^T K\, \mathbf{a}_j = 0. $$

Proof sketch. Write the eigenvalue equations for modes $i$ and $j$, premultiply each by the other mode shape, subtract, and use the symmetry of $M$ and $K$. Since $\omega_i^2 \neq \omega_j^2$, the $M$-inner product must vanish, and the $K$-orthogonality follows immediately. This $M$-orthogonality is the key property that will, in the next section, allow any free motion to be decomposed into independent harmonic oscillators.

Lab Exercise. Mount two equal masses on a frictionless track connected by three springs. Record displacement time series for each mass using motion sensors, then apply a discrete Fourier transform to identify the two natural frequencies experimentally. Compare the ratio $\omega_2/\omega_1$ to the theoretical value $\sqrt{3}$ and compute the percentage error.
