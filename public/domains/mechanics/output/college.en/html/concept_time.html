🌱 Time

Time ($t$) is a fundamental dimension used to quantify change and order events within the physical universe. Conceptually, it represents the parameterization of evolving states of a system. In classical Newtonian mechanics, time was treated as an absolute, universal quantity—a backdrop against which all physical processes unfolded. However, modern physics, particularly Special and General Relativity, reveals that time is not independent but is intrinsically interwoven with space into a single manifold known as spacetime ($\mathcal{M}$). Mathematically, the passage of time is often represented by a coordinate $x^0$, where coordinates are typically written as $(ct, x, y, z)$. The core definition relates to the infinitesimal temporal separation between two events, $\Delta t$. This interval must be analyzed alongside spatial separations to understand how various physical measurements change relative to observers in different inertial frames.

Consider calculating the time elapsed for a particle moving at a constant velocity $v$ between two points separated by a distance $L$. The basic relationship is $\Delta t = L/v$. A more sophisticated approach involves examining the proper time ($\tau$), which is the time interval measured by a clock that moves along the path of the object. If an observer measures a time interval $\Delta t$, but the moving particle experiences only $\Delta \tau$, they are related by the Lorentz transformation: $\Delta \tau = \Delta t / \gamma$, where $\gamma$ is the Lorentz factor, defined as $\gamma = (1 - v^2/c^2)^{-1/2}$. This relationship demonstrates that time measurement itself depends on the relative velocity between the observer and the event being timed.

A crucial concept governing time's geometry is the spacetime interval ($ds^2$), which defines the invariant distance between two events, regardless of the reference frame:

$$ds^2 = c^2 d\tau^2 = \eta_{\mu\nu} dx^\mu dx^\nu$$

where $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ is the metric tensor (e.g., diag$(1, -1, -1, -1)$ in Minkowski space). The magnitude of this interval is invariant under coordinate transformations.

Key Theorem: The Principle of Relativity states that the laws of physics are the same for all observers in uniform motion (inertial frames). This principle necessitates the time dilation and length contraction effects described by the Lorentz transformation, fundamentally linking temporal measurement to relative velocity.

Lab Exercise: Using a modern atomic clock or a GPS receiver, measure the elapsed time $\Delta t$ between two points on Earth that are separated vertically by a significant altitude difference (e.g., measuring synchronization between ground level and an elevated platform). Given your measured distance $h$ and the known gravitational acceleration $g$, calculate the predicted difference in proper time ($\Delta \tau$) due to gravitational time dilation using the weak-field approximation of General Relativity, $\Delta t - \Delta \tau \approx gh/c^2$.
