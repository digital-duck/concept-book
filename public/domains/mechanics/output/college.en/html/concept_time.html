Time

Time

Definition. In classical mechanics and everyday analysis, time is a one-dimensional, totally ordered, continuous parameter $t \in \mathbb{R}$ that indexes the state of a system. Formally, a time domain is a connected subset $\mathcal{T} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, typically $[0, T]$ or $[0, \infty)$. A trajectory is a function $\mathbf{x} : \mathcal{T} \to \mathcal{S}$, where $\mathcal{S}$ is the state space. The rate of change of $\mathbf{x}$ at time $t$ is the derivative

$$\dot{\mathbf{x}}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\mathbf{x}(t + \Delta t) - \mathbf{x}(t)}{\Delta t},$$

provided the limit exists. In special relativity, time is demoted from an absolute parameter to one coordinate of a four-dimensional spacetime manifold $(\mathbb{R}^{1,3}, \eta)$, where $\eta = \operatorname{diag}(-1, 1, 1, 1)$ is the Minkowski metric, and proper time $\tau$ satisfies $d\tau^2 = -\eta_{\mu\nu}\, dx^\mu dx^\nu / c^2$.

Worked Example. A particle moves under constant acceleration $a = 2\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ starting from rest at $x_0 = 0$. Its trajectory is given by Newton's second law integrated twice:

$$v(t) = \int_0^t a\, dt' = at, \qquad x(t) = \int_0^t v(t')\, dt' = \tfrac{1}{2}at^2.$$

At $t = 3\ \mathrm{s}$, the position is $x(3) = \tfrac{1}{2}(2)(9) = 9\ \mathrm{m}$ and the velocity is $v(3) = 6\ \mathrm{m/s}$. This illustrates how specifying initial conditions at a reference time $t_0 = 0$ uniquely determines the future trajectory.

Key Theorem (Uniqueness of Trajectories — Picard–Lindelöf). Let $f : \mathcal{T} \times \mathcal{S} \to \mathcal{S}$ be continuous and Lipschitz continuous in its second argument with constant $L$. Then the initial value problem

$$\dot{\mathbf{x}}(t) = f(t, \mathbf{x}(t)), \quad \mathbf{x}(t_0) = \mathbf{x}_0,$$

has a unique solution on some interval $[t_0 - \epsilon,\, t_0 + \epsilon]$. This guarantees that causal, deterministic evolution is well-posed: a system's present state, together with the dynamical law, uniquely determines past and future trajectories.
