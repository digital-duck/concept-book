🍃 Decay Constant

🍃 Decay Constant

The decay constant, denoted $\lambda$, quantifies the probability per unit time that a single unstable nucleus will undergo radioactive decay. It is a fundamental, nucleus-specific parameter that governs the rate at which a radioactive sample diminishes over time. Formally, if $N(t)$ represents the number of undecayed nuclei at time $t$, the decay law states:

$$ \frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N $$

This first-order differential equation reflects the stochastic nature of radioactive decay: each nucleus decays independently, with no memory of how long it has existed. Solving the equation yields the exponential decay function:

$$ N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} $$

where $N_0$ is the initial number of nuclei at $t = 0$. The activity $A(t) = \lambda N(t)$ gives the number of decays per second, measured in becquerels (Bq), and it too decays exponentially.

Worked Example. Carbon-14 has a decay constant of $\lambda = 3.83 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{s}^{-1}$. A freshly prepared biological sample contains $N_0 = 6.00 \times 10^{10}$ atoms of $^{14}\text{C}$. Find the activity at $t = 0$ and the number of undecayed nuclei remaining after $t = 5730\ \text{yr} \approx 1.807 \times 10^{11}\ \text{s}$.

Initial activity: $A_0 = \lambda N_0 = (3.83 \times 10^{-12})(6.00 \times 10^{10}) \approx 0.230\ \text{Bq}$.

After $5730$ years: $N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} = 6.00 \times 10^{10}\, e^{-(3.83\times10^{-12})(1.807\times10^{11})} = 6.00 \times 10^{10}\, e^{-0.692} \approx 3.00 \times 10^{10}$ nuclei. This confirms that $5730$ years is precisely the half-life of $^{14}\text{C}$, since half the original nuclei remain.

Key Theorem. The half-life $t_{1/2}$ is related to the decay constant by:

$$ t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \approx \frac{0.693}{\lambda} $$

This relation is derived by setting $N(t_{1/2}) = N_0/2$ in the decay law and solving for $t_{1/2}$.

Lab Exercise. Using a Geiger counter, record the count rate of a $^{137}\text{Ba}$ sample every 30 seconds for 10 minutes. Plot $\ln A$ versus $t$ and determine $\lambda$ from the slope of the best-fit line. Compare your experimental value to the accepted half-life of $2.55\ \text{min}$ and calculate the percentage error.
