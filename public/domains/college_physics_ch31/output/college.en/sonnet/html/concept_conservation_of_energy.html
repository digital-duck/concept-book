🌱 Conservation Of Energy

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

Definition. The law of conservation of energy states that the total energy of an isolated system remains constant over time. In nuclear physics, this principle encompasses rest-mass energy through Einstein's mass-energy equivalence, so the conserved quantity in any reaction is the sum of kinetic energies and rest-mass energies of all participants. The single new primitive introduced here is the Q-value, which quantifies the net energy released or absorbed in a nuclear reaction:

$$ Q = \left(\sum m_{\text{reactants}} - \sum m_{\text{products}}\right)c^2 $$

A positive $Q$ indicates an exothermic reaction in which rest mass is converted to kinetic energy; a negative $Q$ indicates an endothermic reaction that requires an external energy input at least equal to $|Q|$ to proceed.

Worked Example. Consider the alpha decay of radium-226:

$$ {}^{226}_{88}\text{Ra} \rightarrow {}^{222}_{86}\text{Rn} + {}^{4}_{2}\text{He} $$

Using atomic masses: $m({}^{226}\text{Ra}) = 226.025410\ \text{u}$, $m({}^{222}\text{Rn}) = 222.017578\ \text{u}$, $m({}^{4}\text{He}) = 4.002602\ \text{u}$.

$$ \Delta m = 226.025410 - (222.017578 + 4.002602) = 0.005230\ \text{u} $$

Converting using $1\ \text{u} = 931.5\ \text{MeV}/c^2$:

$$ Q = 0.005230 \times 931.5\ \text{MeV} = 4.87\ \text{MeV} $$

This energy appears primarily as kinetic energy of the alpha particle. By momentum conservation, the much heavier radon nucleus carries only a small recoil fraction, so the alpha particle receives approximately $4.78\ \text{MeV}$, consistent with experimentally measured alpha energies from this decay.

Key Theorem. For any nuclear reaction, if the total rest mass of reactants exceeds that of products ($\Delta m > 0$), kinetic energy $Q = \Delta m \cdot c^2$ is released. If $\Delta m < 0$, the reaction is endothermic and requires a threshold kinetic energy of at least $|Q|$ in the center-of-mass frame to proceed. This threshold condition governs the minimum beam energies required in particle accelerator experiments.

Lab Exercise. Using a chart of nuclides, calculate the $Q$-value for the beta-minus decay ${}^{14}_{6}\text{C} \rightarrow {}^{14}_{7}\text{N} + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$. Then explain why, despite a well-defined $Q$-value, the observed electron kinetic energies form a continuous spectrum rather than a discrete line, and identify which particle accounts for the missing energy.
