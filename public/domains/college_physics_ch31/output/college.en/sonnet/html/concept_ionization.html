🌱 Ionization

🌱 Ionization

Definition. Ionization is the process by which an atom or molecule acquires a net electric charge by gaining or losing one or more electrons. When an electron is completely removed from a bound state, the minimum energy required is called the ionization energy (or ionization potential), denoted $E_i$. For a hydrogen-like atom with nuclear charge $Z$, the ionization energy from the ground state is given by the Bohr model as

$$ E_i = \frac{Z^2 \cdot 13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}, $$

where $n$ is the principal quantum number of the initial state. For hydrogen ($Z = 1$, $n = 1$), this yields $E_i = 13.6 \text{ eV}$. Ionization can be induced thermally, by photon absorption, by particle collisions, or by strong electric fields.

Worked Example. Consider a photon of wavelength $\lambda = 80 \text{ nm}$ incident on a ground-state hydrogen atom. The photon energy is

$$ E_\gamma = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{80 \times 10^{-9}} \approx 15.5 \text{ eV}. $$

Since $E_\gamma = 15.5 \text{ eV} > E_i = 13.6 \text{ eV}$, photoionization occurs. The ejected electron carries away the excess as kinetic energy:

$$ K_e = E_\gamma - E_i = 15.5 - 13.6 = 1.9 \text{ eV}. $$

This excess is directly measurable in photoelectron spectroscopy.

Key Theorem (Ionization Threshold). For photon-induced ionization, ionization occurs if and only if the photon energy satisfies $h\nu \geq E_i$, defining a threshold frequency $\nu_0 = E_i / h$ below which no ionization occurs regardless of light intensity. Above threshold, the ejected electron's kinetic energy is $K = h\nu - E_i$, which increases linearly with frequency. This threshold condition — energy sufficiency, not intensity — is a direct consequence of the quantized nature of photon absorption and underlies the analysis of atomic spectra throughout quantum mechanics.

Lab Exercise. A discharge lamp filtered to emit at $\lambda = 58.4 \text{ nm}$ (helium resonance line, $E_\gamma \approx 21.2 \text{ eV}$) irradiates a sample of atomic sodium with ionization energy $5.14 \text{ eV}$. Calculate the expected kinetic energy of the photoelectrons, sketch the predicted $K$ vs. $\nu$ curve for sodium, and determine the threshold wavelength $\lambda_0$ below which ionization cannot occur.
