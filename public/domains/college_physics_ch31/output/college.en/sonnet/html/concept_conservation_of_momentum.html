🌱 Conservation Of Momentum

🌱 Conservation Of Momentum

Definition. The law of conservation of momentum states that the total linear momentum of an isolated system remains constant in time. Formally, for a system of $n$ particles with no net external force acting on it:

$$ \frac{d\vec{p}_{\text{total}}}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i \vec{v}_i = \vec{0} $$

This follows directly from Newton's third law: every internal force between particles in the system occurs as an action-reaction pair of equal magnitude and opposite direction, so all internal contributions to $d\vec{p}/dt$ cancel. The conserved quantity is $\vec{p}_{\text{total}} = \sum_i m_i \vec{v}_i$, and in component form each of $p_x$, $p_y$, and $p_z$ is independently conserved when the corresponding net external force component is zero.

Worked Example. A stationary uranium-238 nucleus ($m_U = 238\,\text{u}$) undergoes alpha decay, emitting an alpha particle ($m_\alpha = 4\,\text{u}$) with speed $v_\alpha = 1.50 \times 10^7\,\text{m/s}$. Find the recoil speed of the daughter thorium-234 nucleus ($m_{Th} = 234\,\text{u}$).

Before decay, $\vec{p}_{\text{total}} = \vec{0}$. After decay, conservation of momentum requires:

$$ m_\alpha v_\alpha - m_{Th} v_{Th} = 0 $$ $$ v_{Th} = \frac{m_\alpha}{m_{Th}} v_\alpha = \frac{4}{234}(1.50 \times 10^7\,\text{m/s}) \approx 2.56 \times 10^5\,\text{m/s} $$

The daughter nucleus recoils in the direction opposite the alpha particle, consistent with $\vec{p}_{\text{total}} = \vec{0}$.

Key Theorem. For any two-body collision in one dimension—regardless of the internal forces exchanged—total momentum is conserved as long as no net external force acts during the interaction:

$$ m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f} $$

When the target is initially at rest ($v_{2i} = 0$) and the two objects stick together (a perfectly inelastic collision), the single unknown final velocity is $v_f = \dfrac{m_1}{m_1 + m_2}v_{1i}$. This result holds for any mass ratio, demonstrating that momentum conservation alone—without any assumption about energy—fully determines the outcome of a perfectly inelastic event.

Lab Exercise. Using a linear air track with two gliders fitted with Velcro pads, record velocities before and after perfectly inelastic head-on collisions at three mass ratios ($m_1/m_2 = 0.5,\,1.0,\,2.0$). For each trial, calculate $\vec{p}_{\text{total}}$ before and after impact and compare the measured post-collision speed to the predicted value $v_f = m_1 v_{1i}/(m_1 + m_2)$. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify plausible sources of systematic error, such as track inclination or friction.
