🍃 Half Life

🍃 Half Life

Definition. The half-life $t_{1/2}$ of a radioactive nuclide is the time interval after which exactly one-half of any given initial quantity of that nuclide has undergone radioactive decay. It is related to the decay constant $\lambda$ by

$$ t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \approx \frac{0.693}{\lambda}. $$

The number of undecayed nuclei $N(t)$ at time $t$, given an initial count $N_0$, obeys the exponential decay law:

$$ N(t) = N_0 \, e^{-\lambda t} = N_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{t/t_{1/2}}. $$

The activity $A(t) = \lambda N(t)$ carries the same exponential form and is measured in becquerels ($1\,\text{Bq} = 1\,\text{decay\,s}^{-1}$) or curies ($1\,\text{Ci} = 3.70 \times 10^{10}\,\text{Bq}$).

Worked Example. Iodine-131, used in thyroid treatment, has a half-life of $t_{1/2} = 8.02\,\text{days}$. Suppose a patient receives a dose with an initial activity of $A_0 = 4.00\,\mu\text{Ci}$.

  1. Find $\lambda$: $\lambda = \dfrac{0.693}{8.02\,\text{d}} = 0.0864\,\text{d}^{-1}$.
  2. Activity after $t = 24.06\,\text{days}$ (exactly three half-lives):
$$ A = A_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{3} = \frac{4.00\,\mu\text{Ci}}{8} = 0.500\,\mu\text{Ci}. $$

Each successive half-life halves the remaining activity, so after three intervals the dose has decreased to 12.5% of its original value.

Key Theorem. For any radioactive sample, $t_{1/2}$ is independent of $N_0$, temperature, pressure, and chemical bonding state. This universality follows directly from the probabilistic nature of nuclear decay: each nucleus decays with constant probability $\lambda\,dt$ per infinitesimal interval $dt$, yielding $dN = -\lambda N\,dt$ and hence the exponential solution above.

Lab Exercise. Using a Geiger counter, record the count rate of a $^{137}$Ba sample (produced by eluting a $^{137}$Cs generator) every 30 seconds for 10 minutes. Plot $\ln A$ versus $t$; the slope equals $-\lambda$. From your best-fit slope determine $t_{1/2}$ and compare it to the accepted value of $2.55\,\text{min}$. Estimate the percentage uncertainty arising from Poisson counting statistics at each data point.
