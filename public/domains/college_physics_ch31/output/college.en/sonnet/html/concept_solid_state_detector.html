🌸 Solid State Detector

🌸 Solid State Detector

A solid state detector is a semiconductor-based device that converts ionizing radiation directly into an electrical signal by exploiting the creation of electron-hole pairs within a crystalline material, most commonly silicon or germanium. When a charged particle or high-energy photon traverses the detector volume, it deposits energy along its path, promoting electrons out of their bound states and leaving behind vacancies — together, one liberated electron and its associated vacancy constitute a single electron-hole pair. Each pair requires an average energy of approximately $\varepsilon \approx 3.6\ \text{eV}$ in silicon (compared to $\sim 30\ \text{eV}$ per ion pair in gas detectors), making solid state detectors exceptionally sensitive. A reverse-biased p-n junction sweeps the liberated carriers to opposite electrodes before recombination can occur, producing a measurable current pulse.

Worked Example. A 5.48 MeV alpha particle from $^{241}\text{Am}$ is fully stopped in a silicon detector. Estimate the number of electron-hole pairs produced and the resulting charge pulse.

$$ N = \frac{E_{\alpha}}{\varepsilon} = \frac{5.48 \times 10^6\ \text{eV}}{3.6\ \text{eV/pair}} \approx 1.52 \times 10^6\ \text{pairs} $$

The total charge collected is:

$$ Q = Ne = (1.52 \times 10^6)(1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}) \approx 2.43 \times 10^{-13}\ \text{C} = 0.243\ \text{pC} $$

This pulse, amplified by a charge-sensitive preamplifier, yields a voltage output proportional to the deposited energy, enabling precise energy spectroscopy.

Key Theorem (Fano-Limited Resolution). The theoretical energy resolution of a solid state detector is constrained by statistical fluctuations in pair production, quantified by the Fano factor $F \approx 0.12$ for silicon:

$$ \sigma_E = \sqrt{F \cdot \varepsilon \cdot E} $$

The full-width at half-maximum (FWHM) resolution is:

$$ \text{FWHM} = 2\sqrt{2\ln 2}\,\sigma_E \approx 2.35\sqrt{F \varepsilon E} $$

For the 5.48 MeV alpha source above, $\text{FWHM} \approx 2.35\sqrt{(0.12)(3.6)(5.48 \times 10^6)} \approx 2.16\ \text{keV}$, far superior to proportional gas counters.

Lab Exercise. Connect a reverse-biased silicon detector to a charge-sensitive amplifier and multichannel analyzer. Expose it to a mixed alpha source ($^{241}\text{Am}$, $^{244}\text{Cm}$, $^{239}\text{Pu}$). Record the pulse-height spectrum, identify each peak, and calculate the detector's experimental FWHM. Compare your measured resolution to the Fano-limited theoretical value and account for electronic noise contributions by measuring the noise floor with the source removed.
