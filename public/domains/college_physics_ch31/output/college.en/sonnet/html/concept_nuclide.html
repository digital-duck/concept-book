🍃 Nuclide

🍃 Nuclide

A nuclide is a specific nuclear species characterized by a unique combination of proton number $Z$ (the atomic number), neutron number $N$, and nuclear energy state. It is formally denoted as $^{A}_{Z}\text{X}$, where $\text{X}$ is the chemical symbol of the element, $A = Z + N$ is the mass number (total nucleon count), and $Z$ specifies the element's identity. Two nuclides sharing the same $Z$ but differing in $N$ are called isotopes. A nuclide in an excited nuclear energy state is called a nuclear isomer, denoted $^{A\text{m}}_{Z}\text{X}$.

Worked Example. Consider carbon. The nuclide $^{12}_{6}\text{C}$ has $Z = 6$ protons and $N = 6$ neutrons, giving $A = 12$. The nuclide $^{14}_{6}\text{C}$, by contrast, has $Z = 6$ and $N = 8$, making it an isotope of carbon with $A = 14$. Because $^{14}_{6}\text{C}$ has an unfavorable neutron-to-proton ratio, it is radioactive and undergoes $\beta^{-}$ decay:

$$ ^{14}_{6}\text{C} \;\longrightarrow\; ^{14}_{7}\text{N} + e^{-} + \bar{\nu}_e $$

Here, a neutron converts to a proton, increasing $Z$ from 6 to 7 while $A$ remains 14, producing the stable nuclide $^{14}_{7}\text{N}$.

Key Theorem (Nuclear Stability). Stable nuclides tend to cluster along the band of stability in a plot of $N$ versus $Z$. For light nuclides ($Z \leq 20$), stability occurs near $N \approx Z$. For heavier nuclides, the Coulomb repulsion among protons requires an excess of neutrons for stability, shifting the band toward $N > Z$. Nuclides outside this band decay via $\alpha$, $\beta^{-}$, $\beta^{+}$, or electron-capture processes until a stable configuration is reached.

Laboratory Exercise. Using a chart of the nuclides, identify three nuclides with $A = 40$: $^{40}_{18}\text{Ar}$, $^{40}_{19}\text{K}$, and $^{40}_{20}\text{Ca}$. Classify each as stable or radioactive, determine their $N/Z$ ratios, and predict the decay mode of any unstable member. Record how the resulting daughter nuclides shift position on the chart and confirm whether each daughter falls within or outside the band of stability.
