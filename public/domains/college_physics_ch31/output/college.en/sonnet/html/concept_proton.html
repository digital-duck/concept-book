🌱 Proton

🌱 Proton

Definition. A proton is a positively charged subatomic particle found in the nucleus of every atom. It carries a charge of $+e$, where the elementary charge is $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, and possesses a rest mass of $m_p = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$, approximately 1836 times the electron mass. Protons are classified as baryons — composite particles made of three quarks bound together by the strong nuclear force. The number of protons in a nucleus defines the atomic number $Z$, which uniquely identifies the element.

Worked Example. Consider a proton accelerated from rest through a potential difference of $\Delta V = 5.00\ \text{kV}$. Using conservation of energy, the work done on the proton equals its gain in kinetic energy:

$$ q\,\Delta V = \tfrac{1}{2}m_p v^2 $$

Solving for the final speed:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{2e\,\Delta V}{m_p}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(1.602\times10^{-19})(5.00\times10^3)}{1.673\times10^{-27}}} $$ $$ v \approx 9.79 \times 10^5\ \text{m/s} $$

This speed ($\approx 0.3\%$ of $c$) confirms that a classical treatment is adequate here, since relativistic corrections become significant only above roughly $10\%$ of $c$.

Key Theorem — Magnetic Deflection. A proton moving with velocity $v$ perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field $B$ undergoes uniform circular motion. Equating the magnetic Lorentz force to the centripetal requirement:

$$ evB = \frac{m_p v^2}{r} \implies r = \frac{m_p v}{eB} $$

The ratio $r = m_p v/(eB)$ is called the magnetic rigidity, and it relates the radius of curvature directly to the proton's momentum. This relation is fundamental to particle accelerator design and mass spectrometry.

Lab Exercise. Set up a velocity selector consisting of crossed electric and magnetic fields ($E = 1.20\times10^4\ \text{V/m}$, $B_1 = 0.250\ \text{T}$) to isolate protons of a single speed. The selected protons then enter a second uniform field $B_2 = 0.400\ \text{T}$ oriented perpendicularly. Measure the radius of curvature of the resulting circular arc and verify that the computed mass matches $m_p$ to within experimental uncertainty. Identify two sources of systematic error and estimate their contributions to the mass uncertainty.
