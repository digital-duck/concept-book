🌱 Mass Energy Equivalence

🌱 Mass Energy Equivalence

Definition

Mass-energy equivalence is the principle that mass and energy are interconvertible manifestations of a single physical quantity, related by Einstein's equation:

$$ E_0 = mc^2 $$

where $E_0$ denotes the rest energy of an object, $m$ its rest mass, and $c = 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$ the speed of light in vacuum. This relation emerges directly from special relativity. Recall from earlier in this chapter that the total relativistic energy of a moving particle is $E = \gamma mc^2$, where $\gamma = (1 - v^2/c^2)^{-1/2}$ is the Lorentz factor introduced previously. At rest ($v = 0$, $\gamma = 1$), this expression reduces to $E_0 = mc^2$: the rest energy. The rest energy represents an enormous reservoir locked within mass itself, entirely independent of kinetic or potential energy, and constitutes the single new primitive of this section.

Worked Example

Determine the rest energy of a proton, given its rest mass $m_p = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$.

$$ E_0 = m_p c^2 = (1.673 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg})(2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s})^2 $$ $$ E_0 = 1.503 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{J} = \frac{1.503 \times 10^{-10}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}}\ \text{eV} \approx 938.3\ \text{MeV} $$

This is the standard proton rest energy quoted throughout nuclear and particle physics. The conversion factor $1\ \text{eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{J}$ is essential for expressing nuclear energies in convenient units.

Key Theorem: Conservation of Relativistic Energy

In any isolated system, the total relativistic energy $E_{\text{total}} = \sum_i \gamma_i m_i c^2$ is conserved. A direct corollary is that the measured mass of a bound nucleus is strictly less than the sum of its constituent nucleon masses—a difference accounted for entirely by $E_0 = mc^2$ applied to the energy released during assembly. For helium-4, this accounts for approximately $28.3\ \text{MeV}$, confirming that rest energy governs nuclear stability without requiring any concept beyond $E_0 = mc^2$.

Lab Exercise

Using tabulated atomic masses, compute the energy released when six protons and six neutrons assemble into carbon-12 ($^{12}\text{C}$). Given $m_p = 1.007276\ \text{u}$, $m_n = 1.008665\ \text{u}$, and $m(^{12}\text{C}) = 12.000000\ \text{u}$ (by definition), find the total mass of the separated nucleons, subtract the carbon-12 atomic mass to obtain the mass difference $\Delta m$, and apply $E_0 = \Delta m \cdot c^2$ using $1\ \text{u} = 931.5\ \text{MeV}/c^2$. Divide by $A = 12$ to obtain energy per nucleon and compare your result to the approximate $7.68\ \text{MeV/nucleon}$ expected for carbon-12.
