🌱 Neutron

🌱 Neutron

Definition. The neutron is an electrically neutral subatomic particle residing in the nucleus of atoms. It carries a rest mass of $m_n = 1.674927 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$, nearly identical to the proton mass $m_p$, and a baryon number of $+1$. Despite its zero net charge, the neutron has a nonzero magnetic moment ($\mu_n = -1.9130\ \mu_N$), evidence of internal structure: in the Standard Model, the neutron is composed of one up quark and two down quarks ($udd$), yielding a net charge of $\frac{2}{3}e - \frac{1}{3}e - \frac{1}{3}e = 0$. A free neutron is unstable and undergoes beta-minus decay—the one new process introduced here—in which a down quark converts to an up quark via the weak force, producing a proton, an electron, and an electron antineutrino:

$$ n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e $$

The mean lifetime of a free neutron is $\tau_n \approx 879\ \text{s}$. Inside a stable nucleus, the residual strong force suppresses this decay.

Worked Example. Consider carbon-14, $^{14}_{6}\text{C}$. With atomic number $Z = 6$ and mass number $A = 14$, the neutron count is:

$$ N = A - Z = 14 - 6 = 8 $$

Using the measured atomic mass $m(^{14}\text{C}) = 14.003242\ \text{u}$ along with $m_p = 1.007276\ \text{u}$ and $m_n = 1.008665\ \text{u}$, the mass deficit is $\Delta m = 6(1.007276) + 8(1.008665) - 14.003242 = 0.11298\ \text{u}$, giving a total binding energy $BE = 0.11298 \times 931.5\ \text{MeV} \approx 105.2\ \text{MeV}$, or about $7.51\ \text{MeV}$ per nucleon.

Key Theorem (Neutron Moderation). Fast neutrons ($\sim 1\ \text{MeV}$) must be slowed to thermal energies ($\sim 0.025\ \text{eV}$) to sustain a fission chain reaction efficiently. For elastic collisions with a moderator nucleus of mass number $A$, the fractional energy transferred per collision is $\Delta E/E = 4A/(1+A)^2$, which is maximized at $A = 1$. This makes hydrogen the most effective moderator per collision.

Lab Exercise. Place a $^{252}\text{Cf}$ neutron source behind appropriate shielding and use a Geiger counter to measure count rate as a function of paraffin wax thickness. Plot count rate versus thickness and fit the data to $I = I_0 e^{-x/\lambda}$ to extract the neutron mean free path $\lambda$. Compare your result with tabulated values for paraffin at thermal neutron energies and comment on any discrepancies.
