🍃 Atomic Number

🍃 Atomic Number

Definition. The atomic number of an element, denoted $Z$, is the number of protons contained within the nucleus of an atom of that element. Because each proton carries a charge of $+e = +1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, the total nuclear charge is $Q = Ze$. In a neutral atom, the number of orbiting electrons equals $Z$, so the atomic number simultaneously determines the element's chemical identity and its electronic structure. The atomic number is a dimensionless positive integer ranging from $Z = 1$ (hydrogen) to $Z = 118$ (oganesson) for currently known elements.

Worked Example. Consider the element oxygen. Its atomic number is $Z = 8$, meaning every oxygen nucleus contains exactly 8 protons, giving a nuclear charge of

$$ Q = Ze = 8 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C} = 1.282 \times 10^{-18}\ \text{C}. $$

A neutral oxygen atom therefore has 8 electrons distributed in shells: $1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^4$. The nucleus may also contain neutrons; the neutron number $N$ can vary, producing isotopes with different mass numbers $A = Z + N$ but identical chemical behavior. For oxygen-16, $N = 8$ and $A = 16$; for oxygen-18, $N = 10$ and $A = 18$, yet both share $Z = 8$.

Key Theorem (Moseley's Law). Henry Moseley demonstrated in 1913 that the square root of the characteristic X-ray frequencies emitted by an element is linearly proportional to its atomic number:

$$ \sqrt{f} = a(Z - b), $$

where $a$ and $b$ are experimentally determined constants. This relation confirmed that $Z$, not atomic mass, is the fundamental organizing quantity of the periodic table, and it allowed gaps corresponding to undiscovered elements to be identified unambiguously. For $K_\alpha$ X-rays, $b \approx 1$ and $a \approx 4.97 \times 10^7\ \text{Hz}^{1/2}$.

Lab Exercise. Using a diffraction-grating X-ray spectrometer, measure the $K_\alpha$ emission wavelength $\lambda$ from three unknown metallic targets. Convert each wavelength to frequency via $f = c/\lambda$, then plot $\sqrt{f}$ versus the element's position in the periodic table. Fit a straight line to verify Moseley's law, extract the slope $a$, and identify each unknown element by reading $Z$ from the fitted line.
