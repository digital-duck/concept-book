🍃 Mass Number

🍃 Mass Number

Definition. The mass number of a nuclide, denoted $A$, is the total count of nucleons—protons and neutrons—contained within the atomic nucleus. If $Z$ denotes the atomic number (proton count) and $N$ the neutron number, then

$$ A = Z + N. $$

The mass number is always a positive integer and appears as the superscript in standard nuclide notation ${}^{A}_{Z}\text{X}$, where X is the chemical symbol. Because electrons contribute negligibly to nuclear mass ($m_e \approx 0.00055\,\text{u}$), $A$ serves as a useful integer approximation to the atomic mass expressed in unified atomic mass units (u), where $1\,\text{u} = 1.6605 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$.

Worked Example. Consider the nuclide carbon-14, written ${}^{14}_{6}\text{C}$. Here $Z = 6$ (carbon always has six protons) and $A = 14$, so

$$ N = A - Z = 14 - 6 = 8 \text{ neutrons.} $$

The actual atomic mass of ${}^{14}\text{C}$ is $14.003242\,\text{u}$; the small deviation from the integer $A = 14$ arises from nuclear binding energy (via $E = \Delta m\, c^2$) and the electron masses. This deviation is central to nuclear energy calculations, but its detailed treatment belongs to the binding-energy discussion that follows.

Key Theorem — Conservation of Mass Number. In any nuclear reaction or decay process, the total mass number is conserved:

$$ \sum A_{\text{reactants}} = \sum A_{\text{products}}. $$

For example, in the alpha decay of uranium-238,

$$ {}^{238}_{92}\text{U} \;\longrightarrow\; {}^{234}_{90}\text{Th} + {}^{4}_{2}\text{He}, $$

the mass numbers satisfy $238 = 234 + 4$, and the atomic numbers satisfy $92 = 90 + 2$. This conservation law, combined with charge conservation, constrains which decay and reaction channels are energetically and physically accessible for a given nuclide.

Lab Exercise. Using a chart of the nuclides, identify three isotopes of oxygen ($Z = 8$). For each isotope, record the mass number $A$ and calculate the neutron number $N = A - Z$. Then look up the tabulated atomic mass of each and verify that $A$ provides a good integer approximation in every case. Discuss why heavier isotopes deviate slightly more from their integer mass number, and what this suggests about how nuclear structure changes as neutrons are added.
