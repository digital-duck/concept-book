🌱 Exact Rational

Definition. Let $\mathbb{Q}$ denote the field of rational numbers. An exact rational representation (ERR) for an element $r \in \mathbb{Q}$ is any pair of integers $(p, q)$ such that $r = p/q$, where $p$ and $q$ are coprime ($\gcd(p, q) = 1$) and $q > 0$. This canonical representation ensures a unique minimal form for every element in $\mathbb{Q}$. Such representations are fundamental when algebraic computations must avoid the inherent precision loss associated with finite-precision floating-point arithmetic.

Theorem. Given any computational system designed to maintain exact rational arithmetic, the set of representable numbers forms a subfield isomorphic to $\mathbb{Q}$ over the integers, and all arithmetic operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) are closed within this representation space. Specifically, if $r_1 = p_1/q_1$ and $r_2 = p_2/q_2$ are ERRs, then their sum $r_1 + r_2$ can be expressed as a unique ERR $(p, q)$ where $p = p_1 q_2 + p_2 q_1$ and $q = q_1 q_2$, subsequently reduced by dividing $p$ and $q$ by $\gcd(p, q)$.

Proof. The closure property is evident from the standard definition of rational addition. If $r_1 = p_1/q_1$ and $r_2 = p_2/q_2$, their sum is $(p_1 q_2 + p_2 q_1) / (q_1 q_2)$. Since $p_i, q_i \in \mathbb{Z}$, the numerator and denominator are integers. By dividing both resulting integers by $\gcd(p_1 q_2 + p_2 q_1, q_1 q_2)$, we obtain a new pair $(p', q')$ satisfying the coprime condition, hence maintaining the ERR structure. This formal closure guarantees that the algebraic properties of $\mathbb{Q}$ are preserved exactly in computation.

Remark. The concept of ERR is critical for symbolic mathematics and rigorous proof verification (e.g., implementing computational algebra systems like SymPy or Mathematica for exact arithmetic). Generalizations extend to multivariate rational functions, where the domain remains a field of fractions over a polynomial ring $\mathbb{K}[x_1, \dots, x_n]$. The primary advantage over standard floating-point types is the avoidance of approximation error, making it essential whenever algebraic precision, rather than computational speed or memory efficiency, is the highest priority.
