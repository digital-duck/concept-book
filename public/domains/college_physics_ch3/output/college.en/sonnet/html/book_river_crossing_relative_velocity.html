🌱 Vector

🌱 Vector

Definition. A vector is a mathematical object characterized by both a magnitude and a direction. In two-dimensional Cartesian coordinates, a vector $\vec{A}$ is written in component form as

$$ \vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j}, $$

where $A_x$ and $A_y$ are the scalar components along the $x$- and $y$-axes, respectively, and $\hat{i}$, $\hat{j}$ are the unit vectors along those axes. The magnitude of $\vec{A}$ is

$$ |\vec{A}| = A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}, $$

and the direction angle $\theta$ measured counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis satisfies $\tan\theta = A_y/A_x$. In three dimensions the representation extends to $\vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j} + A_z\,\hat{k}$.

Worked Example. A hiker walks $3.0\,\text{km}$ due east, then $4.0\,\text{km}$ due north. Find the resultant displacement vector.

Let $\vec{A} = 3.0\,\hat{i}\,\text{km}$ and $\vec{B} = 4.0\,\hat{j}\,\text{km}$. Vector addition is performed component-wise:

$$ \vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B} = (3.0\,\hat{i} + 4.0\,\hat{j})\,\text{km}. $$

The magnitude is

$$ R = \sqrt{(3.0)^2 + (4.0)^2} = \sqrt{9.0 + 16.0} = 5.0\,\text{km}, $$

and the direction is

$$ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{4.0}{3.0}\right) \approx 53.1^\circ \text{ north of east.} $$

The result is consistent with the 3-4-5 Pythagorean triple, serving as a useful check.

Key Theorem (Parallelogram Law of Addition). For any two vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$, their sum $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$ is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by placing $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ tail-to-tail. Equivalently, in the triangle (tip-to-tail) method, $\vec{B}$ is placed at the tip of $\vec{A}$, and $\vec{R}$ extends from the tail of $\vec{A}$ to the tip of $\vec{B}$. Both constructions yield identical results, confirming that vector addition is commutative: $\vec{A}+\vec{B} = \vec{B}+\vec{A}$.

🍃 Resultant Vector

🍃 Resultant Vector

Definition. Given a set of vectors $\vec{A}_1, \vec{A}_2, \ldots, \vec{A}_n$ acting on a system, the resultant vector $\vec{R}$ is defined as their vector sum:

$$ \vec{R} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \vec{A}_i = \vec{A}_1 + \vec{A}_2 + \cdots + \vec{A}_n. $$

Unlike scalar addition, vector addition accounts for both magnitude and direction. The standard analytical method decomposes each vector into orthogonal components along chosen coordinate axes. If $\vec{A}$ makes angle $\theta$ with the positive $x$-axis, then $A_x = A\cos\theta$ and $A_y = A\sin\theta$. The resultant components are then $R_x = \sum A_{ix}$ and $R_y = \sum A_{iy}$, yielding magnitude $R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}$ and direction $\phi = \arctan(R_y / R_x)$, with quadrant determined by the signs of $R_x$ and $R_y$.

Worked Example. A hiker walks $\vec{A} = 3.0\,\text{km}$ due east, then $\vec{B} = 4.0\,\text{km}$ due north. Find the resultant displacement.

Decomposing: $A_x = 3.0\,\text{km}$, $A_y = 0$; $B_x = 0$, $B_y = 4.0\,\text{km}$.

$$ R_x = 3.0 + 0 = 3.0\,\text{km}, \qquad R_y = 0 + 4.0 = 4.0\,\text{km}. $$ $$ R = \sqrt{(3.0)^2 + (4.0)^2} = \sqrt{9.0 + 16.0} = 5.0\,\text{km}. $$ $$ \phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{4.0}{3.0}\right) \approx 53.1^\circ \text{ north of east.} $$

The hiker ends up $5.0\,\text{km}$ from the starting point at $53.1^\circ$ north of east.

Key Theorem (Triangle Law of Vector Addition). For any two vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$, the resultant $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$ is the vector closing the triangle formed by placing $\vec{B}$ tip-to-tail after $\vec{A}$. By the law of cosines, $R^2 = A^2 + B^2 + 2AB\cos\alpha$, where $\alpha$ is the angle between $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$. Vector addition is both commutative ($\vec{A} + \vec{B} = \vec{B} + \vec{A}$) and associative.

🌱 Trigonometric Functions

🌱 Trigonometric Functions

Definition. The trigonometric functions relate angles to ratios of side lengths in a right triangle. For a right triangle with an interior angle $\theta$, the three primary functions are defined by the ratios of its sides:

$$ \sin\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \quad \cos\theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} $$

These definitions extend to all real angles via the unit circle: placing angle $\theta$ at the origin measured from the positive $x$-axis, the terminal ray intersects the unit circle at the point $(\cos\theta,\, \sin\theta)$. The three reciprocal functions follow directly: $\csc\theta = 1/\sin\theta$, $\sec\theta = 1/\cos\theta$, and $\cot\theta = \cos\theta/\sin\theta$. Both $\sin$ and $\cos$ are periodic with period $2\pi$, while $\tan$ has period $\pi$. In physics, angles are almost universally expressed in radians, where a full rotation equals $2\pi$ rad.

Worked Example. A projectile is launched at speed $v_0 = 25\ \text{m/s}$ at an angle $\theta = 37°$ above the horizontal. Resolve the initial velocity into components:

$$ v_{0x} = v_0 \cos\theta = 25 \cos 37° \approx 25 \times 0.7986 \approx 19.97\ \text{m/s} $$ $$ v_{0y} = v_0 \sin\theta = 25 \sin 37° \approx 25 \times 0.6018 \approx 15.05\ \text{m/s} $$

These components propagate independently under kinematic equations, illustrating how trigonometric decomposition reduces a two-dimensional problem to two one-dimensional ones.

Key Theorem (Pythagorean Identity). For all $\theta$:

$$ \sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1 $$

Proof sketch: By the unit circle definition, the point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ lies on the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$. Substituting gives the identity immediately. Dividing through by $\cos^2\theta$ yields the companion identity $1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$, which proves useful when analyzing forces on inclined surfaces.

Record the block's acceleration $a$ down the ramp and compare it to the theoretical prediction $a = g\sin\theta$. Identify sources of systematic error such as friction and uncertainty in $h$.

🍃 Vector Components

🍃 Vector Components

Definition. A vector $\vec{A}$ in two dimensions can be uniquely decomposed into mutually perpendicular parts along chosen coordinate axes. If $\theta$ denotes the angle $\vec{A}$ makes with the positive $x$-axis (measured counterclockwise), then the scalar components of $\vec{A}$ are

$$ A_x = A\cos\theta, \qquad A_y = A\sin\theta, $$

where $A = |\vec{A}|$ is the magnitude. The vector is then written

$$ \vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j}, $$

with $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$ the unit vectors along $x$ and $y$, respectively. Conversely, given components, the magnitude and direction are recovered by

$$ A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}, \qquad \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{A_y}{A_x}\right), $$

where the quadrant of $\theta$ must be resolved using the signs of both components.

Worked Example. A displacement vector has magnitude $|\vec{d}| = 35.0\text{ m}$ directed at $\theta = 127°$ from the positive $x$-axis. Find its components.

$$ d_x = (35.0\text{ m})\cos 127° = (35.0)(-0.602) = -21.1\text{ m}, $$ $$ d_y = (35.0\text{ m})\sin 127° = (35.0)(0.799) = +28.0\text{ m}. $$

The negative $x$-component confirms the vector points into the second quadrant, consistent with $90° < 127° < 180°$. To verify: $\sqrt{(-21.1)^2+(28.0)^2} = \sqrt{445+784} = \sqrt{1229} \approx 35.1\text{ m}$, matching the original magnitude within rounding.

Key Theorem (Component Addition). Vector addition reduces to independent scalar addition along each axis. For $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$,

$$ R_x = A_x + B_x, \qquad R_y = A_y + B_y. $$

This follows directly from the distributive property of scalar multiplication over vector addition and the orthogonality of $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$. The theorem extends straightforwardly to three dimensions by appending $R_z = A_z + B_z$.

(c) Reposition one of the three masses on the force table to match your computed $R_x$ and $R_y$, and verify that the adjusted configuration displaces the ring in the predicted direction.

🌱 Reference Frame

🌱 Reference Frame

Definition. A reference frame is a coordinate system, together with a clock, with respect to which the position, velocity, and acceleration of objects are measured. Formally, a reference frame $S$ consists of an origin $O$, three mutually orthogonal spatial axes, and a time coordinate $t$. The position of a particle is then specified by the vector $\mathbf{r}(t) = x\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + y\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} + z\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ relative to $O$. A reference frame is called inertial if Newton's first law holds within it—that is, if a body subject to no net force moves with constant velocity. Any frame translating at constant velocity relative to an inertial frame is itself inertial; accelerating frames require fictitious forces and are not treated here.

Worked Example. A passenger on a train moving eastward at $v_T = 20\,\mathrm{m/s}$ throws a ball forward (eastward) at $v_{B,T} = 5\,\mathrm{m/s}$ relative to the train. Determine the ball's velocity as measured by an observer stationary on the ground.

Let $S$ denote the ground frame and $S'$ the train frame. The Galilean velocity addition formula gives

$$ \mathbf{v}_{B,S} = \mathbf{v}_{B,S'} + \mathbf{v}_{S',S}. $$

Substituting magnitudes (all eastward, so one-dimensional):

$$ v_{B,S} = 5\,\mathrm{m/s} + 20\,\mathrm{m/s} = 25\,\mathrm{m/s}\;\text{(eastward)}. $$

The same ball, observed from $S'$, travels at only $5\,\mathrm{m/s}$—illustrating that velocity is frame-dependent.

Key Theorem (Galilean Transformation). For two inertial frames $S$ and $S'$ where $S'$ moves with constant velocity $\mathbf{V}$ relative to $S$, and their origins coincide at $t = 0$, the coordinates transform as

$$ \mathbf{r}' = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{V}t, \qquad t' = t. $$

Differentiating once yields the velocity addition rule used above; differentiating twice shows that $\mathbf{a}' = \mathbf{a}$, so Newton's second law $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$ takes the same form in all inertial frames. This invariance of acceleration under Galilean transformation is the mathematical expression of Newtonian relativity.

Lab Exercise. Set up two dynamics carts on a low-friction track: cart $A$ at rest and cart $B$ moving at a measured constant velocity $V$. Attach a motion sensor to each cart. Verify numerically that $x_A(t) - x_B(t) = Vt$ for all recorded times, confirming the Galilean position transformation and the frame-dependence of position measurements.

🍃 Resolving Vectors Into Components

🍃 Resolving Vectors Into Components

Definition. Any vector $\vec{A}$ in two dimensions can be uniquely expressed as the sum of two perpendicular vectors aligned with a chosen coordinate system. If $\theta$ is the angle $\vec{A}$ makes with the positive $x$-axis, the components are defined as:

$$ A_x = A\cos\theta, \qquad A_y = A\sin\theta $$

where $A = |\vec{A}|$ is the magnitude. The process of finding $A_x$ and $A_y$ from $\vec{A}$ is called resolving the vector into its components — this is the single analytical operation that replaces geometric construction throughout two-dimensional kinematics and dynamics. The decomposition is reversible: given components, the magnitude and direction are recovered by $A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}$ and $\theta = \arctan(A_y/A_x)$, with quadrant determined by the signs of both components.

Worked Example. A displacement vector has magnitude $|\vec{d}| = 48.0\,\text{m}$ directed at $\theta = 30.0°$ north of east. Resolve $\vec{d}$ into eastward and northward components.

Identifying east as $+x$ and north as $+y$:

$$ d_x = (48.0\,\text{m})\cos 30.0° = (48.0)(0.866) = 41.6\,\text{m (east)} $$ $$ d_y = (48.0\,\text{m})\sin 30.0° = (48.0)(0.500) = 24.0\,\text{m (north)} $$

Verification: $\sqrt{(41.6)^2 + (24.0)^2} = \sqrt{1730.6 + 576} = \sqrt{2306.6} \approx 48.0\,\text{m}$ $\checkmark$

Key Theorem (Component Addition). Because vector addition is commutative and associative, the components of a sum of $n$ vectors satisfy:

$$ R_x = \sum_{i=1}^{n} A_{ix}, \qquad R_y = \sum_{i=1}^{n} A_{iy} $$

Components are scalars, so adding vectors reduces to ordinary arithmetic once each vector has been resolved. The theorem extends naturally to three dimensions by including a $z$-component.

Lab Exercise. Three force vectors act on a particle: $\vec{F}_1 = 20.0\,\text{N}$ at $0°$, $\vec{F}_2 = 35.0\,\text{N}$ at $120°$, and $\vec{F}_3 = 15.0\,\text{N}$ at $240°$. (a) Resolve each force into $x$- and $y$-components. (b) Compute the net force vector $\vec{R}$. (c) Determine the magnitude and direction of $\vec{R}$, expressing the final angle counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis, and discuss whether the three forces produce equilibrium.

🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the vector quantity that describes both the rate of change of position and the direction of that change. For a particle whose position vector is $\vec{r}(t)$, the average velocity over a time interval $[t_1, t_2]$ is

$$ \bar{\vec{v}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{r}(t_2) - \vec{r}(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1}. $$

The instantaneous velocity at time $t$ is the limiting value of this ratio as the interval shrinks to zero:

$$ \vec{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt}. $$

The magnitude $|\vec{v}(t)|$ is called the speed, a scalar, measured in SI units of $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. Speed is the one new primitive introduced here; all other quantities build on the position vector established in the preceding section.

Worked Example. A particle moves along the $x$-axis with position given by $x(t) = 3t^2 - 6t + 1$ (in meters, $t$ in seconds). Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 2\,\mathrm{s}$ and determine when the particle is momentarily at rest.

Differentiating: $v(t) = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 6.$

At $t = 2\,\mathrm{s}$: $v(2) = 6(2) - 6 = 6\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ (in the positive $x$-direction).

Setting $v(t) = 0$: $6t - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow t = 1\,\mathrm{s}$. The particle reverses direction at $t = 1\,\mathrm{s}$, which corresponds to the vertex of the parabolic position curve.

Key Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Kinematics). If the velocity $\vec{v}(t)$ is continuous on $[t_1, t_2]$, then the displacement is recovered by integration:

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{v}(t)\, dt. $$

This result follows directly from the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus and establishes that displacement equals the signed area under the velocity–time graph. Note carefully that displacement $|\Delta \vec{r}|$ differs from total distance traveled, which requires integrating the speed $|\vec{v}(t)|$ rather than the vector velocity.

🍃 Analytical Vector Addition

🍃 Analytical Vector Addition

Definition. Analytical vector addition decomposes each vector into orthogonal scalar components along a chosen coordinate system, performs ordinary arithmetic on like components, and reconstructs the resultant vector from the summed components. For a vector $\vec{A}$ of magnitude $A$ oriented at angle $\theta$ measured counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis, the components are

$$ A_x = A\cos\theta, \qquad A_y = A\sin\theta. $$

Given $n$ vectors $\vec{A}_1, \vec{A}_2, \ldots, \vec{A}_n$, the resultant $\vec{R}$ satisfies

$$ R_x = \sum_{i=1}^n A_{ix}, \qquad R_y = \sum_{i=1}^n A_{iy}. $$

The magnitude and direction of $\vec{R}$ are then recovered by

$$ R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}, \qquad \phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{R_y}{R_x}\right), $$

where $\phi$ must be placed in the correct quadrant by inspecting the signs of $R_x$ and $R_y$.

Worked Example. A hiker walks $\vec{A} = 30\,\text{m}$ at $30°$ north of east, then $\vec{B} = 20\,\text{m}$ due north. Find the resultant displacement.

Decompose each vector:

$$ A_x = 30\cos 30° \approx 25.98\,\text{m}, \quad A_y = 30\sin 30° = 15.00\,\text{m}. $$ $$ B_x = 20\cos 90° = 0\,\text{m}, \quad B_y = 20\sin 90° = 20.00\,\text{m}. $$

Sum the components:

$$ R_x = 25.98 + 0 = 25.98\,\text{m}, \qquad R_y = 15.00 + 20.00 = 35.00\,\text{m}. $$

Reconstruct the resultant:

$$ R = \sqrt{(25.98)^2 + (35.00)^2} \approx \sqrt{675.0 + 1225.0} \approx 43.6\,\text{m}, $$ $$ \phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{35.00}{25.98}\right) \approx 53.4°\text{ north of east.} $$

Key Theorem. Vector addition is commutative: $\vec{A}+\vec{B} = \vec{B}+\vec{A}$. This follows immediately from the component representation—swapping the order of summation in $R_x = A_x + B_x$ and $R_y = A_y + B_y$ leaves the sums unchanged, since real-number addition is commutative. No additional structure beyond component arithmetic is required; associativity follows by the same argument applied to three or more vectors.

🍃 Relative Velocity

🍃 Relative Velocity

Definition. The velocity of an object depends on the reference frame from which it is observed. If frame $S'$ moves with constant velocity $\vec{v}_{S'S}$ relative to frame $S$, and an object $P$ has velocity $\vec{v}_{PS'}$ as measured in $S'$, then the velocity of $P$ as measured in $S$ is given by the Galilean velocity addition rule:

$$ \vec{v}_{PS} = \vec{v}_{PS'} + \vec{v}_{S'S} $$

This relation is a vector equation; directions matter. The subscript notation $\vec{v}_{AB}$ denotes the velocity of $A$ as observed from $B$. The mnemonic "inner subscripts cancel" follows directly: in $\vec{v}_{PS'} + \vec{v}_{S'S}$, the repeated index $S'$ cancels to yield $\vec{v}_{PS}$.

Worked Example. A river flows due east at $\vec{v}_{rg} = 3.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}$ relative to the ground. A boat heads due north at $\vec{v}_{br} = 4.0\,\hat{y}\ \text{m/s}$ relative to the river. Find the boat's velocity relative to the ground.

Applying the addition rule with $S'$ = river and $S$ = ground:

$$ \vec{v}_{bg} = \vec{v}_{br} + \vec{v}_{rg} = 4.0\,\hat{y} + 3.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s} $$

The magnitude is:

$$ |\vec{v}_{bg}| = \sqrt{(3.0)^2 + (4.0)^2} = 5.0\ \text{m/s} $$

and the direction is:

$$ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{3.0}{4.0}\right) \approx 36.9^\circ \text{ east of north} $$

Key Theorem. Galilean relativity asserts that the laws of classical mechanics are invariant under the transformation $\vec{v}_{PS} = \vec{v}_{PS'} + \vec{v}_{S'S}$, provided $\vec{v}_{S'S}$ is constant (inertial frames). Consequently, no mechanical experiment performed within an inertial frame can distinguish that frame from rest. This principle breaks down at speeds approaching $c$, where it is superseded by the Lorentz transformation of special relativity.

🍃 Velocity Addition

🍃 Velocity Addition

Definition. When an object moves within a reference frame that is itself in motion relative to a second, stationary observer, the velocity of the object as measured by the stationary observer is obtained by vector addition of the two velocities. For one-dimensional motion, if frame $S'$ moves with velocity $\vec{v}_{S'}$ relative to frame $S$, and an object moves with velocity $\vec{v}'$ relative to $S'$, then the object's velocity relative to $S$ is

$$ \vec{v} = \vec{v}' + \vec{v}_{S'}. $$

In two dimensions, the same principle applies component-wise:

$$ v_x = v'_x + v_{S',x}, \qquad v_y = v'_y + v_{S',y}. $$

This is the Galilean velocity addition formula, valid when all speeds are negligible compared to the speed of light.

Worked Example. A riverboat heads directly across a river of width $d = 120\ \mathrm{m}$ with speed $v'_x = 4.0\ \mathrm{m/s}$ relative to the water. The river current flows downstream at $v_{S',y} = 3.0\ \mathrm{m/s}$. Find the boat's speed and direction as seen by an observer on the bank.

Applying the addition formula:

$$ v_x = v'_x = 4.0\ \mathrm{m/s}, \qquad v_y = v_{S',y} = 3.0\ \mathrm{m/s}. $$

The resultant speed is

$$ v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} = \sqrt{(4.0)^2 + (3.0)^2} = 5.0\ \mathrm{m/s}. $$

The angle downstream from the perpendicular is

$$ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{v_y}{v_x}\right) = \arctan(0.75) \approx 36.9°. $$

Key Theorem (Frame Independence of Acceleration). Under Galilean relativity, if $\vec{v}_{S'}$ is constant, differentiating $\vec{v} = \vec{v}' + \vec{v}_{S'}$ with respect to time gives $\vec{a} = \vec{a}'$. Acceleration—and therefore the net force via Newton's second law—is identical in all inertial frames. This invariance underpins the classical principle of relativity: the laws of mechanics take the same form in every inertial reference frame, with no experiment capable of distinguishing one from another by purely mechanical means.

🌸 River Crossing Relative Velocity

🌸 River Crossing Relative Velocity

Definition. When an object moves within a medium that is itself in motion, its velocity relative to a stationary observer is the vector sum of its velocity relative to the medium and the medium's velocity relative to the observer. Formally, if a boat moves with velocity $\vec{v}_{b/w}$ relative to the water, and the water flows with velocity $\vec{v}_{w/g}$ relative to the ground, then the boat's velocity relative to the ground is

$$ \vec{v}_{b/g} = \vec{v}_{b/w} + \vec{v}_{w/g}. $$

This vector addition governs both the boat's speed over ground and its actual direction of travel, which generally differs from the heading it steers.

Worked Example. A river flows due east at $3.0\ \text{m/s}$. A rower can propel her boat at $4.0\ \text{m/s}$ relative to the water. She wishes to cross a river $120\ \text{m}$ wide.

Case 1 — Straight across. She aims her bow directly north. Then $\vec{v}_{b/w} = 4.0\,\hat{j}\ \text{m/s}$ and $\vec{v}_{w/g} = 3.0\,\hat{i}\ \text{m/s}$, giving

$$ \vec{v}_{b/g} = 3.0\,\hat{i} + 4.0\,\hat{j}\ \text{m/s}, \qquad |\vec{v}_{b/g}| = \sqrt{3.0^2 + 4.0^2} = 5.0\ \text{m/s}. $$

Crossing time: $t = 120/4.0 = 30\ \text{s}$. Downstream drift: $x = 3.0 \times 30 = 90\ \text{m}$.

Case 2 — Land directly opposite. She must angle upstream so that the eastward component of $\vec{v}_{b/w}$ cancels the current. Setting $v_{b/w}\sin\theta = 3.0\ \text{m/s}$ gives $\theta = \arcsin(3/4) \approx 48.6°$ west of north. Her northward speed is then $v_N = \sqrt{4.0^2 - 3.0^2} = \sqrt{7} \approx 2.65\ \text{m/s}$, and crossing time is $t = 120/2.65 \approx 45.3\ \text{s}$.

Key Theorem (Minimum Crossing Time). The crossing time $t = d/v_\perp$, where $d$ is the river width and $v_\perp$ is the component of $\vec{v}_{b/w}$ perpendicular to the current. This is minimized when the boat is aimed directly across ($v_\perp = v_{b/w}$), regardless of the current speed.

Payoff 🎯

What River Crossing Relative Velocity Achieves

River crossing relative velocity stands as the natural culmination of Chapter 3 because it synthesizes every strand of two-dimensional kinematics into a single, tractable problem: a moving medium carries an object that simultaneously moves through that medium, and the observer on the bank sees the vector superposition of both motions. Formally, if $\vec{v}_{OB}$ denotes the velocity of the object relative to the bank, $\vec{v}_{OM}$ the velocity of the object relative to the medium (water or air), and $\vec{v}_{MB}$ the velocity of the medium relative to the bank, then the Galilean addition law

$$ \vec{v}_{OB} = \vec{v}_{OM} + \vec{v}_{MB} $$

reduces every relative-velocity problem to vector addition. Chapter 3 built each prerequisite in sequence: scalar kinematics gave rates of change, vector decomposition gave the component toolkit, projectile motion demonstrated independence of perpendicular components, and now relative velocity demands that all three be applied simultaneously. No concept introduced earlier is idle here.

Worked Example

A swimmer capable of $3.0\ \text{m/s}$ in still water aims directly across a $120\ \text{m}$ river whose current flows at $2.0\ \text{m/s}$ downstream. Taking $x$ as downstream and $y$ as across, $\vec{v}_{OM} = (0,\,3.0)\ \text{m/s}$ and $\vec{v}_{MB} = (2.0,\,0)\ \text{m/s}$, so $\vec{v}_{OB} = (2.0,\,3.0)\ \text{m/s}$. Crossing time is $t = 120/3.0 = 40\ \text{s}$; downstream drift is $x = 2.0 \times 40 = 80\ \text{m}$. To land directly opposite, the swimmer must angle upstream: setting $v_{OB,x} = 0$ requires $\theta = \arcsin(2.0/3.0) \approx 41.8°$ into the current, yielding a reduced crossing speed of $\sqrt{3.0^2 - 2.0^2} = \sqrt{5} \approx 2.24\ \text{m/s}$ and time $t = 120/2.24 \approx 53.6\ \text{s}$.

Key Theorem

The minimum crossing time is achieved when $\vec{v}_{OM}$ is perpendicular to the bank, regardless of current speed, because the component of $\vec{v}_{OB}$ across the river is then maximized at $|\vec{v}_{OM}|$. Minimum downstream drift, by contrast, requires the heading angle computed above and is achievable only when $|\vec{v}_{OM}| > |\vec{v}_{MB}|$.

Invitation

The same vector addition governs aircraft navigation under crosswinds, where a pilot's heading and airspeed combine with wind velocity to determine ground track and ground speed — a domain where a one-degree error compounds over hundreds of kilometers. If the elegance of the river problem resonates, follow that thread into aviation navigation: the mathematics is identical, the stakes considerably higher, and the precision demanded a compelling reason to master every nuance developed in this chapter.
