🍃 Resolving Vectors Into Components

🍃 Resolving Vectors Into Components

Definition. Any vector $\vec{A}$ in two dimensions can be uniquely expressed as the sum of two perpendicular vectors aligned with a chosen coordinate system. If $\theta$ is the angle $\vec{A}$ makes with the positive $x$-axis, the components are defined as:

$$ A_x = A\cos\theta, \qquad A_y = A\sin\theta $$

where $A = |\vec{A}|$ is the magnitude. The process of finding $A_x$ and $A_y$ from $\vec{A}$ is called resolving the vector into its components — this is the single analytical operation that replaces geometric construction throughout two-dimensional kinematics and dynamics. The decomposition is reversible: given components, the magnitude and direction are recovered by $A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}$ and $\theta = \arctan(A_y/A_x)$, with quadrant determined by the signs of both components.

Worked Example. A displacement vector has magnitude $|\vec{d}| = 48.0\,\text{m}$ directed at $\theta = 30.0°$ north of east. Resolve $\vec{d}$ into eastward and northward components.

Identifying east as $+x$ and north as $+y$:

$$ d_x = (48.0\,\text{m})\cos 30.0° = (48.0)(0.866) = 41.6\,\text{m (east)} $$ $$ d_y = (48.0\,\text{m})\sin 30.0° = (48.0)(0.500) = 24.0\,\text{m (north)} $$

Verification: $\sqrt{(41.6)^2 + (24.0)^2} = \sqrt{1730.6 + 576} = \sqrt{2306.6} \approx 48.0\,\text{m}$ $\checkmark$

Key Theorem (Component Addition). Because vector addition is commutative and associative, the components of a sum of $n$ vectors satisfy:

$$ R_x = \sum_{i=1}^{n} A_{ix}, \qquad R_y = \sum_{i=1}^{n} A_{iy} $$

Components are scalars, so adding vectors reduces to ordinary arithmetic once each vector has been resolved. The theorem extends naturally to three dimensions by including a $z$-component.

Lab Exercise. Three force vectors act on a particle: $\vec{F}_1 = 20.0\,\text{N}$ at $0°$, $\vec{F}_2 = 35.0\,\text{N}$ at $120°$, and $\vec{F}_3 = 15.0\,\text{N}$ at $240°$. (a) Resolve each force into $x$- and $y$-components. (b) Compute the net force vector $\vec{R}$. (c) Determine the magnitude and direction of $\vec{R}$, expressing the final angle counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis, and discuss whether the three forces produce equilibrium.
