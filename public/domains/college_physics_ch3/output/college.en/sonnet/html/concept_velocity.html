🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the vector quantity that describes both the rate of change of position and the direction of that change. For a particle whose position vector is $\vec{r}(t)$, the average velocity over a time interval $[t_1, t_2]$ is

$$ \bar{\vec{v}} = \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{r}(t_2) - \vec{r}(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1}. $$

The instantaneous velocity at time $t$ is the limiting value of this ratio as the interval shrinks to zero:

$$ \vec{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt}. $$

The magnitude $|\vec{v}(t)|$ is called the speed, a scalar, measured in SI units of $\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. Speed is the one new primitive introduced here; all other quantities build on the position vector established in the preceding section.

Worked Example. A particle moves along the $x$-axis with position given by $x(t) = 3t^2 - 6t + 1$ (in meters, $t$ in seconds). Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 2\,\mathrm{s}$ and determine when the particle is momentarily at rest.

Differentiating: $v(t) = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 6.$

At $t = 2\,\mathrm{s}$: $v(2) = 6(2) - 6 = 6\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ (in the positive $x$-direction).

Setting $v(t) = 0$: $6t - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow t = 1\,\mathrm{s}$. The particle reverses direction at $t = 1\,\mathrm{s}$, which corresponds to the vertex of the parabolic position curve.

Key Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Kinematics). If the velocity $\vec{v}(t)$ is continuous on $[t_1, t_2]$, then the displacement is recovered by integration:

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{v}(t)\, dt. $$

This result follows directly from the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus and establishes that displacement equals the signed area under the velocity–time graph. Note carefully that displacement $|\Delta \vec{r}|$ differs from total distance traveled, which requires integrating the speed $|\vec{v}(t)|$ rather than the vector velocity.

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor and dynamics cart on a low-friction track, record position data at $0.05\,\mathrm{s}$ intervals. Compute the average velocity over successive intervals and plot $\bar{v}$ versus $t$. Identify at least one instant where the cart reverses direction by locating sign changes in $\bar{v}$. Compare the numerical derivative of your position data to the velocity reported directly by the sensor software, and quantify the discrepancy as a percentage of peak speed.
