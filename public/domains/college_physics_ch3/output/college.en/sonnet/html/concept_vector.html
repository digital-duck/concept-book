🌱 Vector

Definition. A vector is a mathematical object characterized by both a magnitude and a direction. In two-dimensional Cartesian coordinates, a vector $\vec{A}$ is written in component form as

$$ \vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j}, $$

where $A_x$ and $A_y$ are the scalar components along the $x$- and $y$-axes, respectively, and $\hat{i}$, $\hat{j}$ are the unit vectors along those axes. The magnitude of $\vec{A}$ is

$$ |\vec{A}| = A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}, $$

and the direction angle $\theta$ measured counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis satisfies $\tan\theta = A_y/A_x$. In three dimensions the representation extends to $\vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j} + A_z\,\hat{k}$.

Worked Example. A hiker walks $3.0\,\text{km}$ due east, then $4.0\,\text{km}$ due north. Find the resultant displacement vector.

Let $\vec{A} = 3.0\,\hat{i}\,\text{km}$ and $\vec{B} = 4.0\,\hat{j}\,\text{km}$. Vector addition is performed component-wise:

$$ \vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B} = (3.0\,\hat{i} + 4.0\,\hat{j})\,\text{km}. $$

The magnitude is

$$ R = \sqrt{(3.0)^2 + (4.0)^2} = \sqrt{9.0 + 16.0} = 5.0\,\text{km}, $$

and the direction is

$$ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{4.0}{3.0}\right) \approx 53.1^\circ \text{ north of east.} $$

The result is consistent with the 3-4-5 Pythagorean triple, serving as a useful check.

Key Theorem (Parallelogram Law of Addition). For any two vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$, their sum $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$ is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by placing $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ tail-to-tail. Equivalently, in the triangle (tip-to-tail) method, $\vec{B}$ is placed at the tip of $\vec{A}$, and $\vec{R}$ extends from the tail of $\vec{A}$ to the tip of $\vec{B}$. Both constructions yield identical results, confirming that vector addition is commutative: $\vec{A}+\vec{B} = \vec{B}+\vec{A}$.

Lab Exercise. Using a force table with three pulleys, attach masses to produce force vectors $\vec{F}_1 = 2.0\,\text{N}$ at $0^\circ$ and $\vec{F}_2 = 2.0\,\text{N}$ at $90^\circ$. Record the percent discrepancy between the predicted and measured magnitudes.
