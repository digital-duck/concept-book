🌸 Manometer

🌸 Manometer

A manometer is a pressure-measuring device that uses a column of fluid in a U-shaped tube to compare an unknown pressure against a known reference pressure. In its simplest form, an open-tube manometer consists of a U-tube partially filled with a liquid of known density $\rho$ (typically mercury or water), with one end open to the atmosphere and the other connected to the pressure source under investigation. The gauge pressure of the source is determined by measuring the height difference between the two fluid columns.

Definition. For an open-tube manometer, the gauge pressure $P_g$ of a gas at the closed end is related to the height difference $h$ between the two liquid surfaces by

$$ P_g = P - P_{\text{atm}} = \rho g h, $$

where $P$ is the absolute pressure of the gas, $P_{\text{atm}}$ is atmospheric pressure, $\rho$ is the density of the manometric fluid, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. When the gas pressure exceeds atmospheric pressure, the fluid level on the gas side is depressed and $h > 0$; when the gas pressure is below atmospheric, the fluid rises on the gas side and $h < 0$.

Worked Example. A closed container of gas is connected to an open-tube water manometer. The water level on the gas side sits $12.0\ \text{cm}$ below the level on the open side. Determine the gauge pressure of the gas.

Given: $\rho_{\text{water}} = 1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3$, $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$, $h = 0.120\ \text{m}$.

$$ P_g = \rho g h = (1.00 \times 10^3)(9.80)(0.120) = 1.18 \times 10^3\ \text{Pa}. $$

The gas pressure exceeds atmospheric pressure by approximately $1.18\ \text{kPa}$.

Key Theorem (Pascal's Principle applied). At any two points within a static, connected fluid at the same height, the pressure is equal. This principle guarantees that the pressure transmitted by the gas at the fluid interface is fully communicated to the open column, making the height difference $h$ a faithful measure of the gauge pressure.

Lab Exercise. Connect a bicycle tire pump to a water manometer and record the height difference $h$ after each pump stroke. Plot $P_g$ versus stroke number. Using the slope, estimate the effective volume of air delivered per stroke and compare with the pump's rated displacement, assuming the tire is rigid.
