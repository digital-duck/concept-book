🍃 Pascals Principle

🍃 Pascal's Principle

Definition. Pascal's Principle states that a change in pressure applied to an enclosed, incompressible fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point within the fluid and to the walls of its container. Formally, if an external pressure $\Delta P$ is applied at any point in a static fluid, then at every other point in that fluid the pressure increases by exactly $\Delta P$, regardless of the shape or volume of the container.

This result follows directly from the hydrostatic pressure relation. For an incompressible fluid in static equilibrium, the pressure at depth $h$ below a reference surface is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the reference surface, $\rho$ is the fluid density, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. Because $\rho$ and $g$ are uniform throughout, any increment $\Delta P_0$ applied at the surface propagates identically to every depth, leaving the pressure difference between any two points unchanged.

Worked Example. A hydraulic lift has a small piston of cross-sectional area $A_1 = 5.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2$ and a large piston of area $A_2 = 0.20\ \text{m}^2$. A technician exerts a force $F_1 = 150\ \text{N}$ on the small piston. Find the force $F_2$ exerted on the large piston, neglecting the weight of the fluid.

The applied pressure is

$$ P = \frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{150\ \text{N}}{5.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2} = 3.0 \times 10^{4}\ \text{Pa}. $$

By Pascal's Principle this pressure is transmitted undiminished to the large piston, so

$$ F_2 = P A_2 = (3.0 \times 10^{4}\ \text{Pa})(0.20\ \text{m}^2) = 6.0 \times 10^{3}\ \text{N}. $$

The output force exceeds the input force by the ratio $A_2/A_1 = 40$.

Key Theorem. In any hydraulic machine governed by Pascal's Principle, the output and input forces satisfy

$$ \frac{F_2}{F_1} = \frac{A_2}{A_1}. $$

This ratio can be made arbitrarily large by increasing $A_2$ relative to $A_1$. However, energy conservation requires that the small piston travel a proportionally greater distance: $d_1 A_1 = d_2 A_2$, so the volume displaced on each side is equal and no net work is gained.

Lab Exercise. Connect two syringes of different diameters with flexible tubing filled with water. Measure the force required to depress the small syringe and the force delivered at the large syringe. Compute the ratio of output to input force and compare it to the ratio of cross-sectional areas. Identify sources of deviation, such as fluid compressibility, friction, and air bubbles, and assess how each violates the assumptions underlying Pascal's Principle.
