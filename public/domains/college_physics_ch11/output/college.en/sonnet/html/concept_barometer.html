🌸 Barometer

🌸 Barometer

Definition. A barometer is an instrument that measures atmospheric pressure by balancing a column of liquid — typically mercury — against the weight of the overlying air. In the classical Torricellian design, a glass tube closed at one end is filled with mercury, inverted, and placed with its open end submerged in a mercury reservoir. Atmospheric pressure acting on the exposed surface of the reservoir supports the column of mercury inside the tube. At equilibrium, the pressure at the base of the mercury column equals atmospheric pressure:

$$ P_{\text{atm}} = \rho g h $$

where $\rho$ is the density of mercury ($13{,}600 \ \text{kg/m}^3$), $g = 9.81 \ \text{m/s}^2$ is the gravitational acceleration, and $h$ is the height of the mercury column above the reservoir surface. The space above the mercury column, known as the Torricellian vacuum, is essentially a vacuum (pressure $\approx 0$), since mercury vapor pressure at room temperature is negligible.

Worked Example. Suppose a barometer reads $h = 0.760 \ \text{m}$ on a standard day. Calculate the atmospheric pressure.

$$ P_{\text{atm}} = \rho g h = (13{,}600 \ \text{kg/m}^3)(9.81 \ \text{m/s}^2)(0.760 \ \text{m}) $$ $$ P_{\text{atm}} = 1.013 \times 10^5 \ \text{Pa} = 101.3 \ \text{kPa} $$

This is precisely 1 standard atmosphere (1 atm), confirming the calibration. If the column height were measured as $h = 0.735 \ \text{m}$, then $P_{\text{atm}} = (13{,}600)(9.81)(0.735) \approx 9.79 \times 10^4 \ \text{Pa}$, indicating a low-pressure weather system.

Key Theorem (Pascal's Principle and Hydrostatic Equilibrium). The operation of the barometer rests on the fact that pressure in a static fluid depends only on depth, not on the shape of the container. Specifically, at any horizontal level within a connected fluid, pressure is uniform. This ensures that the pressure at the base of the mercury column inside the tube equals $P_{\text{atm}}$ at the reservoir surface, validating the relation $P_{\text{atm}} = \rho g h$.

Lab Exercise. Using a 1-meter glass tube, mercury, and a beaker, construct a Torricellian barometer in a controlled laboratory setting. Record the column height $h$ every hour over a four-hour period. Using $P = \rho g h$, compute atmospheric pressure at each interval and compare your results against a calibrated digital barometer. Quantify discrepancies and propose sources of systematic error, such as mercury temperature variation or residual gas in the Torricellian vacuum.
