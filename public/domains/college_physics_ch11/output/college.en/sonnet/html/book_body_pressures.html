🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. In classical mechanics, a force is a vector quantity that describes an interaction capable of changing the state of motion of an object. Formally, Newton's Second Law defines net force as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}, $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass of the object (in kilograms) and $\vec{a}$ is its acceleration (in $\text{m/s}^2$). Force is measured in newtons: $1\ \text{N} = 1\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Because force is a vector, both its magnitude and direction must be specified; the net force on an object is the vector sum of all individual forces acting on it.

Worked Example. A crate of mass $m = 20\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 50\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -30\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}$. Find the acceleration of the crate.

Solution. Compute the net force:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{F}_1 + \vec{F}_2 = (50 - 30)\,\hat{x}\ \text{N} = 20\,\hat{x}\ \text{N}. $$

Apply Newton's Second Law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{20\,\hat{x}\ \text{N}}{20\ \text{kg}} = 1.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

The crate accelerates at $1.0\ \text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction.

Key Theorem — Newton's Third Law. For every force exerted by object $A$ on object $B$, there exists an equal and opposite force exerted by $B$ on $A$:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A}. $$

These action–reaction pairs act on different objects and therefore do not cancel in a free-body diagram of a single object. This law is foundational to understanding contact forces, tension, and normal forces in multi-body systems.

Lab Exercise. Using a force sensor and a dynamics cart on a low-friction track, apply three known horizontal forces simultaneously using spring scales oriented at measured angles. Measure the resulting acceleration with a motion detector and verify that $|\vec{F}_{\text{net}}| = m|\vec{a}|$ to within $5\%$ experimental uncertainty. Repeat with the cart's mass doubled and compare the accelerations to test the inverse-mass dependence predicted by Newton's Second Law.

🌱 Area

🌱 Area

Definition. In physics and mathematics, area is a scalar quantity that measures the two-dimensional extent of a surface or planar region. For a rectangle with side lengths $a$ and $b$, the area is $A = ab$. For an arbitrary planar region $\mathcal{R}$, area is defined via the double integral

$$ A = \iint_{\mathcal{R}} dA, $$

where $dA$ is the area element. In Cartesian coordinates, $dA = dx\, dy$; in polar coordinates, $dA = r\, dr\, d\theta$. Area carries SI units of square meters ($\text{m}^2$).

In College Physics 2E, area arises centrally in the study of fluids, thermodynamics, and electromagnetism—wherever a physical law involves a flux or a distributed force acting over a surface.

Worked Example. A circular piston of diameter $d = 0.10\ \text{m}$ is subjected to a gauge pressure of $P = 2.0 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$. Find the net force on the piston face.

First, compute the cross-sectional area:

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi \left(\frac{0.10}{2}\right)^2 = \pi (0.05)^2 \approx 7.85 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2. $$

Applying the relation $F = PA$:

$$ F = (2.0 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa})(7.85 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2) \approx 1.57 \times 10^3\ \text{N}. $$

The net force on the piston is approximately $1.57\ \text{kN}$, directed perpendicular to the piston face.

Key Theorem (Pressure–Force Relation). For a uniform pressure $P$ acting over a flat surface of area $A$, the magnitude of the resulting force is

$$ F = PA. $$

This follows directly from the definition of pressure as force per unit area, $P = F/A$. When pressure varies over the surface, the total force is obtained by integration: $F = \int P\, dA$. This generalization is essential when analyzing non-uniform pressure distributions in fluid statics and aerodynamics.

🍃 Fluid

🍃 Fluid

Definition. A fluid is any substance that cannot sustain a shear stress in static equilibrium and therefore deforms continuously under the application of even an infinitesimally small tangential force. Both liquids and gases satisfy this criterion: liquids are nearly incompressible fluids with a definite volume but no fixed shape, while gases are compressible fluids that expand to fill any available volume. The defining mechanical property of a fluid is that, at rest, the only internal stress it can support is a normal (compressive) stress, called pressure, acting equally in all directions at any interior point. A direct consequence is that the pressure at a depth $h$ below the free surface of a static, incompressible fluid of uniform density $\rho$ subject to gravitational acceleration $g$ is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface. This linear dependence on depth follows from mechanical equilibrium applied to an infinitesimal fluid element: $dP/dy = -\rho g$, where $y$ increases upward.

Worked Example. A sealed tank contains water ($\rho = 1.00 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}$) with a gauge pressure of $P_0 = 2.00 \times 10^4\ \mathrm{Pa}$ at the surface. Find the absolute pressure at a depth of $h = 3.00\ \mathrm{m}$, given $P_\text{atm} = 1.013 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}$.

The absolute surface pressure is $P_\text{surface} = P_\text{atm} + P_0 = 1.213 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}$. Applying the hydrostatic formula:

$$ P = 1.213 \times 10^5 + (1.00 \times 10^3)(9.81)(3.00) = 1.213 \times 10^5 + 2.943 \times 10^4 \approx 1.507 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}. $$

Key Theorem (Pascal's Principle). A pressure change $\Delta P$ applied to any point in an enclosed, static fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. Mathematically, for any two points at the same elevation, $\Delta P_1 = \Delta P_2$. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force $F_1$ applied over area $A_1$ produces pressure $F_1/A_1$, generating a magnified output force $F_2 = F_1(A_2/A_1)$ on a larger piston of area $A_2$.

Lab Exercise. Fill a tall graduated cylinder with water. Using a pressure sensor, measure the gauge pressure at depths of 5 cm, 10 cm, 15 cm, and 20 cm. Plot $P_\text{gauge}$ versus $h$ and determine the slope. Compare the experimental slope to the theoretical value $\rho g$ and compute the percent error. Discuss sources of systematic uncertainty, including sensor calibration and fluid temperature effects on density.

🍃 Pressure

🍃 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the normal force exerted per unit area on a surface. For a uniform force $F$ acting perpendicularly over an area $A$, pressure is given by

$$ P = \frac{F}{A}. $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\ \text{Pa} = 1\ \text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity: it acts equally in all directions at a point within a static fluid. In a fluid at rest, the pressure at depth $h$ below the surface is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface, $\rho$ is the fluid density, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. This relation shows that pressure increases linearly with depth, independent of the shape of the container.

Worked Example. A rectangular swimming pool contains water ($\rho = 1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3$) to a depth of $h = 2.50\ \text{m}$. Atmospheric pressure at the surface is $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$. Find the absolute pressure at the bottom of the pool.

Applying the hydrostatic pressure formula:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1.00 \times 10^3)(9.80)(2.50). $$ $$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 2.45 \times 10^4 = 1.258 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}. $$

The gauge pressure (pressure above atmospheric) at the bottom is $\rho g h = 2.45 \times 10^4\ \text{Pa} \approx 24.5\ \text{kPa}$.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point of the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if an external pressure increment $\Delta P$ is applied at the surface, then at every interior point the new pressure becomes $P' = P + \Delta P$. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small area generates a proportionally larger force over a larger area, with $F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2$.

Lab Exercise. Fill a tall graduated cylinder with water and attach a pressure sensor at three different depths (e.g., 5 cm, 10 cm, 20 cm). Record the gauge pressure at each depth and plot $P_{\text{gauge}}$ versus $h$. Verify that the slope of the best-fit line equals $\rho g$, and compare your experimental value of $\rho$ to the accepted value of $1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3$. Discuss sources of systematic error, including sensor calibration and meniscus effects.

🍃 Atmospheric Pressure

🍃 Atmospheric Pressure

Definition. Atmospheric pressure is the force per unit area exerted by the weight of the overlying column of air on any surface at a given altitude. At sea level, this pressure equals the weight of the entire atmospheric column above a unit area:

$$ P_{\text{atm}} = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{mg}{A} $$

where $m$ is the mass of the air column and $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$. The standard atmosphere is defined as $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$, equivalently $101.3\ \text{kPa}$, $1\ \text{atm}$, or $760\ \text{mmHg}$. Pressure decreases with altitude because the overlying air column grows shorter and less dense.

Worked Example. A mercury barometer consists of a sealed glass tube inverted in an open mercury reservoir. Atmospheric pressure supports a mercury column of height $h$. Given $\rho_{\text{Hg}} = 1.36 \times 10^4\ \text{kg/m}^3$, find $h$ at standard atmospheric pressure.

The hydrostatic relation $P = \rho g h$ gives the pressure at the base of a static fluid column of density $\rho$ and height $h$. Solving for $h$:

$$ h = \frac{P_0}{\rho g} = \frac{1.013 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}}{(1.36 \times 10^4\ \text{kg/m}^3)(9.8\ \text{m/s}^2)} = \frac{1.013 \times 10^5}{1.333 \times 10^5}\ \text{m} \approx 0.760\ \text{m} = 760\ \text{mm} $$

This confirms the definition of $1\ \text{atm} = 760\ \text{mmHg}$.

Key Theorem (Hydrostatic Pressure Variation). In a static fluid of uniform density $\rho$, pressure increases linearly with depth according to:

$$ \Delta P = \rho g \Delta z $$

For the atmosphere, applying this relation over an infinitesimal layer $dz$ and combining with the ideal gas law yields the separable differential equation $dP/dz = -\rho g$, whose solution for an isothermal column is exponential decay $P(z) = P_0\exp(-z/H)$, where $H = RT/(Mg) \approx 8.5\ \text{km}$ for dry air at $288\ \text{K}$. The linear approximation $\Delta P \approx \rho_{\text{air}} g \Delta z$ remains accurate for altitude changes small compared to $H$.

Lab Exercise. Using a digital barometer, record atmospheric pressure at ground level and at the top of a building of known height $\Delta z \approx 20\ \text{m}$. Compute the expected pressure difference $\Delta P = \rho_{\text{air}} g \Delta z$ using $\rho_{\text{air}} \approx 1.225\ \text{kg/m}^3$, and compare with the measured value. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including local temperature variation and instrument resolution.

🍃 Gauge Pressure

🍃 Gauge Pressure

Definition. Gauge pressure is the pressure of a fluid measured relative to the ambient atmospheric pressure, rather than relative to a perfect vacuum. If $P_\text{abs}$ denotes the absolute pressure at a point in a fluid and $P_\text{atm}$ denotes the local atmospheric pressure, then the gauge pressure $P_g$ is defined by

$$ P_g = P_\text{abs} - P_\text{atm}. $$

Gauge pressure may be positive (for pressures exceeding atmospheric), negative (for partial vacuums, sometimes called vacuum pressure), or zero (when the fluid is at atmospheric pressure). Because most practical instruments — tire gauges, sphygmomanometers, pressure transducers — measure the difference between internal and external pressure, gauge pressure is the quantity they report directly.

Worked Example. A scuba diver descends to a depth of $h = 20\,\text{m}$ in seawater of density $\rho = 1025\,\text{kg/m}^3$. Taking $g = 9.81\,\text{m/s}^2$ and $P_\text{atm} = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$, find the gauge pressure and the absolute pressure at that depth.

The hydrostatic relation gives the absolute pressure:

$$ P_\text{abs} = P_\text{atm} + \rho g h = 1.013\times10^5 + (1025)(9.81)(20). $$

Computing the fluid column term: $(1025)(9.81)(20) = 2.011\times10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Therefore

$$ P_\text{abs} = 1.013\times10^5 + 2.011\times10^5 = 3.024\times10^5\,\text{Pa} \approx 2.98\,\text{atm}. $$

The gauge pressure is simply $P_g = \rho g h = 2.011\times10^5\,\text{Pa} \approx 2.0\,\text{atm}$.

Key Theorem — Hydrostatic Gauge Pressure. For a static, incompressible fluid of uniform density $\rho$, the gauge pressure at depth $h$ below a free surface open to the atmosphere is

$$ P_g = \rho g h. $$

This follows directly from the definition: $P_g = P_\text{abs} - P_\text{atm} = (P_\text{atm} + \rho g h) - P_\text{atm}$. The result is independent of the shape or horizontal extent of the container and depends only on vertical depth, making gauge pressure the natural quantity for describing fluid loads on submerged surfaces.

Discuss deviations attributable to sensor offset, temperature variation of density, and meniscus effects near the column walls.

🌸 Body Pressures

🌸 Body Pressures

Definition. Pressure is defined as the normal force exerted per unit area on a surface:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} $$

where $F_\perp$ is the component of force perpendicular to the surface and $A$ is the area over which it acts. The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. In fluid systems, pressure at a depth $h$ below a free surface of a fluid with density $\rho$ is given by the hydrostatic relation:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface and $g = 9.80\,\text{m/s}^2$. In biological contexts, body pressures refer to the pressure distributions that arise within physiological fluid compartments, most notably blood pressure within the cardiovascular system.

Worked Example. A patient lies horizontally. Blood pressure measured at heart level is $P_\text{heart} = 13{,}300\,\text{Pa}$ (approximately 100 mmHg). Estimate the blood pressure at the patient's feet, located $h = 1.20\,\text{m}$ below the heart. Taking $\rho_\text{blood} = 1060\,\text{kg/m}^3$:

$$ \Delta P = \rho g h = (1060)(9.80)(1.20) \approx 1.25 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa} $$ $$ P_\text{feet} = 13{,}300 + 12{,}500 \approx 25{,}800\,\text{Pa} \approx 194\,\text{mmHg} $$

This elevation in pressure explains why prolonged standing can cause fluid accumulation in the lower extremities if venous return is insufficient.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. Any change in pressure applied to an enclosed, incompressible fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point within the fluid and to the walls of its container:

$$ \Delta P_1 = \Delta P_2 $$

This principle underlies hydraulic function in biological systems: a pressure change generated by ventricular contraction propagates uniformly through the arterial network, driving blood through vessels of varying cross-section without loss of the pressure signal.

Lab Exercise. Using a manometer and a vertical column of water ($\rho = 1000\,\text{kg/m}^3$), measure the gauge pressure at intervals of 0.10 m from the surface down to 0.50 m. Plot $P_\text{gauge}$ versus $h$ and verify that the slope equals $\rho g$. Compare your measured slope to the theoretical value $9800\,\text{Pa/m}$ and calculate the percent error. Payoff 🎯

Body Pressures stands as the natural culmination of Chapter 11 because it synthesizes every major principle of fluid statics—hydrostatic pressure gradients, Pascal's law, gauge versus absolute pressure, and surface tension—into a single, living system. The human body is, in thermodynamic terms, a pressurized vessel subdivided into compartments, each maintained at a precise pressure by muscular, elastic, and gravitational forces acting on incompressible and compressible fluids alike. Mastering body pressures means being able to read those forces quantitatively, not merely descriptively.

Definition. The hydrostatic pressure at depth $h$ below a reference surface in a fluid of density $\rho$ is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the reference level and $g = 9.80\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$. In a closed circulatory loop the heart acts as a pump that sustains a mean arterial pressure of roughly $100\ \mathrm{mmHg}$ ($\approx 13.3\ \mathrm{kPa}$) at the level of the heart, with pressure falling by $\rho g h \approx 8\ \mathrm{mmHg}$ per $10\ \mathrm{cm}$ of elevation above that reference.

Worked Example. A standing adult has their heart at $h = 0$ and their cerebral arteries at $h = +35\ \mathrm{cm}$ above the heart. Taking $\rho_{\text{blood}} = 1060\ \mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$ and mean arterial pressure at the heart as $P_\text{heart} = 100\ \mathrm{mmHg}$:

$$ P_\text{brain} = P_\text{heart} - \rho g h = 100\ \mathrm{mmHg} - (1060)(9.80)(0.35)\ \mathrm{Pa}. $$

Converting: $(1060)(9.80)(0.35) \approx 3635\ \mathrm{Pa} \approx 27\ \mathrm{mmHg}$, giving $P_\text{brain} \approx 73\ \mathrm{mmHg}$. This $27\ \mathrm{mmHg}$ drop explains why orthostatic hypotension (dizziness upon standing) occurs when compensatory vasoconstriction is delayed.

Key Theorem (Pascal's Principle). A pressure change $\Delta P$ applied anywhere to an enclosed, static fluid is transmitted undiminished throughout the fluid. In physiological terms, the thoracic pressure drop during inhalation ($\Delta P \approx -3\ \mathrm{mmHg}$) is felt simultaneously at the venous return vessels, driving blood back toward the heart—the respiratory pump mechanism.

Laboratory Exercise. Attach a sphygmomanometer cuff to a fluid-filled column of known height. Record pressure at three vertical positions separated by $\Delta h = 10\ \mathrm{cm}$. Compute $\rho$ from the measured pressure differences and compare with the accepted value for water; estimate the systematic error introduced by assuming an incompressible fluid.

The machinery developed here—pressure gradients, Pascal transmission, and gauge referencing—extends directly into respiratory mechanics, intraocular fluid dynamics, renal filtration pressure, and clinical measurement of intracranial pressure. If you wish to pursue one thread further, consider cardiovascular fluid dynamics: the interplay between the pressure gradient established here and the Bernoulli principle governing blood velocity through constricted vessels forms the quantitative foundation of diagnostic echocardiography and coronary stenosis assessment.
