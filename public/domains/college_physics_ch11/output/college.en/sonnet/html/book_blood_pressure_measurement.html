🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. In classical mechanics, a force is a vector quantity that describes an interaction capable of changing the state of motion of an object. Formally, Newton's Second Law defines net force as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}, $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass of the object (in kilograms) and $\vec{a}$ is its acceleration (in $\text{m/s}^2$). Force is measured in newtons: $1\ \text{N} = 1\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Because force is a vector, both its magnitude and direction must be specified; the net force on an object is the vector sum of all individual forces acting on it.

Worked Example. A crate of mass $m = 20\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 50\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -30\ \hat{x}\ \text{N}$. Find the acceleration of the crate.

Solution. Compute the net force:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{F}_1 + \vec{F}_2 = (50 - 30)\,\hat{x}\ \text{N} = 20\,\hat{x}\ \text{N}. $$

Apply Newton's Second Law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{20\,\hat{x}\ \text{N}}{20\ \text{kg}} = 1.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

The crate accelerates at $1.0\ \text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction.

Key Theorem — Newton's Third Law. For every force exerted by object $A$ on object $B$, there exists an equal and opposite force exerted by $B$ on $A$:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A}. $$

These action–reaction pairs act on different objects and therefore do not cancel in a free-body diagram of a single object. This law is foundational to understanding contact forces, tension, and normal forces in multi-body systems.

Repeat with the cart's mass doubled and compare the accelerations to test the inverse-mass dependence predicted by Newton's Second Law.

🌱 Area

🌱 Area

Definition. In physics and mathematics, area is a scalar quantity that measures the two-dimensional extent of a surface or planar region. For a rectangle with side lengths $a$ and $b$, the area is $A = ab$. For an arbitrary planar region $\mathcal{R}$, area is defined via the double integral

$$ A = \iint_{\mathcal{R}} dA, $$

where $dA$ is the area element. In Cartesian coordinates, $dA = dx\, dy$; in polar coordinates, $dA = r\, dr\, d\theta$. Area carries SI units of square meters ($\text{m}^2$).

In College Physics 2E, area arises centrally in the study of fluids, thermodynamics, and electromagnetism—wherever a physical law involves a flux or a distributed force acting over a surface.

Worked Example. A circular piston of diameter $d = 0.10\ \text{m}$ is subjected to a gauge pressure of $P = 2.0 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$. Find the net force on the piston face.

First, compute the cross-sectional area:

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi \left(\frac{0.10}{2}\right)^2 = \pi (0.05)^2 \approx 7.85 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2. $$

Applying the relation $F = PA$:

$$ F = (2.0 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa})(7.85 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2) \approx 1.57 \times 10^3\ \text{N}. $$

The net force on the piston is approximately $1.57\ \text{kN}$, directed perpendicular to the piston face.

Key Theorem (Pressure–Force Relation). For a uniform pressure $P$ acting over a flat surface of area $A$, the magnitude of the resulting force is

$$ F = PA. $$

This follows directly from the definition of pressure as force per unit area, $P = F/A$. When pressure varies over the surface, the total force is obtained by integration: $F = \int P\, dA$. This generalization is essential when analyzing non-uniform pressure distributions in fluid statics and aerodynamics.

🍃 Pressure

🍃 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the normal force exerted per unit area on a surface. For a uniform force $F$ acting perpendicularly over an area $A$, pressure is given by

$$ P = \frac{F}{A}. $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\ \text{Pa} = 1\ \text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity: it acts equally in all directions at a point within a static fluid. In a fluid at rest, the pressure at depth $h$ below the surface is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface, $\rho$ is the fluid density, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. This relation shows that pressure increases linearly with depth, independent of the shape of the container.

Worked Example. A rectangular swimming pool contains water ($\rho = 1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3$) to a depth of $h = 2.50\ \text{m}$. Atmospheric pressure at the surface is $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$. Find the absolute pressure at the bottom of the pool.

Applying the hydrostatic pressure formula:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1.00 \times 10^3)(9.80)(2.50). $$ $$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 2.45 \times 10^4 = 1.258 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}. $$

The gauge pressure (pressure above atmospheric) at the bottom is $\rho g h = 2.45 \times 10^4\ \text{Pa} \approx 24.5\ \text{kPa}$.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point of the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if an external pressure increment $\Delta P$ is applied at the surface, then at every interior point the new pressure becomes $P' = P + \Delta P$. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small area generates a proportionally larger force over a larger area, with $F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2$.

Verify that the slope of the best-fit line equals $\rho g$, and compare your experimental value of $\rho$ to the accepted value of $1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3$. Discuss sources of systematic error, including sensor calibration and meniscus effects.

🍃 Fluid

🍃 Fluid

Definition. A fluid is any substance that cannot sustain a shear stress in static equilibrium and therefore deforms continuously under the application of even an infinitesimally small tangential force. Both liquids and gases satisfy this criterion: liquids are nearly incompressible fluids with a definite volume but no fixed shape, while gases are compressible fluids that expand to fill any available volume. The defining mechanical property of a fluid is that, at rest, the only internal stress it can support is a normal (compressive) stress, called pressure, acting equally in all directions at any interior point. A direct consequence is that the pressure at a depth $h$ below the free surface of a static, incompressible fluid of uniform density $\rho$ subject to gravitational acceleration $g$ is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface. This linear dependence on depth follows from mechanical equilibrium applied to an infinitesimal fluid element: $dP/dy = -\rho g$, where $y$ increases upward.

Worked Example. A sealed tank contains water ($\rho = 1.00 \times 10^3\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}$) with a gauge pressure of $P_0 = 2.00 \times 10^4\ \mathrm{Pa}$ at the surface. Find the absolute pressure at a depth of $h = 3.00\ \mathrm{m}$, given $P_\text{atm} = 1.013 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}$.

The absolute surface pressure is $P_\text{surface} = P_\text{atm} + P_0 = 1.213 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}$. Applying the hydrostatic formula:

$$ P = 1.213 \times 10^5 + (1.00 \times 10^3)(9.81)(3.00) = 1.213 \times 10^5 + 2.943 \times 10^4 \approx 1.507 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{Pa}. $$

Key Theorem (Pascal's Principle). A pressure change $\Delta P$ applied to any point in an enclosed, static fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. Mathematically, for any two points at the same elevation, $\Delta P_1 = \Delta P_2$. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force $F_1$ applied over area $A_1$ produces pressure $F_1/A_1$, generating a magnified output force $F_2 = F_1(A_2/A_1)$ on a larger piston of area $A_2$.

Lab Exercise. Fill a tall graduated cylinder with water. Using a pressure sensor, measure the gauge pressure at depths of 5 cm, 10 cm, 15 cm, and 20 cm. Plot $P_\text{gauge}$ versus $h$ and determine the slope. Compare the experimental slope to the theoretical value $\rho g$ and compute the percent error. Discuss sources of systematic uncertainty, including sensor calibration and fluid temperature effects on density.

🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. Mass is a fundamental scalar quantity that characterizes two distinct but experimentally equivalent properties of matter: inertial mass and gravitational mass. Inertial mass $m_i$ quantifies an object's resistance to changes in its state of motion, appearing in Newton's second law as

$$ \sum \vec{F} = m_i \vec{a}. $$

Gravitational mass $m_g$ quantifies the strength of an object's coupling to a gravitational field, appearing in the law of universal gravitation as

$$ F_g = G \frac{m_g M_g}{r^2}. $$

The equivalence principle, confirmed experimentally to one part in $10^{12}$, asserts $m_i = m_g$, allowing the single symbol $m$ to serve both roles. In SI units, mass is measured in kilograms (kg), defined since 2019 by fixing Planck's constant to $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$.

Worked Example. A 4.0 kg block rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\ \hat{i}\ \text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\ \hat{i}\ \text{N}$. Find the acceleration.

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ a = \frac{\sum F_x}{m} = \frac{12\ \text{N} - 4\ \text{N}}{4.0\ \text{kg}} = \frac{8.0\ \text{N}}{4.0\ \text{kg}} = 2.0\ \text{m/s}^2\ (\hat{i}). $$

The block accelerates at $2.0\ \text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction. Note that weight $W = mg = (4.0)(9.8) = 39.2\ \text{N}$ acts downward and is cancelled by the normal force; mass, not weight, enters the inertial relation.

Key Theorem — Equivalence of Inertial and Gravitational Mass. For any object in free fall near Earth's surface, the gravitational force $F_g = m_g g$ produces an acceleration $a = F_g / m_i$. If $m_i \neq m_g$, different materials would fall at different rates. Galileo's inclined-plane experiments and Eötvös's torsion-balance measurements confirm $a = g$ universally, establishing $m_i = m_g$ as a foundational postulate of general relativity.

Using a force sensor and a dynamics cart on a low-friction track, apply three different measured forces $F_1, F_2, F_3$ to the same cart and record the resulting accelerations $a_1, a_2, a_3$. Compute the inertial mass as $m = F_k / a_k$ for each trial. Compare the average to the cart's balance-measured mass and calculate the percent discrepancy, verifying Newton's second law quantitatively.

🌱 Volume

🌱 Volume

Definition. Volume is the measure of the three-dimensional extent of a region in space. For a continuous body, it quantifies the amount of space the object occupies and is a scalar quantity. In SI units, volume is measured in cubic meters ($\text{m}^3$), though liters ($\text{L}$, where $1\ \text{L} = 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3$) are common in laboratory contexts. For a fluid or gas contained within a vessel, the volume $V$ is determined by the geometry of the container. For a compressible substance, volume is a thermodynamic state variable related to pressure and temperature through an equation of state.

For simple geometric solids, volume is computed analytically. A rectangular prism with dimensions $\ell$, $w$, and $h$ has volume $V = \ell w h$. A sphere of radius $r$ has volume

$$ V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3. $$

A cylinder of base radius $r$ and height $h$ has volume $V = \pi r^2 h$.

Worked Example. A spherical water tank has an inner diameter of $2.00\ \text{m}$. Determine the volume of water it can hold.

Solution. The radius is $r = 1.00\ \text{m}$. Applying the sphere formula:

$$ V = \frac{4}{3}\pi (1.00\ \text{m})^3 = \frac{4\pi}{3}\ \text{m}^3 \approx 4.19\ \text{m}^3. $$

Since $1\ \text{m}^3 = 1000\ \text{L}$, the tank holds approximately $4190\ \text{L}$ of water.

Key Theorem (Cavalieri's Principle). If two solids have equal cross-sectional areas at every height, they have equal volumes. Formally, if $A_1(z) = A_2(z)$ for all $z \in [0, h]$, then

$$ V_1 = \int_0^h A_1(z)\, dz = \int_0^h A_2(z)\, dz = V_2. $$

This principle underlies the integral definition of volume and justifies computing volumes of irregular solids by slicing.

Lab Exercise. Using a graduated cylinder, measure the volume of an irregularly shaped metal object by water displacement: record the initial water level $V_i$, submerge the object, and record the final level $V_f$. The object's volume is $V = V_f - V_i$. Compare this result with a geometric estimate based on caliper measurements of the object's approximate dimensions and compute the percent discrepancy.

🍃 Atmospheric Pressure

🍃 Atmospheric Pressure

Definition. Atmospheric pressure is the force per unit area exerted by the weight of the overlying column of air on any surface at a given altitude. At sea level, this pressure equals the weight of the entire atmospheric column above a unit area:

$$ P_{\text{atm}} = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{mg}{A} $$

where $m$ is the mass of the air column and $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$. The standard atmosphere is defined as $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$, equivalently $101.3\ \text{kPa}$, $1\ \text{atm}$, or $760\ \text{mmHg}$. Pressure decreases with altitude because the overlying air column grows shorter and less dense.

Worked Example. A mercury barometer consists of a sealed glass tube inverted in an open mercury reservoir. Atmospheric pressure supports a mercury column of height $h$. Given $\rho_{\text{Hg}} = 1.36 \times 10^4\ \text{kg/m}^3$, find $h$ at standard atmospheric pressure.

The hydrostatic relation $P = \rho g h$ gives the pressure at the base of a static fluid column of density $\rho$ and height $h$. Solving for $h$:

$$ h = \frac{P_0}{\rho g} = \frac{1.013 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}}{(1.36 \times 10^4\ \text{kg/m}^3)(9.8\ \text{m/s}^2)} = \frac{1.013 \times 10^5}{1.333 \times 10^5}\ \text{m} \approx 0.760\ \text{m} = 760\ \text{mm} $$

This confirms the definition of $1\ \text{atm} = 760\ \text{mmHg}$.

Key Theorem (Hydrostatic Pressure Variation). In a static fluid of uniform density $\rho$, pressure increases linearly with depth according to:

$$ \Delta P = \rho g \Delta z $$

For the atmosphere, applying this relation over an infinitesimal layer $dz$ and combining with the ideal gas law yields the separable differential equation $dP/dz = -\rho g$, whose solution for an isothermal column is exponential decay $P(z) = P_0\exp(-z/H)$, where $H = RT/(Mg) \approx 8.5\ \text{km}$ for dry air at $288\ \text{K}$. The linear approximation $\Delta P \approx \rho_{\text{air}} g \Delta z$ remains accurate for altitude changes small compared to $H$.

Lab Exercise. Using a digital barometer, record atmospheric pressure at ground level and at the top of a building of known height $\Delta z \approx 20\ \text{m}$. Compute the expected pressure difference $\Delta P = \rho_{\text{air}} g \Delta z$ using $\rho_{\text{air}} \approx 1.225\ \text{kg/m}^3$, and compare with the measured value. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including local temperature variation and instrument resolution.

🍃 Density

🍃 Density

Definition. The density of a substance is defined as its mass per unit volume. For a homogeneous sample of mass $m$ occupying volume $V$, the density is

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} $$

with SI units of kilograms per cubic meter ($\text{kg/m}^3$). For non-uniform materials, density is defined locally as the limit

$$ \rho = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\Delta m}{\Delta V} $$

ensuring the definition applies even when composition varies spatially. Density depends on the material and its thermodynamic state (temperature and pressure), not on the size of the sample.

Worked Example. A rectangular aluminum block measures $5.00 \text{ cm} \times 3.00 \text{ cm} \times 2.00 \text{ cm}$ and has a mass of $81.0 \text{ g}$. Determine its density and identify whether the value is consistent with pure aluminum ($\rho_\text{Al} = 2700 \text{ kg/m}^3$).

First, compute the volume:

$$ V = (0.0500)(0.0300)(0.0200) \text{ m}^3 = 3.00 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^3 $$

Convert the mass: $m = 81.0 \text{ g} = 0.0810 \text{ kg}$.

Then:

$$ \rho = \frac{0.0810 \text{ kg}}{3.00 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^3} = 2700 \text{ kg/m}^3 $$

The result matches the tabulated value for aluminum, confirming the identification.

Key Theorem — Archimedes' Principle. When an object is fully or partially submerged in a fluid of density $\rho_f$, the upward force exerted by the fluid equals the weight of the displaced fluid:

$$ F_b = \rho_f \, g \, V_\text{sub} $$

where $V_\text{sub}$ is the submerged volume. This result follows directly from the definition of density: the displaced fluid has mass $m_f = \rho_f V_\text{sub}$, and its weight $m_f g$ is precisely the force the fluid exerts on the object. An object floats when $\rho_\text{object} \leq \rho_f$, since full submersion is not required to balance gravity.

Lab Exercise. Using a graduated cylinder, a balance, and tap water ($\rho_w = 1000 \text{ kg/m}^3$), determine the density of an irregularly shaped rock via water displacement. Calculate $\rho = m/(V_2 - V_1)$ and compare your result to reference values for common silicate minerals. Estimate your percent error and identify the dominant source of uncertainty.

🌱 Gravitational Acceleration

🌱 Gravitational Acceleration

Definition. Gravitational acceleration is the acceleration imparted to an object solely by the gravitational force acting upon it, in the absence of any other forces. Near Earth's surface, every freely falling body experiences a constant downward acceleration conventionally denoted $g$. From Newton's second law combined with Newton's law of universal gravitation, the gravitational acceleration at a distance $r$ from the center of a spherically symmetric body of mass $M$ is

$$ g(r) = \frac{GM}{r^2} $$

where $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/kg}^2$ is the universal gravitational constant and $M$ is the mass of the attracting body. At Earth's surface ($r = R_\oplus \approx 6.371 \times 10^6\ \text{m}$, $M_\oplus \approx 5.972 \times 10^{24}\ \text{kg}$), this yields $g \approx 9.81\ \text{m/s}^2$. The quantity $g$ is independent of the mass of the falling object, a result consistent with the equivalence of gravitational and inertial mass.

Worked Example. A spacecraft orbits Earth at an altitude $h = 400\ \text{km}$ above the surface. Determine the gravitational acceleration at that altitude.

The orbital radius is $r = R_\oplus + h = (6.371 + 0.400) \times 10^6\ \text{m} = 6.771 \times 10^6\ \text{m}$. Applying the formula:

$$ g(r) = \frac{GM_\oplus}{r^2} = \frac{(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.972 \times 10^{24})}{(6.771 \times 10^6)^2} \approx 8.69\ \text{m/s}^2 $$

This value is approximately $88.6\%$ of surface gravity, confirming that gravitational acceleration decreases with altitude but remains substantial in low Earth orbit.

Key Theorem. For a uniform spherical shell of mass $M$, the gravitational field inside the shell is exactly zero, while outside the shell the field is identical to that of a point mass $M$ located at the center. This shell theorem, proven via direct integration of Newton's law over surface elements, justifies treating Earth as a point mass when computing $g$ at or above the surface. A direct consequence is that $g(r)$ decreases as $1/r^2$ for $r \geq R_\oplus$, and would increase linearly with $r$ inside a uniform solid sphere.

Lab Exercise. Using a free-fall apparatus (photogate timer and steel ball), measure the time $t$ for a ball to fall a known height $d$. From $d = \frac{1}{2}g t^2$, extract $g_{\text{exp}} = \frac{2d}{t^2}$. Repeat the measurement at five different heights, plot $d$ versus $t^2$, and determine $g$ from the slope. Compare your result with the accepted value $9.81\ \text{m/s}^2$ and compute the percent error, discussing sources of systematic uncertainty such as air resistance and timing latency.

🍃 Gauge Pressure

🍃 Gauge Pressure

Definition. Gauge pressure is the pressure of a fluid measured relative to the ambient atmospheric pressure, rather than relative to a perfect vacuum. If $P_\text{abs}$ denotes the absolute pressure at a point in a fluid and $P_\text{atm}$ denotes the local atmospheric pressure, then the gauge pressure $P_g$ is defined by

$$ P_g = P_\text{abs} - P_\text{atm}. $$

Gauge pressure may be positive (for pressures exceeding atmospheric), negative (for partial vacuums, sometimes called vacuum pressure), or zero (when the fluid is at atmospheric pressure). Because most practical instruments — tire gauges, sphygmomanometers, pressure transducers — measure the difference between internal and external pressure, gauge pressure is the quantity they report directly.

Worked Example. A scuba diver descends to a depth of $h = 20\,\text{m}$ in seawater of density $\rho = 1025\,\text{kg/m}^3$. Taking $g = 9.81\,\text{m/s}^2$ and $P_\text{atm} = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$, find the gauge pressure and the absolute pressure at that depth.

The hydrostatic relation gives the absolute pressure:

$$ P_\text{abs} = P_\text{atm} + \rho g h = 1.013\times10^5 + (1025)(9.81)(20). $$

Computing the fluid column term: $(1025)(9.81)(20) = 2.011\times10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Therefore

$$ P_\text{abs} = 1.013\times10^5 + 2.011\times10^5 = 3.024\times10^5\,\text{Pa} \approx 2.98\,\text{atm}. $$

The gauge pressure is simply $P_g = \rho g h = 2.011\times10^5\,\text{Pa} \approx 2.0\,\text{atm}$.

Key Theorem — Hydrostatic Gauge Pressure. For a static, incompressible fluid of uniform density $\rho$, the gauge pressure at depth $h$ below a free surface open to the atmosphere is

$$ P_g = \rho g h. $$

This follows directly from the definition: $P_g = P_\text{abs} - P_\text{atm} = (P_\text{atm} + \rho g h) - P_\text{atm}$. The result is independent of the shape or horizontal extent of the container and depends only on vertical depth, making gauge pressure the natural quantity for describing fluid loads on submerged surfaces.

Lower the sensor in measured increments of $\Delta h = 5\,\text{cm}$ and record $P_g$ at each depth. Plot $P_g$ versus $h$ and determine the slope; compare the measured slope to the predicted value $\rho g$, where $\rho = 1000\,\text{kg/m}^3$ for fresh water. Discuss deviations attributable to sensor offset, temperature variation of density, and meniscus effects near the column walls.

🍃 Pressure Depth Relation

🍃 Pressure Depth Relation

Definition. In a static fluid of uniform density $\rho$, the absolute pressure $P$ at a depth $h$ below a free surface where the pressure is $P_0$ is given by

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $g$ is the magnitude of the gravitational acceleration. This result follows directly from the condition of hydrostatic equilibrium: for any thin horizontal fluid layer of thickness $dh$, the net upward pressure force must balance the downward weight of the layer. Formally, considering a fluid column of cross-sectional area $A$,

$$ P(h + dh)\,A - P(h)\,A = \rho g A\, dh \implies \frac{dP}{dh} = \rho g. $$

Integrating from the surface downward yields the linear pressure–depth relation. Note that pressure depends only on depth, not on the horizontal position or the shape of the container.

Worked Example. A scuba diver descends to a depth of $h = 30\,\text{m}$ in seawater ($\rho = 1025\,\text{kg/m}^3$). Atmospheric pressure at the surface is $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Find the absolute pressure experienced by the diver.

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1025)(9.81)(30) $$ $$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 3.017 \times 10^5 = 4.03 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa} \approx 3.98\,\text{atm}. $$

The pressure in excess of atmospheric is $\rho g h \approx 3.0 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$, roughly three additional atmospheres. This is why pressure equalization in the ears becomes necessary even at moderate depths.

Key Theorem (Hydrostatic Invariance). In a connected static fluid, all points at the same depth are at the same pressure, regardless of the geometry of the vessel. This follows because any two points at equal depth are connected by a horizontal path along which $dP/dh = 0$, so no pressure difference can develop along that path.

Lab Exercise. Fill a tall graduated cylinder with water and attach pressure sensors at depths of $0.10\,\text{m}$, $0.20\,\text{m}$, and $0.30\,\text{m}$. Record the pressure reading in excess of atmospheric at each depth and plot that excess pressure versus $h$. Determine the slope of the best-fit line and compare it to the theoretical value $\rho g = (1000)(9.81) = 9810\,\text{Pa/m}$. Assess whether the relationship is linear and identify possible sources of systematic error, such as temperature gradients or sensor calibration drift.

🌸 Manometer

🌸 Manometer

A manometer is a pressure-measuring device that uses a column of fluid in a U-shaped tube to compare an unknown pressure against a known reference pressure. In its simplest form, an open-tube manometer consists of a U-tube partially filled with a liquid of known density $\rho$ (typically mercury or water), with one end open to the atmosphere and the other connected to the pressure source under investigation. The gauge pressure of the source is determined by measuring the height difference between the two fluid columns.

Definition. For an open-tube manometer, the gauge pressure $P_g$ of a gas at the closed end is related to the height difference $h$ between the two liquid surfaces by

$$ P_g = P - P_{\text{atm}} = \rho g h, $$

where $P$ is the absolute pressure of the gas, $P_{\text{atm}}$ is atmospheric pressure, $\rho$ is the density of the manometric fluid, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. When the gas pressure exceeds atmospheric pressure, the fluid level on the gas side is depressed and $h > 0$; when the gas pressure is below atmospheric, the fluid rises on the gas side and $h < 0$.

Worked Example. A closed container of gas is connected to an open-tube water manometer. The water level on the gas side sits $12.0\ \text{cm}$ below the level on the open side. Determine the gauge pressure of the gas.

Given: $\rho_{\text{water}} = 1.00 \times 10^3\ \text{kg/m}^3$, $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$, $h = 0.120\ \text{m}$.

$$ P_g = \rho g h = (1.00 \times 10^3)(9.80)(0.120) = 1.18 \times 10^3\ \text{Pa}. $$

The gas pressure exceeds atmospheric pressure by approximately $1.18\ \text{kPa}$.

Key Theorem (Pascal's Principle applied). At any two points within a static, connected fluid at the same height, the pressure is equal. This principle guarantees that the pressure transmitted by the gas at the fluid interface is fully communicated to the open column, making the height difference $h$ a faithful measure of the gauge pressure.

🌸 Blood Pressure Measurement

🌸 Blood Pressure Measurement

Definition. Blood pressure is the gauge pressure exerted by circulating blood against the walls of blood vessels. It is reported as two values: systolic pressure $P_s$ (peak pressure during ventricular contraction) and diastolic pressure $P_d$ (minimum pressure during ventricular relaxation), both measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg). The gauge pressure $P_\text{gauge}$ is related to absolute and atmospheric pressure by

$$ P_\text{gauge} = P_\text{abs} - P_\text{atm}. $$

Clinical measurement relies on the sphygmomanometer, which applies an occlusion cuff to the upper arm at heart level. Inflation of the cuff above systolic pressure collapses the brachial artery; as cuff pressure is slowly released, a stethoscope placed over the artery detects the onset of turbulent flow (Korotkoff sounds) at $P_s$ and their disappearance at $P_d$.

Worked Example. A patient's blood pressure is recorded as 120/80 mmHg. Convert both values to SI units (pascals) and compute the mean arterial pressure (MAP), approximated by

$$ \text{MAP} \approx P_d + \tfrac{1}{3}(P_s - P_d). $$

Using the conversion $1\ \text{mmHg} = 133.322\ \text{Pa}$:

$$ P_s = 120 \times 133.322\ \text{Pa} \approx 15{,}999\ \text{Pa}, $$ $$ P_d = 80 \times 133.322\ \text{Pa} \approx 10{,}666\ \text{Pa}. $$

The pulse pressure is $\Delta P = P_s - P_d = 40\ \text{mmHg} \approx 5{,}333\ \text{Pa}$, and

$$ \text{MAP} = 10{,}666 + \tfrac{1}{3}(5{,}333) \approx 12{,}444\ \text{Pa} \approx 93.3\ \text{mmHg}. $$

Key Theorem (Hydrostatic Pressure Correction). Because blood is a fluid of density $\rho \approx 1060\ \text{kg/m}^3$, pressure varies with vertical height $h$ relative to heart level:

$$ \Delta P = \rho g h. $$

A measurement taken at a point $h = 0.30\ \text{m}$ below heart level overestimates true cardiac pressure by $\Delta P = (1060)(9.81)(0.30) \approx 3{,}119\ \text{Pa} \approx 23\ \text{mmHg}$. This underscores the clinical requirement that the cuff be positioned at the level of the right atrium.

Payoff 🎯

Blood pressure measurement stands as the natural culmination of the fluid mechanics and pressure principles developed throughout this chapter. It is the point at which abstract hydrostatic theory—Pascal's law, gauge pressure, the continuity equation, and Bernoulli's principle—converges into a single clinical procedure of profound physiological significance. To measure blood pressure is to interrogate the cardiovascular system's mechanical state: the force per unit area that circulating blood exerts against arterial walls, encoded in the familiar systolic/diastolic pair $P_s / P_d$ expressed in millimeters of mercury ($\text{mmHg}$).

The standard auscultatory method provides a concrete worked example of pressure physics in action. A sphygmomanometer cuff is inflated above systolic pressure, occluding the brachial artery completely. As cuff pressure $P_\text{cuff}$ decreases, the moment it falls below $P_s$, blood forces its way through the compressed vessel in turbulent pulses, generating Korotkoff sounds detectable by stethoscope. Flow ceases to be turbulent when $P_\text{cuff} < P_d$, and silence resumes. The two thresholds directly yield the gauge pressures

$$ P_s = \rho g h_s, \qquad P_d = \rho g h_d, $$

where $h_s$ and $h_d$ are the equivalent mercury column heights read from the manometer, and $\rho = 13{,}600\ \text{kg/m}^3$ for mercury. A typical healthy adult reading of $120/80\ \text{mmHg}$ corresponds to absolute pressures of approximately $16.0\ \text{kPa}$ and $10.7\ \text{kPa}$ above atmospheric.

A key theorem underlying the measurement is that hydrostatic pressure depends on vertical position: $\Delta P = \rho g \Delta h$. This is why cuff placement at heart level is not merely convention but a physical requirement—a cuff positioned $10\ \text{cm}$ too low introduces an error of roughly $7.5\ \text{mmHg}$, sufficient to misclassify a patient's hypertensive status.
