🌱 Coulomb Force

🌱 Coulomb Force

Definition. The Coulomb force is the electrostatic force between two point charges. For charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by a distance $r$ in vacuum, the magnitude of the force is given by Coulomb's Law:

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N \cdot m^2 \cdot C^{-2}}$ is Coulomb's constant, related to the permittivity of free space by $k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$, with $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \mathrm{C^2 \cdot N^{-1} \cdot m^{-2}}$. The force is repulsive when the charges share the same sign and attractive when they are opposite in sign. In vector form, the force exerted on charge $q_2$ by charge $q_1$ is:

$$ \vec{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r}_{12} $$

where $\hat{r}_{12}$ is the unit vector directed from $q_1$ toward $q_2$.

Worked Example. Two point charges, $q_1 = +3.0 \, \mu\mathrm{C}$ and $q_2 = -5.0 \, \mu\mathrm{C}$, are placed $0.20 \, \mathrm{m}$ apart. Find the magnitude and nature of the force between them.

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(5.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-11}}{4.0 \times 10^{-2}} \approx 3.4 \, \mathrm{N} $$

Because the charges are opposite in sign, this force is attractive; each charge is pulled toward the other along the line connecting them.

Key Theorem — Superposition Principle. The total electrostatic force on a charge due to a collection of $n$ other point charges is the vector sum of the individual pairwise Coulomb forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \vec{F}_i = k_e q \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{q_i}{r_i^2} \hat{r}_i $$

This linearity is exact in classical electrostatics and underpins every calculation involving charge distributions.

Lab Exercise. Using a torsion balance apparatus, place two identically charged spheres at separations of $r = 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20 \, \mathrm{m}$. Plot $F$ versus $r^{-2}$ and verify that the relationship is linear, confirming the inverse-square dependence in Coulomb's Law. Estimate $k_e$ from the slope and compare with the accepted value.

🌱 Diffusion

🌱 Diffusion

Definition. Diffusion is the net transport of particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, driven by random thermal motion rather than by bulk fluid flow. For a one-dimensional system, Fick's First Law quantifies the diffusion flux:

$$ J = -D \frac{dC}{dx} $$

where $J$ is the particle flux (particles per unit area per unit time), $C(x)$ is the concentration (particles per unit volume), $x$ is position, and $D$ is the diffusion coefficient (units: $\text{m}^2/\text{s}$). The negative sign indicates that net transport opposes the concentration gradient — particles flow "downhill" in concentration. Fick's Second Law describes how concentration evolves in time:

$$ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} $$

The diffusion coefficient depends on temperature, particle size, and medium viscosity. For small spherical particles in a fluid, the Stokes–Einstein relation gives $D = k_B T / (6\pi \eta r)$, where $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant, $T$ is absolute temperature, $\eta$ is fluid viscosity, and $r$ is particle radius.

Worked Example. A thin layer of dye is introduced at $x = 0$ in a long tube of water at $T = 298\ \text{K}$. The dye molecules have radius $r = 0.5\ \text{nm}$ and water viscosity is $\eta = 8.9 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{Pa·s}$. Estimate the diffusion coefficient and the RMS displacement after $t = 10\ \text{s}$.

$$ D = \frac{(1.38 \times 10^{-23})(298)}{6\pi (8.9 \times 10^{-4})(0.5 \times 10^{-9})} \approx 4.9 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s} $$

The RMS displacement follows from the Einstein relation $\langle x^2 \rangle = 2Dt$:

$$ x_\text{rms} = \sqrt{2Dt} = \sqrt{2(4.9 \times 10^{-10})(10)} \approx 3.1\ \mu\text{m} $$

Key Theorem. For an initially localized source of $N$ particles, the solution to Fick's Second Law is a Gaussian profile:

$$ C(x,t) = \frac{N}{\sqrt{4\pi D t}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{4Dt}\right) $$

This demonstrates that the diffusion front spreads as $\sqrt{t}$, not linearly in time — a hallmark distinguishing diffusion from convective transport.

Lab Exercise. Prepare two identical graduated cylinders: one filled with cold water ($\approx 5\ ^\circ\text{C}$) and one with warm water ($\approx 50\ ^\circ\text{C}$). Carefully introduce one drop of food coloring at the bottom of each cylinder. Photograph both cylinders every 30 seconds for 10 minutes. Estimate $x_\text{rms}(t)$ from each image sequence, plot $x_\text{rms}^2$ versus $t$, and extract $D$ from the slope. Compare the ratio $D_\text{warm}/D_\text{cold}$ with the prediction from the Stokes–Einstein relation using the known viscosities of water at each temperature.

🌱 Potential Difference

🌱 Potential Difference

Definition. The electric potential difference (or voltage) between two points A and B in an electric field is defined as the work done per unit positive charge by an external agent in moving a test charge quasi-statically from A to B against the electric force:

$$ \Delta V = V_B - V_A = -\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{\ell} $$

The SI unit is the volt (V), where $1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C}$. Because the electric field is conservative, this line integral is path-independent — it depends only on the endpoints. Equivalently, the work done by the electric force on a charge $q$ moving from A to B is

$$ W_{A \to B} = q(V_A - V_B) = -q\,\Delta V. $$

A positive charge released from rest accelerates from high potential toward low potential; a negative charge does the opposite.

Worked Example. A uniform electric field $\vec{E} = 500\,\hat{x}\,\text{V/m}$ exists between two parallel plates separated by $d = 0.04\,\text{m}$. Find the potential difference $\Delta V = V_B - V_A$ where A is on the positive plate and B on the negative plate, then find the work done on a proton ($q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$) moving from A to B.

Taking the displacement along $\hat{x}$ from A to B:

$$ \Delta V = -\int_0^d E\,dx = -E\,d = -(500)(0.04) = -20\,\text{V}. $$

The proton loses $20\,\text{V}$ of potential. Work done by the electric force:

$$ W = q(V_A - V_B) = (1.6\times10^{-19})(+20) = 3.2\times10^{-18}\,\text{J}. $$

The proton gains $3.2\,\text{aJ}$ of kinetic energy, consistent with it accelerating toward the negative plate.

Key Theorem (Equipotential Surfaces). Along any surface on which $V$ is constant, $\vec{E} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = 0$, so the electric field is everywhere perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential.

🍃 Resting Membrane Potential

🍃 Resting Membrane Potential

Definition. The resting membrane potential is the steady-state electrical potential difference across the plasma membrane of an excitable cell (neuron or muscle fiber) when the cell is not transmitting a signal. It is defined as

$$ V_m = V_{\text{in}} - V_{\text{out}} $$

and takes a characteristic value of approximately $-70\ \text{mV}$ in mammalian neurons, with the interior of the cell negative relative to the extracellular fluid. This potential arises from the unequal distribution of ions—primarily $\text{K}^+$, $\text{Na}^+$, $\text{Cl}^-$, and large organic anions $A^-$—across a selectively permeable membrane, maintained by the $\text{Na}^+$/$\text{K}^+$-ATPase pump.

Worked Example. Consider a simplified membrane permeable only to $\text{K}^+$, with intracellular concentration $[\text{K}^+]_{\text{in}} = 140\ \text{mM}$ and extracellular concentration $[\text{K}^+]_{\text{out}} = 5\ \text{mM}$. The equilibrium potential for $\text{K}^+$ is given by the Nernst equation:

$$ E_{\text{K}} = \frac{RT}{zF}\ln\frac{[\text{K}^+]_{\text{out}}}{[\text{K}^+]_{\text{in}}} $$

At body temperature $T = 310\ \text{K}$, with $R = 8.314\ \text{J mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$, $z = +1$, and $F = 96485\ \text{C mol}^{-1}$:

$$ E_{\text{K}} = \frac{(8.314)(310)}{(1)(96485)}\ln\frac{5}{140} \approx (0.02669)\,(-3.332) \approx -89\ \text{mV} $$

This value is close to—but more negative than—the actual resting potential, because $\text{Na}^+$ and $\text{Cl}^-$ permeabilities are nonzero. The Goldman–Hodgkin–Katz (GHK) equation generalizes the Nernst equation by weighting each ion by its permeability $P$:

$$ V_m = \frac{RT}{F}\ln\frac{P_{\text{K}}[\text{K}^+]_o + P_{\text{Na}}[\text{Na}^+]_o + P_{\text{Cl}}[\text{Cl}^-]_i}{P_{\text{K}}[\text{K}^+]_i + P_{\text{Na}}[\text{Na}^+]_i + P_{\text{Cl}}[\text{Cl}^-]_o} $$

Key Theorem (Electrochemical Equilibrium). At rest, the net ionic current across the membrane is zero. For each permeable ion species $j$, the electrochemical driving force $V_m - E_j$ determines the direction of current flow. The resting potential $V_m$ is the unique voltage at which the sum of all ionic currents $\sum_j I_j = 0$, making the GHK voltage the electrostatic fixed point of the multi-ion system.

Lab Exercise. Using a patch-clamp amplifier or simulation software (e.g., NEURON), record the membrane potential of a modeled neuron under control conditions. Systematically vary the extracellular $[\text{K}^+]_{\text{out}}$ from $2\ \text{mM}$ to $20\ \text{mM}$ in five steps. Plot $V_m$ versus $\ln[\text{K}^+]_{\text{out}}$ and determine the slope. Compare your measured slope to the theoretical Nernst slope of $\approx 61.5\ \text{mV/decade}$ at $37\ \text{°C}$, and discuss any deviation in terms of non-$\text{K}^+$ permeabilities.

🍃 Action Potential

🍃 Action Potential

Definition. An action potential is a transient, self-propagating reversal of the transmembrane electrical potential in an excitable cell. At rest, the interior of a neuron is approximately $V_{\text{rest}} \approx -70\,\text{mV}$ relative to the extracellular fluid, maintained by the sodium–potassium pump ($\text{Na}^+/\text{K}^+$-ATPase) and selective ion permeability. When a stimulus depolarizes the membrane to the threshold potential $V_{\text{th}} \approx -55\,\text{mV}$, voltage-gated $\text{Na}^+$ channels open rapidly, driving a massive inward sodium current. The membrane potential transiently rises to approximately $+40\,\text{mV}$ (depolarization), after which $\text{Na}^+$ channels inactivate and voltage-gated $\text{K}^+$ channels open, producing an outward potassium current that restores and briefly hyperpolarizes the membrane (repolarization and afterhyperpolarization) before returning to $V_{\text{rest}}$.

Worked Example. The charge displacement during an action potential can be estimated using the membrane capacitance model. Treating the axon membrane as a parallel-plate capacitor with capacitance per unit area $C_m = 1\,\mu\text{F/cm}^2$, the charge per unit area required to shift the membrane potential by $\Delta V = 110\,\text{mV}$ (from $-70\,\text{mV}$ to $+40\,\text{mV}$) is:

$$ \Delta Q = C_m \,\Delta V = (1 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{F/cm}^2)(0.110\,\text{V}) = 1.1 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{C/cm}^2. $$

For an axon segment of area $A = 10^{-4}\,\text{cm}^2$, the total charge transferred is $Q = 1.1 \times 10^{-11}\,\text{C}$, corresponding to roughly $6.9 \times 10^{7}$ monovalent ions — a tiny fraction of the total intracellular ion population, consistent with the cell's ability to fire repeatedly.

Key Theorem (All-or-Nothing Principle). An action potential either fires completely or not at all: if the membrane reaches $V_{\text{th}}$, the subsequent depolarization proceeds to its full amplitude regardless of stimulus strength. This follows from the positive feedback loop between $\text{Na}^+$ influx and further depolarization ($I_{\text{Na}} \propto g_{\text{Na}}(V - E_{\text{Na}}}$), which becomes self-sustaining above threshold. The refractory period following an action potential enforces unidirectional propagation along the axon.

🌸 Electrocardiogram

🌸 Electrocardiogram

Definition. An electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG) is a graphical record of the electrical potential differences generated by the heart's coordinated depolarization and repolarization cycles, measured at the body surface. The heart functions as a biological dipole: as the wave of electrical excitation propagates through the myocardium, it produces a time-varying electric dipole moment $\vec{p}(t)$ whose field can be detected by electrodes placed on the skin. Each electrode pair measures a voltage $\Delta V = V_+ - V_-$, which is amplified and plotted as a function of time to produce the characteristic ECG waveform. The standard 12-lead ECG samples this dipole field from twelve distinct directions by placing electrodes on the limbs and precordium.

The ECG trace is divided into recognizable deflections. The P wave corresponds to atrial depolarization; the QRS complex (typical duration $80$–$100\,\text{ms}$) represents rapid ventricular depolarization; and the T wave reflects ventricular repolarization. The isoelectric baseline between features indicates zero net current flow in the cardiac dipole.

Worked Example. Suppose an ECG records a QRS complex with a peak voltage of $1.2\,\text{mV}$ across Lead II (the potential difference between the left leg electrode and the right arm electrode, separated by an effective distance of approximately $0.70\,\text{m}$ along the body). Estimate the electric field magnitude at the electrode site:

$$ E \approx \frac{\Delta V}{d} = \frac{1.2 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{V}}{0.70\,\text{m}} \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{V/m}. $$

This confirms that cardiac fields at body-surface distances are small but detectable with modern amplification ($\gtrsim 10^4$ gain).

Key Theorem (Einthoven's Law). For the three standard limb leads — Lead I ($V_I$), Lead II ($V_{II}$), and Lead III ($V_{III}$) — the voltage readings satisfy the linear relation

$$ V_I + V_{III} = V_{II} $$

at every instant in time. This follows directly from Kirchhoff's voltage law applied to the triangle formed by the three electrode sites (Einthoven's triangle), and provides an internal consistency check for electrode placement.

Lab Exercise. Connect a three-lead ECG apparatus to a subject at rest and record 10 seconds of data. Identify and measure the duration of the QRS complex and the R–R interval (time between successive R peaks). Compute the instantaneous heart rate as $f = 1/T_{RR}$. Then verify Einthoven's Law numerically at three distinct time points during the recording by computing $V_I + V_{III} - V_{II}$ and confirming it is within the noise floor of the instrument ($\lesssim 0.05\,\text{mV}$).

Payoff 🎯

The electrocardiogram (ECG) represents the culmination of the electrostatic and bioelectric principles developed throughout this chapter: it is a direct, non-invasive measurement of the time-varying electric potential field generated by the coordinated depolarization and repolarization of cardiac muscle cells. Every concept encountered along the way — electric potential, equipotential surfaces, the superposition of point-charge fields, and the behavior of ionic charge distributions in conducting media — converges in this single clinical instrument.

What the ECG achieves. The heart functions as a biological dipole whose orientation and magnitude shift with each phase of the cardiac cycle. As roughly $10^{10}$ myocytes depolarize in sequence, the net dipole moment $\vec{p}(t)$ sweeps through the chest volume. At a skin electrode located at position $\vec{r}$ from the heart's electrical center, the measured potential difference is approximately

$$ \Delta V \approx \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\vec{p}(t)\cdot\hat{r}}{r^2}, $$

where $\hat{r}$ is the unit vector from the dipole to the electrode. By recording $\Delta V(t)$ simultaneously across the standard twelve leads — six limb leads and six precordial leads — clinicians reconstruct the full three-dimensional trajectory of $\vec{p}(t)$, known as the cardiac vector. The characteristic P, QRS, and T deflections visible on the trace correspond, respectively, to atrial depolarization, ventricular depolarization, and ventricular repolarization.

Worked example. Lead II measures the potential difference between the left leg (LL) and right arm (RA) electrodes, oriented at approximately $60°$ below the horizontal axis. If the cardiac vector at peak ventricular depolarization has magnitude $|\vec{p}| = 2.0 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{C·m}$ and points at $50°$ from the horizontal, the projected component along Lead II is $|\vec{p}|\cos(60° - 50°) \approx 1.97 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{C·m}$, producing the dominant R-wave deflection.

Key theorem. The uniqueness theorem for electrostatic potentials guarantees that a sufficiently dense set of surface measurements can, in principle, uniquely reconstruct the internal source distribution — the theoretical foundation for electrocardiographic inverse problems and modern cardiac imaging.
