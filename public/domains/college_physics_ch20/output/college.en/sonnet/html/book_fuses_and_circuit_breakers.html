🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: every observable charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb (SI unit). Charge comes in two polarities—positive and negative—with like charges repelling and unlike charges attracting. The total electric charge of an isolated system is conserved; this principle, charge conservation, holds in all known physical processes. Formally, for any closed system, $\sum q_i = \text{constant}$.

The force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is given by Coulomb's Law:

$$ \vec{F} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant.

Worked Example. Two point charges, $q_1 = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and $q_2 = -5.0\ \mu\text{C}$, are placed $0.20\ \text{m}$ apart. Find the magnitude of the electrostatic force between them.

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \cdot \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(5.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \cdot \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-11}}{4.0 \times 10^{-2}} = 3.37\ \text{N} $$

The force is attractive because the charges are of opposite sign.

Key Theorem. The superposition principle states that the net force on a charge due to multiple surrounding charges equals the vector sum of the individual pairwise Coulomb forces: $\vec{F}_\text{net} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i$. This linearity is exact within classical electrodynamics and underlies all subsequent analysis of electric fields and potentials.

Then, using an electroscope and a known charge source, estimate the sign of the charge deposited on the rod. Record how the divergence angle of the electroscope leaves changes as the rod approaches, and explain your observations in terms of charge redistribution and Coulomb's Law.

🍃 Electric Current

🍃 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional surface. Formally, if a charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface during a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limiting case:

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of positive current follows the direction of positive charge flow; in metallic conductors, where charge carriers are electrons, conventional current flows opposite to electron drift.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries a steady current of $3.0\,\text{A}$. Determine the total charge that passes through a cross-section of the wire in $4.0\,\text{s}$, and estimate how many electrons this represents.

Using the definition of average current:

$$ \Delta Q = I \cdot \Delta t = (3.0\,\text{A})(4.0\,\text{s}) = 12\,\text{C} $$

Since the elementary charge is $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, the number of electrons is:

$$ N = \frac{\Delta Q}{e} = \frac{12\,\text{C}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}} \approx 7.5 \times 10^{19}\,\text{electrons} $$

This underscores that even modest macroscopic currents involve enormous numbers of charge carriers.

Key Theorem — Current Continuity. In a steady-state circuit with no charge accumulation at any node, Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) holds: the algebraic sum of all currents entering a junction equals the sum of all currents leaving it,

$$ \sum_{k} I_k = 0 $$

This is a direct consequence of charge conservation. For time-varying charge distributions, the continuity equation $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0$ (where $\mathbf{J}$ is the current density and $\rho$ is the volume charge density) provides the general statement.

Lab Exercise. Connect a resistor ($R = 100\,\Omega$) in series with an ammeter and a $9\,\text{V}$ battery. Record the ammeter reading and compare it to the predicted value $I = V/R$. Then insert a second identical resistor in series and repeat. Verify that the current decreases by a factor of two and confirm that charge is conserved at each junction using KCL.

🌱 Potential Difference

🌱 Potential Difference

Definition. The electric potential difference (or voltage) between two points A and B in an electric field is defined as the work done per unit positive charge by an external agent in moving a test charge quasi-statically from A to B against the electric force:

$$ \Delta V = V_B - V_A = -\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{\ell} $$

The SI unit is the volt (V), where $1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C}$. Because the electric field is conservative, this line integral is path-independent — it depends only on the endpoints. Equivalently, the work done by the electric force on a charge $q$ moving from A to B is

$$ W_{A \to B} = q(V_A - V_B) = -q\,\Delta V. $$

A positive charge released from rest accelerates from high potential toward low potential; a negative charge does the opposite.

Worked Example. A uniform electric field $\vec{E} = 500\,\hat{x}\,\text{V/m}$ exists between two parallel plates separated by $d = 0.04\,\text{m}$. Find the potential difference $\Delta V = V_B - V_A$ where A is on the positive plate and B on the negative plate, then find the work done on a proton ($q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$) moving from A to B.

Taking the displacement along $\hat{x}$ from A to B:

$$ \Delta V = -\int_0^d E\,dx = -E\,d = -(500)(0.04) = -20\,\text{V}. $$

The proton loses $20\,\text{V}$ of potential. Work done by the electric force:

$$ W = q(V_A - V_B) = (1.6\times10^{-19})(+20) = 3.2\times10^{-18}\,\text{J}. $$

The proton gains $3.2\,\text{aJ}$ of kinetic energy, consistent with it accelerating toward the negative plate.

Key Theorem (Equipotential Surfaces). Along any surface on which $V$ is constant, $\vec{E} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = 0$, so the electric field is everywhere perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential.

🍃 Electric Power

🍃 Electric Power

Definition. Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted into other forms of energy (thermal, mechanical, radiant, etc.) within a circuit element. For a device carrying current $I$ and maintained across a potential difference $V$, the instantaneous power is

$$ P = IV. $$

This follows directly from the definition of voltage as energy per unit charge and current as charge per unit time: $P = \frac{dU}{dt} = V\frac{dq}{dt} = IV$. Applying Ohm's law ($V = IR$) yields two equivalent forms useful when resistance is constant:

$$ P = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R}. $$

The SI unit of power is the watt ($1\,\text{W} = 1\,\text{J/s}$). Electrical energy consumed over a time interval is $U = Pt$, commonly measured in kilowatt-hours ($1\,\text{kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6\,\text{J}$).

Worked Example. A resistor of $R = 47\,\Omega$ is connected to a $V = 12\,\text{V}$ battery. Find the current, the power dissipated, and the thermal energy released in $t = 5\,\text{min}$.

Step 1 — Current:

$$ I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{12}{47} \approx 0.255\,\text{A}. $$

Step 2 — Power:

$$ P = \frac{V^2}{R} = \frac{(12)^2}{47} \approx 3.06\,\text{W}. $$

Step 3 — Energy:

$$ U = Pt = 3.06\,\text{W} \times 300\,\text{s} \approx 918\,\text{J}. $$

Key Theorem (Power Dissipation in Series and Parallel). For $n$ resistors in series carrying the same current $I$, the total power is $P_\text{total} = I^2 R_\text{total} = \sum_{k=1}^n I^2 R_k = \sum_{k=1}^n P_k$. For resistors in parallel sharing the same voltage $V$, $P_\text{total} = V^2/R_\text{eq} = \sum_{k=1}^n V^2/R_k = \sum_{k=1}^n P_k$. In both topologies, total power equals the sum of individual powers — a direct consequence of energy conservation.

Lab Exercise. Assemble a circuit with a $9\,\text{V}$ battery and three resistors ($100\,\Omega$, $220\,\Omega$, $330\,\Omega$) first in series, then in parallel. For each configuration, measure the terminal voltage and branch (or total) current with a multimeter. Calculate the power delivered to each resistor and the total power drawn from the source. Verify that the sum of individual powers equals the total power, and compare measured values with theoretical predictions. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including internal resistance of the battery.

🌸 Thermal Hazard

🌸 Thermal Hazard

Definition. A thermal hazard arises in an electrical circuit when resistive heating—governed by Joule's law—causes the temperature of a conductor, insulator, or surrounding material to rise to a dangerous level. The power dissipated in a resistor of resistance $R$ carrying current $I$ is

$$ P = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R} = IV, $$

where $V$ is the voltage across the element. When $P$ exceeds the rate at which heat is removed by conduction, convection, or radiation, thermal energy accumulates, potentially igniting insulation, damaging components, or starting fires. Thermal hazards are distinct from shock hazards; they depend primarily on sustained power dissipation rather than instantaneous current through the body.

Worked Example. A household extension cord has a total resistance of $R = 0.50\,\Omega$ and is rated for a maximum current of $I_\text{max} = 15\,\text{A}$. Suppose a user connects appliances drawing $I = 20\,\text{A}$.

$$ P = I^2 R = (20)^2 (0.50) = 400\,\text{W}. $$

At the rated current the cord dissipates only $P_\text{rated} = (15)^2(0.50) = 112.5\,\text{W}$. The excess $400 - 112.5 = 287.5\,\text{W}$ must be absorbed by the cord's plastic insulation. Since the insulation's thermal conductivity is low, its temperature rises rapidly toward the autoignition threshold (~300 °C for common PVC), creating a fire risk even though the circuit breaker—typically set at 20 A—may not trip immediately.

Key Theorem (Joule's Law of Heating). For a conductor obeying Ohm's law, the heat $Q$ generated over time interval $\Delta t$ is

$$ Q = P\,\Delta t = I^2 R\,\Delta t. $$

This linear dependence on $\Delta t$ and quadratic dependence on $I$ implies that small overcurrents sustained over long periods are as hazardous as large short-duration overcurrents—a fact exploited by time-delay fuses, which are calibrated to the $I^2 t$ product rather than instantaneous current alone.

Lab Exercise. Connect a resistor of known resistance ($R \approx 10\,\Omega$, 1 W rating) in series with an ammeter and a variable DC supply. Increase the current in 0.1 A steps from 0 to 0.5 A, recording the voltage, current, and surface temperature (via infrared thermometer) every 30 seconds. Plot $T$ versus $I^2$ and verify linearity. Identify the current at which the measured temperature would extrapolate to the component's maximum rated temperature, and compare this threshold to the manufacturer's power rating.

🌸 Fuses And Circuit Breakers

🌸 Fuses And Circuit Breakers

Definition. Fuses and circuit breakers are overcurrent protection devices inserted in series within an electrical circuit to interrupt current flow when it exceeds a predetermined safe limit. A fuse contains a thin metallic element (commonly tin-lead alloy or copper) that melts when the current through it generates sufficient Joule heating to exceed the element's melting point, permanently breaking the circuit. A circuit breaker achieves the same interruption via an electromechanical mechanism—either a bimetallic strip that bends under thermal expansion or an electromagnetic solenoid that trips a latch—and can be reset after the fault condition is cleared.

Both devices exploit the power dissipation relationship:

$$ P = I^2 R $$

where $P$ is the power dissipated in the protective element, $I$ is the current, and $R$ is the element's resistance. Because $P$ scales with $I^2$, even a moderate overcurrent produces a disproportionately large increase in heating.

Worked Example. A household circuit is rated at $15\,\text{A}$ and protected by a fuse with resistance $R_f = 0.010\,\Omega$. Under normal load at $I = 12\,\text{A}$, the fuse dissipates:

$$ P = (12)^2(0.010) = 1.44\,\text{W} $$

During a fault, the current rises to $I = 25\,\text{A}$:

$$ P = (25)^2(0.010) = 6.25\,\text{W} $$

This more than quadrupled power dissipation rapidly raises the fuse element's temperature beyond its melting point, severing the circuit before wiring insulation or connected appliances can be damaged.

Key Theorem — Maximum Safe Current. For a fuse element modeled as a cylindrical conductor of radius $r$, length $\ell$, resistivity $\rho$, and melting temperature $T_m$, the steady-state blow current satisfies:

$$ I_{\text{blow}} \propto r^{3/2} $$

(derivable by equating Joule heating to radiative and conductive losses). This scaling law explains why thicker fuse wire tolerates higher currents, and guides fuse selection for a given circuit rating.

Payoff 🎯

Fuses and circuit breakers represent the culminating synthesis of electromagnetic theory, thermal physics, and materials science as applied to the practical protection of electrical systems. Having progressed through electric charge, potential, current, resistance, and circuit analysis, the student now encounters the device that transforms these abstractions into a life-safety mechanism. The fuse and circuit breaker are not mere afterthoughts in circuit design — they are the physical embodiment of Ohm's Law and Joule's Law operating under failure conditions.

Conceptual Foundation. When current $I$ flows through a conductor of resistance $R$, power is dissipated at the rate $P = I^2 R$. Under normal operating conditions, this heating is manageable. However, a fault condition — a short circuit, an overloaded branch, or insulation breakdown — can drive $I$ to values orders of magnitude above the design rating. The energy deposited per unit time rises quadratically, and without intervention, insulation ignites and conductors melt. A fuse interrupts this process irreversibly: its low-melting-point alloy element, carrying resistance $R_f$, absorbs the excess energy and opens the circuit when temperature reaches the melting threshold $T_m$. A circuit breaker accomplishes the same protective function by an electromechanical or thermal-magnetic mechanism, but is resettable.

Worked Example. A household branch circuit is rated at $I_{\text{rated}} = 15\ \text{A}$ and protected by a 15 A fuse with element resistance $R_f = 0.005\ \Omega$. Under a fault delivering $I_f = 40\ \text{A}$, the power deposited in the fuse element is:

$$ P_f = I_f^2\, R_f = (40)^2 \times 0.005 = 8.0\ \text{W} $$

This elevated dissipation, concentrated in a small mass of alloy, raises element temperature rapidly to $T_m$ and opens the circuit in milliseconds, preventing wire temperatures from reaching the ignition point of insulation (~300 °C).

Key Theorem. The clearing time $t_c$ of a fuse satisfies the adiabatic integral relation:

$$ \int_0^{t_c} I^2\, dt \leq A_{\text{fuse}} $$

where $A_{\text{fuse}}$ is the rated ampere-squared-second (I²t) clearing capacity of the fuse, a materials-dependent constant specifying the thermal energy budget before the element opens.

Lab Exercise. Using a variable DC power supply, connect a 1 A glass fuse in series with a current-sensing resistor and a decade resistance box. Incrementally reduce the load resistance to drive superthreshold current, recording the time-to-open at three overcurrent levels (150%, 200%, and 300% of rating). Plot $t_c$ versus $I$ on a log-log scale and fit the data to verify the $I^2 t = \text{const}$ relationship predicted by adiabatic theory.

The principles governing fuses and circuit breakers extend directly into power distribution engineering, where arc-flash calculations and coordination studies depend on the same $I^2 t$ integral; into semiconductor protection, where fast-acting fuses guard diodes and thyristors against transient surges; and into building codes, where breaker sizing determines the safety margin of every domestic installation.
