🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: every observable charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb (SI unit). Charge comes in two polarities—positive and negative—with like charges repelling and unlike charges attracting. The total electric charge of an isolated system is conserved; this principle, charge conservation, holds in all known physical processes. Formally, for any closed system, $\sum q_i = \text{constant}$.

The force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is given by Coulomb's Law:

$$ \vec{F} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant.

Worked Example. Two point charges, $q_1 = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and $q_2 = -5.0\ \mu\text{C}$, are placed $0.20\ \text{m}$ apart. Find the magnitude of the electrostatic force between them.

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \cdot \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(5.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \cdot \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-11}}{4.0 \times 10^{-2}} = 3.37\ \text{N} $$

The force is attractive because the charges are of opposite sign.

Key Theorem. The superposition principle states that the net force on a charge due to multiple surrounding charges equals the vector sum of the individual pairwise Coulomb forces: $\vec{F}_\text{net} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i$. This linearity is exact within classical electrodynamics and underlies all subsequent analysis of electric fields and potentials.

Record how the divergence angle of the electroscope leaves changes as the rod approaches, and explain your observations in terms of charge redistribution and Coulomb's Law.

🍃 Electric Current

🍃 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional surface. Formally, if a charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface during a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limiting case:

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of positive current follows the direction of positive charge flow; in metallic conductors, where charge carriers are electrons, conventional current flows opposite to electron drift.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries a steady current of $3.0\,\text{A}$. Determine the total charge that passes through a cross-section of the wire in $4.0\,\text{s}$, and estimate how many electrons this represents.

Using the definition of average current:

$$ \Delta Q = I \cdot \Delta t = (3.0\,\text{A})(4.0\,\text{s}) = 12\,\text{C} $$

Since the elementary charge is $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, the number of electrons is:

$$ N = \frac{\Delta Q}{e} = \frac{12\,\text{C}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}} \approx 7.5 \times 10^{19}\,\text{electrons} $$

This underscores that even modest macroscopic currents involve enormous numbers of charge carriers.

Key Theorem — Current Continuity. In a steady-state circuit with no charge accumulation at any node, Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) holds: the algebraic sum of all currents entering a junction equals the sum of all currents leaving it,

$$ \sum_{k} I_k = 0 $$

This is a direct consequence of charge conservation. For time-varying charge distributions, the continuity equation $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0$ (where $\mathbf{J}$ is the current density and $\rho$ is the volume charge density) provides the general statement.

Lab Exercise. Connect a resistor ($R = 100\,\Omega$) in series with an ammeter and a $9\,\text{V}$ battery. Record the ammeter reading and compare it to the predicted value $I = V/R$. Then insert a second identical resistor in series and repeat. Verify that the current decreases by a factor of two and confirm that charge is conserved at each junction using KCL.

🍃 Alternating Current

🍃 Alternating Current

Definition. An alternating current (AC) is an electric current whose magnitude and direction vary sinusoidally with time. The instantaneous current is described by

$$ i(t) = I_0 \sin(2\pi f t) = I_0 \sin(\omega t), $$

where $I_0$ is the peak (amplitude) current in amperes, $f$ is the frequency in hertz, and $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency in radians per second. The corresponding instantaneous voltage across a purely resistive load is

$$ v(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t), $$

with $V_0 = I_0 R$. Because power depends on $i^2$, the effective, or root-mean-square (rms), values are the quantities of practical importance:

$$ I_\text{rms} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}}, \qquad V_\text{rms} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}}. $$

These rms values are equivalent to the DC values that would dissipate the same average power in a resistor.

Worked Example. A household outlet in North America supplies $V_\text{rms} = 120\ \text{V}$ at $f = 60\ \text{Hz}$. Determine the peak voltage $V_0$, the angular frequency $\omega$, and the peak current through a $240\ \Omega$ resistive heater.

$$ V_0 = V_\text{rms}\sqrt{2} = 120\sqrt{2} \approx 170\ \text{V}. $$ $$ \omega = 2\pi(60) \approx 377\ \text{rad/s}. $$ $$ I_0 = \frac{V_0}{R} = \frac{170}{240} \approx 0.707\ \text{A}, \qquad I_\text{rms} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}} = 0.500\ \text{A}. $$

The average power dissipated is $P = I_\text{rms}^2 R = (0.500)^2(240) = 60\ \text{W}$.

Key Theorem — Average Power in a Resistive Circuit. For a purely resistive circuit driven by AC, the average power over one full cycle is

$$ \langle P \rangle = \frac{1}{2}I_0 V_0 = I_\text{rms} V_\text{rms} = \frac{V_\text{rms}^2}{R}. $$

This result follows directly from $\langle \sin^2(\omega t) \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}$ over a complete period and confirms that rms quantities carry the same power-calculation rules as their DC counterparts.

Lab Exercise. Connect a signal generator set to $f = 1\ \text{kHz}$ and a $1\ \text{k}\Omega$ resistor in series. Using an oscilloscope, measure $V_0$ across the resistor and compute $V_\text{rms}$. Compare the measured rms value to the generator's stated output, and calculate the average power. Repeat at $f = 5\ \text{kHz}$ and verify that frequency does not affect average power in a purely resistive circuit.

🌸 Shock Hazard

🌸 Shock Hazard

Definition. A shock hazard exists whenever a person forms part of a closed circuit through which electric current can flow. The physiological danger depends primarily on the magnitude of the current through the body, not the voltage alone, though voltage drives the current through the body's resistance. The relevant relation is Ohm's law applied to the human body:

$$ I = \frac{V}{R_{\text{body}}} $$

where $R_{\text{body}}$ typically ranges from $10^3\,\Omega$ (wet skin, internal path) to $5 \times 10^5\,\Omega$ (dry, intact skin). Currents as low as $1\,\text{mA}$ produce a perceptible tingle; $10\,\text{mA}$ causes muscle tetany sufficient to prevent releasing a gripped conductor; $100\,\text{mA}$ sustained for one second is lethal due to ventricular fibrillation.

Worked Example. A technician with dry hands (resistance $R = 1.5 \times 10^5\,\Omega$) accidentally contacts both terminals of a $120\,\text{V}$ household circuit. Determine the current through the technician and assess the hazard level.

$$ I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{120\,\text{V}}{1.5 \times 10^5\,\Omega} = 8.0 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{A} = 0.80\,\text{mA} $$

This falls below the $1\,\text{mA}$ threshold for sensation, representing a marginal hazard. However, if the same contact occurs with wet hands ($R \approx 1000\,\Omega$):

$$ I = \frac{120\,\text{V}}{1.0 \times 10^3\,\Omega} = 0.12\,\text{A} = 120\,\text{mA} $$

This exceeds the fibrillation threshold by 20%, constituting a life-threatening hazard under sustained contact.

Key Theorem (Equipotential Safety Principle). No current flows between two points at the same electric potential. A bird perched on a high-voltage line survives because both feet are at essentially the same potential; no current path through the body is established. This principle underlies the design of double-insulated tools and grounded enclosures: proper grounding ensures the accessible surface remains at earth potential ($V = 0$), eliminating the potential difference needed to drive current through a person who simultaneously contacts the device and ground.

Payoff 🎯

A shock hazard exists whenever an energized conductor can complete a circuit through the human body, driving sufficient current through tissue to disrupt normal physiological function. Formally, a shock hazard is present when the potential difference $\Delta V$ between two points accessible to a person, combined with the effective body resistance $R_\text{body}$, produces a current

$$ I = \frac{\Delta V}{R_\text{body}} $$

that meets or exceeds the threshold for physiological harm. Established safety standards place the perception threshold near $1\,\text{mA}$, the "let-go" threshold near $10\,\text{mA}$, and ventricular fibrillation risk above roughly $100\,\text{mA}$ for sustained contact — all values far below the current capacities of ordinary household circuits.

Worked Example. A technician contacts a 120 V (rms) line while standing on a damp concrete floor. Dry skin resistance is typically $10^5\,\Omega$, but moisture reduces it to $\sim 1000\,\Omega$. The resulting current is

$$ I = \frac{120\,\text{V}}{1000\,\Omega} = 120\,\text{mA}, $$

well into the fibrillation range. If the technician wore rubber-soled boots adding $50\,\text{k}\Omega$ in series, the current drops to $I \approx 2.4\,\text{mA}$ — below the physiological danger threshold. This arithmetic, elementary as it appears, underpins every electrical safety standard in use today.

Key Theorem (Ohm's Law as Safety Criterion). For a linear resistive path, the hazard current scales linearly with voltage and inversely with total series resistance. Consequently, protective measures either reduce the driving voltage (isolation transformers, grounding referenced to a common potential) or increase the series resistance (insulation, personal protective equipment, ground-fault circuit interrupters that trip when $|I_\text{hot} - I_\text{return}| > 5\,\text{mA}$).

Shock hazard is the natural capstone of introductory electrostatics and circuit analysis because it synthesizes Coulomb's law, Ohm's law, series resistance, and the physics of dielectric breakdown into a single quantitative criterion with immediate human consequence. Every principle in this volume — charge, field, potential, current, resistance — converges here.
