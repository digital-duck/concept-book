🌱 Electric Charge

🌱 Electric Charge

Definition. Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter that determines how particles interact via the electromagnetic force. Charge is quantized: every observable charge is an integer multiple of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, where C denotes the coulomb (SI unit). Charge comes in two polarities—positive and negative—with like charges repelling and unlike charges attracting. The total electric charge of an isolated system is conserved; this principle, charge conservation, holds in all known physical processes. Formally, for any closed system, $\sum q_i = \text{constant}$.

The force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$ in vacuum is given by Coulomb's Law:

$$ \vec{F} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant.

Worked Example. Two point charges, $q_1 = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ and $q_2 = -5.0\ \mu\text{C}$, are placed $0.20\ \text{m}$ apart. Find the magnitude of the electrostatic force between them.

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \cdot \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(5.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \cdot \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-11}}{4.0 \times 10^{-2}} = 3.37\ \text{N} $$

The force is attractive because the charges are of opposite sign.

Key Theorem. The superposition principle states that the net force on a charge due to multiple surrounding charges equals the vector sum of the individual pairwise Coulomb forces: $\vec{F}_\text{net} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i$. This linearity is exact within classical electrodynamics and underlies all subsequent analysis of electric fields and potentials.

Record how the divergence angle of the electroscope leaves changes as the rod approaches, and explain your observations in terms of charge redistribution and Coulomb's Law.

🍃 Electric Current

🍃 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional surface. Formally, if a charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface during a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limiting case:

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of positive current follows the direction of positive charge flow; in metallic conductors, where charge carriers are electrons, conventional current flows opposite to electron drift.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries a steady current of $3.0\,\text{A}$. Determine the total charge that passes through a cross-section of the wire in $4.0\,\text{s}$, and estimate how many electrons this represents.

Using the definition of average current:

$$ \Delta Q = I \cdot \Delta t = (3.0\,\text{A})(4.0\,\text{s}) = 12\,\text{C} $$

Since the elementary charge is $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, the number of electrons is:

$$ N = \frac{\Delta Q}{e} = \frac{12\,\text{C}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}} \approx 7.5 \times 10^{19}\,\text{electrons} $$

This underscores that even modest macroscopic currents involve enormous numbers of charge carriers.

Key Theorem — Current Continuity. In a steady-state circuit with no charge accumulation at any node, Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) holds: the algebraic sum of all currents entering a junction equals the sum of all currents leaving it,

$$ \sum_{k} I_k = 0 $$

This is a direct consequence of charge conservation. For time-varying charge distributions, the continuity equation $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0$ (where $\mathbf{J}$ is the current density and $\rho$ is the volume charge density) provides the general statement.

Lab Exercise. Connect a resistor ($R = 100\,\Omega$) in series with an ammeter and a $9\,\text{V}$ battery. Record the ammeter reading and compare it to the predicted value $I = V/R$. Then insert a second identical resistor in series and repeat. Verify that the current decreases by a factor of two and confirm that charge is conserved at each junction using KCL.

🌱 Potential Difference

🌱 Potential Difference

Definition. The electric potential difference (or voltage) between two points A and B in an electric field is defined as the work done per unit positive charge by an external agent in moving a test charge quasi-statically from A to B against the electric force:

$$ \Delta V = V_B - V_A = -\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{\ell} $$

The SI unit is the volt (V), where $1\,\text{V} = 1\,\text{J/C}$. Because the electric field is conservative, this line integral is path-independent — it depends only on the endpoints. Equivalently, the work done by the electric force on a charge $q$ moving from A to B is

$$ W_{A \to B} = q(V_A - V_B) = -q\,\Delta V. $$

A positive charge released from rest accelerates from high potential toward low potential; a negative charge does the opposite.

Worked Example. A uniform electric field $\vec{E} = 500\,\hat{x}\,\text{V/m}$ exists between two parallel plates separated by $d = 0.04\,\text{m}$. Find the potential difference $\Delta V = V_B - V_A$ where A is on the positive plate and B on the negative plate, then find the work done on a proton ($q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$) moving from A to B.

Taking the displacement along $\hat{x}$ from A to B:

$$ \Delta V = -\int_0^d E\,dx = -E\,d = -(500)(0.04) = -20\,\text{V}. $$

The proton loses $20\,\text{V}$ of potential. Work done by the electric force:

$$ W = q(V_A - V_B) = (1.6\times10^{-19})(+20) = 3.2\times10^{-18}\,\text{J}. $$

The proton gains $3.2\,\text{aJ}$ of kinetic energy, consistent with it accelerating toward the negative plate.

Key Theorem (Equipotential Surfaces). Along any surface on which $V$ is constant, $\vec{E} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = 0$, so the electric field is everywhere perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential.

🍃 Alternating Current

🍃 Alternating Current

Definition. An alternating current (AC) is an electric current whose magnitude and direction vary sinusoidally with time. The instantaneous current is described by

$$ i(t) = I_0 \sin(2\pi f t) = I_0 \sin(\omega t), $$

where $I_0$ is the peak (amplitude) current in amperes, $f$ is the frequency in hertz, and $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency in radians per second. The corresponding instantaneous voltage across a purely resistive load is

$$ v(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t), $$

with $V_0 = I_0 R$. Because power depends on $i^2$, the effective, or root-mean-square (rms), values are the quantities of practical importance:

$$ I_\text{rms} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}}, \qquad V_\text{rms} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}}. $$

These rms values are equivalent to the DC values that would dissipate the same average power in a resistor.

Worked Example. A household outlet in North America supplies $V_\text{rms} = 120\ \text{V}$ at $f = 60\ \text{Hz}$. Determine the peak voltage $V_0$, the angular frequency $\omega$, and the peak current through a $240\ \Omega$ resistive heater.

$$ V_0 = V_\text{rms}\sqrt{2} = 120\sqrt{2} \approx 170\ \text{V}. $$ $$ \omega = 2\pi(60) \approx 377\ \text{rad/s}. $$ $$ I_0 = \frac{V_0}{R} = \frac{170}{240} \approx 0.707\ \text{A}, \qquad I_\text{rms} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}} = 0.500\ \text{A}. $$

The average power dissipated is $P = I_\text{rms}^2 R = (0.500)^2(240) = 60\ \text{W}$.

Key Theorem — Average Power in a Resistive Circuit. For a purely resistive circuit driven by AC, the average power over one full cycle is

$$ \langle P \rangle = \frac{1}{2}I_0 V_0 = I_\text{rms} V_\text{rms} = \frac{V_\text{rms}^2}{R}. $$

This result follows directly from $\langle \sin^2(\omega t) \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}$ over a complete period and confirms that rms quantities carry the same power-calculation rules as their DC counterparts.

Lab Exercise. Connect a signal generator set to $f = 1\ \text{kHz}$ and a $1\ \text{k}\Omega$ resistor in series. Using an oscilloscope, measure $V_0$ across the resistor and compute $V_\text{rms}$. Compare the measured rms value to the generator's stated output, and calculate the average power. Repeat at $f = 5\ \text{kHz}$ and verify that frequency does not affect average power in a purely resistive circuit.

🍃 Electric Power

🍃 Electric Power

Definition. Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted into other forms of energy (thermal, mechanical, radiant, etc.) within a circuit element. For a device carrying current $I$ and maintained across a potential difference $V$, the instantaneous power is

$$ P = IV. $$

This follows directly from the definition of voltage as energy per unit charge and current as charge per unit time: $P = \frac{dU}{dt} = V\frac{dq}{dt} = IV$. Applying Ohm's law ($V = IR$) yields two equivalent forms useful when resistance is constant:

$$ P = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R}. $$

The SI unit of power is the watt ($1\,\text{W} = 1\,\text{J/s}$). Electrical energy consumed over a time interval is $U = Pt$, commonly measured in kilowatt-hours ($1\,\text{kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6\,\text{J}$).

Worked Example. A resistor of $R = 47\,\Omega$ is connected to a $V = 12\,\text{V}$ battery. Find the current, the power dissipated, and the thermal energy released in $t = 5\,\text{min}$.

Step 1 — Current:

$$ I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{12}{47} \approx 0.255\,\text{A}. $$

Step 2 — Power:

$$ P = \frac{V^2}{R} = \frac{(12)^2}{47} \approx 3.06\,\text{W}. $$

Step 3 — Energy:

$$ U = Pt = 3.06\,\text{W} \times 300\,\text{s} \approx 918\,\text{J}. $$

Key Theorem (Power Dissipation in Series and Parallel). For $n$ resistors in series carrying the same current $I$, the total power is $P_\text{total} = I^2 R_\text{total} = \sum_{k=1}^n I^2 R_k = \sum_{k=1}^n P_k$. For resistors in parallel sharing the same voltage $V$, $P_\text{total} = V^2/R_\text{eq} = \sum_{k=1}^n V^2/R_k = \sum_{k=1}^n P_k$. In both topologies, total power equals the sum of individual powers — a direct consequence of energy conservation.

Lab Exercise. Assemble a circuit with a $9\,\text{V}$ battery and three resistors ($100\,\Omega$, $220\,\Omega$, $330\,\Omega$) first in series, then in parallel. For each configuration, measure the terminal voltage and branch (or total) current with a multimeter. Calculate the power delivered to each resistor and the total power drawn from the source. Verify that the sum of individual powers equals the total power, and compare measured values with theoretical predictions. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including internal resistance of the battery.

🍃 Resistance

🍃 Resistance

Definition. The electrical resistance $R$ of a conductor is the ratio of the potential difference $V$ across it to the current $I$ flowing through it:

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} $$

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm ($\Omega$), where $1\,\Omega = 1\,\text{V/A}$. For ohmic materials — those obeying Ohm's Law — $R$ is constant over a wide range of applied voltages. At the microscopic level, resistance arises from collisions between conduction electrons and the ionic lattice of the material. These collisions impede electron flow, converting kinetic energy into thermal energy. For a uniform conductor of length $L$ and cross-sectional area $A$, resistance is given by:

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} $$

where $\rho$ is the resistivity of the material (in $\Omega\cdot\text{m}$), an intrinsic property that depends on temperature and material composition.

Worked Example. A nichrome heating element is $0.50\,\text{m}$ long with a circular cross-section of diameter $d = 0.80\,\text{mm}$. The resistivity of nichrome at room temperature is $\rho = 1.10 \times 10^{-6}\,\Omega\cdot\text{m}$. Find its resistance.

First, compute the cross-sectional area:

$$ A = \pi\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi\left(4.0 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}\right)^2 = 5.03 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{m}^2 $$

Then apply the resistivity formula:

$$ R = \rho\frac{L}{A} = \left(1.10 \times 10^{-6}\right)\frac{0.50}{5.03 \times 10^{-7}} \approx 1.09\,\Omega $$

If connected to a $120\,\text{V}$ supply, the current drawn would be $I = V/R \approx 110\,\text{A}$, illustrating why heating elements use alloys of higher resistivity.

Key Theorem (Temperature Dependence). For most metals, resistivity increases linearly with temperature near room temperature:

$$ \rho(T) = \rho_0\left[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)\right] $$

where $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistivity and $T_0$ is the reference temperature. Consequently, $R(T) = R_0[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)]$. This relationship underlies resistance thermometry and explains why incandescent filaments draw higher current at startup than at operating temperature.

Lab Exercise. Using a digital multimeter, measure the resistance of three resistors with nominal values $100\,\Omega$, $1\,\text{k}\Omega$, and $10\,\text{k}\Omega$. Then connect each resistor in a simple series circuit with a $9\,\text{V}$ battery, measure the current with an ammeter, and verify that $R = V/I$ agrees with your direct resistance measurement to within experimental uncertainty.

🌸 High Voltage Power Transmission

🌸 High Voltage Power Transmission

Definition. High voltage power transmission is the practice of conveying electrical energy over long distances using alternating current (AC) at voltages significantly elevated above the generation or consumption levels—typically 115 kV to 765 kV in modern grids. The governing principle derives from the relationship between power, voltage, and current. For a transmission line carrying power $P$ at voltage $V$, the line current is

$$ I = \frac{P}{V} $$

(assuming unity power factor). The resistive power loss in a line of resistance $R$ is

$$ P_{\text{loss}} = I^2 R = \frac{P^2 R}{V^2} $$

Doubling the transmission voltage therefore reduces resistive losses by a factor of four, making high-voltage operation essential for economic and efficient energy delivery.

Worked Example. A generating station produces $P = 500\,\text{MW}$ of power. The transmission line has total resistance $R = 10\,\Omega$. Compare losses at $V_1 = 20\,\text{kV}$ versus $V_2 = 500\,\text{kV}$.

At $V_1 = 20\,\text{kV}$:

$$ I_1 = \frac{500 \times 10^6}{20 \times 10^3} = 25{,}000\,\text{A}, \quad P_{\text{loss},1} = (25{,}000)^2 \times 10 = 6{,}250\,\text{MW} $$

This exceeds the generated power—transmission at 20 kV is physically impossible at this scale.

At $V_2 = 500\,\text{kV}$:

$$ I_2 = \frac{500 \times 10^6}{500 \times 10^3} = 1{,}000\,\text{A}, \quad P_{\text{loss},2} = (1{,}000)^2 \times 10 = 10\,\text{MW} $$

This represents only 2% of the transmitted power—an operationally acceptable loss.

Key Theorem (Transformer Voltage-Current Reciprocity). An ideal transformer with turns ratio $n = N_s / N_p$ satisfies

$$ \frac{V_s}{V_p} = n, \qquad \frac{I_s}{I_p} = \frac{1}{n} $$

Conservation of power ($V_p I_p = V_s I_s$) is preserved exactly. Step-up transformers at the generation end raise voltage (and proportionally reduce current), while step-down transformers at the distribution end restore safe consumer voltages. This reciprocity is what makes the $V^{-2}$ loss reduction practically achievable.

Payoff 🎯

High voltage power transmission represents the culmination of electromagnetic theory applied at civilizational scale. Every concept developed across this chapter — Ohm's law, resistive power dissipation, transformer action, and alternating current — converges in a single engineering imperative: deliver electrical energy across vast distances with minimal loss. This is not merely a technological convenience; it is the physical principle that makes modern electrical grids possible.

The governing constraint is Joule's law. Power dissipated in a transmission line of resistance $R$ carrying current $I$ is given by

$$ P_{\text{loss}} = I^2 R. $$

Since the useful power delivered to a load is $P = IV$, the same power can be transmitted at high voltage and proportionally low current. Doubling the transmission voltage while halving the current reduces resistive losses by a factor of four. In practice, generation occurs at voltages on the order of $10^4\ \text{V}$, step-up transformers raise this to $10^5$–$10^6\ \text{V}$ for long-distance transmission, and step-down transformers restore safe distribution voltages near the consumer. An ideal transformer satisfies

$$ \frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p}, \qquad V_p I_p = V_s I_s, $$

so the product $VI$ — and therefore power — is conserved while the ratio $V/I$ is freely adjusted by the turns ratio $N_s/N_p$.

To make this concrete, consider a generating station delivering $P = 500\ \text{MW}$ over a line with total resistance $R = 10\ \Omega$. At a transmission voltage of $V = 500\ \text{kV}$, the line current is $I = P/V = 1000\ \text{A}$, and the dissipated power is $P_{\text{loss}} = (10^3)^2 \times 10 = 10\ \text{MW}$, representing a 2% loss. Had the same power been sent at $10\ \text{kV}$, the current would rise to $50{,}000\ \text{A}$ and the loss would reach $25\ \text{GW}$ — fifty times the available power, an obvious physical impossibility.

A key theorem underlying transformer operation is Faraday's law of induction: $\mathcal{E} = -N\,d\Phi_B/dt$. Without time-varying flux — that is, without alternating current — transformer action ceases entirely. The historical adoption of AC over DC for grid power was therefore not arbitrary; it was dictated by this fundamental law.

Verify the predicted $10\ \text{V}$ output, and then introduce a resistive load on the secondary while monitoring the primary current — observe how it rises in accordance with power conservation.

High voltage transmission unlocks the architecture of the entire electrical grid: renewable energy harvested at remote wind farms and solar arrays can reach urban centers, industrial facilities can draw from distant hydroelectric dams, and intercontinental HVDC links now make continental-scale energy balancing a practical reality. Each of these applications rests on the same transformer equations and Joule dissipation analysis developed here.
