🌸 Thermal Hazard

🌸 Thermal Hazard

Definition. A thermal hazard arises in an electrical circuit when resistive heating—governed by Joule's law—causes the temperature of a conductor, insulator, or surrounding material to rise to a dangerous level. The power dissipated in a resistor of resistance $R$ carrying current $I$ is

$$ P = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R} = IV, $$

where $V$ is the voltage across the element. When $P$ exceeds the rate at which heat is removed by conduction, convection, or radiation, thermal energy accumulates, potentially igniting insulation, damaging components, or starting fires. Thermal hazards are distinct from shock hazards; they depend primarily on sustained power dissipation rather than instantaneous current through the body.

Worked Example. A household extension cord has a total resistance of $R = 0.50\,\Omega$ and is rated for a maximum current of $I_\text{max} = 15\,\text{A}$. Suppose a user connects appliances drawing $I = 20\,\text{A}$.

$$ P = I^2 R = (20)^2 (0.50) = 400\,\text{W}. $$

At the rated current the cord dissipates only $P_\text{rated} = (15)^2(0.50) = 112.5\,\text{W}$. The excess $400 - 112.5 = 287.5\,\text{W}$ must be absorbed by the cord's plastic insulation. Since the insulation's thermal conductivity is low, its temperature rises rapidly toward the autoignition threshold (~300 °C for common PVC), creating a fire risk even though the circuit breaker—typically set at 20 A—may not trip immediately.

Key Theorem (Joule's Law of Heating). For a conductor obeying Ohm's law, the heat $Q$ generated over time interval $\Delta t$ is

$$ Q = P\,\Delta t = I^2 R\,\Delta t. $$

This linear dependence on $\Delta t$ and quadratic dependence on $I$ implies that small overcurrents sustained over long periods are as hazardous as large short-duration overcurrents—a fact exploited by time-delay fuses, which are calibrated to the $I^2 t$ product rather than instantaneous current alone.

Lab Exercise. Connect a resistor of known resistance ($R \approx 10\,\Omega$, 1 W rating) in series with an ammeter and a variable DC supply. Increase the current in 0.1 A steps from 0 to 0.5 A, recording the voltage, current, and surface temperature (via infrared thermometer) every 30 seconds. Plot $T$ versus $I^2$ and verify linearity. Identify the current at which the measured temperature would extrapolate to the component's maximum rated temperature, and compare this threshold to the manufacturer's power rating.
