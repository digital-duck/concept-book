🍃 Resistance

🍃 Resistance

Definition. The electrical resistance $R$ of a conductor is the ratio of the potential difference $V$ across it to the current $I$ flowing through it:

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} $$

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm ($\Omega$), where $1\,\Omega = 1\,\text{V/A}$. For ohmic materials — those obeying Ohm's Law — $R$ is constant over a wide range of applied voltages. At the microscopic level, resistance arises from collisions between conduction electrons and the ionic lattice of the material. These collisions impede electron flow, converting kinetic energy into thermal energy. For a uniform conductor of length $L$ and cross-sectional area $A$, resistance is given by:

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} $$

where $\rho$ is the resistivity of the material (in $\Omega\cdot\text{m}$), an intrinsic property that depends on temperature and material composition.

Worked Example. A nichrome heating element is $0.50\,\text{m}$ long with a circular cross-section of diameter $d = 0.80\,\text{mm}$. The resistivity of nichrome at room temperature is $\rho = 1.10 \times 10^{-6}\,\Omega\cdot\text{m}$. Find its resistance.

First, compute the cross-sectional area:

$$ A = \pi\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi\left(4.0 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}\right)^2 = 5.03 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{m}^2 $$

Then apply the resistivity formula:

$$ R = \rho\frac{L}{A} = \left(1.10 \times 10^{-6}\right)\frac{0.50}{5.03 \times 10^{-7}} \approx 1.09\,\Omega $$

If connected to a $120\,\text{V}$ supply, the current drawn would be $I = V/R \approx 110\,\text{A}$, illustrating why heating elements use alloys of higher resistivity.

Key Theorem (Temperature Dependence). For most metals, resistivity increases linearly with temperature near room temperature:

$$ \rho(T) = \rho_0\left[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)\right] $$

where $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistivity and $T_0$ is the reference temperature. Consequently, $R(T) = R_0[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)]$. This relationship underlies resistance thermometry and explains why incandescent filaments draw higher current at startup than at operating temperature.

Lab Exercise. Using a digital multimeter, measure the resistance of three resistors with nominal values $100\,\Omega$, $1\,\text{k}\Omega$, and $10\,\text{k}\Omega$. Record the actual measured values and compute the percent deviation from the nominal value. Then connect each resistor in a simple series circuit with a $9\,\text{V}$ battery, measure the current with an ammeter, and verify that $R = V/I$ agrees with your direct resistance measurement to within experimental uncertainty.
