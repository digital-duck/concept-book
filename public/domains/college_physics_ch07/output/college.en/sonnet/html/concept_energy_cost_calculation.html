🌸 Energy Cost Calculation

🌸 Energy Cost Calculation

Definition. The energy cost of a mechanical process is the total work done against resistive forces, measured in joules (J) in SI units. For practical applications—particularly those involving electrical energy—cost is often expressed in kilowatt-hours (kWh), where $1\,\text{kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^{6}\,\text{J}$. When a system moves at constant velocity, the work-energy theorem requires the net work to be zero, so every joule of input work goes directly toward overcoming dissipative forces such as friction or gravity. The energy cost of the process therefore equals the work done against those resistive forces over the displacement.

Worked Example. A warehouse forklift lifts a $500\,\text{kg}$ pallet through a height of $h = 4.0\,\text{m}$ at constant velocity, then moves it horizontally $d = 20\,\text{m}$ against a rolling friction force of $f_k = 150\,\text{N}$.

Lifting phase: The work done against gravity is

$$ W_{\text{lift}} = mgh = (500)(9.8)(4.0) = 1.96 \times 10^4\,\text{J} $$

Horizontal phase: The work done against friction is

$$ W_{\text{friction}} = f_k \cdot d = (150)(20) = 3.0 \times 10^3\,\text{J} $$

Total energy cost:

$$ W_{\text{total}} = 1.96 \times 10^4 + 3.0 \times 10^3 = 2.26 \times 10^4\,\text{J} \approx 6.28 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{kWh} $$

Key Theorem (Work-Energy Theorem with Non-Conservative Forces). For a system subject to both conservative forces and non-conservative resistive forces $W_{\text{nc}}$:

$$ W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta KE + \Delta PE $$

At constant velocity, $\Delta KE = 0$, so $W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta PE$ for gravitational processes, or $W_{\text{nc}} = 0$ on a horizontal surface where $\Delta PE = 0$ as well—confirming that all input work equals the frictional energy cost. Non-conservative work exits the mechanical domain as thermal energy and cannot be recovered, making it the irreducible cost of any real process.

Lab Exercise. Using a dynamics cart, a known mass, and a force sensor on a horizontal track, measure the braking force as a function of velocity. Calculate the total energy dissipated as the cart decelerates from $v_i = 1.5\,\text{m/s}$ to rest over a measured distance. Compare the work calculated via $W = f_k \cdot d$ against $\Delta KE$ computed from timing gates, and quantify any discrepancy as a percentage error.
