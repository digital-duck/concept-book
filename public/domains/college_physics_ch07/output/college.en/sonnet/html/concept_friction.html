🌸 Friction

🌸 Friction

Definition. Friction is a contact force that opposes the relative motion (or tendency of motion) between two surfaces in contact. It arises from microscopic interactions—adhesion, surface roughness, and deformation—at the interface. Two regimes are distinguished:

  • Static friction $f_s$ acts when surfaces are stationary relative to each other and satisfies $f_s \leq \mu_s N$, where $\mu_s$ is the coefficient of static friction and $N$ is the normal force.
  • Kinetic friction $f_k$ acts when surfaces slide and is given by $f_k = \mu_k N$, with $\mu_k < \mu_s$ in virtually all practical cases.

Both coefficients are dimensionless and depend on the materials in contact, not on the contact area.

Worked Example. A 10.0 kg wooden crate rests on a concrete floor ($\mu_s = 0.50$, $\mu_k = 0.40$). A horizontal force $F$ is applied. Determine (a) the minimum force required to initiate motion and (b) the friction force once the crate slides.

(a) The normal force equals the weight: $N = mg = (10.0)(9.80) = 98.0\ \text{N}$. Static friction reaches its maximum just before motion begins:

$$ f_{s,\max} = \mu_s N = (0.50)(98.0) = 49.0\ \text{N} $$

A force $F > 49.0\ \text{N}$ is required.

(b) Once sliding occurs, kinetic friction acts:

$$ f_k = \mu_k N = (0.40)(98.0) = 39.2\ \text{N} $$

The net force on the crate is $F - f_k$, which accelerates it according to Newton's second law.

Key Theorem (Amontons–Coulomb Laws). For dry solid surfaces, (1) friction force is proportional to the normal force, (2) friction force is independent of the apparent contact area, and (3) kinetic friction is independent of sliding speed (for moderate velocities). These empirical laws hold well for macroscopic objects but break down at nanoscales or under lubricated conditions.

Lab Exercise. Place a block of known mass on an inclined plane. Gradually increase the angle $\theta$ until the block begins to slide. At the critical angle, static equilibrium fails and $\tan\theta = \mu_s$, yielding $\mu_s$ directly from the geometry. Measure $\theta$ with a protractor and compute $\mu_s = \tan\theta$, then compare with tabulated values for the material pair to assess experimental accuracy.
