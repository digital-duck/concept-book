🍃 Nonconservative Force

🍃 Nonconservative Force

Definition. A force is nonconservative if the work it does on an object moving between two points depends on the path taken, not merely on the endpoints. Equivalently, a force $\mathbf{F}$ is nonconservative if its work around any closed path is nonzero:

$$ \oint \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{s} \neq 0. $$

This distinguishes nonconservative forces from conservative ones (e.g., gravity, ideal springs), for which a potential energy function $U$ exists such that $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla U$. No such scalar potential can be defined for a nonconservative force. Friction and air drag are the canonical examples: they always oppose relative motion, dissipating mechanical energy as thermal energy regardless of direction of travel, so traversing a loop always removes net energy from the mechanical system.

Worked Example. A 2.0 kg block slides along a horizontal surface from point A to point B, a distance of 4.0 m, then returns from B to A along the same path. The coefficient of kinetic friction is $\mu_k = 0.30$, and $g = 9.8\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$. Find the total work done by friction over the round trip.

The friction force has magnitude $f_k = \mu_k m g = (0.30)(2.0)(9.8) = 5.88\ \mathrm{N}$. Because friction always opposes displacement, the work done by friction on each leg is negative:

$$ W_{A \to B} = -f_k d = -(5.88)(4.0) = -23.5\ \mathrm{J}, $$ $$ W_{B \to A} = -f_k d = -23.5\ \mathrm{J}. $$

Total work: $W_{\text{total}} = -47.1\ \mathrm{J} \neq 0$. A conservative force would yield zero net work over any closed path; friction emphatically does not.

Key Theorem — Work–Energy Theorem with Nonconservative Forces. The generalized energy equation states:

$$ W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta K + \Delta U = \Delta E_{\text{mech}}, $$

where $W_{\text{nc}}$ is the total work done by all nonconservative forces. When friction acts, $W_{\text{nc}} < 0$ and mechanical energy is not conserved; it converts to internal (thermal) energy $\Delta E_{\text{th}} = -W_{\text{nc}}$, keeping total energy conserved.

Lab Exercise. Release a block from rest at the top of an inclined air track with the air supply off (friction present), measuring its speed at the bottom with a photogate. Repeat with the air supply on (frictionless). Compare $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$ in both cases to $mgh$, and quantify $W_{\text{nc}}$ as the deficit. Verify that $W_{\text{nc}}$ scales linearly with track length by repeating at two different release heights.
