🌱 Velocity

🌱 Velocity

Definition. Velocity is the rate of change of displacement with respect to time. Unlike speed, which is a scalar quantity, velocity is a vector quantity possessing both magnitude and direction. The average velocity of a particle over a time interval $[t_1, t_2]$ is defined as

$$ \bar{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\Delta \mathbf{x}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\mathbf{x}(t_2) - \mathbf{x}(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1}, $$

where $\Delta \mathbf{x}$ is the displacement vector. The instantaneous velocity at time $t$ is the limit of this ratio as the interval shrinks to zero:

$$ \mathbf{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \mathbf{x}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}. $$

The magnitude $|\mathbf{v}(t)|$ is the instantaneous speed, measured in SI units of meters per second ($\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$).

Worked Example. A particle moves along the $x$-axis according to the position function $x(t) = 3t^2 - 6t + 1$ (in meters, with $t$ in seconds). Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 2\,\mathrm{s}$ and determine when the particle is momentarily at rest.

Differentiating with respect to time:

$$ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 6t - 6. $$

At $t = 2\,\mathrm{s}$:

$$ v(2) = 6(2) - 6 = 6\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}. $$

The particle is at rest when $v(t) = 0$, which gives $6t - 6 = 0$, so $t = 1\,\mathrm{s}$. Note that at $t = 1\,\mathrm{s}$ the velocity changes sign, indicating a reversal in direction.

Key Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus applied to kinematics). If the instantaneous velocity $\mathbf{v}(t)$ is known over an interval $[t_0, t]$, the displacement is recovered by integration:

$$ \Delta \mathbf{x} = \int_{t_0}^{t} \mathbf{v}(t')\, dt'. $$

This relation establishes that displacement is the area under the velocity-time graph, forming the kinematic bridge between velocity and position.

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor and a cart on a frictionless track, record position data $x(t)$ at 50 ms intervals over a 3-second run. Compute the average velocity over successive intervals to construct a numerical $v(t)$ profile. Compare this with the instantaneous velocity obtained by fitting a polynomial to the position data and differentiating analytically. Quantify the discrepancy as a function of interval width $\Delta t$ and explain the limiting behavior as $\Delta t \to 0$.
