🌱 Displacement

🌱 Displacement

Definition. Displacement is a vector quantity defined as the change in position of an object. If a particle moves from an initial position $\vec{r}_i$ to a final position $\vec{r}_f$, its displacement is

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i. $$

In one-dimensional motion along the $x$-axis this reduces to $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$. Crucially, displacement depends only on the initial and final positions, not on the path taken between them; this distinguishes it from the scalar quantity distance, which accumulates the total length of the path traversed.

Worked Example. A student walks $8\,\text{m}$ due east, then $6\,\text{m}$ due north. Find the displacement magnitude and direction.

Setting up a coordinate system with east as $+x$ and north as $+y$, the position vector after the journey is

$$ \vec{r}_f = (8\,\hat{i} + 6\,\hat{j})\,\text{m}. $$

Starting from the origin $\vec{r}_i = \vec{0}$, the displacement is $\Delta\vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i = (8\,\hat{i} + 6\,\hat{j})\,\text{m}$. Its magnitude is

$$ |\Delta\vec{r}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{100} = 10\,\text{m}, $$

and the direction is

$$ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{6}{8}\right) \approx 36.9°\text{ north of east.} $$

The total distance walked is $8 + 6 = 14\,\text{m}$, confirming that displacement and distance differ whenever the path is not straight.

Key Theorem. For motion in multiple segments, displacement is additive as a vector sum: $\Delta\vec{r}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{k} \Delta\vec{r}_k$. This follows directly from telescoping cancellation of intermediate positions,

$$ \Delta\vec{r}_{\text{total}} = (\vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i) = \sum_{k}(\vec{r}_{k} - \vec{r}_{k-1}), $$

and underpins the superposition principle used throughout kinematics and dynamics.

🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. In classical mechanics, a force is any interaction that, when unopposed, alters the state of motion of an object. Formally, Newton's Second Law defines net force as

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a}, $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass of the object (in kilograms) and $\vec{a}$ is its acceleration (in $\text{m/s}^2$). Force is a vector quantity measured in newtons ($1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$), possessing both magnitude and direction. When multiple forces act on a body, the principle of superposition applies: the net force equals the vector sum $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_i \vec{F}_i$.

Worked Example. A crate of mass $m = 20\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 50\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -20\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$. Find the acceleration.

Solution. Applying the superposition principle:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{F}_1 + \vec{F}_2 = (50 - 20)\,\hat{x}\,\text{N} = 30\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}. $$

From Newton's Second Law:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{30\,\hat{x}}{20} = 1.5\,\hat{x}\,\text{m/s}^2. $$

The crate accelerates at $1.5\,\text{m/s}^2$ in the positive $x$-direction.

Key Theorem — Newton's Third Law. For every force exerted by object A on object B, there exists a reaction force of equal magnitude and opposite direction exerted by B on A:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A}. $$

These action–reaction pairs act on different bodies and therefore do not cancel when analyzing the motion of a single object. This theorem is foundational to understanding contact forces, tension, and normal forces in multi-body systems.

🍃 Work

🍃 Work

Definition. In classical mechanics, work is the scalar measure of energy transfer that occurs when a force displaces an object. For a constant force $\vec{F}$ acting over a displacement $\vec{d}$, work is defined as the dot product

$$ W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d} = Fd\cos\theta, $$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector, $F = |\vec{F}|$, and $d = |\vec{d}|$. The SI unit of work is the joule (J), with $1\,\text{J} = 1\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$. Work is positive when the force has a component parallel to displacement, negative when antiparallel, and zero when $\theta = 90°$.

Worked Example. A student pushes a 15 kg crate across a horizontal floor through a displacement of 4.0 m, applying a force of 80 N at an angle of $30°$ below the horizontal. Calculate the work done by the applied force.

Solution: The component of the applied force along the displacement is $F\cos\theta = 80\cos 30° = 80 \times 0.866 = 69.3\,\text{N}$. Therefore,

$$ W = Fd\cos\theta = (80)(4.0)\cos 30° = 320 \times 0.866 \approx 277\,\text{J}. $$

The normal force and gravity do zero net work here since they are perpendicular to the horizontal displacement.

Key Theorem — Work–Energy Theorem. The net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2. $$

This theorem follows directly from Newton's second law combined with kinematics. It provides a powerful scalar alternative to vector force analysis: rather than tracking forces at every instant, one needs only the initial and final speeds to relate net work to the resulting change in motion.

🍃 Power

🍃 Power

Definition. Power is the rate at which work is done, or equivalently, the rate at which energy is transferred. If an agent performs work $W$ over a time interval $\Delta t$, the average power is

$$ \bar{P} = \frac{W}{\Delta t}. $$

The instantaneous power is the limiting case as $\Delta t \to 0$:

$$ P = \frac{dW}{dt}. $$

Since $dW = \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r}$, this becomes

$$ P = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v}, $$

where $\vec{v}$ is the instantaneous velocity of the point of application of force $\vec{F}$. The SI unit of power is the watt (W), where $1\,\text{W} = 1\,\text{J/s}$.

Worked Example. A motor lifts a crate of mass $m = 200\,\text{kg}$ vertically at constant speed $v = 0.50\,\text{m/s}$. Determine the power output of the motor.

Because the crate moves at constant speed, the net force is zero, so the motor's upward force equals the gravitational force:

$$ F = mg = (200\,\text{kg})(9.8\,\text{m/s}^2) = 1960\,\text{N}. $$

Since $\vec{F}$ and $\vec{v}$ are both directed upward, $\vec{F} \cdot \vec{v} = Fv$, giving

$$ P = Fv = (1960\,\text{N})(0.50\,\text{m/s}) = 980\,\text{W} \approx 1.0\,\text{kW}. $$

Key Theorem (Instantaneous Power and Kinetic Energy). For a particle acted on by a net force, the instantaneous power delivered by that net force equals the time rate of change of the particle's kinetic energy:

$$ P_{\text{net}} = \frac{dK}{dt}. $$

Proof sketch. From Newton's second law, $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\,d\vec{v}/dt$. Then

$$ P_{\text{net}} = \vec{F}_{\text{net}} \cdot \vec{v} = m\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} \cdot \vec{v} = m\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}v^2\right) = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\right) = \frac{dK}{dt}. \qquad \square $$

This result confirms that power, as defined above, is the mechanism by which a force changes the kinetic energy of a particle.

🌸 Metabolic Rate

🌸 Metabolic Rate

Definition. The metabolic rate is the rate at which an organism converts stored chemical energy into work and heat. It is formally defined as the power output of the body:

$$ P_{\text{met}} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} $$

where $\Delta E$ is the total energy consumed (from food oxidation) over time interval $\Delta t$. The SI unit is the watt (W), though kilocalories per hour (kcal/h) and METs (metabolic equivalents) appear frequently in physiology. By the first law of thermodynamics, this energy is partitioned into useful mechanical work $W$ and heat $Q$ dissipated to the environment:

$$ \Delta E = W + Q $$

At rest, virtually all metabolic output is heat; during vigorous exercise, mechanical efficiency $\eta = W/\Delta E$ reaches roughly 20–25% for human skeletal muscle, with the remainder still dissipated thermally.

Worked Example. A 70 kg cyclist sustains a power output of 200 W at the pedals for 1.00 h. Laboratory calorimetry shows the cyclist's total metabolic energy expenditure is 3600 kJ during this interval. Determine the mechanical efficiency and the rate of heat dissipation.

Mechanical work delivered:

$$ W = P_{\text{mech}} \cdot t = 200\ \text{W} \times 3600\ \text{s} = 720\ \text{kJ} $$

Efficiency:

$$ \eta = \frac{W}{\Delta E} = \frac{720\ \text{kJ}}{3600\ \text{kJ}} = 0.200 \quad (20.0\%) $$

Heat dissipation rate:

$$ P_Q = \frac{Q}{t} = \frac{3600\ \text{kJ} - 720\ \text{kJ}}{3600\ \text{s}} = \frac{2880\ \text{kJ}}{3600\ \text{s}} = 800\ \text{W} $$

The body must shed 800 W through conduction, convection, radiation, and evaporation to maintain thermal homeostasis.

Key Theorem (Kleiber's Law). Empirically, basal metabolic rate $P_0$ scales with body mass $m$ as:

$$ P_0 \approx 70 \cdot m^{3/4} \quad [\text{kcal/day},\ m\ \text{in kg}] $$

This three-quarter-power scaling, rather than the geometrically expected $m^{2/3}$ surface-area scaling, reflects the fractal architecture of nutrient-delivery networks and is a central result connecting physics and biology.

🌸 Basal Metabolic Rate

🌸 Basal Metabolic Rate

Definition. The basal metabolic rate (BMR) is the minimum power required to sustain the vital functions of an organism at complete rest in a thermoneutral environment, following a period of fasting. It represents the baseline rate of energy expenditure needed to maintain homeostasis — driving respiration, circulation, neurological activity, and cellular repair. BMR is expressed as power (watts) or as energy per unit time (kcal/day), and is formally defined by

$$ \text{BMR} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t}\Bigg|_{\text{rest, fasting}} $$

where $\Delta E$ is the metabolic energy expended over the time interval $\Delta t$. Because metabolism involves chemical oxidation of nutrients, BMR can be measured either by direct calorimetry (heat released to surroundings) or indirect calorimetry (oxygen consumption rate, using the approximation that consuming $1\,\text{L}$ of O$_2$ releases approximately $20\,\text{kJ}$ of metabolic energy).

Worked Example. A person at rest consumes oxygen at a rate of $0.24\,\text{L/min}$. Estimate their BMR in watts.

Convert the oxygen consumption rate to an energy rate:

$$ \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = 0.24\,\frac{\text{L}}{\text{min}} \times 20{,}000\,\frac{\text{J}}{\text{L}} = 4{,}800\,\frac{\text{J}}{\text{min}} $$

Converting to SI units:

$$ \text{BMR} = \frac{4{,}800\,\text{J}}{60\,\text{s}} = 80\,\text{W} $$

This result is consistent with the commonly cited value of roughly $80\,\text{W}$ for an average adult at rest — a figure comparable to a standard incandescent light bulb.

Key Theorem (Kleiber's Law). Across species, the BMR scales with body mass $M$ according to the empirical power law

$$ \text{BMR} \propto M^{3/4} $$

This relationship, known as Kleiber's Law, holds over many orders of magnitude in body mass (from mice to elephants) and reflects fundamental constraints in nutrient and oxygen transport through fractal vascular networks. It implies that larger organisms are metabolically more efficient per unit mass than smaller ones.

Payoff 🎯

Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) represents the minimum power output required to sustain the vital functions of a living organism at rest — breathing, circulation, thermoregulation, and cellular maintenance — in a thermally neutral environment following a period of fasting. Formally, BMR is expressed as a rate of energy expenditure per unit time:

$$ \text{BMR} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} \quad [\text{W}] $$

where $\Delta E$ is the metabolic energy consumed over interval $\Delta t$. For an average adult human, BMR falls in the range of 60–100 W, a value that places biology squarely within the domain of thermodynamics and energy conservation studied throughout this chapter.

BMR is the natural capstone of Chapter 7 precisely because it demands synthesis. Calculating it requires applying the work-energy theorem to biological systems, recognizing heat as a form of energy transfer, and invoking the first law of thermodynamics in the form $\Delta U = Q - W$. A living body at rest performs no mechanical work on the environment ($W \approx 0$), so all metabolic energy appears as heat released to the surroundings — a direct, measurable consequence of energy conservation.

Worked Example. A person consumes 2000 kcal per day. Converting: $2000 \times 10^3 \, \text{cal} \times 4.184 \, \text{J/cal} = 8.37 \times 10^6 \, \text{J}$. Dividing by the number of seconds in a day gives an average metabolic power of approximately $\frac{8.37 \times 10^6}{86400} \approx 97 \, \text{W}$. This figure, close to the power of a standard incandescent bulb, illuminates why the human body warms a room.

Key Theorem. By the first law, any deviation of metabolic input from BMR must appear as stored chemical energy (mass gain) or as mechanical work performed on the environment — there is no thermodynamic escape from this accounting.
