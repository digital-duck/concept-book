🍃 Numerical Aperture

🍃 Numerical Aperture

Definition. The numerical aperture (NA) of an optical system—such as a microscope objective or optical fiber—quantifies its ability to gather light and resolve fine detail. For an objective lens in air, the numerical aperture is defined as

$$ \text{NA} = n \sin\theta $$

where $n$ is the refractive index of the medium between the lens and the specimen, and $\theta$ is the half-angle of the maximum cone of light that can enter or exit the lens. In air ($n = 1.00$), the theoretical maximum is $\text{NA} < 1$; immersion objectives use oil ($n \approx 1.515$) to raise the NA above unity.

The resolving power of a microscope is directly governed by the NA. The minimum resolvable separation between two point sources is given by the Rayleigh criterion adapted for microscopy:

$$ d_{\min} = \frac{0.61\,\lambda}{\text{NA}} $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength of illuminating light. A larger NA yields a smaller $d_{\min}$, hence finer spatial resolution.

Worked Example. A dry objective ($n = 1.00$) has a half-angle $\theta = 30°$ and is used with green light ($\lambda = 550\,\text{nm}$). Compute the NA and the minimum resolvable separation.

$$ \text{NA} = 1.00 \times \sin 30° = 1.00 \times 0.500 = 0.500 $$ $$ d_{\min} = \frac{0.61 \times 550\,\text{nm}}{0.500} = \frac{335.5\,\text{nm}}{0.500} = 671\,\text{nm} $$

Features separated by less than $671\,\text{nm}$ cannot be resolved with this objective under green illumination.

Key Theorem (Abbe Diffraction Limit). Ernst Abbe showed that the resolution of any far-field optical microscope is fundamentally bounded by diffraction. No lens design or aberration correction can circumvent the limit $d_{\min} = 0.61\lambda/\text{NA}$; improvement requires either shorter wavelength or higher NA—the motivation behind oil-immersion objectives and electron microscopy.

Lab Exercise. Using a calibrated stage micrometer and three objectives (NA = 0.25, 0.65, 1.25 oil), illuminate the slide with a $\lambda = 480\,\text{nm}$ source. For each objective, predict $d_{\min}$ from the formula, then determine experimentally the smallest grating spacing you can resolve. Tabulate predicted versus observed values and discuss any systematic discrepancies, considering the role of condenser NA in the effective system aperture.
