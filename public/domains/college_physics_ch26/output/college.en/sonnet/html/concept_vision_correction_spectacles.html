🌸 Vision Correction Spectacles

🌸 Vision Correction Spectacles

Definition. The human eye forms images by refracting light through the cornea and a flexible crystalline lens, focusing rays onto the retina at a distance of approximately 2.0 cm behind the lens. A relaxed emmetropic (normal) eye brings parallel rays (from infinity) to focus exactly on the retina. Refractive errors arise when this conjugate relationship fails. In myopia (nearsightedness), the far point $F_p$ is at a finite distance, so parallel rays converge in front of the retina; in hyperopia (farsightedness), the far point is virtual — the eye cannot bring even nearby objects into focus. Corrective spectacle lenses restore the proper conjugate by shifting the effective object distance. The required lens power is

$$ P = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}, $$

where distances are measured in metres and power $P$ is expressed in dioptres (D).

Worked Example. A myopic patient has a far point at $F_p = 0.50\ \text{m}$ from the eye. Spectacles are worn approximately 0.015 m in front of the eye, so the relevant far point distance from the lens is $d_i = -(0.50 - 0.015)\ \text{m} = -0.485\ \text{m}$ (negative because the image must be virtual and on the same side as the incoming light). For a distant object, $d_o \to \infty$, giving

$$ P = \frac{1}{\infty} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{-0.485\ \text{m}} \approx -2.06\ \text{D}. $$

The prescription is therefore approximately $-2.00\ \text{D}$, a diverging lens, consistent with the negative power required for myopia correction.

Key Theorem (Lens-Maker's Equation for Thin Lenses). For a thin lens of refractive index $n$ with radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$,

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n-1)\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right). $$

Combined with the thin-lens equation, this establishes that the corrective power depends entirely on lens geometry and material, independent of the patient's visual system beyond specifying the required focal condition.

Lab Exercise. Using an optical bench, a white-light source, and a 50 cm screen representing the retina plane, measure the image distance produced by a +2.00 D and a $-2.00$ D lens for object distances of 25 cm, 50 cm, and infinity. Record whether each image falls on, in front of, or behind the screen, and identify which configuration models myopia and which models hyperopia correction. Calculate the theoretical image distances using the thin-lens equation and compute the percent discrepancy from your measured values.
