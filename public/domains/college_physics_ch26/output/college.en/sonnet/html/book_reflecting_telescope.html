🌱 Convex Lens

🌱 Convex Lens

Definition. A convex lens (also called a converging lens) is a transparent optical element bounded by two surfaces, at least one of which curves outward, such that the lens is thicker at its optical axis than at its edges. When paraxial rays traveling parallel to the principal axis pass through a convex lens, they refract and converge at the focal point $F$ on the far side, at a focal length $f > 0$ from the optical center. The behavior of a convex lens is governed by the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} $$

where $d_o$ is the object distance and $d_i$ is the image distance, both measured from the lens center. The lateral magnification follows as:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} $$

A negative magnification indicates an inverted image; $|m| > 1$ indicates enlargement, $|m| < 1$ reduction.

Worked Example. A candle is placed $30\,\text{cm}$ in front of a convex lens with focal length $f = 10\,\text{cm}$. Find the image distance and magnification.

Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{10} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3-1}{30} = \frac{2}{30} $$ $$ d_i = 15\,\text{cm} $$

The image forms $15\,\text{cm}$ beyond the lens on the transmission side — a real, inverted image. The magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{15}{30} = -0.5 $$

The image is inverted and half the size of the object. Because $d_o > f$, a real image always forms on the opposite side of the lens from the object, a result consistent with the sign convention embedded in the thin-lens equation.

Key Theorem (Lensmaker's Equation). The focal length of a thin lens with radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$ and refractive index $n$ satisfies:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n-1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) $$

This establishes that focal length depends solely on the lens geometry and material, independent of object position — a result central to optical instrument design.

Lab Exercise. Using a convex lens of unknown focal length, place a bright object at several measured distances $d_o > f$ and record the corresponding image distances $d_i$ on a screen. Plot $1/d_i$ versus $1/d_o$ and determine $f$ from the intercepts. Verify that your experimental value matches the manufacturer specification, and calculate the percent error.

🍃 Aberration

🍃 Aberration

Definition. Optical aberration refers to any deviation from ideal image formation in which rays from a single object point fail to converge to a single, sharp image point after passing through an optical system. In geometric optics, a perfect (stigmatic) lens maps every object point to exactly one image point; aberrations represent departures from this ideal. Aberrations arise from two principal sources: monochromatic aberrations, caused by the geometry of the lens surfaces and described by Seidel's five primary (third-order) aberrations, and chromatic aberration, caused by the wavelength dependence of the index of refraction (dispersion).

Chromatic aberration is particularly significant in introductory contexts. Because the index of refraction $n$ of glass varies with wavelength $\lambda$ (typically $n$ increases as $\lambda$ decreases), the focal length $f$ of a thin lens also varies with wavelength via the lensmaker's equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) $$

Violet light ($\lambda \approx 400\ \text{nm}$) is focused more strongly than red light ($\lambda \approx 700\ \text{nm}$), producing a spread of focal points along the optical axis known as longitudinal chromatic aberration.

Worked Example. A thin converging lens made of crown glass has $n_{\text{red}} = 1.512$ and $n_{\text{violet}} = 1.530$ for red and violet light, respectively, with radii of curvature $R_1 = +20.0\ \text{cm}$ and $R_2 = -20.0\ \text{cm}$. The focal lengths are:

$$ f_{\text{red}} = \left[(1.512 - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{0.200} - \frac{1}{-0.200}\right)\right]^{-1} = \frac{1}{(0.512)(10.0\ \text{m}^{-1})} \approx 19.5\ \text{cm} $$ $$ f_{\text{violet}} \approx \frac{1}{(0.530)(10.0)} \approx 18.9\ \text{cm} $$

The longitudinal chromatic aberration is $\Delta f = f_{\text{red}} - f_{\text{violet}} \approx 0.6\ \text{cm}$.

Key Theorem (Achromatic Doublet). A cemented doublet consisting of a converging lens of focal length $f_1$ (crown glass, dispersive power $\omega_1$) and a diverging lens of focal length $f_2$ (flint glass, dispersive power $\omega_2$) eliminates first-order chromatic aberration when:

$$ \frac{\omega_1}{f_1} + \frac{\omega_2}{f_2} = 0 $$

where $\omega = (n_F - n_C)/(n_D - 1)$ is the Abbe dispersive power.

Compute the measured $\Delta f$ and compare it to the theoretical prediction using the lensmaker's equation and published dispersion data for the lens material.

🌱 Focal Length

🌱 Focal Length

Definition. The focal length of a thin lens (or spherical mirror) is the distance $f$ from the optical center to the focal point $F$ — the point at which rays initially parallel to the principal axis converge (converging lens/concave mirror) or appear to diverge from (diverging lens/convex mirror). For a thin lens in air, the focal length is related to the radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$ of its two surfaces by the lensmaker's equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right), $$

where $n$ is the index of refraction of the lens material. By convention, a radius is positive when the center of curvature lies to the right of the surface. A positive $f$ denotes a converging lens; a negative $f$ denotes a diverging lens.

Worked Example. A biconvex glass lens ($n = 1.52$) has equal radii of curvature $|R| = 20.0\ \text{cm}$. With light traveling left to right, $R_1 = +20.0\ \text{cm}$ and $R_2 = -20.0\ \text{cm}$. Substituting:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (1.52 - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{20.0} - \frac{1}{-20.0}\right) = 0.52 \times \frac{2}{20.0} = 0.052\ \text{cm}^{-1}. $$

Therefore $f = 1/0.052 \approx 19.2\ \text{cm}$. The positive value confirms the lens is converging.

Key Theorem (Thin-Lens Equation). Once $f$ is known, the image distance $d_i$ produced by an object at distance $d_o$ satisfies:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}. $$

This relation, derivable from similar-triangle geometry applied to ray diagrams, is the central predictive tool of geometric optics. The lateral magnification follows as $m = -d_i/d_o$. For $|m| > 1$ the image is enlarged; a negative $m$ indicates an inverted image.

Lab Exercise. Mount a converging lens on an optical bench. Place a bright object at five different distances $d_o$ ranging from $1.5f$ to $4f$. For each position, locate the sharpest image on a screen and record $d_i$. Plot $1/d_o$ versus $1/d_i$; fit a line of slope $-1$ and extract $f$ from the intercept. Compare the measured focal length with the manufacturer's value and compute the percent error, discussing sources of systematic uncertainty such as lens thickness and chromatic aberration.

🌱 Refractive Index

🌱 Refractive Index

Definition. The refractive index (or index of refraction) of a medium is a dimensionless quantity $n$ defined as the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum $c$ to the phase speed of light $v$ in that medium:

$$ n = \frac{c}{v} $$

Since light travels slower in any material than in vacuum, $n \geq 1$ for all physical media ($n = 1$ exactly for vacuum, and approximately $1.0003$ for air at standard conditions). The refractive index governs how much a light ray bends at the interface between two media, as quantified by Snell's Law:

$$ n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2 $$

where $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are the angles of incidence and refraction measured from the normal to the interface.

Worked Example. A ray of light passes from water ($n_1 = 1.333$) into glass ($n_2 = 1.500$) at an angle of incidence $\theta_1 = 45.0°$. Find the angle of refraction.

Applying Snell's Law:

$$ \sin\theta_2 = \frac{n_1}{n_2}\sin\theta_1 = \frac{1.333}{1.500}\sin 45.0° = (0.8887)(0.7071) = 0.6284 $$ $$ \theta_2 = \arcsin(0.6284) \approx 38.9° $$

The ray bends toward the normal because it enters a denser optical medium ($n_2 > n_1$), consistent with the general rule that light refracts toward the normal when transitioning from a lower to a higher refractive index.

Key Theorem — Critical Angle. When light travels from a medium of higher refractive index $n_1$ into one of lower index $n_2 < n_1$, total internal reflection occurs at and beyond the critical angle $\theta_c$:

$$ \sin\theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1} $$

At $\theta_1 = \theta_c$, the refracted ray emerges parallel to the interface ($\theta_2 = 90°$); for $\theta_1 > \theta_c$, no transmitted ray exists. This principle underlies optical fiber technology, where light is confined within a high-index core surrounded by a lower-index cladding.

Lab Exercise. Using a semicircular acrylic block ($n \approx 1.49$) and a laser pointer on a ray-optics bench, measure the transmitted angle for five incident angles between $10°$ and $60°$. Plot $\sin\theta_2$ versus $\sin\theta_1$; the slope should equal $n_{\text{air}}/n_{\text{acrylic}}$. Determine the critical angle experimentally and compare it to the theoretical value $\theta_c = \arcsin(1/1.49) \approx 42.2°$.

🌱 Wavelength

🌱 Wavelength

Definition. The wavelength of a periodic wave is the spatial distance over which the wave's shape repeats exactly once. For a sinusoidal wave propagating in the positive $x$-direction, the wave function is

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\,x - \frac{2\pi}{T}\,t + \phi_0\right), $$

where $A$ is the amplitude, $T$ is the period, $\phi_0$ is the initial phase, and $\lambda$ is the wavelength measured in metres. Equivalently, $\lambda$ is the distance between any two nearest points that are in phase — for instance, crest to crest or trough to trough. The wavelength is related to the wave speed $v$ and frequency $f$ by the fundamental dispersion relation

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Worked Example. Yellow sodium light has a frequency of $f = 5.09 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}$ and travels through air at $v \approx c = 3.00 \times 10^{8}\ \text{m/s}$. Find its wavelength.

Applying the dispersion relation:

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^{8}\ \text{m/s}}{5.09 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}} = 5.89 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m} = 589\ \text{nm}. $$

This result places sodium light squarely in the yellow-orange portion of the visible spectrum ($\approx 570$–$590\ \text{nm}$), consistent with observation.

Key Theorem (Snell's Law and Wavelength Change). When light crosses an interface from a medium of refractive index $n_1$ into one of index $n_2$, the frequency remains constant while the wavelength changes:

$$ \lambda_n = \frac{\lambda_0}{n}, $$

where $\lambda_0$ is the free-space wavelength and $n$ is the refractive index of the medium. The wave speed in the medium becomes $v = c/n$, so $v = f\lambda_n$ is preserved. This theorem underpins the analysis of lenses, thin-film interference, and optical fiber design.

🍃 Chromatic Aberration

🍃 Chromatic Aberration

Definition. Chromatic aberration is an optical defect in which a lens focuses different wavelengths of light at different axial positions, producing colored fringes around imaged objects. Because the index of refraction $n$ of any dispersive medium depends on wavelength $\lambda$ (a relationship described by the dispersion relation), the focal length

$$ f = \left[(n-1)\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right)\right]^{-1} $$

is itself a function of $\lambda$. Shorter wavelengths (violet, $\lambda \approx 400\,\text{nm}$) are refracted more strongly than longer wavelengths (red, $\lambda \approx 700\,\text{nm}$), so violet light converges closer to the lens than red light. The longitudinal separation between these focal points is called longitudinal chromatic aberration, while the difference in image height at a fixed image plane is transverse chromatic aberration.

Worked Example. A thin converging lens made of crown glass has a focal length $f_{\text{red}} = 50.0\,\text{cm}$ for red light ($n_{\text{red}} = 1.512$) and $f_{\text{violet}} = 48.5\,\text{cm}$ for violet light ($n_{\text{violet}} = 1.530$). An object is placed $150\,\text{cm}$ to the left of the lens. Using the thin-lens equation $\frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o}$:

$$ d_{i,\text{red}} = \frac{(50.0)(150)}{150 - 50.0} = 75.0\,\text{cm}, \qquad d_{i,\text{violet}} = \frac{(48.5)(150)}{150 - 48.5} \approx 71.7\,\text{cm}. $$

The longitudinal chromatic aberration is $\Delta d_i = 75.0 - 71.7 = 3.3\,\text{cm}$, meaning the red and violet images form $3.3\,\text{cm}$ apart along the optical axis.

Key Theorem (Achromatic Doublet Condition). Two thin lenses of powers $P_1 = 1/f_1$ and $P_2 = 1/f_2$, made from materials with Abbe numbers $V_1$ and $V_2$, form an achromatic combination when

$$ \frac{P_1}{V_1} + \frac{P_2}{V_2} = 0. $$

Since $V_1 \neq V_2$ requires one lens to be converging and the other diverging, the combination cancels first-order chromatic error while retaining net converging power — the principle underlying the achromatic doublet used in quality camera lenses and telescopes.

Lab Exercise. Illuminate a white-light point source with a single converging lens (focal length $\approx 15\,\text{cm}$). Compute the longitudinal chromatic aberration and compare with the value predicted from the manufacturer's dispersive data. Then introduce a diverging lens of flint glass to form a doublet and verify that the colored fringe is suppressed.

🍃 Telescope

🍃 Telescope

Definition. A telescope is an optical instrument that collects and magnifies light from distant objects by using a combination of lenses or mirrors. The refracting telescope (or refractor) employs two converging lenses: an objective lens of long focal length $f_o$ that forms a real, diminished, inverted image of a distant object, and an eyepiece lens of short focal length $f_e$ that acts as a magnifying glass to view that intermediate image. When the telescope is adjusted so that the final image forms at infinity (normal adjustment), the angular magnification is

$$ M = -\frac{f_o}{f_e} $$

where the negative sign indicates an inverted image. A larger objective focal length and a shorter eyepiece focal length together yield greater magnification.

Worked Example. A student uses a refracting telescope whose objective lens has focal length $f_o = 900\,\text{mm}$ and whose eyepiece has focal length $f_e = 25\,\text{mm}$. Find the angular magnification and the required tube length in normal adjustment.

Magnification:

$$ M = -\frac{f_o}{f_e} = -\frac{900}{25} = -36 $$

The magnitude $|M| = 36$ means the Moon's angular diameter appears 36 times larger than with the naked eye; the minus sign confirms the image is inverted.

Tube length: In normal adjustment the intermediate image sits at the front focal point of the eyepiece, so the optical tube length equals

$$ L = f_o + f_e = 900 + 25 = 925\,\text{mm} $$

Key Theorem — Rayleigh Criterion. The angular resolution $\theta_{\min}$ of a telescope—the smallest angular separation between two point sources that the instrument can distinguish—is limited by diffraction at the objective aperture:

$$ \theta_{\min} = 1.22\,\frac{\lambda}{D} $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength of light and $D$ is the diameter of the objective. A larger aperture $D$ reduces $\theta_{\min}$, improving the ability to distinguish fine detail. This is why research telescopes are built with the largest possible mirrors or lenses rather than simply increasing magnification: boosting $M$ without increasing $D$ magnifies the diffraction blur along with the image, yielding no gain in resolved detail.

🌸 Reflecting Telescope

🌸 Reflecting Telescope

Definition. A reflecting telescope (or reflector) uses a concave primary mirror to collect and focus incoming light rather than a lens. Light from a distant object travels down the telescope tube, strikes the primary mirror, and is reflected to a focal point. In the most common design, the Newtonian reflector, a small flat secondary mirror intercepts the converging beam at 45° and redirects it to an eyepiece mounted at the side of the tube. The focal length $f$ of a concave spherical mirror is related to its radius of curvature $R$ by

$$ f = \frac{R}{2}. $$

The angular magnification produced by a telescope is

$$ m = -\frac{f_\text{obj}}{f_\text{eye}}, $$

where $f_\text{obj}$ is the focal length of the objective (primary mirror) and $f_\text{eye}$ is the focal length of the eyepiece. The negative sign indicates an inverted image, which is acceptable for astronomical observation.

Worked Example. A Newtonian reflector has a primary mirror with radius of curvature $R = 2.00\ \text{m}$ and an eyepiece with focal length $f_\text{eye} = 0.025\ \text{m}$. Find the focal length of the primary mirror and the angular magnification of the system.

Primary focal length:

$$ f_\text{obj} = \frac{R}{2} = \frac{2.00}{2} = 1.00\ \text{m}. $$

Angular magnification:

$$ |m| = \frac{f_\text{obj}}{f_\text{eye}} = \frac{1.00}{0.025} = 40. $$

The telescope magnifies the apparent angular size of a distant object by a factor of 40.

Key Theorem — Mirror Equation. For a concave spherical mirror with focal length $f$, object distance $d_o$, and image distance $d_i$, the mirror equation states

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}. $$

Reflecting telescopes achieve superior performance by accommodating very large mirror diameters, since mirrors do not suffer from chromatic aberration and can be mechanically supported from behind — advantages unavailable to large refracting lenses. Increasing the mirror diameter directly increases light-gathering power, which scales as $D^2$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a concave mirror of known radius of curvature $R$ at one end of an optical bench. Place a small bright object at a distance $d_o > f$ from the mirror and locate the real image by moving a screen along the bench until a sharp image forms. Measure $d_o$ and $d_i$, then verify the mirror equation

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{2}{R}. $$

Compare the experimentally determined $f$ with the value predicted from the measured $R$, and quantify the percentage discrepancy.

Payoff 🎯

The reflecting telescope represents the natural culmination of geometric optics as studied in this chapter, unifying the principles of reflection, focal length, mirror curvature, and image formation into a single instrument of profound scientific consequence. Unlike the refracting telescope, which relies on lenses and suffers from chromatic aberration — the wavelength-dependent bending of light described by $n(\lambda)$ — the reflecting telescope employs a concave primary mirror to converge all wavelengths to a common focus. Because reflection obeys $\theta_r = \theta_i$ regardless of $\lambda$, the instrument achieves achromatic imaging without the dispersion penalty inherent to glass optics.

Definition and optical geometry. A Newtonian reflecting telescope consists of a parabolic primary mirror of focal length $f_1$ and a small flat secondary mirror that redirects the converging beam to a side-mounted eyepiece of focal length $f_e$. The angular magnification of the system is

$$ M = -\frac{f_1}{f_e}, $$

where the negative sign indicates an inverted image. For a primary mirror of radius of curvature $R$, the focal length is $f_1 = R/2$. The parabolic (rather than spherical) profile eliminates spherical aberration for on-axis point sources, ensuring that all paraxial and marginal rays converge to the same focal point.

Worked example. A student constructs a Newtonian reflector with a primary mirror of $R = 1.20\ \text{m}$ and an eyepiece of $f_e = 25.0\ \text{mm}$. The primary focal length is $f_1 = 0.60\ \text{m}$. The magnification is $M = -(0.60\ \text{m})/(0.025\ \text{m}) = -24\times$, yielding an inverted image magnified 24 times relative to the naked eye.

Key theorem. The Rayleigh criterion for angular resolution of a circular aperture of diameter $D$ is

$$ \theta_{\min} = 1.22\frac{\lambda}{D}. $$

Larger primary mirrors directly decrease $\theta_{\min}$, which is why professional observatories build mirrors of diameter $8$–$10\ \text{m}$: resolving finer angular structure demands aperture, not merely magnification.

Laboratory exercise. Using a concave mirror of known $R$, measure the focal length by locating the image of a distant light source. Compare your measured $f$ to $R/2$ and compute the percent deviation. Then swap in a shorter eyepiece and quantify how magnification scales, verifying the relation $M = -f_1/f_e$ experimentally.

The reflecting telescope connects directly to stellar spectroscopy (dispersing collected light to read atomic emission lines), photometry (measuring flux to infer luminosity and distance via the inverse-square law $I \propto r^{-2}$), and modern adaptive optics (correcting atmospheric wavefront distortions in real time). Each of these applications begins precisely where this chapter ends — at the focal plane of a mirror. If you wish to go further, consider exploring stellar spectroscopy: the same instrument that forms an image can, with the addition of a diffraction grating, decode the chemical composition, temperature, and radial velocity of a star from nothing more than its light.
