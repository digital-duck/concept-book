🌸 Contact Lenses

🌸 Contact Lenses

Definition. A contact lens is a thin refracting element placed directly on the cornea, functioning as an additional lens in the eye's optical system. Unlike spectacles, which sit approximately 12–15 mm from the cornea, a contact lens sits at the eye itself ($d_\text{lens} \approx 0$), so the lens power required to correct a refractive error differs from that of an equivalent spectacle lens. The corrective power $P$ of a lens is defined as

$$ P = \frac{1}{f}, $$

where $f$ is the focal length in meters and $P$ is expressed in diopters (D, units of m$^{-1}$). For a contact lens, the required power equals the difference between the far-point vergence and the normal far-point vergence at the corneal plane.

Worked Example. A myopic patient has a far point at 25 cm from the eye. Find the contact lens power needed to correct the vision to infinity.

For an emmetropic (normal) eye, the far point is at infinity, so the vergence of parallel incoming rays is $V_\infty = 0$ D. The patient's eye, however, requires incoming rays to diverge as if from 25 cm. For a contact lens at the corneal plane, the image of an infinitely distant object must be formed at the far point, 25 cm in front of the lens. Using the thin-lens equation with object distance $d_o = \infty$:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_i} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{-0.25\,\text{m}} - 0 = -4.00\,\text{D}. $$

The required contact lens power is $P = -4.00$ D (a diverging lens), consistent with myopia correction.

Key Theorem (Vertex Power Conversion). When converting a spectacle prescription of power $P_s$ at vertex distance $d$ (in meters) to a contact lens prescription, the equivalent contact power is

$$ P_c = \frac{P_s}{1 - d\,P_s}. $$

This relation arises from the requirement that both lens systems produce the same image vergence at the corneal plane. For strong prescriptions ($|P_s| > 4$ D), the correction is clinically significant.

Lab Exercise. Using a optical bench, a diverging lens of known focal length, and a distant light source, place the lens at 0 mm and then 15 mm from a model eye (a converging lens–screen system). Measure the screen position for a focused image in each case. Calculate $P_c$ from the vertex conversion formula and compare to your direct measurement. Quantify the percentage difference and assess when the approximation $P_c \approx P_s$ is valid.
