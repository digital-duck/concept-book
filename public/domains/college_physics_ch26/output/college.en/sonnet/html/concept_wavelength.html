🌱 Wavelength

Definition. The wavelength of a periodic wave is the spatial distance over which the wave's shape repeats exactly once. For a sinusoidal wave propagating in the positive $x$-direction, the wave function is

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\,x - \frac{2\pi}{T}\,t + \phi_0\right), $$

where $A$ is the amplitude, $T$ is the period, $\phi_0$ is the initial phase, and $\lambda$ is the wavelength measured in metres. Equivalently, $\lambda$ is the distance between any two nearest points that are in phase — for instance, crest to crest or trough to trough. The wavelength is related to the wave speed $v$ and frequency $f$ by the fundamental dispersion relation

$$ v = f\lambda. $$

Worked Example. Yellow sodium light has a frequency of $f = 5.09 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}$ and travels through air at $v \approx c = 3.00 \times 10^{8}\ \text{m/s}$. Find its wavelength.

Applying the dispersion relation:

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^{8}\ \text{m/s}}{5.09 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}} = 5.89 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m} = 589\ \text{nm}. $$

This result places sodium light squarely in the yellow-orange portion of the visible spectrum ($\approx 570$–$590\ \text{nm}$), consistent with observation.

Key Theorem (Snell's Law and Wavelength Change). When light crosses an interface from a medium of refractive index $n_1$ into one of index $n_2$, the frequency remains constant while the wavelength changes:

$$ \lambda_n = \frac{\lambda_0}{n}, $$

where $\lambda_0$ is the free-space wavelength and $n$ is the refractive index of the medium. The wave speed in the medium becomes $v = c/n$, so $v = f\lambda_n$ is preserved. This theorem underpins the analysis of lenses, thin-film interference, and optical fiber design.

Lab Exercise. Set up a two-slit interference pattern using a laser pointer ($\lambda$ unknown) with slit separation $d = 0.250\ \text{mm}$ and a screen at distance $L = 1.20\ \text{m}$. Measure the fringe spacing $\Delta y$. Use the relation $\lambda = d\,\Delta y / L$ to determine $\lambda$, then compare your result against the manufacturer's specification. Quantify the percentage error and identify at least two sources of systematic uncertainty in your measurement.
