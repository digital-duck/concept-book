🍃 Hyperopia

🍃 Hyperopia

Definition. Hyperopia, commonly called farsightedness, is a refractive error of the eye in which parallel rays of light from a distant object are brought to focus behind the retina rather than precisely on it. This condition arises when the axial length of the eyeball is shorter than normal, when the refracting power of the cornea-lens system is insufficient, or both. The near point of a hyperopic eye—the closest distance at which an object can be held in focus—is farther than the standard 25 cm (the near point of a normal eye). Formally, if the optical power of the relaxed eye is $P_{\text{eye}}$ and the image distance required to focus on the retina is $d_i$, then hyperopia occurs whenever $d_i > d_{\text{retina}}$ for an object at infinity.

Worked Example. A patient has a far point at infinity (the relaxed eye can focus distant objects) but a near point of 75 cm rather than the normal 25 cm. Determine the power of the corrective lens needed to restore a 25 cm near point, assuming the lens is placed directly at the eye.

The corrective lens must form a virtual image of an object at $d_o = 25\ \text{cm}$ at the patient's actual near point $d_i = -75\ \text{cm}$ (virtual image, same side as object, so $d_i$ is negative in the sign convention).

Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{0.25\ \text{m}} + \frac{1}{-0.75\ \text{m}} = 4.00 - 1.33 = +2.67\ \text{D}. $$

A converging lens of power $P \approx +2.67\ \text{D}$ is required.

Key Theorem. A converging (positive-power) lens corrects hyperopia. By adding refracting power $\Delta P > 0$ to the eye's system, the effective focal length is shortened sufficiently to shift the image forward onto the retina for near objects. The required corrective power satisfies $P_{\text{lens}} = \frac{1}{d_{\text{near,desired}}} - \frac{1}{d_{\text{near,actual}}}$.

Lab Exercise. Using a ray-optics kit, position a converging lens of known focal length in front of a screen representing the retina. Vary the object distance and record the minimum distance at which a sharp image forms without and with a supplementary converging lens. Compare your measured corrective power to the theoretical value computed from the thin-lens equation and quantify the percent error.
