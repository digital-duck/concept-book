🍃 Aberration

🍃 Aberration

Definition. Optical aberration refers to any deviation from ideal image formation in which rays from a single object point fail to converge to a single, sharp image point after passing through an optical system. In geometric optics, a perfect (stigmatic) lens maps every object point to exactly one image point; aberrations represent departures from this ideal. Aberrations arise from two principal sources: monochromatic aberrations, caused by the geometry of the lens surfaces and described by Seidel's five primary (third-order) aberrations, and chromatic aberration, caused by the wavelength dependence of the index of refraction (dispersion).

Chromatic aberration is particularly significant in introductory contexts. Because the index of refraction $n$ of glass varies with wavelength $\lambda$ (typically $n$ increases as $\lambda$ decreases), the focal length $f$ of a thin lens also varies with wavelength via the lensmaker's equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) $$

Violet light ($\lambda \approx 400\ \text{nm}$) is focused more strongly than red light ($\lambda \approx 700\ \text{nm}$), producing a spread of focal points along the optical axis known as longitudinal chromatic aberration.

Worked Example. A thin converging lens made of crown glass has $n_{\text{red}} = 1.512$ and $n_{\text{violet}} = 1.530$ for red and violet light, respectively, with radii of curvature $R_1 = +20.0\ \text{cm}$ and $R_2 = -20.0\ \text{cm}$. The focal lengths are:

$$ f_{\text{red}} = \left[(1.512 - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{0.200} - \frac{1}{-0.200}\right)\right]^{-1} = \frac{1}{(0.512)(10.0\ \text{m}^{-1})} \approx 19.5\ \text{cm} $$ $$ f_{\text{violet}} \approx \frac{1}{(0.530)(10.0)} \approx 18.9\ \text{cm} $$

The longitudinal chromatic aberration is $\Delta f = f_{\text{red}} - f_{\text{violet}} \approx 0.6\ \text{cm}$.

Key Theorem (Achromatic Doublet). A cemented doublet consisting of a converging lens of focal length $f_1$ (crown glass, dispersive power $\omega_1$) and a diverging lens of focal length $f_2$ (flint glass, dispersive power $\omega_2$) eliminates first-order chromatic aberration when:

$$ \frac{\omega_1}{f_1} + \frac{\omega_2}{f_2} = 0 $$

where $\omega = (n_F - n_C)/(n_D - 1)$ is the Abbe dispersive power.

Lab Exercise. Observe white-light images formed by a single converging lens on a screen. Slowly translate the screen along the optical axis and record at which position the image core appears sharpest for red versus blue light (use color filters). Compute the measured $\Delta f$ and compare it to the theoretical prediction using the lensmaker's equation and published dispersion data for the lens material.
