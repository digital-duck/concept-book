🍃 Myopia

🍃 Myopia

Definition. Myopia, commonly called nearsightedness, is a refractive error of the eye in which the optical system forms a focused image of a distant object in front of the retina rather than upon it. This occurs when the eyeball is elongated along the optical axis or when the cornea and lens together possess excessive refracting power. A myopic eye can focus on nearby objects (within the near point) but cannot bring parallel rays from a distant object to a sharp retinal image. The far point of a myopic eye, denoted $d_f$, is finite and located at some distance less than infinity in front of the eye.

Worked Example. A patient has a far point of $d_f = 0.50\ \text{m}$. Determine the power (in diopters) of the corrective lens required to allow the patient to see objects at infinity.

For a corrective lens worn at the eye, the lens must form a virtual image of an infinitely distant object at the patient's far point. Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} $$

with $d_o \to \infty$ and $d_i = -0.50\ \text{m}$ (virtual image, so negative by sign convention):

$$ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{\infty} + \frac{1}{-0.50} = -2.0\ \text{m}^{-1} $$

The required lens power is $P = -2.0\ \text{D}$ (a diverging lens).

Key Theorem. For any myopic eye with far point at distance $d_f$, the corrective lens power is:

$$ P = -\frac{1}{d_f} $$

This follows directly from the thin-lens equation under the condition $d_o \to \infty$, yielding $f = -d_f$. A diverging (concave) lens of negative focal length spreads incoming parallel rays so that they appear to diverge from the far point, enabling the eye's natural optics to converge them onto the retina.

Lab Exercise. Using an optical bench with a converging lens (to model the eye) and a screen (retina), place the screen closer than the focal length to simulate myopia. Introduce a series of diverging lenses of known power until the projected image of a distant source sharpens on the screen. Record the corrective power and compare it with $P = -1/d_f$ computed from the measured image-shift distance.
