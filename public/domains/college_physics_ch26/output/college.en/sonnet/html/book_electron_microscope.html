🌱 Convex Lens

🌱 Convex Lens

Definition. A convex lens (also called a converging lens) is a transparent optical element bounded by two surfaces, at least one of which curves outward, such that the lens is thicker at its optical axis than at its edges. When paraxial rays traveling parallel to the principal axis pass through a convex lens, they refract and converge at the focal point $F$ on the far side, at a focal length $f > 0$ from the optical center. The behavior of a convex lens is governed by the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} $$

where $d_o$ is the object distance and $d_i$ is the image distance, both measured from the lens center. The lateral magnification follows as:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} $$

A negative magnification indicates an inverted image; $|m| > 1$ indicates enlargement, $|m| < 1$ reduction.

Worked Example. A candle is placed $30\,\text{cm}$ in front of a convex lens with focal length $f = 10\,\text{cm}$. Find the image distance and magnification.

Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{10} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3-1}{30} = \frac{2}{30} $$ $$ d_i = 15\,\text{cm} $$

The image forms $15\,\text{cm}$ beyond the lens on the transmission side — a real, inverted image. The magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{15}{30} = -0.5 $$

The image is inverted and half the size of the object. Because $d_o > f$, a real image always forms on the opposite side of the lens from the object, a result consistent with the sign convention embedded in the thin-lens equation.

Key Theorem (Lensmaker's Equation). The focal length of a thin lens with radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$ and refractive index $n$ satisfies:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n-1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) $$

This establishes that focal length depends solely on the lens geometry and material, independent of object position — a result central to optical instrument design.

Lab Exercise. Using a convex lens of unknown focal length, place a bright object at several measured distances $d_o > f$ and record the corresponding image distances $d_i$ on a screen. Plot $1/d_i$ versus $1/d_o$ and determine $f$ from the intercepts. Verify that your experimental value matches the manufacturer specification, and calculate the percent error.

🌱 Focal Length

🌱 Focal Length

Definition. The focal length of a thin lens (or spherical mirror) is the distance $f$ from the optical center to the focal point $F$ — the point at which rays initially parallel to the principal axis converge (converging lens/concave mirror) or appear to diverge from (diverging lens/convex mirror). For a thin lens in air, the focal length is related to the radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$ of its two surfaces by the lensmaker's equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right), $$

where $n$ is the index of refraction of the lens material. By convention, a radius is positive when the center of curvature lies to the right of the surface. A positive $f$ denotes a converging lens; a negative $f$ denotes a diverging lens.

Worked Example. A biconvex glass lens ($n = 1.52$) has equal radii of curvature $|R| = 20.0\ \text{cm}$. With light traveling left to right, $R_1 = +20.0\ \text{cm}$ and $R_2 = -20.0\ \text{cm}$. Substituting:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (1.52 - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{20.0} - \frac{1}{-20.0}\right) = 0.52 \times \frac{2}{20.0} = 0.052\ \text{cm}^{-1}. $$

Therefore $f = 1/0.052 \approx 19.2\ \text{cm}$. The positive value confirms the lens is converging.

Key Theorem (Thin-Lens Equation). Once $f$ is known, the image distance $d_i$ produced by an object at distance $d_o$ satisfies:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}. $$

This relation, derivable from similar-triangle geometry applied to ray diagrams, is the central predictive tool of geometric optics. The lateral magnification follows as $m = -d_i/d_o$. For $|m| > 1$ the image is enlarged; a negative $m$ indicates an inverted image.

Lab Exercise. Mount a converging lens on an optical bench. Place a bright object at five different distances $d_o$ ranging from $1.5f$ to $4f$. For each position, locate the sharpest image on a screen and record $d_i$. Plot $1/d_o$ versus $1/d_i$; fit a line of slope $-1$ and extract $f$ from the intercept. Compare the measured focal length with the manufacturer's value and compute the percent error, discussing sources of systematic uncertainty such as lens thickness and chromatic aberration.

🌱 Magnification

🌱 Magnification

Definition. Magnification quantifies how much larger or smaller an image appears compared to the original object. For optical systems described by the thin-lens equation, two distinct magnification measures are employed. Lateral (transverse) magnification $m$ compares image height $h_i$ to object height $h_o$:

$$ m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} $$

where $d_o$ is the object distance and $d_i$ is the image distance, both measured from the lens. The negative sign encodes orientation: $m < 0$ indicates an inverted image, $m > 0$ an upright one. $|m| > 1$ signifies magnification (enlargement); $|m| < 1$ signifies minification (reduction).

Worked Example. A converging lens of focal length $f = 20\,\text{cm}$ has an object placed $30\,\text{cm}$ in front of it. Find the image distance and lateral magnification.

Apply the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} \implies \frac{1}{30} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{20} $$

Solving: $\frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{20} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3-2}{60} = \frac{1}{60}$, so $d_i = 60\,\text{cm}$.

The lateral magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{60}{30} = -2 $$

The image is real (positive $d_i$), inverted ($m < 0$), and twice the size of the object ($|m| = 2$).

Key Theorem (Newtonian Form). Measuring object and image distances from the focal points rather than the lens center — letting $x_o = d_o - f$ and $x_i = d_i - f$ — yields Newton's lens equation $x_o x_i = f^2$. In this form the magnification becomes $m = -f/x_o = -x_i/f$, which proves that magnification is entirely determined by the ratio of the image-side or object-side focal displacement to $f$. This form is particularly useful in microscopy, where object distances are only slightly beyond $f$.

Investigate how $m$ changes as you move the object from $d_o = 2f$ toward $d_o = f$, recording whether images remain real or become virtual.

🍃 Compound Microscope

🍃 Compound Microscope

Definition

A compound microscope is an optical instrument that uses two converging lens systems in series to achieve magnifications far exceeding what a single lens can provide. The objective lens (focal length $f_o$, typically 2–10 mm) forms a real, inverted, magnified intermediate image of the specimen. The eyepiece (focal length $f_e$, typically 25–50 mm) then acts as a simple magnifier, producing a virtual image that the observer views at the near point $d_n = 25$ cm. The total angular magnification is

$$ M = m_o \cdot M_e = -\frac{d}{f_o} \cdot \frac{d_n}{f_e}, $$

where $d$ is the tube length — the distance from the rear focal point of the objective to the front focal point of the eyepiece (standardized at $d = 160$ mm in many instruments). The negative sign reflects the inverted final image.

Worked Example

A compound microscope has an objective with $f_o = 4.00\text{ mm}$ and an eyepiece with $f_e = 25.0\text{ mm}$. The tube length is $d = 160\text{ mm}$. Find the total magnification.

Objective magnification:

$$ m_o = -\frac{d}{f_o} = -\frac{160\text{ mm}}{4.00\text{ mm}} = -40. $$

Eyepiece magnification (treating it as a simple magnifier with $d_n = 250\text{ mm}$):

$$ M_e = \frac{d_n}{f_e} = \frac{250\text{ mm}}{25.0\text{ mm}} = 10. $$

Total magnification:

$$ M = m_o \cdot M_e = (-40)(10) = -400. $$

The specimen appears 400 times larger and inverted, consistent with markings such as "40×" on the objective and "10×" on the eyepiece.

Key Theorem — Rayleigh Resolution Criterion

The minimum resolvable separation between two point sources through a circular aperture is

$$ \Delta y_{\min} = \frac{1.22\,\lambda\, f_o}{D}, $$

where $\lambda$ is the illumination wavelength and $D$ is the objective aperture diameter. This limit means that increasing magnification without reducing $\lambda$ or enlarging $D$ reveals no additional structural detail — the image grows larger but remains blurred at the same spatial frequency. Resolution is therefore governed by the objective's aperture and the wavelength of light, not by magnification alone.

🌸 Electron Microscope

🌸 Electron Microscope

Definition. An electron microscope is an imaging instrument that uses a beam of accelerated electrons, rather than visible light, to form magnified images of specimens. Because the resolving power of any microscope is fundamentally limited by the wavelength of the illuminating radiation, replacing photons (wavelengths $\lambda \approx 400$–$700\ \text{nm}$) with electrons (whose de Broglie wavelength can be made smaller than $0.01\ \text{nm}$) allows resolution at the atomic scale. The de Broglie wavelength of an electron accelerated through a potential difference $V$ is

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m_e eV}}, $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant, $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$ is the electron rest mass, and $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$ is the elementary charge. Magnetic or electrostatic lenses focus the beam, and the Rayleigh criterion gives the minimum resolvable feature size as approximately $d_{\min} \approx 0.61\lambda / \text{NA}$, where NA is the numerical aperture of the objective lens.

Worked Example. An electron is accelerated through $V = 100\ \text{kV}$.

$$ \lambda = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{\sqrt{2(9.109 \times 10^{-31})(1.602 \times 10^{-19})(1.00 \times 10^{5})}} $$

Computing the denominator: $2m_e eV = 2(9.109\times10^{-31})(1.602\times10^{-19})(10^5) = 2.919\times10^{-44}\ \text{kg}^2\text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2}$, so $\sqrt{2m_e eV} = 1.709\times10^{-22}\ \text{kg·m·s}^{-1}$. Therefore

$$ \lambda = \frac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{1.709\times10^{-22}} \approx 3.88\times10^{-12}\ \text{m} = 3.88\ \text{pm}. $$

This wavelength is roughly $10^5$ times smaller than visible light, permitting sub-nanometer resolution.

Key Theorem (Abbe Resolution Limit). The minimum resolvable separation between two point objects is

$$ d_{\min} = \frac{0.61\,\lambda}{\text{NA}}. $$

For a transmission electron microscope operating at $100\ \text{kV}$ with NA $\approx 0.01$, this yields $d_{\min} \approx 0.24\ \text{nm}$, sufficient to resolve individual atomic columns in crystalline materials.

Payoff 🎯

The electron microscope stands as the natural culmination of the optics principles developed throughout this chapter because it exposes the fundamental physical limit of all wave-based imaging: resolution is bounded by wavelength. Visible light, with wavelengths spanning roughly $\lambda \approx 400\text{–}700\ \text{nm}$, cannot resolve structures smaller than a few hundred nanometers—a ceiling imposed by the Rayleigh criterion, $d_{\min} \approx 1.22\,\lambda / (2\,\text{NA})$, where $\text{NA}$ is the numerical aperture of the objective. The electron microscope circumvents this barrier entirely by replacing photons with electrons whose de Broglie wavelength, $\lambda = h / p = h / \sqrt{2m_e eV}$, falls in the picometer range at accessible accelerating voltages. At $V = 100\ \text{kV}$, for example, $\lambda \approx 3.7\ \text{pm}$—roughly $10^5$ times shorter than green light—enabling sub-nanometer spatial resolution.

Worked example. An electron is accelerated through $V = 50\ \text{kV}$. Its kinetic energy is $K = eV = (1.602\times10^{-19}\ \text{C})(5.0\times10^4\ \text{V}) = 8.01\times10^{-15}\ \text{J}$. The momentum follows from $p = \sqrt{2m_e K}$:

$$ p = \sqrt{2\,(9.109\times10^{-31}\ \text{kg})\,(8.01\times10^{-15}\ \text{J})} = 1.207\times10^{-22}\ \text{kg\cdot m\cdot s}^{-1}. $$

The de Broglie wavelength is therefore $\lambda = h/p = (6.626\times10^{-34})/(1.207\times10^{-22}) \approx 5.5\ \text{pm}$, placing theoretical resolution well below the diameter of a single atom.

Key theorem. The Abbe diffraction limit generalizes across all wave-based microscopes: no instrument can resolve two point sources separated by less than $\lambda / (2\,\text{NA})$ regardless of optical quality. Because $\lambda$ for electrons at practical voltages is five to six orders of magnitude smaller than visible light, the electron microscope transcends every constraint that bounds conventional optics.

The electron microscope does not merely extend the reach of classical optics—it redefines what imaging means at the atomic scale.
