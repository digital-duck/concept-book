🌱 Convex Lens

🌱 Convex Lens

Definition. A convex lens (also called a converging lens) is a transparent optical element bounded by two surfaces, at least one of which curves outward, such that the lens is thicker at its optical axis than at its edges. When paraxial rays traveling parallel to the principal axis pass through a convex lens, they refract and converge at the focal point $F$ on the far side, at a focal length $f > 0$ from the optical center. The behavior of a convex lens is governed by the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} $$

where $d_o$ is the object distance and $d_i$ is the image distance, both measured from the lens center. The lateral magnification follows as:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} $$

A negative magnification indicates an inverted image; $|m| > 1$ indicates enlargement, $|m| < 1$ reduction.

Worked Example. A candle is placed $30\,\text{cm}$ in front of a convex lens with focal length $f = 10\,\text{cm}$. Find the image distance and magnification.

Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{10} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3-1}{30} = \frac{2}{30} $$ $$ d_i = 15\,\text{cm} $$

The image forms $15\,\text{cm}$ beyond the lens on the transmission side — a real, inverted image. The magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{15}{30} = -0.5 $$

The image is inverted and half the size of the object. Because $d_o > f$, a real image always forms on the opposite side of the lens from the object, a result consistent with the sign convention embedded in the thin-lens equation.

Key Theorem (Lensmaker's Equation). The focal length of a thin lens with radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$ and refractive index $n$ satisfies:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n-1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) $$

This establishes that focal length depends solely on the lens geometry and material, independent of object position — a result central to optical instrument design.

🌱 Focal Length

🌱 Focal Length

Definition. The focal length of a thin lens (or spherical mirror) is the distance $f$ from the optical center to the focal point $F$ — the point at which rays initially parallel to the principal axis converge (converging lens/concave mirror) or appear to diverge from (diverging lens/convex mirror). For a thin lens in air, the focal length is related to the radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$ of its two surfaces by the lensmaker's equation:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right), $$

where $n$ is the index of refraction of the lens material. By convention, a radius is positive when the center of curvature lies to the right of the surface. A positive $f$ denotes a converging lens; a negative $f$ denotes a diverging lens.

Worked Example. A biconvex glass lens ($n = 1.52$) has equal radii of curvature $|R| = 20.0\ \text{cm}$. With light traveling left to right, $R_1 = +20.0\ \text{cm}$ and $R_2 = -20.0\ \text{cm}$. Substituting:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (1.52 - 1)\!\left(\frac{1}{20.0} - \frac{1}{-20.0}\right) = 0.52 \times \frac{2}{20.0} = 0.052\ \text{cm}^{-1}. $$

Therefore $f = 1/0.052 \approx 19.2\ \text{cm}$. The positive value confirms the lens is converging.

Key Theorem (Thin-Lens Equation). Once $f$ is known, the image distance $d_i$ produced by an object at distance $d_o$ satisfies:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}. $$

This relation, derivable from similar-triangle geometry applied to ray diagrams, is the central predictive tool of geometric optics. The lateral magnification follows as $m = -d_i/d_o$. For $|m| > 1$ the image is enlarged; a negative $m$ indicates an inverted image.

🌱 Magnification

🌱 Magnification

Definition. Magnification quantifies how much larger or smaller an image appears compared to the original object. For optical systems described by the thin-lens equation, two distinct magnification measures are employed. Lateral (transverse) magnification $m$ compares image height $h_i$ to object height $h_o$:

$$ m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} $$

where $d_o$ is the object distance and $d_i$ is the image distance, both measured from the lens. The negative sign encodes orientation: $m < 0$ indicates an inverted image, $m > 0$ an upright one. $|m| > 1$ signifies magnification (enlargement); $|m| < 1$ signifies minification (reduction).

Worked Example. A converging lens of focal length $f = 20\,\text{cm}$ has an object placed $30\,\text{cm}$ in front of it. Find the image distance and lateral magnification.

Apply the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} \implies \frac{1}{30} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{20} $$

Solving: $\frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{20} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3-2}{60} = \frac{1}{60}$, so $d_i = 60\,\text{cm}$.

The lateral magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{60}{30} = -2 $$

The image is real (positive $d_i$), inverted ($m < 0$), and twice the size of the object ($|m| = 2$).

Key Theorem (Newtonian Form). Measuring object and image distances from the focal points rather than the lens center — letting $x_o = d_o - f$ and $x_i = d_i - f$ — yields Newton's lens equation $x_o x_i = f^2$. In this form the magnification becomes $m = -f/x_o = -x_i/f$, which proves that magnification is entirely determined by the ratio of the image-side or object-side focal displacement to $f$. This form is particularly useful in microscopy, where object distances are only slightly beyond $f$.

🌱 Refractive Index

🌱 Refractive Index

Definition. The refractive index (or index of refraction) of a medium is a dimensionless quantity $n$ defined as the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum $c$ to the phase speed of light $v$ in that medium:

$$ n = \frac{c}{v} $$

Since light travels slower in any material than in vacuum, $n \geq 1$ for all physical media ($n = 1$ exactly for vacuum, and approximately $1.0003$ for air at standard conditions). The refractive index governs how much a light ray bends at the interface between two media, as quantified by Snell's Law:

$$ n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2 $$

where $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are the angles of incidence and refraction measured from the normal to the interface.

Worked Example. A ray of light passes from water ($n_1 = 1.333$) into glass ($n_2 = 1.500$) at an angle of incidence $\theta_1 = 45.0°$. Find the angle of refraction.

Applying Snell's Law:

$$ \sin\theta_2 = \frac{n_1}{n_2}\sin\theta_1 = \frac{1.333}{1.500}\sin 45.0° = (0.8887)(0.7071) = 0.6284 $$ $$ \theta_2 = \arcsin(0.6284) \approx 38.9° $$

The ray bends toward the normal because it enters a denser optical medium ($n_2 > n_1$), consistent with the general rule that light refracts toward the normal when transitioning from a lower to a higher refractive index.

Key Theorem — Critical Angle. When light travels from a medium of higher refractive index $n_1$ into one of lower index $n_2 < n_1$, total internal reflection occurs at and beyond the critical angle $\theta_c$:

$$ \sin\theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1} $$

At $\theta_1 = \theta_c$, the refracted ray emerges parallel to the interface ($\theta_2 = 90°$); for $\theta_1 > \theta_c$, no transmitted ray exists. This principle underlies optical fiber technology, where light is confined within a high-index core surrounded by a lower-index cladding.

🍃 Compound Microscope

🍃 Compound Microscope

Definition

A compound microscope is an optical instrument that uses two converging lens systems in series to achieve magnifications far exceeding what a single lens can provide. The objective lens (focal length $f_o$, typically 2–10 mm) forms a real, inverted, magnified intermediate image of the specimen. The eyepiece (focal length $f_e$, typically 25–50 mm) then acts as a simple magnifier, producing a virtual image that the observer views at the near point $d_n = 25$ cm. The total angular magnification is

$$ M = m_o \cdot M_e = -\frac{d}{f_o} \cdot \frac{d_n}{f_e}, $$

where $d$ is the tube length — the distance from the rear focal point of the objective to the front focal point of the eyepiece (standardized at $d = 160$ mm in many instruments). The negative sign reflects the inverted final image.

Worked Example

A compound microscope has an objective with $f_o = 4.00\text{ mm}$ and an eyepiece with $f_e = 25.0\text{ mm}$. The tube length is $d = 160\text{ mm}$. Find the total magnification.

Objective magnification:

$$ m_o = -\frac{d}{f_o} = -\frac{160\text{ mm}}{4.00\text{ mm}} = -40. $$

Eyepiece magnification (treating it as a simple magnifier with $d_n = 250\text{ mm}$):

$$ M_e = \frac{d_n}{f_e} = \frac{250\text{ mm}}{25.0\text{ mm}} = 10. $$

Total magnification:

$$ M = m_o \cdot M_e = (-40)(10) = -400. $$

The specimen appears 400 times larger and inverted, consistent with markings such as "40×" on the objective and "10×" on the eyepiece.

Key Theorem — Rayleigh Resolution Criterion

The minimum resolvable separation between two point sources through a circular aperture is

$$ \Delta y_{\min} = \frac{1.22\,\lambda\, f_o}{D}, $$

where $\lambda$ is the illumination wavelength and $D$ is the objective aperture diameter. This limit means that increasing magnification without reducing $\lambda$ or enlarging $D$ reveals no additional structural detail — the image grows larger but remains blurred at the same spatial frequency. Resolution is therefore governed by the objective's aperture and the wavelength of light, not by magnification alone.

🍃 Numerical Aperture

🍃 Numerical Aperture

Definition. The numerical aperture (NA) of an optical system—such as a microscope objective or optical fiber—quantifies its ability to gather light and resolve fine detail. For an objective lens in air, the numerical aperture is defined as

$$ \text{NA} = n \sin\theta $$

where $n$ is the refractive index of the medium between the lens and the specimen, and $\theta$ is the half-angle of the maximum cone of light that can enter or exit the lens. In air ($n = 1.00$), the theoretical maximum is $\text{NA} < 1$; immersion objectives use oil ($n \approx 1.515$) to raise the NA above unity.

The resolving power of a microscope is directly governed by the NA. The minimum resolvable separation between two point sources is given by the Rayleigh criterion adapted for microscopy:

$$ d_{\min} = \frac{0.61\,\lambda}{\text{NA}} $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength of illuminating light. A larger NA yields a smaller $d_{\min}$, hence finer spatial resolution.

Worked Example. A dry objective ($n = 1.00$) has a half-angle $\theta = 30°$ and is used with green light ($\lambda = 550\,\text{nm}$). Compute the NA and the minimum resolvable separation.

$$ \text{NA} = 1.00 \times \sin 30° = 1.00 \times 0.500 = 0.500 $$ $$ d_{\min} = \frac{0.61 \times 550\,\text{nm}}{0.500} = \frac{335.5\,\text{nm}}{0.500} = 671\,\text{nm} $$

Features separated by less than $671\,\text{nm}$ cannot be resolved with this objective under green illumination.

Key Theorem (Abbe Diffraction Limit). Ernst Abbe showed that the resolution of any far-field optical microscope is fundamentally bounded by diffraction. No lens design or aberration correction can circumvent the limit $d_{\min} = 0.61\lambda/\text{NA}$; improvement requires either shorter wavelength or higher NA—the motivation behind oil-immersion objectives and electron microscopy.

🌸 Immersion Lens

🌸 Immersion Lens

Definition. An immersion lens is an optical system in which the space between the objective lens and the specimen is filled with a medium of refractive index $n > 1$ — typically immersion oil with $n \approx 1.515$ — rather than air ($n = 1$). This technique exploits the dependence of numerical aperture on the medium's refractive index. The numerical aperture of an objective is defined as

$$ \text{NA} = n \sin\theta, $$

where $\theta$ is the half-angle of the maximum cone of light that can enter or exit the lens. Because immersion oil closely matches the refractive index of glass, light passes from the coverslip into the oil and into the objective with minimal refraction, allowing larger effective cone angles and thus a higher NA than an equivalent air objective.

Worked Example. Consider an oil-immersion objective with $\theta = 67°$ used in immersion oil ($n = 1.515$). Compute the numerical aperture and compare it with an identical dry objective ($n_\text{air} = 1.00$).

$$ \text{NA}_\text{oil} = 1.515 \times \sin 67° = 1.515 \times 0.921 \approx 1.40 $$ $$ \text{NA}_\text{dry} = 1.00 \times \sin 67° \approx 0.921 $$

The immersion objective achieves $\text{NA} \approx 1.40$, a factor of $1.515$ improvement. Since the minimum resolvable separation is

$$ d = \frac{0.61\,\lambda}{\text{NA}}, $$

for $\lambda = 550\,\text{nm}$:

$$ d_\text{oil} = \frac{0.61 \times 550\,\text{nm}}{1.40} \approx 240\,\text{nm}, \qquad d_\text{dry} \approx 364\,\text{nm}. $$

The immersion lens resolves structures roughly 35% finer.

Key Theorem (Abbe Diffraction Limit). Ernst Abbe showed that the resolution limit of a far-field optical microscope is bounded below by

$$ d_\min = \frac{\lambda}{2\,\text{NA}}. $$

This result is fundamental: no conventional refractive optic can surpass it. The immersion lens maximizes NA — and therefore minimizes $d_\min$ — within the constraints of classical wave optics.

Payoff 🎯

An immersion lens achieves something that no air-coupled optical system can match: it raises the numerical aperture beyond the barrier imposed by the refractive index of air. The numerical aperture of a lens is defined as

$$ \text{NA} = n \sin\theta, $$

where $n$ is the refractive index of the medium between the objective and the specimen, and $\theta$ is the half-angle of the maximum cone of light that the lens can collect. Because $n_{\text{air}} = 1.000$, a dry objective is bounded by $\text{NA} < 1$. An immersion objective filled with cedar oil ($n \approx 1.515$) or water ($n \approx 1.333$) pushes $\text{NA}$ to $1.4$ or higher, directly increasing the resolving power as given by the Rayleigh criterion:

$$ d_{\min} = \frac{0.61\,\lambda}{\text{NA}}. $$

This is the natural endpoint of the chapter because every preceding concept—refraction at curved surfaces, thin-lens equations, aberration theory, and the wave nature of light—converges here. Raising $n$ simultaneously reduces the effective wavelength inside the medium to $\lambda/n$, yielding a two-fold compounding of resolution from both factors in the Rayleigh formula.

Worked example. A microscope objective has $\theta = 67°$. Compare its resolving power in air versus oil immersion ($n = 1.515$) at $\lambda = 550\,\text{nm}$.

  • Air: $\text{NA} = 1.000 \times \sin 67° = 0.921$; $d_{\min} = 0.61(550)/0.921 \approx 364\,\text{nm}$.
  • Oil: $\text{NA} = 1.515 \times \sin 67° = 1.395$; $d_{\min} = 0.61(550)/1.395 \approx 241\,\text{nm}$.

Oil immersion delivers roughly a 34% improvement in resolving power—enough to distinguish subcellular organelles invisible to the dry objective.

Key theorem. Abbe's diffraction limit states that structural detail of spatial frequency $f > \text{NA}/\lambda$ cannot be conveyed by a lens system; immersion maximizes $\text{NA}$ and thereby extends the recoverable spatial bandwidth to its physical ceiling for a given wavelength.
