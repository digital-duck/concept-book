🍃 Compound Microscope

🍃 Compound Microscope

Definition

A compound microscope is an optical instrument that uses two converging lens systems in series to achieve magnifications far exceeding what a single lens can provide. The objective lens (focal length $f_o$, typically 2–10 mm) forms a real, inverted, magnified intermediate image of the specimen. The eyepiece (focal length $f_e$, typically 25–50 mm) then acts as a simple magnifier, producing a virtual image that the observer views at the near point $d_n = 25$ cm. The total angular magnification is

$$ M = m_o \cdot M_e = -\frac{d}{f_o} \cdot \frac{d_n}{f_e}, $$

where $d$ is the tube length — the distance from the rear focal point of the objective to the front focal point of the eyepiece (standardized at $d = 160$ mm in many instruments). The negative sign reflects the inverted final image.

Worked Example

A compound microscope has an objective with $f_o = 4.00\text{ mm}$ and an eyepiece with $f_e = 25.0\text{ mm}$. The tube length is $d = 160\text{ mm}$. Find the total magnification.

Objective magnification:

$$ m_o = -\frac{d}{f_o} = -\frac{160\text{ mm}}{4.00\text{ mm}} = -40. $$

Eyepiece magnification (treating it as a simple magnifier with $d_n = 250\text{ mm}$):

$$ M_e = \frac{d_n}{f_e} = \frac{250\text{ mm}}{25.0\text{ mm}} = 10. $$

Total magnification:

$$ M = m_o \cdot M_e = (-40)(10) = -400. $$

The specimen appears 400 times larger and inverted, consistent with markings such as "40×" on the objective and "10×" on the eyepiece.

Key Theorem — Rayleigh Resolution Criterion

The minimum resolvable separation between two point sources through a circular aperture is

$$ \Delta y_{\min} = \frac{1.22\,\lambda\, f_o}{D}, $$

where $\lambda$ is the illumination wavelength and $D$ is the objective aperture diameter. This limit means that increasing magnification without reducing $\lambda$ or enlarging $D$ reveals no additional structural detail — the image grows larger but remains blurred at the same spatial frequency. Resolution is therefore governed by the objective's aperture and the wavelength of light, not by magnification alone.

Lab Exercise

Using a calibrated stage micrometer, measure the effective magnification of the 40× objective by comparing the image scale to the known 10 μm grid spacing. Record the illumination wavelength from the condenser filter, then compute the theoretical Rayleigh limit $\Delta y_{\min}$ for that objective and compare it to the smallest grid line you can resolve. Discuss any discrepancy in terms of lens aberrations or imprecise centering of the illumination cone.
