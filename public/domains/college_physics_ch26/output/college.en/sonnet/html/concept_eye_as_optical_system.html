🍃 Eye As Optical System

🍃 Eye As Optical System

Definition

The human eye functions as a compound optical system whose primary refracting elements are the cornea and the crystalline lens. The cornea provides roughly two-thirds of the eye's total refractive power (~43 diopters), while the flexible lens contributes the remaining power (~20 diopters at rest) and enables accommodation—active adjustment of focal length to image objects at varying distances. The retina serves as the light-sensitive image plane, located approximately 24 mm behind the cornea. The refractive power $P$ of any thin lens in diopters is

$$ P = \frac{1}{f}, $$

where $f$ is the focal length in meters. For a compound system of two thin lenses separated by distance $d$,

$$ P_{\text{total}} = P_1 + P_2 - d\, P_1 P_2. $$

Worked Example

A relaxed adult eye has a corneal power of $P_c = 43\text{ D}$ and a lens power of $P_l = 20\text{ D}$, with the principal planes separated by approximately $d = 0.005\text{ m}$. Applying the combination formula:

$$ P_{\text{total}} = 43 + 20 - (0.005)(43)(20) = 63 - 4.3 = 58.7\text{ D}. $$

The corresponding image distance from the system is

$$ v = \frac{1}{P_{\text{total}} - u^{-1}}, $$

where $u$ is the object distance. For a distant object ($u \to \infty$), $v = 1/P \approx 17\text{ mm}$, consistent with the vitreous-to-retina path in the reduced eye model.

Key Theorem: Thin-Lens Equation Applied to Accommodation

During accommodation for a near object at distance $d_o$, the ciliary muscles increase lens curvature, raising $P_l$ so that the combined power satisfies:

$$ \frac{1}{f_{\text{eye}}} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}, $$

where $d_i \approx 0.024\text{ m}$ (retinal distance). The maximum accommodative amplitude in a young adult corresponds to an increase of roughly 4 D, enabling clear vision to the near point (~25 cm).

Lab Exercise

Using a convex lens of known focal length and a translucent screen at fixed distance (simulating the retina), place a lit object at three distances: 25 cm, 50 cm, and 1 m. Record the lens power required to form a sharp image at each distance. Plot $P_{\text{required}}$ versus $1/d_o$ and determine the slope; confirm it equals unity as predicted by the thin-lens equation.
