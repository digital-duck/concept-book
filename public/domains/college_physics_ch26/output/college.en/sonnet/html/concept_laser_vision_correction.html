🌸 Laser Vision Correction

🌸 Laser Vision Correction

Definition. Laser vision correction encompasses a family of refractive surgical procedures—most prominently LASIK (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis) and PRK (Photorefractive Keratectomy)—that reshape the corneal surface to alter its focusing power. The cornea accounts for approximately two-thirds of the eye's total refractive power, with the remaining third contributed by the crystalline lens. In a geometrically ideal eye, parallel rays from a distant object are brought to focus precisely on the retina. When the corneal curvature deviates from this ideal, the focal point shifts anterior to the retina (myopia) or posterior to it (hyperopia). The lensmaker's equation governs the relationship between corneal radius of curvature $R$, refractive indices, and focal length:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = (n_\text{cornea} - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) $$

For a simplified single-surface model with the aqueous humor index $n \approx 1.336$ and air index $n_0 = 1.000$, the corneal power in diopters is

$$ P = \frac{n - n_0}{R} = \frac{0.336}{R} $$

where $R$ is in meters. An excimer laser ($\lambda = 193\,\text{nm}$) ablates stromal tissue with submicron precision by breaking molecular bonds via photoablation rather than thermal heating, reducing the risk of collateral damage.

Worked Example. A patient presents with a prescription of $-3.00\,\text{D}$ (myopia). The surgeon must reduce corneal power by $3.00\,\text{D}$. Using $P = 0.336/R$, the pre-operative radius is $R_1 = 0.336/43.00\,\text{D} \approx 7.81\,\text{mm}$ (typical value). The required post-operative radius satisfies $P_2 = 43.00 - 3.00 = 40.00\,\text{D}$, giving $R_2 = 0.336/40.00 \approx 8.40\,\text{mm}$. The central cornea must be flattened by $\Delta R = 0.59\,\text{mm}$, corresponding to tissue removal of approximately $15\,\mu\text{m}$ at the optical center.

Key Theorem. By the thin-lens approximation, a change $\Delta P$ in corneal power shifts the retinal image plane by $\Delta f = -f^2\,\Delta P / n$. Maintaining $\Delta P$ within $\pm 0.125\,\text{D}$ limits residual defocus to under half a diopter, the threshold for clinically significant blur.

Lab Exercise. Using an optical bench with a biconvex lens representing the cornea and an adjustable screen for the retina, measure the screen displacement when a thin glass shim (simulating tissue removal) is inserted. Compare the measured focal shift to the theoretical prediction $\Delta f = -f^2 \Delta P / n$ and calculate the percent error.
