🍃 Capacitive Reactance

🍃 Capacitive Reactance

Definition. Capacitive reactance, denoted $X_C$, quantifies the opposition a capacitor presents to alternating current. For a capacitor of capacitance $C$ driven by a sinusoidal source at angular frequency $\omega = 2\pi f$, the capacitive reactance is

$$ X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C} = \frac{1}{2\pi f C}. $$

The SI unit of $X_C$ is the ohm ($\Omega$). Unlike resistance, reactance does not dissipate energy; it stores and returns energy each cycle. Crucially, $X_C$ decreases as frequency increases: a capacitor passes high-frequency signals readily but impedes low-frequency and DC signals strongly (at $f = 0$, $X_C \to \infty$, confirming that a capacitor blocks steady current). The peak voltage and peak current across a capacitor are related by

$$ V_0 = I_0 X_C, $$

and the current leads the voltage by a phase angle of $90°$ ($\pi/2$ radians).

Worked Example. A $25\,\mu\text{F}$ capacitor is connected to a $120\,\text{V}$ (rms), $60\,\text{Hz}$ source. Find the capacitive reactance and the rms current.

$$ X_C = \frac{1}{2\pi (60)(25 \times 10^{-6})} = \frac{1}{9.42 \times 10^{-3}} \approx 106\,\Omega. $$

Applying the AC analogue of Ohm's law:

$$ I_{\text{rms}} = \frac{V_{\text{rms}}}{X_C} = \frac{120}{106} \approx 1.13\,\text{A}. $$

Key Theorem — Phase Relationship. If the applied voltage is $v(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t)$, then the charge on the capacitor is $q(t) = CV_0 \cos(\omega t)$, and the current is

$$ i(t) = \frac{dq}{dt} = -CV_0 \omega \sin(\omega t) = I_0 \cos\!\left(\omega t + \frac{\pi}{2}\right), $$

where $I_0 = V_0/X_C$. This confirms the $90°$ current-leads-voltage relationship, a defining signature of purely capacitive circuits and the basis for phasor analysis in AC circuit design.

Lab Exercise. Connect a $10\,\mu\text{F}$ capacitor in series with a function generator set to $1\,\text{kHz}$ and a precision resistor ($100\,\Omega$). Use an oscilloscope to measure the peak voltage across the resistor (equal to $I_0 R$) and across the capacitor. Compute $X_C$ experimentally and compare with the theoretical value $X_C = 1/(2\pi \times 10^3 \times 10^{-5}) \approx 15.9\,\Omega$. Repeat at $500\,\text{Hz}$ and $2\,\text{kHz}$, and verify that $X_C$ scales inversely with frequency.
