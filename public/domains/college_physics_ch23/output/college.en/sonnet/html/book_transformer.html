🌱 Magnetic Field

🌱 Magnetic Field

Definition. A magnetic field is a vector field $\mathbf{B}$ that permeates space in the vicinity of moving electric charges or magnetic dipoles, exerting forces on other moving charges placed within it. The SI unit of magnetic field strength is the tesla (T), where $1\,\text{T} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{s}^{-2}\cdot\text{A}^{-1}$. The magnetic force on a point charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ is given by the Lorentz force law:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} $$

This force is always perpendicular to both $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, so a magnetic field does no work on a moving charge and cannot alter its kinetic energy—only its direction of motion.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, $m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$) moves with speed $v = 2.0 \times 10^6\,\text{m/s}$ in the $+x$-direction through a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\text{T}\,\hat{z}$.

The magnetic force is:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} = q(v\hat{x}) \times (B\hat{z}) = qvB(\hat{x} \times \hat{z}) = -qvB\,\hat{y} $$ $$ |\mathbf{F}| = (1.602\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^6)(0.50) \approx 1.60 \times 10^{-13}\,\text{N} $$

The force acts in the $-y$-direction, deflecting the proton into circular motion in the $xy$-plane.

Key Theorem — Radius of Circular Motion. When a charged particle moves perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field, the magnetic force provides the centripetal acceleration. Setting $|\mathbf{F}| = qvB$ equal to $mv^2/r$:

$$ r = \frac{mv}{|q|B} $$

This result, known as the cyclotron radius or Larmor radius, shows that the orbital radius is proportional to momentum and inversely proportional to field strength. For the proton above: $r = (1.673\times10^{-27})(2.0\times10^6)/[(1.602\times10^{-19})(0.50)] \approx 0.042\,\text{m} = 4.2\,\text{cm}$.

🍃 Magnetic Flux

🍃 Magnetic Flux

Definition. Magnetic flux quantifies the total magnetic field passing through a given surface. For a uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$ intersecting a flat surface of area $A$ whose outward normal makes an angle $\theta$ with $\vec{B}$, the magnetic flux is defined as

$$ \Phi_B = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A} = BA\cos\theta, $$

where $\vec{A} = A\hat{n}$ is the area vector. The SI unit of magnetic flux is the weber (Wb), where $1\,\text{Wb} = 1\,\text{T}\cdot\text{m}^2$. For a non-uniform field or curved surface, the definition generalizes to the surface integral

$$ \Phi_B = \iint_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A}. $$

The concept of flux captures the notion of "how much" of the field threads through the surface, accounting for both the field strength and the orientation of the surface relative to the field lines.

Worked Example. A rectangular loop of width $w = 0.30\,\text{m}$ and height $h = 0.20\,\text{m}$ lies in the $xy$-plane. A uniform magnetic field $\vec{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}\,\text{T}$ points in the $+z$-direction. Since the area vector of the loop is $\vec{A} = wh\,\hat{z} = (0.06\,\text{m}^2)\hat{z}$, the angle between $\vec{B}$ and $\hat{n}$ is $\theta = 0°$. Therefore,

$$ \Phi_B = BA\cos 0° = (0.50\,\text{T})(0.06\,\text{m}^2)(1) = 0.030\,\text{Wb}. $$

If the loop is tilted so that its normal makes $60°$ with $\vec{B}$, the flux reduces to $\Phi_B = (0.50)(0.06)\cos 60° = 0.015\,\text{Wb}$.

Key Theorem: Gauss's Law for Magnetism. For any closed surface $S$,

$$ \oint_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A} = 0. $$

This law reflects the non-existence of magnetic monopoles: every field line entering a closed surface must exit it. Unlike electric flux, which can be nonzero when net charge is enclosed, the net magnetic flux through any closed surface is identically zero.

🍃 Electromagnetic Induction

🍃 Electromagnetic Induction

Definition. Electromagnetic induction is the phenomenon by which a time-varying magnetic flux through a conducting loop produces an electromotive force (EMF) in that loop. Faraday's Law quantifies this relationship: the induced EMF is equal to the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through the loop,

$$ \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

where the magnetic flux is defined as $\Phi_B = \int \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$. The negative sign encodes Lenz's Law: the induced EMF drives a current whose magnetic field opposes the change in flux that caused it, a direct consequence of energy conservation.

For a coil of $N$ turns, the expression generalizes to

$$ \mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

since each turn contributes independently to the total EMF.

Worked Example. A circular loop of radius $r = 0.10\ \text{m}$ lies in the $xy$-plane. A uniform magnetic field directed along $\hat{z}$ increases linearly from $B_0 = 0.20\ \text{T}$ to $B_f = 0.80\ \text{T}$ over $\Delta t = 0.40\ \text{s}$. Find the magnitude of the induced EMF.

The area of the loop is $A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.10)^2 \approx 3.14 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{m}^2$. Since $\mathbf{B}$ is perpendicular to the loop, $\Phi_B = BA$. The rate of change of flux is

$$ \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = A\frac{\Delta B}{\Delta t} = (3.14 \times 10^{-2})\frac{0.80 - 0.20}{0.40} \approx 4.7 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{V} $$

Thus $|\mathcal{E}| \approx 47\ \text{mV}$. By Lenz's Law, the induced current flows in the direction that opposes the increasing upward flux, so it circulates clockwise when viewed from above.

Key Theorem. The integral form of Faraday's Law,

$$ \oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} $$

reveals that a time-varying magnetic field is inseparably linked to a circulating electric field, establishing the electromagnetic coupling that underlies generators, transformers, and Maxwell's displacement current formulation.

Lab Exercise. Wind a 50-turn coil around a cylindrical core and connect it to a galvanometer. Insert a bar magnet into the coil at varying speeds and record the deflection. Verify that doubling the insertion speed doubles the peak current reading, and confirm that reversing the magnet's orientation reverses the current direction, as Lenz's Law predicts.

🌱 Electric Current

🌱 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional area of a conductor. If a charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface in a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limit

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\ \text{A} = 1\ \text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of positive current flow is taken as the direction in which positive charges move, even though in metallic conductors the actual charge carriers are electrons moving in the opposite direction.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries a steady current of $2.50\ \text{A}$. Determine the total charge that passes through a cross-section of the wire in $4.00\ \text{min}$.

Given $I = 2.50\ \text{A}$ and $\Delta t = 4.00\ \text{min} = 240\ \text{s}$, rearranging the definition yields

$$ \Delta Q = I\,\Delta t = (2.50\ \text{A})(240\ \text{s}) = 600\ \text{C} $$

This corresponds to the passage of

$$ N = \frac{\Delta Q}{e} = \frac{600\ \text{C}}{1.602\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}} \approx 3.75\times10^{21}\ \text{electrons} $$

highlighting that even modest macroscopic currents involve an enormous number of charge carriers.

Key Theorem (Continuity of Current). For any steady-state (DC) circuit, the current entering any node equals the current leaving that node. Formally, this is Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL):

$$ \sum_{k} I_k = 0 $$

where currents directed into the node are positive and those directed away are negative. KCL is a direct consequence of conservation of charge: in steady state, charge cannot accumulate at any point in a conductor.

Lab Exercise. Connect a $10\ \Omega$ resistor to a 9 V battery in series with a digital multimeter set to measure current. Record $I$ and compute the predicted value using Ohm's Law ($I = V/R$). Next, insert a second identical resistor in series and repeat. Verify that the measured current decreases to half its original value, confirming that current is identical at every cross-section of a series circuit, consistent with KCL.

🍃 Faradays Law

🍃 Faradays Law

Definition

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction states that the electromotive force (EMF) induced in a closed loop is equal in magnitude and opposite in sign to the time rate of change of the magnetic flux through the loop:

$$ \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

where the magnetic flux is defined as:

$$ \Phi_B = \int \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int B \cos\theta \, dA $$

Here, $\theta$ is the angle between the magnetic field vector $\mathbf{B}$ and the area normal $d\mathbf{A}$. The negative sign reflects Lenz's Law: the induced EMF drives a current whose magnetic field opposes the change in flux that produced it.

Worked Example

Consider a rectangular loop of width $w = 0.20\ \text{m}$ and height $h = 0.10\ \text{m}$ lying in the $xy$-plane, within a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = B_0(t)\,\hat{z}$ where $B_0(t) = 4.0t\ \text{T}$ (with $t$ in seconds). The flux through the loop is:

$$ \Phi_B = B_0(t) \cdot A = (4.0t)(0.20)(0.10) = 0.080t\ \text{Wb} $$

Applying Faraday's Law:

$$ \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -\frac{d}{dt}(0.080t) = -0.080\ \text{V} $$

The induced EMF has magnitude $80\ \text{mV}$, constant in time since $B$ increases linearly. By Lenz's Law, the induced current flows in the direction that generates a magnetic field opposing the increasing $+\hat{z}$ flux — namely, in the $-\hat{z}$ direction inside the loop, requiring counterclockwise current when viewed from above.

Key Theorem

For a coil of $N$ turns, the law generalizes to:

$$ \mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

This is the operating principle behind electric generators, transformers, and inductors. In integral form, Faraday's Law is one of Maxwell's equations:

$$ \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = -\frac{d}{dt}\iint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} $$

establishing that a time-varying magnetic field induces a circulating electric field, even in free space.

🍃 Mutual Inductance

🍃 Mutual Inductance

Definition. When two conducting loops are placed in proximity, a time-varying current in one loop generates a changing magnetic flux through the second loop, inducing an EMF in it. This coupling is quantified by the mutual inductance $M$, defined by

$$ \mathcal{E}_2 = -M \frac{dI_1}{dt} $$

where $\mathcal{E}_2$ is the EMF induced in loop 2 and $I_1$ is the current in loop 1. Equivalently, $M$ can be expressed in terms of flux linkage:

$$ M = \frac{N_2 \Phi_{21}}{I_1} $$

where $N_2$ is the number of turns in coil 2 and $\Phi_{21}$ is the magnetic flux through one turn of coil 2 due to $I_1$. The SI unit of mutual inductance is the henry (H), where $1\,\text{H} = 1\,\text{V·s/A}$. By the Neumann formula, mutual inductance is symmetric: $M_{12} = M_{21} = M$.

Worked Example. Consider two coaxial solenoids sharing the same axis. The inner solenoid has $n_1 = 800\,\text{turns/m}$, radius $r_1 = 2.0\,\text{cm}$, and length $\ell = 0.30\,\text{m}$. The outer solenoid has $N_2 = 150$ turns wound over the inner one. When current $I_1$ flows in the inner solenoid, the field inside is $B = \mu_0 n_1 I_1$. The flux through one turn of the outer coil equals the flux through the inner cross-section:

$$ \Phi_{21} = B \cdot \pi r_1^2 = \mu_0 n_1 I_1 \pi r_1^2 $$

Therefore,

$$ M = \frac{N_2 \Phi_{21}}{I_1} = \mu_0 n_1 N_2 \pi r_1^2 = (4\pi \times 10^{-7})(800)(150)\pi(0.02)^2 \approx 9.5\,\mu\text{H} $$

If $I_1$ changes at $dI_1/dt = 200\,\text{A/s}$, the induced EMF in the outer coil is $\mathcal{E}_2 = -M(dI_1/dt) \approx -1.9\,\text{mV}$.

Key Theorem. The energy stored in a system of two magnetically coupled inductors with self-inductances $L_1$, $L_2$ and mutual inductance $M$ carrying currents $I_1$, $I_2$ is

$$ U = \frac{1}{2}L_1 I_1^2 + M I_1 I_2 + \frac{1}{2}L_2 I_2^2 $$

Physical realizability requires $U \geq 0$ for all current values, which constrains $M \leq \sqrt{L_1 L_2}$. The coupling coefficient $k = M/\sqrt{L_1 L_2}$ satisfies $0 \leq k \leq 1$, with $k = 1$ representing perfect (ideal) coupling.

🌸 Transformer

🌸 Transformer

Definition. A transformer is a device that transfers electrical energy between two or more circuits through electromagnetic induction, simultaneously stepping voltage up or down while (ideally) conserving power. It consists of a primary coil of $N_P$ turns and a secondary coil of $N_S$ turns wound on a common ferromagnetic core. When an alternating current drives the primary, the time-varying flux $\Phi_B$ threading the core induces an EMF in each turn. By Faraday's law, the induced EMF per turn is $-d\Phi_B/dt$, so the terminal voltages satisfy

$$ \frac{V_S}{V_P} = \frac{N_S}{N_P}. $$

This is the transformer equation. A ratio $N_S/N_P > 1$ is a step-up transformer; $N_S/N_P < 1$ is step-down.

Worked Example. A power plant outputs $V_P = 10\,\text{kV}$ (rms) at $P = 500\,\text{kW}$ and must transmit over long-distance lines to a neighborhood substation. A step-up transformer with $N_P = 100$ turns and $N_S = 2000$ turns is used.

Step 1 — secondary voltage:

$$ V_S = V_P \cdot \frac{N_S}{N_P} = 10{,}000 \times \frac{2000}{100} = 200\,\text{kV}. $$

Step 2 — transmission current: Assuming an ideal transformer ($P_S = P_P$),

$$ I_S = \frac{P}{V_S} = \frac{500 \times 10^3}{200 \times 10^3} = 2.5\,\text{A}. $$

Reducing current by a factor of 20 reduces resistive ($I^2 R$) line losses by a factor of 400, demonstrating why high-voltage transmission is economically essential.

Key Theorem — Power Conservation in an Ideal Transformer. For a lossless transformer with perfect flux linkage and negligible winding resistance,

$$ V_P I_P = V_S I_S \implies \frac{I_S}{I_P} = \frac{N_P}{N_S}. $$

Voltage and current transform inversely, so impedance scales as $(N_S/N_P)^2$: a load $Z_L$ on the secondary presents an effective impedance $Z_{\text{eff}} = (N_P/N_S)^2 Z_L$ to the primary circuit.

Payoff 🎯

What the Transformer Achieves

The transformer stands as the natural culmination of Chapter 23 because it synthesizes every major principle encountered in the study of electromagnetic induction into a single, elegant device. Faraday's law, mutual inductance, alternating current theory, and the energy conservation of magnetic flux linkage all converge in the transformer's operation. At its core, the device exploits the fact that a time-varying magnetic flux threading a coil induces an electromotive force proportional to the number of turns: for a primary coil with $N_p$ turns and a secondary coil with $N_s$ turns sharing a common core, the voltage transformation ratio is

$$ \frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p} $$

and, by conservation of energy in an ideal transformer, the current transforms inversely:

$$ \frac{I_s}{I_p} = \frac{N_p}{N_s} $$

Worked Example. A step-up transformer on a power line has $N_p = 500$ turns and $N_s = 10{,}000$ turns. If the primary is supplied at $V_p = 120\ \text{V}$ (rms) and draws $I_p = 20\ \text{A}$, find the secondary voltage and current. Applying the ratio: $V_s = 120 \times (10{,}000/500) = 2{,}400\ \text{V}$. By power conservation, $I_s = 20 \times (500/10{,}000) = 1.0\ \text{A}$. The output power $P = V_s I_s = 2{,}400\ \text{W}$ matches the input $P = V_p I_p = 2{,}400\ \text{W}$, confirming the ideal limit.

Key Theorem. For an ideal transformer with negligible core losses and perfect flux coupling, the apparent impedance seen by the primary source is $Z_{\text{in}} = (N_p/N_s)^2 \, Z_L$, where $Z_L$ is the load impedance on the secondary. This impedance transformation property is as significant as the voltage transformation: it allows a source to deliver maximum power to a mismatched load, the condition known as impedance matching.

Compare the observed ratio to theory, then insert an air gap in the core and document how the ratio and magnetizing current change.

Invitation to Deeper Exploration

The transformer's reach extends far beyond power transmission. In audio electronics, output transformers match amplifier impedance to loudspeaker loads. In medical imaging, isolation transformers protect patients from ground-fault currents. In switched-mode power supplies, high-frequency ferrite-core transformers shrink to thumbnail size while converting voltages with efficiencies exceeding 95%. Each of these domains repays close study. If you are drawn to the intersection of electromagnetism and modern electronics, follow the transformer into the switched-mode power supply: there you will find resonant topologies, pulse-width modulation, and the full richness of high-frequency magnetics waiting to be mastered.
