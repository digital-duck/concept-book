🍃 Resonant Frequency

🍃 Resonant Frequency

Definition. In an RLC series circuit driven by a sinusoidal voltage source, the resonant frequency $f_0$ is the driving frequency at which the inductive reactance exactly cancels the capacitive reactance. Because the net reactance vanishes, the circuit impedance reduces to its minimum possible value — the pure resistance $R$ — and the current amplitude reaches its maximum. Quantitatively, the condition $X_L = X_C$ gives

$$ \omega_0 L = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C} \implies \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}, $$

where $\omega_0 = 2\pi f_0$ is the angular resonant frequency, $L$ is inductance in henries, and $C$ is capacitance in farads. Equivalently,

$$ f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}. $$

At resonance the phase angle between voltage and current is zero, so the source delivers power to the resistor with maximum efficiency and the circuit behaves as purely resistive.

Worked Example. A series RLC circuit contains $R = 50\,\Omega$, $L = 20\,\text{mH}$, and $C = 5\,\mu\text{F}$. Find the resonant frequency and the peak current when the source delivers $V_{\text{rms}} = 120\,\text{V}$.

$$ f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{(0.020\,\text{H})(5\times10^{-6}\,\text{F})}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{1.0\times10^{-7}}} \approx \frac{1}{2\pi(3.16\times10^{-4})} \approx 503\,\text{Hz}. $$

At resonance, $Z = R = 50\,\Omega$, so the rms current is

$$ I_{\text{rms}} = \frac{V_{\text{rms}}}{R} = \frac{120}{50} = 2.4\,\text{A}, $$

and the average power dissipated is $P = I_{\text{rms}}^2 R = (2.4)^2(50) = 288\,\text{W}$.

Key Theorem — Bandwidth and Quality Factor. The sharpness of the resonance peak is characterized by the dimensionless quality factor

$$ Q = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}}. $$

The half-power bandwidth satisfies $\Delta\omega = \omega_0/Q$, so a large $Q$ produces a narrow, selective resonance. This principle underlies radio tuning circuits, where $L$ or $C$ is varied to bring $f_0$ to the desired station frequency.

Lab Exercise. Assemble a series RLC circuit with $L = 10\,\text{mH}$ and a decade capacitance box. Using a function generator swept from $100\,\text{Hz}$ to $10\,\text{kHz}$, measure the rms voltage across $R$ at each frequency setting. Plot $V_R$ versus $f$, identify the peak, and compare the measured $f_0$ to the theoretical value. Repeat for three values of $C$ and verify that $f_0 \propto C^{-1/2}$.
