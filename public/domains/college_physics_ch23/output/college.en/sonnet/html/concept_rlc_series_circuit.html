🌸 Rlc Series Circuit

🌸 Rlc Series Circuit

An RLC series circuit consists of a resistor ($R$), an inductor ($L$), and a capacitor ($C$) connected in series with an AC voltage source of the form $v(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t)$. The instantaneous current $i(t)$ is the same through all elements, while the voltages across each element differ in phase. The resistive voltage $v_R = iR$ is in phase with $i$; the inductive voltage $v_L = L\,di/dt$ leads $i$ by $90°$; and the capacitive voltage $v_C = q/C$ lags $i$ by $90°$. Using phasor analysis, the total impedance of the circuit is

$$ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} $$

where $X_L = \omega L$ is the inductive reactance and $X_C = 1/(\omega C)$ is the capacitive reactance. The phase angle between the source voltage and the current is

$$ \phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{X_L - X_C}{R}\right). $$

Worked Example. Consider an RLC series circuit with $R = 50\,\Omega$, $L = 0.20\,\text{H}$, $C = 5.0\,\mu\text{F}$, driven at $f = 200\,\text{Hz}$ ($\omega = 2\pi \times 200 \approx 1257\,\text{rad/s}$). Compute:

$$ X_L = (1257)(0.20) \approx 251\,\Omega, \qquad X_C = \frac{1}{(1257)(5.0\times10^{-6})} \approx 159\,\Omega. $$

Thus $Z = \sqrt{50^2 + (251-159)^2} = \sqrt{2500 + 8464} \approx 105\,\Omega$. With a peak source voltage $V_0 = 120\,\text{V}$, the peak current is $I_0 = V_0/Z \approx 1.14\,\text{A}$, and the circuit is net inductive since $X_L > X_C$.

Key Theorem — Resonance. The current amplitude is maximized when $X_L = X_C$, yielding the resonant frequency

$$ \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}. $$

At resonance $Z = R$ (its minimum value), $\phi = 0$, and the source and current are in phase. The quality factor $Q = \omega_0 L / R$ quantifies the sharpness of the resonance peak.

Lab Exercise. Assemble an RLC series circuit with $L = 50\,\text{mH}$, $C = 10\,\mu\text{F}$, and $R = 20\,\Omega$. Predict the resonant frequency using $f_0 = 1/(2\pi\sqrt{LC})$, then sweep the driving frequency from $50\,\text{Hz}$ to $500\,\text{Hz}$ while measuring $I_0$ with an ammeter. Plot $I_0$ versus $f$, locate the experimental peak, and compare it to your prediction. Compute $Q$ from the half-power bandwidth $\Delta f$ via $Q = f_0/\Delta f$ and reconcile with the theoretical value.
