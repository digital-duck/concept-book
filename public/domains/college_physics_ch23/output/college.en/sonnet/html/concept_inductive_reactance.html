🍃 Inductive Reactance

🍃 Inductive Reactance

Definition. When an alternating current passes through an inductor, the inductor opposes changes in current through electromagnetic induction. This opposition, distinct from resistance, is called inductive reactance and is denoted $X_L$. Unlike resistance, inductive reactance does not dissipate energy; instead, it stores energy in the magnetic field during one half-cycle and returns it during the next. Inductive reactance is defined as

$$ X_L = \omega L = 2\pi f L, $$

where $\omega$ is the angular frequency of the AC source (in rad/s), $f$ is the ordinary frequency (in Hz), and $L$ is the inductance (in henries). The SI unit of $X_L$ is the ohm ($\Omega$). The peak voltage across an ideal inductor and the peak current through it are related by

$$ V_L = I X_L, $$

with the important phase consequence that the current lags the voltage by exactly $90°$ (or $\pi/2$ rad).

Worked Example. A pure inductor of $L = 0.150\ \text{H}$ is connected to a $120\ \text{V}$ (rms), $60.0\ \text{Hz}$ household source. Find the inductive reactance and the rms current.

$$ X_L = 2\pi (60.0\ \text{Hz})(0.150\ \text{H}) = 56.5\ \Omega. $$

Applying the AC version of Ohm's law for reactance:

$$ I_{\text{rms}} = \frac{V_{\text{rms}}}{X_L} = \frac{120\ \text{V}}{56.5\ \Omega} = 2.12\ \text{A}. $$

Note that doubling the frequency to $120\ \text{Hz}$ would double $X_L$ to $113\ \Omega$ and halve the current to $1.06\ \text{A}$, illustrating the direct proportionality between $X_L$ and $f$.

Key Theorem. For a sinusoidal source $v(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t)$, Faraday's law gives $v = L\,di/dt$. Integrating yields $i(t) = -\frac{V_0}{\omega L}\cos(\omega t) = \frac{V_0}{\omega L}\sin\!\left(\omega t - \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$, confirming the $90°$ current lag and establishing that the amplitude ratio $V_0/I_0 = \omega L = X_L$.

Lab Exercise. Connect an inductor of known inductance to a function generator set to $500\ \text{Hz}$. Using an oscilloscope, measure the peak voltage across the inductor and the peak current (via a small series resistor). Compute $X_L = V_0/I_0$ and compare to the theoretical value $2\pi f L$. Repeat at $1000\ \text{Hz}$ and $2000\ \text{Hz}$, then plot $X_L$ versus $f$ to verify the linear relationship.
