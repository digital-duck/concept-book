🍃 Mutual Inductance

Definition. When two conducting loops are placed in proximity, a time-varying current in one loop generates a changing magnetic flux through the second loop, inducing an EMF in it. This coupling is quantified by the mutual inductance $M$, defined by

$$ \mathcal{E}_2 = -M \frac{dI_1}{dt} $$

where $\mathcal{E}_2$ is the EMF induced in loop 2 and $I_1$ is the current in loop 1. Equivalently, $M$ can be expressed in terms of flux linkage:

$$ M = \frac{N_2 \Phi_{21}}{I_1} $$

where $N_2$ is the number of turns in coil 2 and $\Phi_{21}$ is the magnetic flux through one turn of coil 2 due to $I_1$. The SI unit of mutual inductance is the henry (H), where $1\,\text{H} = 1\,\text{V·s/A}$. By the Neumann formula, mutual inductance is symmetric: $M_{12} = M_{21} = M$.

Worked Example. Consider two coaxial solenoids sharing the same axis. The inner solenoid has $n_1 = 800\,\text{turns/m}$, radius $r_1 = 2.0\,\text{cm}$, and length $\ell = 0.30\,\text{m}$. The outer solenoid has $N_2 = 150$ turns wound over the inner one. When current $I_1$ flows in the inner solenoid, the field inside is $B = \mu_0 n_1 I_1$. The flux through one turn of the outer coil equals the flux through the inner cross-section:

$$ \Phi_{21} = B \cdot \pi r_1^2 = \mu_0 n_1 I_1 \pi r_1^2 $$

Therefore,

$$ M = \frac{N_2 \Phi_{21}}{I_1} = \mu_0 n_1 N_2 \pi r_1^2 = (4\pi \times 10^{-7})(800)(150)\pi(0.02)^2 \approx 9.5\,\mu\text{H} $$

If $I_1$ changes at $dI_1/dt = 200\,\text{A/s}$, the induced EMF in the outer coil is $\mathcal{E}_2 = -M(dI_1/dt) \approx -1.9\,\text{mV}$.

Key Theorem. The energy stored in a system of two magnetically coupled inductors with self-inductances $L_1$, $L_2$ and mutual inductance $M$ carrying currents $I_1$, $I_2$ is

$$ U = \frac{1}{2}L_1 I_1^2 + M I_1 I_2 + \frac{1}{2}L_2 I_2^2 $$

Physical realizability requires $U \geq 0$ for all current values, which constrains $M \leq \sqrt{L_1 L_2}$. The coupling coefficient $k = M/\sqrt{L_1 L_2}$ satisfies $0 \leq k \leq 1$, with $k = 1$ representing perfect (ideal) coupling.

Lab Exercise. Wind a 50-turn secondary coil around a solenoid of known geometry. Drive the primary with a function generator at $f = 1\,\text{kHz}$ and amplitude $I_0 = 0.10\,\text{A}$. Measure the peak secondary voltage with an oscilloscope and extract $M$ from $|\mathcal{E}_2| = M \omega I_0$. Compare your measured $M$ to the theoretical value calculated from the solenoid dimensions and turn counts, and estimate the coupling coefficient $k$.
