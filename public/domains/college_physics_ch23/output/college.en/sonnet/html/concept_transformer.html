🌸 Transformer

🌸 Transformer

Definition. A transformer is a device that transfers electrical energy between two or more circuits through electromagnetic induction, simultaneously stepping voltage up or down while (ideally) conserving power. It consists of a primary coil of $N_P$ turns and a secondary coil of $N_S$ turns wound on a common ferromagnetic core. When an alternating current drives the primary, the time-varying flux $\Phi_B$ threading the core induces an EMF in each turn. By Faraday's law, the induced EMF per turn is $-d\Phi_B/dt$, so the terminal voltages satisfy

$$ \frac{V_S}{V_P} = \frac{N_S}{N_P}. $$

This is the transformer equation. A ratio $N_S/N_P > 1$ is a step-up transformer; $N_S/N_P < 1$ is step-down.

Worked Example. A power plant outputs $V_P = 10\,\text{kV}$ (rms) at $P = 500\,\text{kW}$ and must transmit over long-distance lines to a neighborhood substation. A step-up transformer with $N_P = 100$ turns and $N_S = 2000$ turns is used.

Step 1 — secondary voltage:

$$ V_S = V_P \cdot \frac{N_S}{N_P} = 10{,}000 \times \frac{2000}{100} = 200\,\text{kV}. $$

Step 2 — transmission current: Assuming an ideal transformer ($P_S = P_P$),

$$ I_S = \frac{P}{V_S} = \frac{500 \times 10^3}{200 \times 10^3} = 2.5\,\text{A}. $$

Reducing current by a factor of 20 reduces resistive ($I^2 R$) line losses by a factor of 400, demonstrating why high-voltage transmission is economically essential.

Key Theorem — Power Conservation in an Ideal Transformer. For a lossless transformer with perfect flux linkage and negligible winding resistance,

$$ V_P I_P = V_S I_S \implies \frac{I_S}{I_P} = \frac{N_P}{N_S}. $$

Voltage and current transform inversely, so impedance scales as $(N_S/N_P)^2$: a load $Z_L$ on the secondary presents an effective impedance $Z_{\text{eff}} = (N_P/N_S)^2 Z_L$ to the primary circuit.

Lab Exercise. Wind a primary coil ($N_P = 50$ turns) and secondary coil ($N_S = 100$ turns) on a soft-iron toroid. Apply $V_P = 6\,\text{V}$ (rms, 60 Hz) to the primary. Measure $V_S$ with a multimeter and verify the ratio $V_S/V_P \approx 2$. Next, connect a $10\,\Omega$ resistive load to the secondary, measure $I_S$ and $I_P$, and confirm that $I_P/I_S \approx N_S/N_P = 2$. Compute input and output power; quantify transformer efficiency $\eta = P_S/P_P$.
