🍃 Self Inductance

🍃 Self Inductance

Definition. Self-inductance is the property of a circuit element—typically a coil or solenoid—by which a changing current through the element induces an electromotive force (EMF) in that same element. Quantitatively, if the current $i$ through a coil changes at rate $di/dt$, the self-induced EMF is

$$ \mathcal{E} = -L\frac{di}{dt}, $$

where $L$ is the self-inductance (or simply inductance) of the coil, measured in henries ($1\,\text{H} = 1\,\text{V\cdot s/A}$). The negative sign reflects Lenz's law: the induced EMF opposes the change in current that produced it. Equivalently, $L$ is defined through the flux linkage $N\Phi_B$ as

$$ L = \frac{N\Phi_B}{i}, $$

where $N$ is the number of turns and $\Phi_B$ is the magnetic flux through one turn.

Worked Example. Consider a toroidal solenoid with $N = 500$ turns, mean radius $R = 0.10\,\text{m}$, and circular cross-sectional area $A = 1.2 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2$. The magnetic field inside the toroid is $B = \mu_0 N i / (2\pi R)$. The flux through one turn is $\Phi_B = BA$, so the self-inductance is

$$ L = \frac{N\Phi_B}{i} = \frac{N \cdot \mu_0 N A}{2\pi R} = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{2\pi R}. $$

Substituting values:

$$ L = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(500)^2(1.2\times 10^{-3})}{2\pi(0.10)} = 6.0 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{H} = 0.60\,\text{mH}. $$

If the current drops from $2.0\,\text{A}$ to $0$ in $0.010\,\text{s}$, the magnitude of the self-induced EMF is $|\mathcal{E}| = L\,|\Delta i/\Delta t| = (6.0\times10^{-4})(200) = 0.12\,\text{V}$.

Key Theorem. The energy stored in an inductor carrying current $I$ is

$$ U_L = \frac{1}{2}LI^2. $$

This result follows by integrating the instantaneous power $p = \mathcal{E}\,i = Li\,di/dt$ from $i = 0$ to $i = I$. The energy resides in the magnetic field itself, with volume energy density $u = B^2/(2\mu_0)$.

Lab Exercise. Construct an RL series circuit with a known resistor $R$ and an inductor of unknown $L$. Apply a step voltage $V_0$ and record the current as a function of time. Fit the data to $i(t) = (V_0/R)(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$ to extract the time constant $\tau = L/R$, and hence determine $L$. Compare your result to the theoretical value computed from the coil geometry.
