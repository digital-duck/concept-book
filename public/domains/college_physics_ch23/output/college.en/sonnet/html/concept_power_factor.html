🍃 Power Factor

🍃 Power Factor

Definition. In an AC circuit driven by a sinusoidal voltage $v(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t)$, the current generally takes the form $i(t) = I_0 \cos(\omega t - \phi)$, where $\phi$ is the phase angle between voltage and current. The power factor is defined as

$$ \text{PF} = \cos\phi, $$

and it quantifies the fraction of apparent power that performs real (useful) work. The average power delivered to the circuit is

$$ P_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{2} V_0 I_0 \cos\phi = V_{\text{rms}} I_{\text{rms}} \cos\phi, $$

while the apparent power is $S = V_{\text{rms}} I_{\text{rms}}$. Thus $P_{\text{avg}} = S \cdot \text{PF}$. A purely resistive load has $\phi = 0$ and $\text{PF} = 1$; a purely reactive load has $|\phi| = 90°$ and $\text{PF} = 0$, consuming no average power despite carrying current.

Worked Example. A series RLC circuit has $R = 40\ \Omega$, $X_L = 70\ \Omega$, and $X_C = 40\ \Omega$, connected to a $120\ \text{V}_{\text{rms}}$ source. The net reactance is $X = X_L - X_C = 30\ \Omega$, giving impedance

$$ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + X^2} = \sqrt{1600 + 900} = 50\ \Omega. $$

The phase angle is $\phi = \arctan(X/R) = \arctan(0.75) \approx 36.9°$, so

$$ \text{PF} = \cos(36.9°) \approx 0.80. $$

The rms current is $I_{\text{rms}} = 120/50 = 2.4\ \text{A}$, yielding average power $P_{\text{avg}} = (120)(2.4)(0.80) = 230.4\ \text{W}$, while apparent power is $S = 288\ \text{VA}$.

Key Theorem. In a series RLC circuit, the power factor can be expressed directly in terms of circuit elements:

$$ \cos\phi = \frac{R}{Z} = \frac{R}{\sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2}}. $$

At resonance ($X_L = X_C$), $Z = R$ and $\text{PF} = 1$, maximizing power transfer for a given source voltage and current amplitude.

Lab Exercise. Connect a resistor ($R = 100\ \Omega$), inductor ($L = 50\ \text{mH}$), and capacitor ($C = 10\ \mu\text{F}$) in series to a function generator set at $f = 500\ \text{Hz}$. Measure $V_{\text{rms}}$ and $I_{\text{rms}}$ with a multimeter, and determine the phase angle $\phi$ using an oscilloscope. Compute $\text{PF} = \cos\phi$ and compare with the theoretical value from the formula above. Vary the frequency and record how the power factor changes as the circuit approaches and passes through resonance.
