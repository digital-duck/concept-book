🌱 Resistance

🌱 Resistance

Definition. The electrical resistance $R$ of a conductor quantifies its opposition to the flow of electric current. For a conductor carrying current $I$ under an applied potential difference $V$, resistance is defined by Ohm's Law:

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} $$

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm ($\Omega$), where $1\,\Omega = 1\,\text{V/A}$. At the microscopic level, resistance arises from collisions between conduction electrons and the ionic lattice of the material. For a uniform conductor of length $L$ and cross-sectional area $A$, resistance is related to the material's resistivity $\rho$ by:

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} $$

Resistivity $\rho$ (units: $\Omega\cdot\text{m}$) is an intrinsic material property that depends on temperature. For most metals, $\rho$ increases with temperature according to $\rho = \rho_0[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)]$, where $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistivity.

Worked Example. A nichrome wire used in a heating element has resistivity $\rho = 1.10 \times 10^{-6}\,\Omega\cdot\text{m}$ at $20^\circ\text{C}$. The wire is $2.00\,\text{m}$ long with a circular cross-section of diameter $d = 0.500\,\text{mm}$. Find its resistance.

The cross-sectional area is:

$$ A = \pi\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi\left(2.50 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}\right)^2 = 1.963 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{m}^2 $$

Applying the resistivity formula:

$$ R = \rho\frac{L}{A} = (1.10 \times 10^{-6})\frac{2.00}{1.963 \times 10^{-7}} \approx 11.2\,\Omega $$

Key Theorem: Power Dissipation. When current $I$ flows through a resistor $R$, electrical energy is converted to thermal energy (Joule heating) at the rate:

$$ P = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R} = IV $$

This result follows directly from $P = IV$ combined with Ohm's Law. Because power scales as $I^2$, even modest increases in current produce disproportionately large heating effects — a critical design constraint in electrical engineering.

Lab Exercise. Connect a resistor of unknown resistance in series with an ammeter and a variable DC power supply. Record the current $I$ for applied voltages $V \in \{2, 4, 6, 8, 10\}\,\text{V}$. Plot $V$ versus $I$ and determine $R$ from the slope. Compare your measured value against the color-band specification and calculate the percent error. Investigate whether the resistor's value drifts as it warms under sustained current.
