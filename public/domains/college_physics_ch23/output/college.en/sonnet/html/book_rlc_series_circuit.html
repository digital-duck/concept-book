🌱 Magnetic Field

🌱 Magnetic Field

Definition. A magnetic field is a vector field $\mathbf{B}$ that permeates space in the vicinity of moving electric charges or magnetic dipoles, exerting forces on other moving charges placed within it. The SI unit of magnetic field strength is the tesla (T), where $1\,\text{T} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{s}^{-2}\cdot\text{A}^{-1}$. The magnetic force on a point charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ is given by the Lorentz force law:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} $$

This force is always perpendicular to both $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, so a magnetic field does no work on a moving charge and cannot alter its kinetic energy—only its direction of motion.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, $m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$) moves with speed $v = 2.0 \times 10^6\,\text{m/s}$ in the $+x$-direction through a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\text{T}\,\hat{z}$.

The magnetic force is:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} = q(v\hat{x}) \times (B\hat{z}) = qvB(\hat{x} \times \hat{z}) = -qvB\,\hat{y} $$ $$ |\mathbf{F}| = (1.602\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^6)(0.50) \approx 1.60 \times 10^{-13}\,\text{N} $$

The force acts in the $-y$-direction, deflecting the proton into circular motion in the $xy$-plane.

Key Theorem — Radius of Circular Motion. When a charged particle moves perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field, the magnetic force provides the centripetal acceleration. Setting $|\mathbf{F}| = qvB$ equal to $mv^2/r$:

$$ r = \frac{mv}{|q|B} $$

This result, known as the cyclotron radius or Larmor radius, shows that the orbital radius is proportional to momentum and inversely proportional to field strength. For the proton above: $r = (1.673\times10^{-27})(2.0\times10^6)/[(1.602\times10^{-19})(0.50)] \approx 0.042\,\text{m} = 4.2\,\text{cm}$.

🍃 Magnetic Flux

🍃 Magnetic Flux

Definition. Magnetic flux quantifies the total magnetic field passing through a given surface. For a uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$ intersecting a flat surface of area $A$ whose outward normal makes an angle $\theta$ with $\vec{B}$, the magnetic flux is defined as

$$ \Phi_B = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A} = BA\cos\theta, $$

where $\vec{A} = A\hat{n}$ is the area vector. The SI unit of magnetic flux is the weber (Wb), where $1\,\text{Wb} = 1\,\text{T}\cdot\text{m}^2$. For a non-uniform field or curved surface, the definition generalizes to the surface integral

$$ \Phi_B = \iint_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A}. $$

The concept of flux captures the notion of "how much" of the field threads through the surface, accounting for both the field strength and the orientation of the surface relative to the field lines.

Worked Example. A rectangular loop of width $w = 0.30\,\text{m}$ and height $h = 0.20\,\text{m}$ lies in the $xy$-plane. A uniform magnetic field $\vec{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}\,\text{T}$ points in the $+z$-direction. Since the area vector of the loop is $\vec{A} = wh\,\hat{z} = (0.06\,\text{m}^2)\hat{z}$, the angle between $\vec{B}$ and $\hat{n}$ is $\theta = 0°$. Therefore,

$$ \Phi_B = BA\cos 0° = (0.50\,\text{T})(0.06\,\text{m}^2)(1) = 0.030\,\text{Wb}. $$

If the loop is tilted so that its normal makes $60°$ with $\vec{B}$, the flux reduces to $\Phi_B = (0.50)(0.06)\cos 60° = 0.015\,\text{Wb}$.

Key Theorem: Gauss's Law for Magnetism. For any closed surface $S$,

$$ \oint_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A} = 0. $$

This law reflects the non-existence of magnetic monopoles: every field line entering a closed surface must exit it. Unlike electric flux, which can be nonzero when net charge is enclosed, the net magnetic flux through any closed surface is identically zero.

🍃 Electromagnetic Induction

🍃 Electromagnetic Induction

Definition. Electromagnetic induction is the phenomenon by which a time-varying magnetic flux through a conducting loop produces an electromotive force (EMF) in that loop. Faraday's Law quantifies this relationship: the induced EMF is equal to the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through the loop,

$$ \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

where the magnetic flux is defined as $\Phi_B = \int \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$. The negative sign encodes Lenz's Law: the induced EMF drives a current whose magnetic field opposes the change in flux that caused it, a direct consequence of energy conservation.

For a coil of $N$ turns, the expression generalizes to

$$ \mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

since each turn contributes independently to the total EMF.

Worked Example. A circular loop of radius $r = 0.10\ \text{m}$ lies in the $xy$-plane. A uniform magnetic field directed along $\hat{z}$ increases linearly from $B_0 = 0.20\ \text{T}$ to $B_f = 0.80\ \text{T}$ over $\Delta t = 0.40\ \text{s}$. Find the magnitude of the induced EMF.

The area of the loop is $A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.10)^2 \approx 3.14 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{m}^2$. Since $\mathbf{B}$ is perpendicular to the loop, $\Phi_B = BA$. The rate of change of flux is

$$ \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = A\frac{\Delta B}{\Delta t} = (3.14 \times 10^{-2})\frac{0.80 - 0.20}{0.40} \approx 4.7 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{V} $$

Thus $|\mathcal{E}| \approx 47\ \text{mV}$. By Lenz's Law, the induced current flows in the direction that opposes the increasing upward flux, so it circulates clockwise when viewed from above.

Key Theorem. The integral form of Faraday's Law,

$$ \oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} $$

reveals that a time-varying magnetic field is inseparably linked to a circulating electric field, establishing the electromagnetic coupling that underlies generators, transformers, and Maxwell's displacement current formulation.

Verify that doubling the insertion speed doubles the peak current reading, and confirm that reversing the magnet's orientation reverses the current direction, as Lenz's Law predicts.

🌱 Electric Current

🌱 Electric Current

Definition. Electric current is the net rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional area of a conductor. If a charge $\Delta Q$ passes through a surface in a time interval $\Delta t$, the average current is

$$ I_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} $$

The instantaneous current is defined as the limit

$$ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} $$

The SI unit of current is the ampere (A), where $1\ \text{A} = 1\ \text{C/s}$. By convention, the direction of positive current flow is taken as the direction in which positive charges move, even though in metallic conductors the actual charge carriers are electrons moving in the opposite direction.

Worked Example. A copper wire carries a steady current of $2.50\ \text{A}$. Determine the total charge that passes through a cross-section of the wire in $4.00\ \text{min}$.

Given $I = 2.50\ \text{A}$ and $\Delta t = 4.00\ \text{min} = 240\ \text{s}$, rearranging the definition yields

$$ \Delta Q = I\,\Delta t = (2.50\ \text{A})(240\ \text{s}) = 600\ \text{C} $$

This corresponds to the passage of

$$ N = \frac{\Delta Q}{e} = \frac{600\ \text{C}}{1.602\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}} \approx 3.75\times10^{21}\ \text{electrons} $$

highlighting that even modest macroscopic currents involve an enormous number of charge carriers.

Key Theorem (Continuity of Current). For any steady-state (DC) circuit, the current entering any node equals the current leaving that node. Formally, this is Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL):

$$ \sum_{k} I_k = 0 $$

where currents directed into the node are positive and those directed away are negative. KCL is a direct consequence of conservation of charge: in steady state, charge cannot accumulate at any point in a conductor.

Next, insert a second identical resistor in series and repeat. 🍃 Faradays Law

🍃 Faradays Law

Definition

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction states that the electromotive force (EMF) induced in a closed loop is equal in magnitude and opposite in sign to the time rate of change of the magnetic flux through the loop:

$$ \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

where the magnetic flux is defined as:

$$ \Phi_B = \int \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int B \cos\theta \, dA $$

Here, $\theta$ is the angle between the magnetic field vector $\mathbf{B}$ and the area normal $d\mathbf{A}$. The negative sign reflects Lenz's Law: the induced EMF drives a current whose magnetic field opposes the change in flux that produced it.

Worked Example

Consider a rectangular loop of width $w = 0.20\ \text{m}$ and height $h = 0.10\ \text{m}$ lying in the $xy$-plane, within a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = B_0(t)\,\hat{z}$ where $B_0(t) = 4.0t\ \text{T}$ (with $t$ in seconds). The flux through the loop is:

$$ \Phi_B = B_0(t) \cdot A = (4.0t)(0.20)(0.10) = 0.080t\ \text{Wb} $$

Applying Faraday's Law:

$$ \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -\frac{d}{dt}(0.080t) = -0.080\ \text{V} $$

The induced EMF has magnitude $80\ \text{mV}$, constant in time since $B$ increases linearly. By Lenz's Law, the induced current flows in the direction that generates a magnetic field opposing the increasing $+\hat{z}$ flux — namely, in the $-\hat{z}$ direction inside the loop, requiring counterclockwise current when viewed from above.

Key Theorem

For a coil of $N$ turns, the law generalizes to:

$$ \mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

This is the operating principle behind electric generators, transformers, and inductors. In integral form, Faraday's Law is one of Maxwell's equations:

$$ \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = -\frac{d}{dt}\iint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} $$

establishing that a time-varying magnetic field induces a circulating electric field, even in free space.

🌱 Capacitance

🌱 Capacitance

Definition. A capacitor is a device that stores electric charge and, consequently, electrical energy. When a potential difference $V$ is established between two conducting surfaces separated by an insulating medium, charge accumulates: positive charge $+Q$ on one conductor and negative charge $-Q$ on the other. The capacitance $C$ of the device is defined as the ratio of the magnitude of charge stored on either conductor to the magnitude of the potential difference between them:

$$ C = \frac{Q}{V} $$

Capacitance is a purely geometric property — it depends on the shape, size, and separation of the conductors, as well as the permittivity of the intervening medium, but not on $Q$ or $V$ individually. The SI unit of capacitance is the farad (F), where $1\,\text{F} = 1\,\text{C/V}$. Practical devices range from picofarads ($\text{pF}$) to millifarads ($\text{mF}$).

Worked Example. Consider a parallel-plate capacitor with plate area $A = 0.020\,\text{m}^2$ and plate separation $d = 1.5\,\text{mm} = 1.5 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}$ in vacuum. The capacitance is given by:

$$ C = \varepsilon_0 \frac{A}{d} $$

where $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{F/m}$ is the permittivity of free space. Substituting:

$$ C = \frac{(8.85 \times 10^{-12})(0.020)}{1.5 \times 10^{-3}} = 1.18 \times 10^{-10}\,\text{F} \approx 118\,\text{pF} $$

If this capacitor is connected to a $9.0\,\text{V}$ battery, the stored charge is $Q = CV = (1.18 \times 10^{-10})(9.0) \approx 1.06\,\text{nC}$.

Key Theorem. For a parallel-plate capacitor, $C = \varepsilon_0 A/d$ follows directly from applying Gauss's law to a pillbox surface enclosing one plate, yielding $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0$ between the plates, and integrating: $V = Ed$. If the gap is filled with a dielectric of dielectric constant $\kappa$, the capacitance increases to $C = \kappa\varepsilon_0 A/d$, because the dielectric reduces the effective electric field, allowing more charge to be stored at the same potential.

🍃 Self Inductance

🍃 Self Inductance

Definition. Self-inductance is the property of a circuit element—typically a coil or solenoid—by which a changing current through the element induces an electromotive force (EMF) in that same element. Quantitatively, if the current $i$ through a coil changes at rate $di/dt$, the self-induced EMF is

$$ \mathcal{E} = -L\frac{di}{dt}, $$

where $L$ is the self-inductance (or simply inductance) of the coil, measured in henries ($1\,\text{H} = 1\,\text{V\cdot s/A}$). The negative sign reflects Lenz's law: the induced EMF opposes the change in current that produced it. Equivalently, $L$ is defined through the flux linkage $N\Phi_B$ as

$$ L = \frac{N\Phi_B}{i}, $$

where $N$ is the number of turns and $\Phi_B$ is the magnetic flux through one turn.

Worked Example. Consider a toroidal solenoid with $N = 500$ turns, mean radius $R = 0.10\,\text{m}$, and circular cross-sectional area $A = 1.2 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2$. The magnetic field inside the toroid is $B = \mu_0 N i / (2\pi R)$. The flux through one turn is $\Phi_B = BA$, so the self-inductance is

$$ L = \frac{N\Phi_B}{i} = \frac{N \cdot \mu_0 N A}{2\pi R} = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{2\pi R}. $$

Substituting values:

$$ L = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(500)^2(1.2\times 10^{-3})}{2\pi(0.10)} = 6.0 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{H} = 0.60\,\text{mH}. $$

If the current drops from $2.0\,\text{A}$ to $0$ in $0.010\,\text{s}$, the magnitude of the self-induced EMF is $|\mathcal{E}| = L\,|\Delta i/\Delta t| = (6.0\times10^{-4})(200) = 0.12\,\text{V}$.

Key Theorem. The energy stored in an inductor carrying current $I$ is

$$ U_L = \frac{1}{2}LI^2. $$

This result follows by integrating the instantaneous power $p = \mathcal{E}\,i = Li\,di/dt$ from $i = 0$ to $i = I$. The energy resides in the magnetic field itself, with volume energy density $u = B^2/(2\mu_0)$.

Lab Exercise. Construct an RL series circuit with a known resistor $R$ and an inductor of unknown $L$. Apply a step voltage $V_0$ and record the current as a function of time. Fit the data to $i(t) = (V_0/R)(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$ to extract the time constant $\tau = L/R$, and hence determine $L$. Compare your result to the theoretical value computed from the coil geometry.

🍃 Capacitive Reactance

🍃 Capacitive Reactance

Definition. Capacitive reactance, denoted $X_C$, quantifies the opposition a capacitor presents to alternating current. For a capacitor of capacitance $C$ driven by a sinusoidal source at angular frequency $\omega = 2\pi f$, the capacitive reactance is

$$ X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C} = \frac{1}{2\pi f C}. $$

The SI unit of $X_C$ is the ohm ($\Omega$). Unlike resistance, reactance does not dissipate energy; it stores and returns energy each cycle. Crucially, $X_C$ decreases as frequency increases: a capacitor passes high-frequency signals readily but impedes low-frequency and DC signals strongly (at $f = 0$, $X_C \to \infty$, confirming that a capacitor blocks steady current). The peak voltage and peak current across a capacitor are related by

$$ V_0 = I_0 X_C, $$

and the current leads the voltage by a phase angle of $90°$ ($\pi/2$ radians).

Worked Example. A $25\,\mu\text{F}$ capacitor is connected to a $120\,\text{V}$ (rms), $60\,\text{Hz}$ source. Find the capacitive reactance and the rms current.

$$ X_C = \frac{1}{2\pi (60)(25 \times 10^{-6})} = \frac{1}{9.42 \times 10^{-3}} \approx 106\,\Omega. $$

Applying the AC analogue of Ohm's law:

$$ I_{\text{rms}} = \frac{V_{\text{rms}}}{X_C} = \frac{120}{106} \approx 1.13\,\text{A}. $$

Key Theorem — Phase Relationship. If the applied voltage is $v(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t)$, then the charge on the capacitor is $q(t) = CV_0 \cos(\omega t)$, and the current is

$$ i(t) = \frac{dq}{dt} = -CV_0 \omega \sin(\omega t) = I_0 \cos\!\left(\omega t + \frac{\pi}{2}\right), $$

where $I_0 = V_0/X_C$. This confirms the $90°$ current-leads-voltage relationship, a defining signature of purely capacitive circuits and the basis for phasor analysis in AC circuit design.

Repeat at $500\,\text{Hz}$ and $2\,\text{kHz}$, and verify that $X_C$ scales inversely with frequency.

🍃 Inductive Reactance

🍃 Inductive Reactance

Definition. When an alternating current passes through an inductor, the inductor opposes changes in current through electromagnetic induction. This opposition, distinct from resistance, is called inductive reactance and is denoted $X_L$. Unlike resistance, inductive reactance does not dissipate energy; instead, it stores energy in the magnetic field during one half-cycle and returns it during the next. Inductive reactance is defined as

$$ X_L = \omega L = 2\pi f L, $$

where $\omega$ is the angular frequency of the AC source (in rad/s), $f$ is the ordinary frequency (in Hz), and $L$ is the inductance (in henries). The SI unit of $X_L$ is the ohm ($\Omega$). The peak voltage across an ideal inductor and the peak current through it are related by

$$ V_L = I X_L, $$

with the important phase consequence that the current lags the voltage by exactly $90°$ (or $\pi/2$ rad).

Worked Example. A pure inductor of $L = 0.150\ \text{H}$ is connected to a $120\ \text{V}$ (rms), $60.0\ \text{Hz}$ household source. Find the inductive reactance and the rms current.

$$ X_L = 2\pi (60.0\ \text{Hz})(0.150\ \text{H}) = 56.5\ \Omega. $$

Applying the AC version of Ohm's law for reactance:

$$ I_{\text{rms}} = \frac{V_{\text{rms}}}{X_L} = \frac{120\ \text{V}}{56.5\ \Omega} = 2.12\ \text{A}. $$

Note that doubling the frequency to $120\ \text{Hz}$ would double $X_L$ to $113\ \Omega$ and halve the current to $1.06\ \text{A}$, illustrating the direct proportionality between $X_L$ and $f$.

Key Theorem. For a sinusoidal source $v(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t)$, Faraday's law gives $v = L\,di/dt$. Integrating yields $i(t) = -\frac{V_0}{\omega L}\cos(\omega t) = \frac{V_0}{\omega L}\sin\!\left(\omega t - \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$, confirming the $90°$ current lag and establishing that the amplitude ratio $V_0/I_0 = \omega L = X_L$.

Repeat at $1000\ \text{Hz}$ and $2000\ \text{Hz}$, then plot $X_L$ versus $f$ to verify the linear relationship.

🌱 Resistance

🌱 Resistance

Definition. The electrical resistance $R$ of a conductor quantifies its opposition to the flow of electric current. For a conductor carrying current $I$ under an applied potential difference $V$, resistance is defined by Ohm's Law:

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} $$

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm ($\Omega$), where $1\,\Omega = 1\,\text{V/A}$. At the microscopic level, resistance arises from collisions between conduction electrons and the ionic lattice of the material. For a uniform conductor of length $L$ and cross-sectional area $A$, resistance is related to the material's resistivity $\rho$ by:

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} $$

Resistivity $\rho$ (units: $\Omega\cdot\text{m}$) is an intrinsic material property that depends on temperature. For most metals, $\rho$ increases with temperature according to $\rho = \rho_0[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)]$, where $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistivity.

Worked Example. A nichrome wire used in a heating element has resistivity $\rho = 1.10 \times 10^{-6}\,\Omega\cdot\text{m}$ at $20^\circ\text{C}$. The wire is $2.00\,\text{m}$ long with a circular cross-section of diameter $d = 0.500\,\text{mm}$. Find its resistance.

The cross-sectional area is:

$$ A = \pi\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi\left(2.50 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}\right)^2 = 1.963 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{m}^2 $$

Applying the resistivity formula:

$$ R = \rho\frac{L}{A} = (1.10 \times 10^{-6})\frac{2.00}{1.963 \times 10^{-7}} \approx 11.2\,\Omega $$

Key Theorem: Power Dissipation. When current $I$ flows through a resistor $R$, electrical energy is converted to thermal energy (Joule heating) at the rate:

$$ P = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R} = IV $$

This result follows directly from $P = IV$ combined with Ohm's Law. Because power scales as $I^2$, even modest increases in current produce disproportionately large heating effects — a critical design constraint in electrical engineering.

🍃 Impedance

🍃 Impedance

Definition. In an AC circuit, impedance $Z$ is the complex-valued generalization of resistance that accounts for the combined opposition to alternating current from resistors, inductors, and capacitors. For a series RLC circuit driven at angular frequency $\omega$, the impedance is defined as

$$ Z = R + j\left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right), $$

where $R$ is resistance (in ohms), $L$ is inductance (in henries), $C$ is capacitance (in farads), and $j = \sqrt{-1}$. The real part $R$ represents dissipative opposition; the imaginary part $X = \omega L - 1/(\omega C)$ is the reactance. The magnitude of impedance is

$$ |Z| = \sqrt{R^2 + X^2} = \sqrt{R^2 + \left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right)^2}, $$

and the phase angle $\phi$ between voltage and current satisfies $\tan\phi = X/R$. By Ohm's law generalized to AC, the peak voltage and peak current are related by $V_0 = |Z|\, I_0$.

Worked Example. A series RLC circuit has $R = 50\,\Omega$, $L = 0.20\,\text{H}$, and $C = 10\,\mu\text{F}$, driven at $f = 200\,\text{Hz}$ (so $\omega = 2\pi \times 200 \approx 1257\,\text{rad/s}$). Compute:

$$ \omega L = 1257 \times 0.20 = 251.3\,\Omega, \qquad \frac{1}{\omega C} = \frac{1}{1257 \times 10^{-5}} \approx 79.6\,\Omega. $$

Thus $X = 251.3 - 79.6 = 171.7\,\Omega$, and

$$ |Z| = \sqrt{50^2 + 171.7^2} = \sqrt{2500 + 29\,481} \approx 178.5\,\Omega. $$

If the source peak voltage is $V_0 = 120\,\text{V}$, then $I_0 = 120/178.5 \approx 0.672\,\text{A}$.

Key Theorem (Resonance Condition). The impedance magnitude $|Z|$ is minimized — and current is maximized — when $X = 0$, i.e., when

$$ \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}. $$

At resonance, $|Z| = R$, the circuit behaves as a purely resistive load, and voltage and current are in phase ($\phi = 0$).

🍃 Phase Angle

🍃 Phase Angle

Definition. In the analysis of alternating-current (AC) circuits, the phase angle $\phi$ is the angular difference between the voltage phasor and the current phasor at a given frequency. For a series RLC circuit driven by a sinusoidal source $v(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t)$, the steady-state current takes the form $i(t) = I_0 \cos(\omega t - \phi)$, where

$$ \tan \phi = \frac{X_L - X_C}{R} = \frac{\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}}{R}. $$

Here $X_L = \omega L$ is the inductive reactance, $X_C = 1/(\omega C)$ is the capacitive reactance, and $R$ is the resistance. The phase angle lies in the range $-\pi/2 \leq \phi \leq \pi/2$; a positive $\phi$ indicates an inductive circuit (current lags voltage), while a negative $\phi$ indicates a capacitive circuit (current leads voltage).

Worked Example. A series RLC circuit has $R = 50\,\Omega$, $L = 0.20\,\text{H}$, and $C = 10\,\mu\text{F}$, driven at $f = 200\,\text{Hz}$.

Compute the reactances:

$$ X_L = 2\pi (200)(0.20) \approx 251.3\,\Omega, \qquad X_C = \frac{1}{2\pi (200)(10 \times 10^{-6})} \approx 79.6\,\Omega. $$

Then:

$$ \tan \phi = \frac{251.3 - 79.6}{50} = \frac{171.7}{50} \approx 3.43 \implies \phi \approx \arctan(3.43) \approx 73.7°. $$

The circuit is strongly inductive; the current lags the voltage by nearly $74°$.

Key Theorem. The average power delivered to the circuit is

$$ \langle P \rangle = \frac{1}{2} V_0 I_0 \cos \phi = I_{\text{rms}}^2 R, $$

where $\cos \phi$ is called the power factor. This follows from expanding $v(t)\,i(t)$ and time-averaging over one period; only the resistive term survives. At resonance ($X_L = X_C$), $\phi = 0$ and the power factor is unity, so maximum power is transferred.

🍃 Power Factor

🍃 Power Factor

Definition. In an AC circuit driven by a sinusoidal voltage $v(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t)$, the current generally takes the form $i(t) = I_0 \cos(\omega t - \phi)$, where $\phi$ is the phase angle between voltage and current. The power factor is defined as

$$ \text{PF} = \cos\phi, $$

and it quantifies the fraction of apparent power that performs real (useful) work. The average power delivered to the circuit is

$$ P_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{2} V_0 I_0 \cos\phi = V_{\text{rms}} I_{\text{rms}} \cos\phi, $$

while the apparent power is $S = V_{\text{rms}} I_{\text{rms}}$. Thus $P_{\text{avg}} = S \cdot \text{PF}$. A purely resistive load has $\phi = 0$ and $\text{PF} = 1$; a purely reactive load has $|\phi| = 90°$ and $\text{PF} = 0$, consuming no average power despite carrying current.

Worked Example. A series RLC circuit has $R = 40\ \Omega$, $X_L = 70\ \Omega$, and $X_C = 40\ \Omega$, connected to a $120\ \text{V}_{\text{rms}}$ source. The net reactance is $X = X_L - X_C = 30\ \Omega$, giving impedance

$$ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + X^2} = \sqrt{1600 + 900} = 50\ \Omega. $$

The phase angle is $\phi = \arctan(X/R) = \arctan(0.75) \approx 36.9°$, so

$$ \text{PF} = \cos(36.9°) \approx 0.80. $$

The rms current is $I_{\text{rms}} = 120/50 = 2.4\ \text{A}$, yielding average power $P_{\text{avg}} = (120)(2.4)(0.80) = 230.4\ \text{W}$, while apparent power is $S = 288\ \text{VA}$.

Key Theorem. In a series RLC circuit, the power factor can be expressed directly in terms of circuit elements:

$$ \cos\phi = \frac{R}{Z} = \frac{R}{\sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2}}. $$

At resonance ($X_L = X_C$), $Z = R$ and $\text{PF} = 1$, maximizing power transfer for a given source voltage and current amplitude.

🍃 Resonant Frequency

🍃 Resonant Frequency

Definition. In an RLC series circuit driven by a sinusoidal voltage source, the resonant frequency $f_0$ is the driving frequency at which the inductive reactance exactly cancels the capacitive reactance. Because the net reactance vanishes, the circuit impedance reduces to its minimum possible value — the pure resistance $R$ — and the current amplitude reaches its maximum. Quantitatively, the condition $X_L = X_C$ gives

$$ \omega_0 L = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C} \implies \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}, $$

where $\omega_0 = 2\pi f_0$ is the angular resonant frequency, $L$ is inductance in henries, and $C$ is capacitance in farads. Equivalently,

$$ f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}. $$

At resonance the phase angle between voltage and current is zero, so the source delivers power to the resistor with maximum efficiency and the circuit behaves as purely resistive.

Worked Example. A series RLC circuit contains $R = 50\,\Omega$, $L = 20\,\text{mH}$, and $C = 5\,\mu\text{F}$. Find the resonant frequency and the peak current when the source delivers $V_{\text{rms}} = 120\,\text{V}$.

$$ f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{(0.020\,\text{H})(5\times10^{-6}\,\text{F})}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{1.0\times10^{-7}}} \approx \frac{1}{2\pi(3.16\times10^{-4})} \approx 503\,\text{Hz}. $$

At resonance, $Z = R = 50\,\Omega$, so the rms current is

$$ I_{\text{rms}} = \frac{V_{\text{rms}}}{R} = \frac{120}{50} = 2.4\,\text{A}, $$

and the average power dissipated is $P = I_{\text{rms}}^2 R = (2.4)^2(50) = 288\,\text{W}$.

Key Theorem — Bandwidth and Quality Factor. The sharpness of the resonance peak is characterized by the dimensionless quality factor

$$ Q = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}}. $$

The half-power bandwidth satisfies $\Delta\omega = \omega_0/Q$, so a large $Q$ produces a narrow, selective resonance. This principle underlies radio tuning circuits, where $L$ or $C$ is varied to bring $f_0$ to the desired station frequency.

🌸 Rlc Series Circuit

🌸 Rlc Series Circuit

An RLC series circuit consists of a resistor ($R$), an inductor ($L$), and a capacitor ($C$) connected in series with an AC voltage source of the form $v(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t)$. The instantaneous current $i(t)$ is the same through all elements, while the voltages across each element differ in phase. The resistive voltage $v_R = iR$ is in phase with $i$; the inductive voltage $v_L = L\,di/dt$ leads $i$ by $90°$; and the capacitive voltage $v_C = q/C$ lags $i$ by $90°$. Using phasor analysis, the total impedance of the circuit is

$$ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} $$

where $X_L = \omega L$ is the inductive reactance and $X_C = 1/(\omega C)$ is the capacitive reactance. The phase angle between the source voltage and the current is

$$ \phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{X_L - X_C}{R}\right). $$

Worked Example. Consider an RLC series circuit with $R = 50\,\Omega$, $L = 0.20\,\text{H}$, $C = 5.0\,\mu\text{F}$, driven at $f = 200\,\text{Hz}$ ($\omega = 2\pi \times 200 \approx 1257\,\text{rad/s}$). Compute:

$$ X_L = (1257)(0.20) \approx 251\,\Omega, \qquad X_C = \frac{1}{(1257)(5.0\times10^{-6})} \approx 159\,\Omega. $$

Thus $Z = \sqrt{50^2 + (251-159)^2} = \sqrt{2500 + 8464} \approx 105\,\Omega$. With a peak source voltage $V_0 = 120\,\text{V}$, the peak current is $I_0 = V_0/Z \approx 1.14\,\text{A}$, and the circuit is net inductive since $X_L > X_C$.

Key Theorem — Resonance. The current amplitude is maximized when $X_L = X_C$, yielding the resonant frequency

$$ \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}. $$

At resonance $Z = R$ (its minimum value), $\phi = 0$, and the source and current are in phase. The quality factor $Q = \omega_0 L / R$ quantifies the sharpness of the resonance peak.

Payoff 🎯

An RLC series circuit is a single-loop circuit containing a resistor of resistance $R$, an inductor of inductance $L$, and a capacitor of capacitance $C$ connected in series and driven by a sinusoidal voltage source $\mathcal{E}(t) = \mathcal{E}_0 \sin(\omega t)$. The governing equation follows directly from Kirchhoff's voltage law:

$$ L\frac{dI}{dt} + RI + \frac{q}{C} = \mathcal{E}_0 \sin(\omega t) $$

This is a driven, damped harmonic oscillator — the same differential structure that appears throughout classical physics — making the RLC circuit the natural culminating object of Chapter 23. Its solution yields the steady-state current amplitude

$$ I_0 = \frac{\mathcal{E}_0}{\sqrt{R^2 + \left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right)^2}} $$

where the denominator defines the impedance $Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2}$, with inductive reactance $X_L = \omega L$ and capacitive reactance $X_C = 1/(\omega C)$.

Worked example. Consider a circuit with $R = 50\ \Omega$, $L = 0.10\ \text{H}$, $C = 10\ \mu\text{F}$, and $\mathcal{E}_0 = 120\ \text{V}$ driven at $\omega = 1000\ \text{rad/s}$. Then $X_L = 100\ \Omega$, $X_C = 100\ \Omega$, and $Z = \sqrt{50^2 + 0^2} = 50\ \Omega$. The current amplitude reaches its maximum value $I_0 = 2.4\ \text{A}$. This condition, $\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}$, is resonance: the circuit draws maximum power from the source and the phase angle $\phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{X_L - X_C}{R}\right)$ vanishes, so current and voltage are perfectly in phase.

Key theorem. At resonance the average power dissipated is $\bar{P} = \frac{1}{2}I_0^2 R$, the maximum attainable for a given source amplitude. The sharpness of the resonance peak is quantified by the quality factor $Q = \omega_0 L / R$; a high-$Q$ circuit responds selectively to a narrow band of frequencies, a property fundamental to signal filtering and frequency selection.

The RLC circuit is the endpoint of this chapter because it unifies resistive dissipation, inductive energy storage, and capacitive energy storage into a single frequency-dependent response function. Every AC phenomenon studied earlier — phase shifts, reactance, impedance — converges here into one coherent framework.

This framework directly enables radio and wireless communication (antenna circuits tuned to $\omega_0$ select one broadcast frequency from a superposition of many), medical imaging (MRI scanner gradient coils are driven RLC systems whose resonant frequencies encode spatial information), and power-grid engineering (reactive power compensation exploits the $X_L = X_C$ condition to maximize transmission efficiency).

To see how deeply resonance shapes the modern world, follow the thread into radio-frequency circuit design: there, the quality factor $Q$ governs bandwidth, selectivity, and signal-to-noise ratio in every receiver ever built.
