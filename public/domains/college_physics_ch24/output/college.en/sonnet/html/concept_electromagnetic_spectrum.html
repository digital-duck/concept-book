🍃 Electromagnetic Spectrum

🍃 Electromagnetic Spectrum

Definition. The electromagnetic spectrum is the complete, ordered continuum of all electromagnetic radiation, classified by frequency $f$ (or equivalently, wavelength $\lambda$) according to the relation

$$ c = f\lambda, $$

where $c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$ is the speed of light in vacuum. All electromagnetic waves are transverse waves consisting of mutually perpendicular, oscillating electric and magnetic fields propagating through space without a material medium. The spectrum spans more than 24 orders of magnitude in frequency, from radio waves ($f \sim 10^3\ \text{Hz}$) through microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, to gamma rays ($f > 10^{20}\ \text{Hz}$). The energy carried per photon is governed by the Planck relation:

$$ E = hf, $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant. Higher frequency therefore corresponds to greater photon energy.

Worked Example. A microwave oven operates at $f = 2.45\ \text{GHz}$. Determine the wavelength and photon energy of its radiation.

Wavelength:

$$ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}{2.45 \times 10^9\ \text{Hz}} \approx 0.122\ \text{m} = 12.2\ \text{cm}. $$

Photon energy:

$$ E = hf = (6.626 \times 10^{-34})(2.45 \times 10^9) \approx 1.62 \times 10^{-24}\ \text{J} \approx 1.01 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{eV}. $$

This energy is far too small to ionize molecules, which is why microwave radiation heats food through molecular rotation rather than chemical bond disruption.

Key Theorem (Maxwell's Unification). Maxwell's equations predict that any accelerating charge radiates electromagnetic energy at speed $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}$, where $\mu_0$ and $\varepsilon_0$ are the permeability and permittivity of free space. This result demonstrates that visible light, radio waves, and all other electromagnetic radiation are manifestations of the same underlying phenomenon, differing only in frequency.

Lab Exercise. Using a diffraction grating with $d = 600\ \text{lines/mm}$, a student observes a first-order maximum ($m = 1$) for a sodium lamp at $\theta = 20.5°$. Apply $d\sin\theta = m\lambda$ to determine the wavelength and identify the corresponding spectral region of the electromagnetic spectrum.
