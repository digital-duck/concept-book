🌱 Permeability Of Free Space

🌱 Permeability Of Free Space

Definition

The permeability of free space, denoted $\mu_0$, is a fundamental physical constant that quantifies the ability of a vacuum to support the formation of a magnetic field. It appears in Ampère's law and the Biot–Savart law, serving as the proportionality constant relating magnetic field strength to the currents that produce it. Its defined (exact) value in SI units is:

$$ \mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A} \approx 1.2566 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{T·m/A} $$

Physically, $\mu_0$ sets the scale of magnetic interactions in vacuum. Because electromagnetic waves propagate at the speed of light $c$, this constant is connected to other well-established electromagnetic quantities through $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}$, where $\varepsilon_0$ is the permittivity of free space — a constant treated as already known from electrostatics.

Worked Example

Consider a long, straight wire carrying a steady current $I = 5.0 \, \text{A}$. Find the magnetic field magnitude at a perpendicular distance $r = 0.02 \, \text{m}$ from the wire.

Using the result derived from Ampère's law for an infinite straight conductor:

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$

Substituting values:

$$ B = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A})(5.0 \, \text{A})}{2\pi (0.02 \, \text{m})} = \frac{2\pi \times 10^{-6}}{2\pi \times 0.02} = 5.0 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{T} $$

The field at $2 \, \text{cm}$ from the wire is $50 \, \mu\text{T}$, directed in circles around the wire according to the right-hand rule.

Key Theorem

Ampère's law in integral form states:

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} $$

where the line integral is taken over any closed Amperian loop enclosing a net current $I_{\text{enc}}$. This law, analogous to Gauss's law for electric fields, reveals that $\mu_0$ fundamentally governs how much magnetic circulation a given enclosed current produces. The constant also appears in the energy density stored in a magnetic field: $u = B^2 / (2\mu_0)$.

Lab Exercise

Wind a solenoid with $n = 500$ turns per meter and pass a current of $I = 2.0 \, \text{A}$ through it. Using the relation $B = \mu_0 n I$, predict the interior magnetic field magnitude. Then measure $B$ using a Hall-effect probe placed at the solenoid's center and compare your experimental result with the theoretical prediction. Calculate the percent discrepancy and identify possible sources of systematic error, such as finite solenoid length and winding irregularities.
