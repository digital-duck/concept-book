🌱 Amperes Law

🌱 Ampère's Law

Definition. Ampère's Law relates the line integral of the magnetic field around a closed loop to the total electric current passing through any surface bounded by that loop. This section introduces one new primitive: the Amperian loop, a closed mathematical curve chosen to exploit the symmetry of a current configuration. In SI units, the law states:

$$ \oint_C \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} $$

where $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field, $d\vec{\ell}$ is an infinitesimal element of the Amperian loop $C$, $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space, and $I_{\text{enc}}$ is the net current threading through the surface bounded by $C$. The sign of $I_{\text{enc}}$ is determined by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of traversal around the loop; the thumb points in the positive current direction.

Worked Example. Consider a long, straight wire carrying a steady current $I$ along its axis. By the cylindrical symmetry of the configuration, $\vec{B}$ must be tangential and constant in magnitude at any fixed radius $r$ from the wire. Choose an Amperian loop that is a circle of radius $r$ centered on the wire and lying in a plane perpendicular to it. Then $\vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = B\,d\ell$ at every point, so:

$$ \oint_C \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{\ell} = B \oint_C d\ell = B(2\pi r) $$

Since the only current enclosed is $I$, Ampère's Law gives:

$$ B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I \implies B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$

This recovers the Biot–Savart result without performing a vector integration over the source distribution.

Key Theorem. For any static current distribution, Ampère's Law in differential form follows from taking the curl of $\vec{B}$:

$$ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} $$

where $\vec{J}$ is the current density. This is one of the four Maxwell equations in the magnetostatic limit and encodes the local relationship between the magnetic field and its source, placing Ampère's Law within the broader framework of classical electromagnetism.

Apply Ampère's Law to derive the interior field $B = \mu_0 n I$, compute its numerical value, and verify the result with a Hall-effect probe placed at the solenoid's center.

🌱 Electric Field

🌱 Electric Field

Definition. The electric field $\mathbf{E}$ at a point in space is defined as the force per unit positive test charge placed at that point:

$$ \mathbf{E} = \lim_{q_0 \to 0} \frac{\mathbf{F}}{q_0} $$

where $\mathbf{F}$ is the electrostatic force experienced by a small positive test charge $q_0$. The SI unit of electric field is newtons per coulomb ($\text{N/C}$), equivalently volts per meter ($\text{V/m}$). The electric field is a vector field — it has both magnitude and direction at every point in space, with direction defined as the direction a positive test charge would accelerate. For a point charge $Q$, Coulomb's law yields the field:

$$ \mathbf{E} = k_e \frac{Q}{r^2} \hat{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9\ \text{N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$ is Coulomb's constant, $r$ is the distance from $Q$, and $\hat{r}$ is the unit vector pointing away from $Q$. The field points radially outward for positive $Q$ and inward for negative $Q$.

Worked Example. Two point charges are placed on the $x$-axis: $Q_1 = +3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ at $x = 0$ and $Q_2 = -3.0\ \mu\text{C}$ at $x = 4.0\ \text{cm}$. Find $\mathbf{E}$ at the midpoint $P$ located at $x = 2.0\ \text{cm}$.

The distance from each charge to $P$ is $r = 0.020\ \text{m}$. The magnitude of each contribution is:

$$ E = k_e \frac{|Q|}{r^2} = (8.99\times10^9)\frac{3.0\times10^{-6}}{(0.020)^2} \approx 6.74\times10^7\ \text{N/C} $$

$Q_1$ pushes a positive test charge to the right ($+\hat{x}$); $Q_2$ pulls it to the right ($+\hat{x}$). Both contributions point in the same direction, so:

$$ \mathbf{E}_{\text{net}} = 2\,(6.74\times10^7)\,\hat{x} \approx 1.35\times10^8\ \hat{x}\ \text{N/C} $$

Key Theorem — Superposition Principle. For a collection of $n$ discrete charges, the total electric field is the vector sum of individual contributions:

$$ \mathbf{E}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} k_e \frac{Q_i}{r_i^2}\hat{r}_i $$

This linearity follows directly from the linearity of Coulomb's law and is foundational to all electrostatics.

🌱 Magnetic Field

🌱 Magnetic Field

Definition. A magnetic field is a vector field $\mathbf{B}$ that permeates space in the vicinity of moving charges, electric currents, and magnetic materials. It is defined operationally by the force it exerts on a moving test charge $q$ with velocity $\mathbf{v}$:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} $$

This is the magnetic component of the Lorentz force. The SI unit of magnetic field strength is the tesla (T), where $1\,\text{T} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{s}^{-2}\cdot\text{A}^{-1}$. The direction of $\mathbf{B}$ at any point is tangent to the magnetic field lines passing through that point, with the lines emerging from north poles and entering south poles by convention.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$, $m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$) moves with velocity $\mathbf{v} = 3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}\,\text{m/s}$ through a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = 0.50\,\hat{z}\,\text{T}$. Find the magnitude and direction of the magnetic force.

$$ \mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} = (1.60 \times 10^{-19})(3.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}) \times (0.50\,\hat{z}) $$

Since $\hat{x} \times \hat{z} = -\hat{y}$:

$$ \mathbf{F} = (1.60 \times 10^{-19})(1.5 \times 10^6)(-\hat{y}) = -2.4 \times 10^{-13}\,\hat{y}\,\text{N} $$

The force is $2.4 \times 10^{-13}\,\text{N}$ directed in the $-y$ direction. Because the magnetic force is always perpendicular to $\mathbf{v}$, it does no work and changes the direction of the proton's motion but not its speed.

Key Theorem — Biot–Savart Law. The magnetic field produced by a steady current element $I\,d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ at a displacement $\mathbf{r}$ from the element is:

$$ d\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{I\,d\boldsymbol{\ell} \times \hat{r}}{r^2} $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\text{T}\cdot\text{m}\cdot\text{A}^{-1}$ is the permeability of free space. Integrating over a complete circuit recovers the full field configuration for any current geometry.

Using a calibrated Hall-effect probe, measure $|\mathbf{B}|$ at perpendicular distances $r = 1,\,2,\,4,\,8\,\text{cm}$ from the wire. Plot $|\mathbf{B}|$ versus $1/r$ and verify the linear relationship predicted by Ampère's law: $B = \mu_0 I / (2\pi r)$. Determine $\mu_0$ from the slope and compare to the accepted value.

🌱 Permeability Of Free Space

🌱 Permeability Of Free Space

Definition

The permeability of free space, denoted $\mu_0$, is a fundamental physical constant that quantifies the ability of a vacuum to support the formation of a magnetic field. It appears in Ampère's law and the Biot–Savart law, serving as the proportionality constant relating magnetic field strength to the currents that produce it. Its defined (exact) value in SI units is:

$$ \mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A} \approx 1.2566 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{T·m/A} $$

Physically, $\mu_0$ sets the scale of magnetic interactions in vacuum. Because electromagnetic waves propagate at the speed of light $c$, this constant is connected to other well-established electromagnetic quantities through $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}$, where $\varepsilon_0$ is the permittivity of free space — a constant treated as already known from electrostatics.

Worked Example

Consider a long, straight wire carrying a steady current $I = 5.0 \, \text{A}$. Find the magnetic field magnitude at a perpendicular distance $r = 0.02 \, \text{m}$ from the wire.

Using the result derived from Ampère's law for an infinite straight conductor:

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$

Substituting values:

$$ B = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T·m/A})(5.0 \, \text{A})}{2\pi (0.02 \, \text{m})} = \frac{2\pi \times 10^{-6}}{2\pi \times 0.02} = 5.0 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{T} $$

The field at $2 \, \text{cm}$ from the wire is $50 \, \mu\text{T}$, directed in circles around the wire according to the right-hand rule.

Key Theorem

Ampère's law in integral form states:

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} $$

where the line integral is taken over any closed Amperian loop enclosing a net current $I_{\text{enc}}$. This law, analogous to Gauss's law for electric fields, reveals that $\mu_0$ fundamentally governs how much magnetic circulation a given enclosed current produces. The constant also appears in the energy density stored in a magnetic field: $u = B^2 / (2\mu_0)$.

Then measure $B$ using a Hall-effect probe placed at the solenoid's center and compare your experimental result with the theoretical prediction. Calculate the percent discrepancy and identify possible sources of systematic error, such as finite solenoid length and winding irregularities.

🌱 Permittivity Of Free Space

🌱 Permittivity Of Free Space

Definition. The permittivity of free space, denoted $\varepsilon_0$, is a fundamental physical constant that quantifies the ability of a vacuum to permit (support) electric field lines. It appears in Coulomb's law and throughout electromagnetic theory as the proportionality constant relating electric fields to their source charges in the absence of any material medium. Its accepted value is

$$ \varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \mathrm{C^2 \, N^{-1} \, m^{-2}} $$

or equivalently $8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \mathrm{F \, m^{-1}}$ (farads per meter). It enters Coulomb's law as

$$ F = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} $$

where the factor $\frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \approx 8.988 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N \, m^2 \, C^{-2}}$ is often written as $k_e$, Coulomb's constant. The physical meaning of $\varepsilon_0$ is subtle: a larger permittivity would weaken the electric force between charges, since the vacuum itself would more readily "accommodate" the field.

Worked Example. Two point charges $q_1 = +3.0 \, \mu\mathrm{C}$ and $q_2 = -5.0 \, \mu\mathrm{C}$ are separated by $r = 0.20 \, \mathrm{m}$ in vacuum. Find the magnitude of the electrostatic force.

$$ F = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{|q_1||q_2|}{r^2} = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{-6})(5.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} $$ $$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{1.5 \times 10^{-11}}{0.04} = (8.988 \times 10^9)(3.75 \times 10^{-10}) \approx 3.4 \, \mathrm{N} $$

The force is attractive because the charges have opposite signs.

Key Theorem. Gauss's law, the cornerstone of electrostatics, is expressed directly in terms of $\varepsilon_0$:

$$ \oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_\mathrm{enc}}{\varepsilon_0} $$

This states that the total electric flux through any closed surface equals the net enclosed charge divided by $\varepsilon_0$. The constant thus sets the absolute scale of electric fields produced by a given charge distribution in vacuum.

Lab Exercise. Using a parallel-plate capacitor with plate area $A = 0.050 \, \mathrm{m^2}$ and separation $d = 1.0 \, \mathrm{mm}$, measure the capacitance $C$ with an LCR meter. Verify that $C = \varepsilon_0 A / d$ and extract an experimental value of $\varepsilon_0$. Compare your result to the accepted value and compute the percent error, discussing sources of systematic uncertainty such as fringe fields at plate edges.

🍃 Changing Electric Field Creates Magnetic Field

🍃 Changing Electric Field Creates Magnetic Field

Definition. Maxwell's modification of Ampère's Law introduces the displacement current $I_d \equiv \varepsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$, which demonstrates that a time-varying electric field acts as a source of magnetic field, entirely analogous to a conduction current. The generalized Ampère–Maxwell Law states:

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 \left( I_{\text{enc}} + \varepsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} \right) $$

where $I_{\text{enc}}$ is the enclosed conduction current, $\varepsilon_0$ is the permittivity of free space, and $\frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$ is the rate of change of electric flux through the chosen Amperian surface. Crucially, $I_d$ produces a magnetic field even in regions devoid of charge flow — no physical current is required.

Worked Example. Consider a parallel-plate capacitor with circular plates of radius $R = 0.05\,\text{m}$ being charged so that the uniform electric field between the plates increases at a rate $\frac{dE}{dt} = 1.0 \times 10^{12}\,\text{V/m/s}$. Find the magnetic field at a radial distance $r = 0.03\,\text{m}$ from the axis (inside the capacitor region).

The electric flux through a circular Amperian loop of radius $r < R$ is:

$$ \Phi_E = E \cdot \pi r^2 $$

The displacement current enclosed:

$$ I_d = \varepsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} = \varepsilon_0 \pi r^2 \frac{dE}{dt} = (8.85 \times 10^{-12})(\pi)(0.03)^2(1.0 \times 10^{12}) \approx 2.50 \times 10^{-2}\,\text{A} $$

Applying the Ampère–Maxwell Law with no conduction current ($I_{\text{enc}} = 0$):

$$ B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I_d \implies B = \frac{\mu_0 I_d}{2\pi r} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(2.50 \times 10^{-2})}{2\pi(0.03)} \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{T} $$

Key Theorem. Maxwell's extension of Ampère's Law, combined with Faraday's Law, establishes the self-sustaining electromagnetic wave: a changing electric field generates a magnetic field, which in turn generates a changing electric field, propagating through vacuum at speed $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}}$. The displacement current is thus the single primitive concept introduced here, and it is sufficient to complete Maxwell's equations and predict electromagnetic radiation.

Lab Exercise. A parallel-plate capacitor with circular plates ($R = 0.10\,\text{m}$) is charged so that $\frac{dE}{dt} = 5.0 \times 10^{11}\,\text{V/m/s}$. Calculate the displacement current enclosed within a circular Amperian loop of radius $r = 0.04\,\text{m}$, and determine the resulting magnetic field magnitude at that radius. Verify that your result scales linearly with $r$ inside the plates, and compare this spatial dependence to the field outside the plates ($r > R$), where the enclosed displacement current no longer grows with loop size.

🌱 Faradays Law

🌱 Faraday's Law

Definition. Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction states that the electromotive force (EMF) induced in a closed conducting loop equals the negative time rate of change of the magnetic flux through the loop:

$$ \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

where $\Phi_B = \int \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$ is the magnetic flux, assumed to be prior knowledge from the preceding chapter on static fields. The negative sign has a physical consequence: the induced EMF drives a current whose own magnetic field opposes the flux change that produced it. This opposition is known as Lenz's Law and follows directly from the negative sign rather than standing as a separate postulate. For a coil of $N$ turns, each contributing the same flux linkage, the law becomes

$$ \mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}. $$

Worked Example. A circular loop of radius $r = 0.10\ \text{m}$ lies in the $xy$-plane. A uniform magnetic field directed along $+z$ increases linearly from $B_0 = 0.20\ \text{T}$ to $B_f = 0.80\ \text{T}$ over $\Delta t = 0.40\ \text{s}$. Find the induced EMF.

The loop area is $A = \pi r^2 \approx 3.14 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{m}^2$. Because $\mathbf{B}$ is perpendicular to the loop plane, $\Phi_B = BA$. The rate of change of flux is

$$ \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = \frac{\Delta B}{\Delta t} \cdot A = \frac{0.60}{0.40} \times 3.14 \times 10^{-2} \approx 4.7 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{Wb/s}. $$

Therefore $|\mathcal{E}| \approx 47\ \text{mV}$. The induced current flows counterclockwise when viewed from $+z$, opposing the increasing upward flux.

Key Theorem. In integral form, Faraday's Law reads

$$ \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{E} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = -\frac{d}{dt}\iint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}, $$

relating the circulation of the electric field around any closed path to the time-varying magnetic flux through the bounded surface. Crucially, this relation holds in vacuum: the EMF arises from the changing field itself, regardless of whether a physical conductor occupies the path.

🌱 Gauss Law Electricity

🌱 Gauss's Law for Electricity

Definition. Gauss's Law for electricity states that the total electric flux through any closed surface (a Gaussian surface) is proportional to the net electric charge enclosed within that surface. Electric flux $\Phi_E$ through a surface element $d\vec{A} = \hat{n}\,dA$ is defined as $d\Phi_E = \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}$, capturing how much of the field passes through rather than along the surface. In integral form:

$$ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0} $$

where $\vec{E}$ is the electric field, $\hat{n}$ is the outward unit normal, $Q_{\text{enc}}$ is the total enclosed charge, and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{C}^2/(\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)$ is the permittivity of free space. The law is one of Maxwell's four equations and is equivalent to Coulomb's Law for static distributions, but far more powerful when the charge distribution possesses sufficient symmetry.

Worked Example. Consider an infinitely long line of charge with uniform linear charge density $\lambda\ (\text{C/m})$. Choose a coaxial cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius $r$ and length $L$. By symmetry, $\vec{E}$ points radially outward and has constant magnitude on the curved surface; on the two flat end caps, $\vec{E} \perp \hat{n}$, so those contributions vanish. The flux integral reduces to:

$$ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = E(2\pi r L) $$

The enclosed charge is $Q_{\text{enc}} = \lambda L$. Applying Gauss's Law:

$$ E(2\pi r L) = \frac{\lambda L}{\varepsilon_0} \implies E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r} $$

This matches the result obtained by direct integration of Coulomb's Law, but the derivation required only symmetry arguments and the law itself.

Key Theorem. For any charge distribution with planar, cylindrical, or spherical symmetry, Gauss's Law yields the electric field in a single algebraic step once the appropriate Gaussian surface is chosen. Outside a uniformly charged sphere of total charge $Q$ and radius $R$, the field is identical to that of a point charge: $E = Q/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2)$ for $r > R$.

🌱 Gauss Law Magnetism

🌱 Gauss Law Magnetism

Definition

Gauss's Law for Magnetism is one of Maxwell's four fundamental equations of electromagnetism. It states that the net magnetic flux through any closed surface is identically zero:

$$ \oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0 $$

where $\mathbf{B}$ is the magnetic field vector and $d\mathbf{A}$ is the outward-pointing area element of the closed surface $S$. This result encodes a profound physical truth: magnetic monopoles do not exist in nature. Unlike electric charges, which can exist in isolation as sources or sinks of electric field lines, magnetic field lines form continuous closed loops. Every field line that enters a closed surface must also exit it, yielding zero net flux. The quantity $\Phi_B = \oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$ is called the magnetic flux through the surface.

Worked Example

Consider a bar magnet enclosed entirely within a spherical Gaussian surface of radius $R$. The north pole generates outward field lines while the south pole draws them inward. Every field line originating at the north pole and piercing the sphere outward must return through the sphere inward toward the south pole. Therefore:

$$ \Phi_B = \oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \Phi_{\text{out}} + \Phi_{\text{in}} = \Phi_{\text{out}} - \Phi_{\text{out}} = 0 $$

This holds regardless of where the magnet is positioned within the surface or how asymmetric the field distribution may be.

Key Theorem

Gauss's Law for Magnetism is equivalent to the statement that the divergence of the magnetic field vanishes everywhere:

$$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0 $$

This differential form follows directly from the divergence theorem, $\oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B})\, dV$. Since the volume integral must equal zero for any arbitrary volume, the integrand itself must be identically zero. This condition mathematically forbids isolated magnetic charges and ensures that $\mathbf{B}$ is a solenoidal (divergence-free) field.

🍃 Speed Of Light

🍃 Speed Of Light

Definition. The speed of light in a vacuum, denoted $c$, is a universal physical constant defined as exactly $c = 299{,}792{,}458 \ \text{m/s}$, often approximated as $3.00 \times 10^8 \ \text{m/s}$. This value is not merely an empirical measurement but forms the foundation of the SI unit system: the meter is defined such that $c$ has precisely this value. In any medium, light travels at a reduced speed $v = c/n$, where $n \geq 1$ is the refractive index of that medium. In electromagnetic theory, Maxwell showed that light is an electromagnetic wave whose speed in vacuum is determined entirely by the permittivity $\varepsilon_0$ and permeability $\mu_0$ of free space:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_0 \mu_0}} $$

Substituting $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \ \text{C}^2/(\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)$ and $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \ \text{T}\cdot\text{m/A}$ reproduces the measured value of $c$ to high precision, a profound confirmation that light and electromagnetism are unified phenomena.

Worked Example. Light from the Sun must travel approximately $1.496 \times 10^{11} \ \text{m}$ (one astronomical unit) to reach Earth. Find the travel time $t$.

$$ t = \frac{d}{c} = \frac{1.496 \times 10^{11} \ \text{m}}{3.00 \times 10^8 \ \text{m/s}} = 4.99 \times 10^2 \ \text{s} \approx 8.32 \ \text{min} $$

This result implies that observations of the Sun are always delayed by roughly 8.3 minutes — we see the Sun as it was, not as it is, a concrete illustration of the finite propagation speed of electromagnetic information.

Key Theorem. Maxwell's wave equations predict that electromagnetic disturbances propagate in vacuum with speed $c = 1/\sqrt{\varepsilon_0\mu_0}$, independent of the frequency or amplitude of the wave. This frequency-independence (non-dispersion) in vacuum means all colors of light arrive simultaneously from a distant source, distinguishing vacuum propagation from propagation through dispersive media where $n = n(\lambda)$.

🍃 Maxwells Equations

🍃 Maxwells Equations

Definition. Maxwell's equations are the four fundamental laws governing classical electromagnetism, unifying electric fields, magnetic fields, electric charges, and currents into a single coherent framework. In differential form, they are:

$$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \quad \text{(Gauss's law for electricity)} $$ $$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0 \quad \text{(Gauss's law for magnetism)} $$ $$ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \quad \text{(Faraday's law)} $$ $$ \nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \quad \text{(Ampère-Maxwell law)} $$

Here $\rho$ is the free charge density, $\mathbf{J}$ is the current density, $\varepsilon_0$ is the permittivity of free space, and $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space. The term $\mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$ is Maxwell's critical addition — the displacement current — which he introduced to resolve an inconsistency in Ampère's original law for time-varying electric fields. The three remaining equations were established by prior work; displacement current is the single new primitive Maxwell contributed to complete the system.

Worked Example. Consider a parallel-plate capacitor with plate area $A$ being charged by a current $I$. The electric field between the plates is $E = Q/(\varepsilon_0 A)$, so $\frac{\partial E}{\partial t} = I/(\varepsilon_0 A)$. The displacement current density is therefore $J_d = \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial E}{\partial t} = I/A$, giving a total displacement current $I_d = J_d \cdot A = I$. Applying the Ampère-Maxwell law to a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$ between the plates yields the same magnetic field $B = \mu_0 I/(2\pi r)$ as found outside the plates, where conduction current passes through the surface. This confirms that displacement current acts as a source of $\mathbf{B}$ on equal footing with conduction current, restoring self-consistency to the law.

Key Theorem. In vacuum ($\rho = 0$, $\mathbf{J} = 0$), combining Faraday's law and the Ampère-Maxwell law by taking the curl of each and substituting yields a wave equation for $\mathbf{E}$:

$$ \nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} $$

The propagation speed is $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0} \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}$, which matches the measured speed of light — a result that would be impossible without the displacement current term.

🌱 Wave Frequency Wavelength

🌱 Wave Frequency Wavelength

Definition. Electromagnetic waves, like all periodic waves, are characterized by two fundamental spatial and temporal parameters. The wavelength $\lambda$ is the distance between two successive points in phase (e.g., crest to crest), measured in meters. The frequency $f$ is the number of complete oscillation cycles passing a fixed point per unit time, measured in hertz ($\text{Hz} = \text{s}^{-1}$). These quantities are related to the wave speed $c$ — the speed of light in vacuum, $c \approx 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$ — through the fundamental wave relation:

$$ c = f\lambda $$

The period $T = 1/f$ gives the time for one complete cycle. For electromagnetic waves in a medium with index of refraction $n$, the speed becomes $v = c/n$, modifying the wavelength while the frequency remains unchanged: $\lambda_{\text{medium}} = \lambda_{\text{vacuum}}/n$.

Worked Example. A radio station broadcasts at a frequency of $f = 101.5\ \text{MHz} = 1.015 \times 10^8\ \text{Hz}$. Find the wavelength of these radio waves in vacuum.

Applying $c = f\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}{1.015 \times 10^8\ \text{Hz}} = 2.95\ \text{m} $$

The period is $T = 1/f = 9.85 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{s} \approx 9.85\ \text{ns}$.

Key Theorem. For electromagnetic radiation in vacuum, the product of frequency and wavelength is invariant and equal to $c$:

$$ f\lambda = c \quad \text{(constant)} $$

This implies an inverse relationship: higher-frequency radiation (e.g., X-rays, $f \sim 10^{18}\ \text{Hz}$) carries shorter wavelengths ($\lambda \sim 10^{-10}\ \text{m}$), while lower-frequency radiation (radio waves, $f \sim 10^6\ \text{Hz}$) spans meter-scale wavelengths. This relationship underpins the entire electromagnetic spectrum classification, from radio waves through visible light ($\lambda \approx 400\text{–}700\ \text{nm}$) to gamma rays.

🍃 Electromagnetic Wave

🍃 Electromagnetic Wave

Definition. An electromagnetic wave is a self-propagating disturbance in the electromagnetic field, consisting of mutually perpendicular, oscillating electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ vectors, both perpendicular to the direction of propagation. These waves require no medium and travel through vacuum at the speed of light $c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8$ m/s, which Maxwell showed follows directly from the known constants $\mu_0$ and $\varepsilon_0$:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}}. $$

In a plane wave propagating in the $+x$ direction, the fields take the form

$$ E_y = E_0 \sin(kx - \omega t), \qquad B_z = B_0 \sin(kx - \omega t), $$

with the amplitude constraint $E_0 = cB_0$, arising directly from Faraday's law and the Ampère–Maxwell law.

Worked Example. A radio station broadcasts at $f = 100$ MHz. Determine the wavelength and the peak magnetic field amplitude given a peak electric field amplitude $E_0 = 0.50$ V/m.

The wavelength follows from $c = f\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{1.00 \times 10^8 \text{ Hz}} = 3.00 \text{ m}. $$

The peak magnetic field amplitude is

$$ B_0 = \frac{E_0}{c} = \frac{0.50 \text{ V/m}}{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}} \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-9} \text{ T}. $$

Although $B_0$ is many orders of magnitude smaller than $E_0$ in SI units, both fields carry equal energy density.

Key Theorem (Intensity). The time-averaged intensity (power per unit area) transported by an electromagnetic wave is

$$ I = \frac{1}{2} c \varepsilon_0 E_0^2 = \frac{E_0 B_0}{2\mu_0}. $$

This result is the time average of the Poynting vector $\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0}(\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B})$, which quantifies the directional energy flux. Because $I \propto E_0^2$ and a point source spreads energy over a sphere of area $4\pi r^2$, intensity falls as $r^{-2}$.

🍃 Wave Equation Em

🍃 Wave Equation Em

Definition. Electromagnetic waves arise as solutions to a coupled system of partial differential equations derived directly from Maxwell's equations in vacuum. Eliminating either E or B from the four Maxwell equations yields the electromagnetic wave equation. For the electric field:

$$ \nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} $$

and identically for the magnetic field:

$$ \nabla^2 \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{B}}{\partial t^2} $$

These are three-dimensional vector wave equations of the form $\nabla^2 f = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial t^2}$, where the wave propagation speed is $v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}}$. Substituting $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\ \text{T·m/A}$ and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{C}^2/\text{N·m}^2$ yields $v = c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$, the speed of light — Maxwell's profound identification of light as an electromagnetic wave.

Worked Example. Consider a plane wave propagating in the $+x$ direction with electric field polarized along $\hat{z}$:

$$ E_z(x,t) = E_0 \cos(kx - \omega t) $$

Verify this satisfies the wave equation. Computing:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 E_z}{\partial x^2} = -k^2 E_0 \cos(kx - \omega t), \qquad \frac{\partial^2 E_z}{\partial t^2} = -\omega^2 E_0 \cos(kx - \omega t) $$

Substituting into $\frac{\partial^2 E_z}{\partial x^2} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 E_z}{\partial t^2}$ requires $k^2 = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\,\omega^2$, giving the dispersion relation $\omega = ck$, consistent with $v = \omega/k = c$.

Key Theorem. For any electromagnetic plane wave in vacuum, the electric and magnetic fields are mutually perpendicular, both perpendicular to the direction of propagation, and related in magnitude by:

$$ \frac{E_0}{B_0} = c $$

This follows from applying Faraday's law to the plane-wave solution, which forces $\mathbf{B} = \frac{1}{c}\hat{k} \times \mathbf{E}$.

🍃 Electromagnetic Spectrum

🍃 Electromagnetic Spectrum

Definition. The electromagnetic spectrum is the complete, ordered continuum of all electromagnetic radiation, classified by frequency $f$ (or equivalently, wavelength $\lambda$) according to the relation

$$ c = f\lambda, $$

where $c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$ is the speed of light in vacuum. All electromagnetic waves are transverse waves consisting of mutually perpendicular, oscillating electric and magnetic fields propagating through space without a material medium. The spectrum spans more than 24 orders of magnitude in frequency, from radio waves ($f \sim 10^3\ \text{Hz}$) through microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, to gamma rays ($f > 10^{20}\ \text{Hz}$). The energy carried per photon is governed by the Planck relation:

$$ E = hf, $$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$ is Planck's constant. Higher frequency therefore corresponds to greater photon energy.

Worked Example. A microwave oven operates at $f = 2.45\ \text{GHz}$. Determine the wavelength and photon energy of its radiation.

Wavelength:

$$ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}{2.45 \times 10^9\ \text{Hz}} \approx 0.122\ \text{m} = 12.2\ \text{cm}. $$

Photon energy:

$$ E = hf = (6.626 \times 10^{-34})(2.45 \times 10^9) \approx 1.62 \times 10^{-24}\ \text{J} \approx 1.01 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{eV}. $$

This energy is far too small to ionize molecules, which is why microwave radiation heats food through molecular rotation rather than chemical bond disruption.

Key Theorem (Maxwell's Unification). Maxwell's equations predict that any accelerating charge radiates electromagnetic energy at speed $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}$, where $\mu_0$ and $\varepsilon_0$ are the permeability and permittivity of free space. This result demonstrates that visible light, radio waves, and all other electromagnetic radiation are manifestations of the same underlying phenomenon, differing only in frequency.

Payoff 🎯

Infrared radiation represents the natural culmination of electromagnetic wave theory as applied to thermal and optical phenomena. Having established the full Maxwell framework — wave propagation, the electromagnetic spectrum, and the quantum nature of photon emission — the study of infrared radiation synthesizes these elements into a coherent account of how matter at ordinary temperatures interacts with electromagnetic fields. Every object above absolute zero emits radiation governed by Planck's law,

$$ B(\nu, T) = \frac{2h\nu^3}{c^2} \cdot \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/k_BT} - 1}, $$

and for terrestrial temperatures ($T \sim 200$–$400\ \text{K}$), the peak emission falls squarely in the infrared band ($\lambda_{\text{peak}} \approx 10\ \mu\text{m}$ by Wien's displacement law, $\lambda_{\text{peak}} T = 2.898 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m·K}$). Infrared radiation is therefore not an exotic phenomenon but the dominant radiative mechanism of everyday physical reality.

Worked Example. Consider Earth's surface at $T = 288\ \text{K}$. Wien's law gives $\lambda_{\text{peak}} = 2.898 \times 10^{-3} / 288 \approx 10.1\ \mu\text{m}$. Treating the surface as a blackbody, the total emitted power per unit area follows the Stefan–Boltzmann law: $j = \sigma T^4 = (5.67 \times 10^{-8})(288)^4 \approx 390\ \text{W·m}^{-2}$. This flux, directed upward into the atmosphere, is the radiative input that drives global energy balance.

Key Theorem (Kirchhoff's Law). For any body in thermal equilibrium, the spectral absorptivity equals the spectral emissivity: $\alpha(\nu) = \varepsilon(\nu)$. This identity constrains how atmospheric gases interact with infrared flux — a molecule that absorbs strongly at a given frequency must re-emit at that same frequency, redistributing energy rather than transmitting it.

Connection to the Greenhouse Effect. Atmospheric constituents such as $\text{CO}_2$ and $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ are largely transparent to incoming solar shortwave radiation yet absorb the longwave infrared emitted by Earth's surface. By Kirchhoff's law they re-radiate a portion of this energy back downward, reducing the net outgoing flux and elevating equilibrium surface temperature — the greenhouse mechanism in quantitative form.
