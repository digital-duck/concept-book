🌱 Wave Frequency Wavelength

🌱 Wave Frequency Wavelength

Definition. Electromagnetic waves, like all periodic waves, are characterized by two fundamental spatial and temporal parameters. The wavelength $\lambda$ is the distance between two successive points in phase (e.g., crest to crest), measured in meters. The frequency $f$ is the number of complete oscillation cycles passing a fixed point per unit time, measured in hertz ($\text{Hz} = \text{s}^{-1}$). These quantities are related to the wave speed $c$ — the speed of light in vacuum, $c \approx 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$ — through the fundamental wave relation:

$$ c = f\lambda $$

The period $T = 1/f$ gives the time for one complete cycle. For electromagnetic waves in a medium with index of refraction $n$, the speed becomes $v = c/n$, modifying the wavelength while the frequency remains unchanged: $\lambda_{\text{medium}} = \lambda_{\text{vacuum}}/n$.

Worked Example. A radio station broadcasts at a frequency of $f = 101.5\ \text{MHz} = 1.015 \times 10^8\ \text{Hz}$. Find the wavelength of these radio waves in vacuum.

Applying $c = f\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}{1.015 \times 10^8\ \text{Hz}} = 2.95\ \text{m} $$

The period is $T = 1/f = 9.85 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{s} \approx 9.85\ \text{ns}$.

Key Theorem. For electromagnetic radiation in vacuum, the product of frequency and wavelength is invariant and equal to $c$:

$$ f\lambda = c \quad \text{(constant)} $$

This implies an inverse relationship: higher-frequency radiation (e.g., X-rays, $f \sim 10^{18}\ \text{Hz}$) carries shorter wavelengths ($\lambda \sim 10^{-10}\ \text{m}$), while lower-frequency radiation (radio waves, $f \sim 10^6\ \text{Hz}$) spans meter-scale wavelengths. This relationship underpins the entire electromagnetic spectrum classification, from radio waves through visible light ($\lambda \approx 400\text{–}700\ \text{nm}$) to gamma rays.

Lab Exercise. Using a diffraction grating spectrometer, observe the visible emission spectrum of a hydrogen discharge tube. Measure the angular positions of at least three spectral lines and apply the grating equation $d\sin\theta = m\lambda$ (where $d$ is the grating spacing and $m$ is the order) to calculate each line's wavelength. From these wavelengths, compute the corresponding frequencies and verify they fall within the range $4.3 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz} \leq f \leq 7.5 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}$ for visible light. Compare your measured wavelengths against the known Balmer series values.
