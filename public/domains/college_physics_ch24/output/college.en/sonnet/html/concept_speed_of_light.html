🍃 Speed Of Light

🍃 Speed Of Light

Definition. The speed of light in a vacuum, denoted $c$, is a universal physical constant defined as exactly $c = 299{,}792{,}458 \ \text{m/s}$, often approximated as $3.00 \times 10^8 \ \text{m/s}$. This value is not merely an empirical measurement but forms the foundation of the SI unit system: the meter is defined such that $c$ has precisely this value. In any medium, light travels at a reduced speed $v = c/n$, where $n \geq 1$ is the refractive index of that medium. In electromagnetic theory, Maxwell showed that light is an electromagnetic wave whose speed in vacuum is determined entirely by the permittivity $\varepsilon_0$ and permeability $\mu_0$ of free space:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_0 \mu_0}} $$

Substituting $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \ \text{C}^2/(\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)$ and $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \ \text{T}\cdot\text{m/A}$ reproduces the measured value of $c$ to high precision, a profound confirmation that light and electromagnetism are unified phenomena.

Worked Example. Light from the Sun must travel approximately $1.496 \times 10^{11} \ \text{m}$ (one astronomical unit) to reach Earth. Find the travel time $t$.

$$ t = \frac{d}{c} = \frac{1.496 \times 10^{11} \ \text{m}}{3.00 \times 10^8 \ \text{m/s}} = 4.99 \times 10^2 \ \text{s} \approx 8.32 \ \text{min} $$

This result implies that observations of the Sun are always delayed by roughly 8.3 minutes — we see the Sun as it was, not as it is, a concrete illustration of the finite propagation speed of electromagnetic information.

Key Theorem. Maxwell's wave equations predict that electromagnetic disturbances propagate in vacuum with speed $c = 1/\sqrt{\varepsilon_0\mu_0}$, independent of the frequency or amplitude of the wave. This frequency-independence (non-dispersion) in vacuum means all colors of light arrive simultaneously from a distant source, distinguishing vacuum propagation from propagation through dispersive media where $n = n(\lambda)$.

Lab Exercise. Using a rotating-mirror apparatus (Foucault method), a laser pulse travels a known round-trip distance of $d = 600 \ \text{m}$. If the mirror must rotate by $\theta = 0.0314 \ \text{rad}$ during transit to produce a detectable deflection at angular velocity $\omega$, express $\omega$ in terms of $c$ and $d$, and calculate the required rotation rate. (Hint: $t = 2d/c$.)
