🍃 Electromagnetic Wave

🍃 Electromagnetic Wave

Definition. An electromagnetic wave is a self-propagating disturbance in the electromagnetic field, consisting of mutually perpendicular, oscillating electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ vectors, both perpendicular to the direction of propagation. These waves require no medium and travel through vacuum at the speed of light $c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8$ m/s, which Maxwell showed follows directly from the known constants $\mu_0$ and $\varepsilon_0$:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}}. $$

In a plane wave propagating in the $+x$ direction, the fields take the form

$$ E_y = E_0 \sin(kx - \omega t), \qquad B_z = B_0 \sin(kx - \omega t), $$

with the amplitude constraint $E_0 = cB_0$, arising directly from Faraday's law and the Ampère–Maxwell law.

Worked Example. A radio station broadcasts at $f = 100$ MHz. Determine the wavelength and the peak magnetic field amplitude given a peak electric field amplitude $E_0 = 0.50$ V/m.

The wavelength follows from $c = f\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}}{1.00 \times 10^8 \text{ Hz}} = 3.00 \text{ m}. $$

The peak magnetic field amplitude is

$$ B_0 = \frac{E_0}{c} = \frac{0.50 \text{ V/m}}{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}} \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-9} \text{ T}. $$

Although $B_0$ is many orders of magnitude smaller than $E_0$ in SI units, both fields carry equal energy density.

Key Theorem (Intensity). The time-averaged intensity (power per unit area) transported by an electromagnetic wave is

$$ I = \frac{1}{2} c \varepsilon_0 E_0^2 = \frac{E_0 B_0}{2\mu_0}. $$

This result is the time average of the Poynting vector $\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0}(\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B})$, which quantifies the directional energy flux. Because $I \propto E_0^2$ and a point source spreads energy over a sphere of area $4\pi r^2$, intensity falls as $r^{-2}$.

Lab Exercise. Using a calibrated dipole antenna and oscilloscope, measure the peak electric field amplitude of a 2.45 GHz microwave signal at distances $r = 0.5, 1.0, 1.5$ m from a point source. Plot $I$ versus $r^{-2}$ and verify the inverse-square law. From your measured $E_0$ at each distance, calculate $B_0$ and estimate the total radiated power of the source.
