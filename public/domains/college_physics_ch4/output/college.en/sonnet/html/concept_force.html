🌱 Force

🌱 Force

Definition. A force is a vector quantity representing an interaction between two objects that causes, or tends to cause, a change in the motion of either object. Formally, force $\vec{F}$ is defined through Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m\vec{a} $$

where $m$ is the inertial mass of the object (in kilograms) and $\vec{a}$ is its acceleration (in $\text{m/s}^2$). The SI unit of force is the newton: $1\,\text{N} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2$. Because force is a vector, the net force is the vector sum of all individual forces acting on the object:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \vec{F}_i $$

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 5.0\,\text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\vec{F}_1 = 12\,\hat{x}\,\text{N}$ and $\vec{F}_2 = -4\,\hat{x}\,\text{N} + 3\,\hat{y}\,\text{N}$. Find the net force and the resulting acceleration.

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = (12 - 4)\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y} = 8\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y}\;\text{N} $$

The magnitude is $|\vec{F}_{\text{net}}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{73} \approx 8.54\,\text{N}$, directed at angle $\theta = \arctan(3/8) \approx 20.6°$ above the $x$-axis. The acceleration follows directly:

$$ \vec{a} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8\,\hat{x} + 3\,\hat{y}}{5.0} = 1.6\,\hat{x} + 0.6\,\hat{y}\;\text{m/s}^2 $$

Key Theorem (Newton's Third Law). For every force exerted by object A on object B, there exists an equal and opposite force exerted by B on A:

$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A} $$

These action-reaction pairs always act on different objects and therefore do not cancel in any single free-body diagram. This principle is essential for analyzing systems of interacting bodies.

Lab Exercise. Using a force sensor and a dynamics cart on a low-friction track, apply a series of measured horizontal forces and record the resulting accelerations. Plot $a$ versus $F_{\text{net}}$ and determine the slope. Verify that the slope equals $1/m$ and confirm Newton's second law within experimental uncertainty. Repeat with additional mass added to the cart and comment on how the slope changes.
