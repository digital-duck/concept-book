🍃 Changing Electric Field Creates Magnetic Field

🍃 Changing Electric Field Creates Magnetic Field

Definition. Maxwell's modification of Ampère's Law introduces the displacement current $I_d \equiv \varepsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$, which demonstrates that a time-varying electric field acts as a source of magnetic field, entirely analogous to a conduction current. The generalized Ampère–Maxwell Law states:

$$ \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 \left( I_{\text{enc}} + \varepsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} \right) $$

where $I_{\text{enc}}$ is the enclosed conduction current, $\varepsilon_0$ is the permittivity of free space, and $\frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$ is the rate of change of electric flux through the chosen Amperian surface. Crucially, $I_d$ produces a magnetic field even in regions devoid of charge flow — no physical current is required.

Worked Example. Consider a parallel-plate capacitor with circular plates of radius $R = 0.05\,\text{m}$ being charged so that the uniform electric field between the plates increases at a rate $\frac{dE}{dt} = 1.0 \times 10^{12}\,\text{V/m/s}$. Find the magnetic field at a radial distance $r = 0.03\,\text{m}$ from the axis (inside the capacitor region).

The electric flux through a circular Amperian loop of radius $r < R$ is:

$$ \Phi_E = E \cdot \pi r^2 $$

The displacement current enclosed:

$$ I_d = \varepsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} = \varepsilon_0 \pi r^2 \frac{dE}{dt} = (8.85 \times 10^{-12})(\pi)(0.03)^2(1.0 \times 10^{12}) \approx 2.50 \times 10^{-2}\,\text{A} $$

Applying the Ampère–Maxwell Law with no conduction current ($I_{\text{enc}} = 0$):

$$ B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I_d \implies B = \frac{\mu_0 I_d}{2\pi r} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(2.50 \times 10^{-2})}{2\pi(0.03)} \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-7}\,\text{T} $$

Key Theorem. Maxwell's extension of Ampère's Law, combined with Faraday's Law, establishes the self-sustaining electromagnetic wave: a changing electric field generates a magnetic field, which in turn generates a changing electric field, propagating through vacuum at speed $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}}$. The displacement current is thus the single primitive concept introduced here, and it is sufficient to complete Maxwell's equations and predict electromagnetic radiation.

Lab Exercise. A parallel-plate capacitor with circular plates ($R = 0.10\,\text{m}$) is charged so that $\frac{dE}{dt} = 5.0 \times 10^{11}\,\text{V/m/s}$. Calculate the displacement current enclosed within a circular Amperian loop of radius $r = 0.04\,\text{m}$, and determine the resulting magnetic field magnitude at that radius. Verify that your result scales linearly with $r$ inside the plates, and compare this spatial dependence to the field outside the plates ($r > R$), where the enclosed displacement current no longer grows with loop size.
