🌱 Gauss's Law for Electricity

Definition. Gauss's Law for electricity states that the total electric flux through any closed surface (a Gaussian surface) is proportional to the net electric charge enclosed within that surface. Electric flux $\Phi_E$ through a surface element $d\vec{A} = \hat{n}\,dA$ is defined as $d\Phi_E = \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}$, capturing how much of the field passes through rather than along the surface. In integral form:

$$ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0} $$

where $\vec{E}$ is the electric field, $\hat{n}$ is the outward unit normal, $Q_{\text{enc}}$ is the total enclosed charge, and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{C}^2/(\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)$ is the permittivity of free space. The law is one of Maxwell's four equations and is equivalent to Coulomb's Law for static distributions, but far more powerful when the charge distribution possesses sufficient symmetry.

Worked Example. Consider an infinitely long line of charge with uniform linear charge density $\lambda\ (\text{C/m})$. Choose a coaxial cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius $r$ and length $L$. By symmetry, $\vec{E}$ points radially outward and has constant magnitude on the curved surface; on the two flat end caps, $\vec{E} \perp \hat{n}$, so those contributions vanish. The flux integral reduces to:

$$ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = E(2\pi r L) $$

The enclosed charge is $Q_{\text{enc}} = \lambda L$. Applying Gauss's Law:

$$ E(2\pi r L) = \frac{\lambda L}{\varepsilon_0} \implies E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r} $$

This matches the result obtained by direct integration of Coulomb's Law, but the derivation required only symmetry arguments and the law itself.

Key Theorem. For any charge distribution with planar, cylindrical, or spherical symmetry, Gauss's Law yields the electric field in a single algebraic step once the appropriate Gaussian surface is chosen. Outside a uniformly charged sphere of total charge $Q$ and radius $R$, the field is identical to that of a point charge: $E = Q/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2)$ for $r > R$.

Lab Exercise. A solid insulating sphere of radius $R = 0.05\ \text{m}$ carries uniform volume charge density $\rho = 4.0\ \mu\text{C/m}^3$. (a) Construct a spherical Gaussian surface of radius $r < R$ and derive $E(r)$ inside the sphere. (b) Compute $E$ at $r = 0.03\ \text{m}$. (c) Verify that your inside and outside expressions agree at $r = R$.
