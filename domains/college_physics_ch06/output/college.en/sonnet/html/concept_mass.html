🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. Mass is a fundamental scalar quantity that characterizes two distinct but experimentally equivalent properties of matter: inertial mass and gravitational mass. Inertial mass $m_i$ measures a body's resistance to changes in its state of motion, formalized through Newton's second law:

$$ \vec{F}_{\text{net}} = m_i \vec{a} $$

Gravitational mass $m_g$ quantifies the strength of a body's coupling to a gravitational field, appearing in Newton's law of universal gravitation:

$$ F_g = G \frac{m_{g,1} \, m_{g,2}}{r^2} $$

The equivalence principle asserts $m_i = m_g$ for all bodies, a result confirmed experimentally to better than one part in $10^{12}$ (Eötvös experiments). In the SI system, mass is measured in kilograms (kg) and is an intrinsic property independent of location, distinguishing it from weight, which is the gravitational force $W = mg$ acting on a body and varies with local gravitational acceleration $g$.

Worked Example. A block of unknown mass rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A horizontal net force of $F = 12.0\ \text{N}$ produces an acceleration of $a = 3.00\ \text{m/s}^2$. Find the mass.

Applying Newton's second law:

$$ m = \frac{F_{\text{net}}}{a} = \frac{12.0\ \text{N}}{3.00\ \text{m/s}^2} = 4.00\ \text{kg} $$

Note that this result is independent of any gravitational field; the measurement isolates inertial mass through kinematics alone.

Key Theorem — Equivalence of Inertial and Gravitational Mass. For any body, $m_i = m_g$. A direct consequence is that all bodies in a uniform gravitational field experience the same free-fall acceleration $g$, regardless of composition or size:

$$ a = \frac{F_g}{m_i} = \frac{m_g \, g}{m_i} = g $$

This universality underlies Galileo's inclined-plane experiments and is the empirical cornerstone of general relativity.

Lab Exercise. Using a force sensor and a dynamics cart on a low-friction track, apply three different measured forces $F_1, F_2, F_3$ to the same cart and record the resulting accelerations $a_1, a_2, a_3$. Compute $m = F_k / a_k$ for each trial. Compare your three values and calculate the percent deviation. Then place the cart on a scale to obtain its gravitational mass; discuss whether the two values agree within experimental uncertainty and what sources of systematic error (friction, sensor calibration) might explain any discrepancy.
