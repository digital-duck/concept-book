🍃 Heat Transfer

🍃 Heat Transfer

Definition. Heat transfer is the process by which thermal energy moves from a region of higher temperature to one of lower temperature, driven by a temperature difference $\Delta T$. Three distinct mechanisms govern this transport: conduction, the diffusion of thermal energy through direct molecular collisions; convection, the bulk movement of a fluid carrying thermal energy; and radiation, the emission and absorption of electromagnetic waves. Each mechanism obeys its own quantitative law, yet all share the fundamental constraint that spontaneous heat flow proceeds only from hot to cold, consistent with the second law of thermodynamics.

For conduction through a slab of material, Fourier's Law states

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = -kA\frac{dT}{dx}, $$

where $k$ is the thermal conductivity of the material $(\text{W m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1})$, $A$ is the cross-sectional area, and $dT/dx$ is the temperature gradient. For convection from a surface to a fluid, Newton's Law of Cooling gives

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = hA(T_s - T_f), $$

with $h$ the convective heat-transfer coefficient. Thermal radiation from a surface follows the Stefan–Boltzmann Law:

$$ P = \varepsilon \sigma A T^4, $$

where $\varepsilon$ is the emissivity, $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{W m}^{-2}\text{K}^{-4}$, and $T$ is the absolute temperature.

Worked Example. A glass window pane of thickness $L = 5.0\ \text{mm}$, area $A = 1.2\ \text{m}^2$, and thermal conductivity $k = 0.96\ \text{W m}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}$ separates an interior at $21^\circ\text{C}$ from an exterior at $-5^\circ\text{C}$. The conductive heat-loss rate is

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = kA\frac{\Delta T}{L} = (0.96)(1.2)\frac{26}{0.005} = 5990\ \text{W} \approx 6.0\ \text{kW}. $$

This illustrates why thin, single-pane windows produce substantial heating loads.

Key Theorem (Thermal Resistance). Conduction through a composite wall of $n$ layers in series obeys

$$ R_\text{total} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{L_i}{k_i A},\quad \frac{dQ}{dt} = \frac{\Delta T}{R_\text{total}}, $$

in direct analogy with Ohm's Law for electrical circuits. Adding low-$k$ insulating layers increases $R_\text{total}$ and reduces heat flow proportionally.

Lab Exercise. Using two copper rods of equal length but different cross-sectional areas connected end-to-end with a thermal paste joint, measure the steady-state temperature at three points along the assembly. Compute $dQ/dt$ independently for each segment using Fourier's Law, verify that the values agree within experimental uncertainty (confirming energy conservation), and determine whether the interface resistance is negligible.
