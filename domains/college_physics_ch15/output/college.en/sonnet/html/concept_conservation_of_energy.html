🌱 Conservation Of Energy

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

Definition. The law of conservation of energy states that the total mechanical energy of an isolated system—the sum of kinetic energy $K$ and potential energy $U$—remains constant over time, provided no non-conservative forces (such as friction or air resistance) do work on the system. Formally,

$$ E_{\text{mech}} = K + U = \text{constant}, $$

or equivalently,

$$ K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f. $$

When non-conservative forces are present, the work-energy theorem generalizes to

$$ W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta E_{\text{mech}}, $$

where $W_{\text{nc}}$ represents the net work done by non-conservative forces, which typically manifest as thermal energy dissipated into the environment.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 2.0\ \text{kg}$ is released from rest at the top of a frictionless incline of height $h = 5.0\ \text{m}$. Find the speed of the block at the bottom.

Taking the base of the incline as the reference level ($U = 0$), the initial state has $K_i = 0$ and $U_i = mgh$. At the bottom, $U_f = 0$. Applying conservation of energy:

$$ K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f $$ $$ 0 + mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + 0 $$ $$ v_f = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8\ \text{m/s}^2)(5.0\ \text{m})} \approx 9.9\ \text{m/s}. $$

Note that $m$ cancels, confirming that the result is independent of the object's mass.

Key Theorem. For a conservative system, the mechanical energy $E_{\text{mech}}$ is a constant of the motion. This follows directly from Newton's second law and the definition of a conservative force: the work done by such a force around any closed path is zero, ensuring that energy stored as potential can be fully recovered as kinetic energy and vice versa. This theorem underpins the analysis of oscillatory systems, orbital mechanics, and collision dynamics.

Lab Exercise. Using a pendulum of length $L = 1.2\ \text{m}$, release the bob from an angle of $\theta_0 = 30°$ with zero initial velocity. (a) Calculate the theoretical speed at the lowest point using $v = \sqrt{2gL(1 - \cos\theta_0)}$. (b) Measure the actual speed using a photogate timer. (c) Compute $W_{\text{nc}}$ and identify the source of any energy deficit. Repeat the experiment at $\theta_0 = 45°$ and compare the fractional energy loss between trials.
