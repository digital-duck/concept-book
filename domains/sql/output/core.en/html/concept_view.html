View

View

When you learn that the Earth rotates rather than the Sun orbiting it, nothing in the sky changes — only your *frame of reference* does. The same shift happens constantly in linear algebra: the same object looks entirely different depending on which view you adopt.

Definition. A *view* of a matrix equation $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is a consistent interpretation that fixes *what plays the role of the basic unit*. The two canonical views are:

  • Row view: each row $\mathbf{a}_i^\top$ of $A$ defines a hyperplane $\mathbf{a}_i^\top \mathbf{x} = b_i$; the solution is their intersection.
  • Column view: $A\mathbf{x}$ is a *linear combination* of the columns of $A$, with the entries of $\mathbf{x}$ as coefficients.

    • Worked example. Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, $\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

    *Row view*: the product checks that $(1)(2)+(2)(1)=4$ and $(3)(2)+(1)(1)=7$, so $A\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix}4\\7\end{pmatrix}$.

    *Column view*: $A\mathbf{x} = 2\begin{pmatrix}1\\3\end{pmatrix} + 1\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2\\6\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4\\7\end{pmatrix}$.

    Same answer — completely different story.

    Why it matters. The column view reveals that $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is solvable *if and only if* $\mathbf{b}$ lies in the column space $\mathcal{C}(A)$, the span of $A$'s columns. This geometric picture is the foundation for understanding rank, null space, and least-squares. The row view, by contrast, is natural for Gaussian elimination, where you're intersecting constraints one equation at a time.

    Choosing the right view does not change the mathematics; it changes what you *see*, and therefore what you can prove.

    Practice. For $A = \begin{pmatrix}2 & -1\\4 & 0\end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\8\end{pmatrix}$, express $\mathbf{b}$ as a linear combination of the columns of $A$, then verify the same result using the row view.
