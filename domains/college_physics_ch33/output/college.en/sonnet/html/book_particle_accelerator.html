🌱 Electromagnetic Force

🌱 Electromagnetic Force

Definition. The electromagnetic force is one of the four fundamental interactions in nature, governing the behavior of electrically charged particles and magnetic fields. It is described by the Lorentz force law, which specifies the force $\mathbf{F}$ acting on a particle carrying charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ through an electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$:

$$ \mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) $$

The electric component $q\mathbf{E}$ acts along the field direction and does work on the charge, while the magnetic component $q(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})$ acts perpendicular to both velocity and magnetic field, does no work, and changes only the direction of motion. Together, these two components constitute the complete electromagnetic interaction at the classical level, unified through Maxwell's equations.

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C, $m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg) moves with velocity $\mathbf{v} = 2.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}$ m/s through a region where $\mathbf{E} = 500\,\hat{y}$ N/C and $\mathbf{B} = 0.30\,\hat{z}$ T. Compute the total electromagnetic force.

Electric contribution: $\mathbf{F}_E = q\mathbf{E} = (1.6 \times 10^{-19})(500\,\hat{y}) = 8.0 \times 10^{-17}\,\hat{y}$ N.

Magnetic contribution: $\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B} = (2.0 \times 10^6\,\hat{x}) \times (0.30\,\hat{z}) = 6.0 \times 10^5\,(\hat{x} \times \hat{z}) = -6.0 \times 10^5\,\hat{y}$ m/s, so $\mathbf{F}_B = q(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) = -9.6 \times 10^{-14}\,\hat{y}$ N.

The total force is $\mathbf{F} \approx -9.6 \times 10^{-14}\,\hat{y}$ N, dominated by the magnetic term.

Key Theorem. Maxwell's unification establishes that electric and magnetic forces are manifestations of a single electromagnetic field tensor $F^{\mu\nu}$. Under Lorentz transformations, a purely electric field in one inertial frame acquires magnetic components in another, confirming that $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are frame-dependent projections of a single relativistic entity. This invariance is expressed through the field tensor contraction $F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} = 2(B^2 - E^2/c^2)$, which remains constant across all inertial frames.

🌱 Conservation Of Mass Energy

🌱 Conservation Of Mass Energy

Definition. The principle of conservation of mass-energy, arising from Einstein's special theory of relativity, asserts that the total relativistic energy of an isolated system remains constant. Einstein's famous relation unifies mass and energy through

$$ E = mc^2, $$

where $E$ is the rest energy, $m$ is the rest mass, and $c = 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$ is the speed of light in vacuum. More generally, the total relativistic energy of a particle with rest mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is

$$ E_{\text{total}} = \gamma mc^2, \quad \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}, $$

and the relativistic kinetic energy is $K = (\gamma - 1)mc^2$. Mass is therefore not separately conserved; rather, rest mass can be converted into kinetic energy or radiation, provided the total energy-momentum four-vector is conserved.

Worked Example. Consider electron-positron annihilation: $e^- + e^+ \rightarrow 2\gamma$. Each particle has rest mass $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$. The rest energy of each is

$$ E_0 = m_e c^2 = (9.109 \times 10^{-31})(2.998 \times 10^8)^2 \approx 8.187 \times 10^{-14}\ \text{J} = 0.511\ \text{MeV}. $$

Assuming both particles are nearly at rest, the total initial energy is $2 \times 0.511\ \text{MeV} = 1.022\ \text{MeV}$. By conservation of mass-energy and momentum, two photons must be emitted in opposite directions, each carrying energy $E_\gamma = 0.511\ \text{MeV}$. This is confirmed by the photon energy formula $E_\gamma = hf$, yielding $f \approx 1.24 \times 10^{20}\ \text{Hz}$, firmly in the gamma-ray range.

Key Theorem. For any isolated system, the total four-momentum $p^\mu = (E/c,\ \vec{p})$ is conserved. In the center-of-momentum frame, this reduces to conservation of invariant mass: $E^2 - (pc)^2 = (mc^2)^2 = \text{const}$. Nuclear binding energies and mass defects follow directly: the binding energy $B = \Delta m \cdot c^2$, where $\Delta m$ is the difference between the sum of constituent masses and the actual nuclear mass.

🌱 Conservation Of Momentum

🌱 Conservation Of Momentum

Definition. The law of conservation of momentum states that the total linear momentum of an isolated system remains constant in time. For a system of $n$ particles with masses $m_i$ and velocities $\mathbf{v}_i$, the total momentum is

$$ \mathbf{p}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i \mathbf{v}_i. $$

If the net external force on the system is zero, Newton's second law gives $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = \dfrac{d\mathbf{p}_{\text{total}}}{dt} = \mathbf{0}$, so $\mathbf{p}_{\text{total}}$ is constant. Internal forces between particles obey Newton's third law and therefore cancel in pairs, contributing nothing to the net change in total momentum.

Worked Example. A railway car of mass $M = 2000\ \text{kg}$ moves at $v_0 = 4.0\ \text{m/s}$ along a frictionless track. A worker inside the car throws a $m = 50\ \text{kg}$ crate horizontally off the back of the car at $u = 6.0\ \text{m/s}$ relative to the ground. Find the final speed of the car.

Before the throw, total momentum: $p_i = (M + m)v_0 = 2050 \times 4.0 = 8200\ \text{kg·m/s}$.

After the throw, applying conservation:

$$ (M - m)v_f + m(-u) = p_i $$

Wait — the crate leaves at $-6.0\ \text{m/s}$ (backward) and the car has mass $M$ (the worker remains aboard, so we treat the crate as separating from mass $M$):

$$ M\, v_f + m(-u) = (M+m)v_0 $$ $$ 2000\, v_f = 8200 - 50(-6.0) = 8200 + 300 = 8500 $$ $$ v_f = \frac{8500}{2000} = 4.25\ \text{m/s}. $$

The car speeds up slightly, consistent with the crate carrying backward momentum.

Key Theorem. In any collision between two objects, regardless of whether the collision is elastic or inelastic, total momentum is conserved provided external forces are negligible during the interaction interval:

$$ m_1 \mathbf{v}_{1i} + m_2 \mathbf{v}_{2i} = m_1 \mathbf{v}_{1f} + m_2 \mathbf{v}_{2f}. $$

Kinetic energy is conserved only in perfectly elastic collisions; momentum conservation is the more universal constraint.

🍃 Cyclotron

🍃 Cyclotron

Definition. A cyclotron is a type of particle accelerator that uses a static magnetic field combined with an oscillating electric field to accelerate charged particles to high kinetic energies in a compact, spiral trajectory. The device consists of two hollow, semicircular metallic chambers called dees (for their D-shape), placed within a uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$ perpendicular to the plane of the dees. An alternating potential difference $V$ is applied across the gap between the dees, synchronized so that the electric field always accelerates the particle as it crosses the gap.

Within each dee, the magnetic force acts as a centripetal force, causing the particle to follow a semicircular path. For a particle of charge $q$, mass $m$, and speed $v$, the radius of circular motion satisfies:

$$ r = \frac{mv}{qB} $$

Since $v$ increases each time the particle crosses the gap, $r$ increases as well, producing a characteristic outward spiral. The period of one full revolution is:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi m}{qB} $$

which is independent of speed. This isochronous property allows the oscillating electric field to remain in resonance with the particle at a fixed cyclotron frequency:

$$ f_c = \frac{qB}{2\pi m} $$

Worked Example. A proton ($q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, $m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$) moves in a cyclotron with $B = 1.20\ \text{T}$. Find the cyclotron frequency and the radius when the proton's kinetic energy is $K = 5.00\ \text{MeV}$.

$$ f_c = \frac{(1.60\times10^{-19})(1.20)}{2\pi(1.67\times10^{-27})} \approx 18.3\ \text{MHz} $$

Converting energy: $K = 5.00 \times 10^6 \times 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{J} = 8.00\times10^{-13}\ \text{J}$. Since $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, we get $v = \sqrt{2K/m} \approx 3.09\times10^7\ \text{m/s}$, giving:

$$ r = \frac{mv}{qB} = \frac{(1.67\times10^{-27})(3.09\times10^7)}{(1.60\times10^{-19})(1.20)} \approx 0.269\ \text{m} $$

Key Theorem — Relativistic Limit. At high energies, the relativistic mass $m_{\text{rel}} = \gamma m$ increases with speed, causing $f_c$ to drop below the driving frequency. The particle falls out of resonance, setting an upper energy limit for conventional cyclotrons. Synchrocyclotrons address this by decreasing the oscillation frequency to match $\gamma m$.

🍃 Colliding Beams

🍃 Colliding Beams

Definition. A colliding-beam accelerator is a particle physics facility in which two beams of charged particles are directed toward each other so that collisions occur between beam particles rather than between a moving projectile and a stationary target. Because both beams carry momentum, the center-of-mass (CM) frame of the collision can coincide with the laboratory frame, allowing the entire kinetic energy of both particles to be available for producing new particles or probing short-distance structure.

For two identical particles each with total relativistic energy $E$ and rest-mass energy $m_0 c^2$, colliding head-on, the CM energy is

$$ E_{\text{CM}} = 2E. $$

In contrast, for a beam particle of energy $E$ striking an identical target particle at rest, the CM energy is

$$ E_{\text{CM}} = \sqrt{2m_0 c^2 \cdot E + 2(m_0 c^2)^2} \approx \sqrt{2m_0 c^2 \, E} \quad (E \gg m_0 c^2). $$

The colliding-beam configuration scales far more efficiently: doubling the beam energy doubles $E_{\text{CM}}$, whereas for a fixed-target accelerator, doubling $E$ increases $E_{\text{CM}}$ only by a factor of $\sqrt{2}$.

Worked Example. At the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), each proton beam carries $E = 6.5 \text{ TeV}$, with $m_p c^2 \approx 938 \text{ MeV}$. For head-on proton–proton collisions:

$$ E_{\text{CM}} = 2 \times 6.5 \text{ TeV} = 13 \text{ TeV}. $$

If instead a 6.5 TeV proton struck a stationary proton, the available CM energy would be

$$ E_{\text{CM}} = \sqrt{2(938 \times 10^{-6}\text{ TeV})(6.5 \text{ TeV}) + 2(938 \times 10^{-6})^2 \text{ TeV}^2} \approx \sqrt{0.01220} \text{ TeV} \approx 110 \text{ GeV}, $$

roughly 120 times smaller—demonstrating the enormous advantage of colliding beams.

Key Theorem. The four-momentum invariant $s = (p_1 + p_2)^2 c^2$ is Lorentz invariant. In the CM frame, $s = E_{\text{CM}}^2 / c^2$. For head-on identical-particle collisions, $s = 4E^2/c^2$; for fixed-target geometry, $s = 2m_0 c^2 E + 2(m_0 c^2)^2$. The collider advantage is therefore a direct consequence of relativistic four-momentum addition.

🍃 Synchrotron

🍃 Synchrotron

Definition. A synchrotron is a type of cyclic particle accelerator in which charged particles are confined to a fixed circular orbit by magnetic fields that are synchronized with the particles' increasing relativistic momentum. Unlike the classical cyclotron—where the orbital radius grows as the particle gains energy—the synchrotron maintains a constant orbital radius $R$ by simultaneously increasing the magnetic field strength $B$ and the oscillating electric field frequency $f$ to match the relativistic momentum $p = \gamma m v$. The relativistic condition requires

$$ qvB = \frac{\gamma m v^2}{R} \implies B = \frac{\gamma m v}{qR} = \frac{p}{qR}, $$

so $B$ must increase proportionally with $p$ as the particle accelerates toward relativistic speeds.

Worked Example. A proton ($m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$, $q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$) circulates in a synchrotron of radius $R = 100\ \text{m}$ and is accelerated to a kinetic energy of $K = 10\ \text{GeV}$. Find the required magnetic field at this energy.

The total relativistic energy is $E = K + m_p c^2 = 10 + 0.938 \approx 10.938\ \text{GeV}$. The relativistic momentum magnitude follows from $E^2 = (pc)^2 + (m_p c^2)^2$:

$$ pc = \sqrt{E^2 - (m_p c^2)^2} = \sqrt{(10.938)^2 - (0.938)^2}\ \text{GeV} \approx 10.90\ \text{GeV}. $$

Thus $p \approx 10.90\ \text{GeV}/c = 5.78 \times 10^{-18}\ \text{kg·m/s}$. The required field is

$$ B = \frac{p}{qR} = \frac{5.78 \times 10^{-18}}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})(100)} \approx 0.361\ \text{T}. $$

Key Theorem — Synchrotron Radiation. A relativistic charged particle undergoing centripetal acceleration radiates electromagnetic energy at a power

$$ P = \frac{q^2 c}{6\pi \epsilon_0} \frac{\gamma^4}{R^2}, $$

where $\gamma$ is the Lorentz factor. This $\gamma^4$ dependence makes synchrotron radiation the dominant energy-loss mechanism at ultra-high energies and is simultaneously harnessed as an intense, tunable X-ray source in light-source facilities worldwide.

🌸 Particle Accelerator

🌸 Particle Accelerator

Definition. A particle accelerator is a device that uses electric and magnetic fields to accelerate charged particles to high kinetic energies. Electric fields do work on charges, increasing their kinetic energy, while magnetic fields provide centripetal force to bend particle trajectories without doing work. The two principal architectures are linear accelerators (linacs), where particles travel in a straight path through successive accelerating gaps, and circular accelerators (cyclotrons, synchrotrons), where magnetic fields constrain particles to repeated circular orbits so that the same accelerating structure can be applied multiple times.

In a cyclotron, two hollow D-shaped electrodes (dees) are placed in a uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$ perpendicular to the orbital plane. An alternating electric potential $V$ is applied across the gap. Each time a particle of charge $q$ and mass $m$ crosses the gap, it gains kinetic energy $\Delta K = qV$. Inside each dee, the particle follows a semicircular path of radius

$$ r = \frac{mv}{qB}, $$

and the period of revolution is

$$ T = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}, $$

which is independent of speed (for non-relativistic particles), allowing the alternating voltage to remain in resonance with the orbital motion.

Worked example. A proton ($q = 1.60\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, $m = 1.67\times10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$) is accelerated in a cyclotron with $B = 1.20\ \text{T}$ and dee radius $R = 0.50\ \text{m}$. Find the maximum kinetic energy.

The maximum speed occurs when $r = R$:

$$ v_{\max} = \frac{qBR}{m} = \frac{(1.60\times10^{-19})(1.20)(0.50)}{1.67\times10^{-27}} \approx 5.75\times10^{7}\ \text{m/s}. $$ $$ K_{\max} = \tfrac{1}{2}mv_{\max}^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(1.67\times10^{-27})(5.75\times10^7)^2 \approx 2.76\times10^{-12}\ \text{J} \approx 17.2\ \text{MeV}. $$

Key theorem — relativistic limit. As particle speed approaches $c$, the relativistic mass $\gamma m$ increases, violating the fixed-frequency resonance condition $T = 2\pi m/(qB)$. Synchrotrons compensate by increasing $B$ or adjusting the RF frequency synchronously with $\gamma$, maintaining resonance at arbitrarily high energies.

Payoff 🎯

A particle accelerator represents the supreme synthesis of classical and modern physics: it harnesses electromagnetic fields to drive charged particles to relativistic speeds, then exploits those particles as precision probes of matter at its most fundamental scale. The concept stands as the natural culmination of College Physics 2E because it demands fluency in every major domain traversed throughout the course—Newton's laws, electric and magnetic forces, special relativity, and quantum mechanical wave-particle duality—and unifies them into a single, coherent experimental instrument.

What the Accelerator Achieves. At its core, an accelerator applies the work-energy theorem to a charged particle of charge $q$ traversing a potential difference $\Delta V$, gaining kinetic energy $\Delta K = q\Delta V$. Repeated application across many accelerating gaps—or continuous circulation through a magnetic guide field satisfying $r = \frac{mv}{qB}$—drives the particle's total energy toward the relativistic regime, where $E^2 = (pc)^2 + (m_0 c^2)^2$ governs its motion rather than Newtonian mechanics. At the Large Hadron Collider, protons reach center-of-mass energies of $\sqrt{s} = 13\,\text{TeV}$, a regime utterly inaccessible to any classical description.

Worked Example. A proton ($m_p = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\,\text{kg}$, $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$) is accelerated through a potential difference of $\Delta V = 1.0\,\text{GV}$. Its kinetic energy is $K = q\Delta V = 1.6 \times 10^{-10}\,\text{J} = 1.0\,\text{GeV}$. Since $m_p c^2 \approx 938\,\text{MeV}$, this is firmly relativistic; the Lorentz factor is $\gamma = 1 + K/(m_p c^2) \approx 2.07$, and the proton travels at $v = c\sqrt{1 - \gamma^{-2}} \approx 0.88c$.

Key Theorem. The cyclotron resonance condition, $f = \frac{qB}{2\pi m}$, holds only in the non-relativistic limit. Once $\gamma$ becomes significant, the effective mass $\gamma m$ grows, destroying resonance unless the driving frequency is modulated—giving rise to the synchrotron design, where both $B$ and the RF frequency are ramped together to maintain orbit radius.

The accelerator's reach extends into nuclear medicine (proton therapy), materials science (synchrotron X-ray imaging), and fundamental particle discovery. To explore how the same relativistic beam that probes quark structure can be tuned to destroy a tumor with millimeter precision, follow the thread into proton therapy—where physics becomes medicine.
