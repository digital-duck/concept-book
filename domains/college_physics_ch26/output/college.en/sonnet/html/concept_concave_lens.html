🌱 Concave Lens

🌱 Concave Lens

Definition. A concave lens (also called a diverging lens) is a refracting optical element that is thinner at its center than at its edges — this geometry is the single new idea introduced here; the thin-lens equation, sign convention, and magnification formula carry over from the treatment of converging lenses. Light rays passing through a concave lens diverge after refraction, and the refracted rays appear to originate from a point on the same side as the incoming light. By convention, the focal length $f < 0$ for any diverging lens, reflecting the fact that parallel incident rays are spread outward rather than converged. Image formation is still governed by the familiar thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} $$

with $d_o > 0$ for a real object and $d_i < 0$ indicating a virtual image on the incoming-light side.

Worked Example. An object is placed $d_o = 30\text{ cm}$ in front of a concave lens with focal length $f = -15\text{ cm}$. Find the image distance and lateral magnification.

Applying the thin-lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{-15} - \frac{1}{30} = -\frac{2}{30} - \frac{1}{30} = -\frac{3}{30} = -\frac{1}{10} $$

Thus $d_i = -10\text{ cm}$. The negative sign confirms a virtual, upright image located $10\text{ cm}$ on the same side as the object. The lateral magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{-10}{30} = +\frac{1}{3} $$

The image is reduced to one-third the object's size and is upright.

Key Theorem. A concave lens always produces a virtual, upright, and diminished image for any real object, because solving the thin-lens equation explicitly gives $d_i = \frac{f\,d_o}{d_o + f}$, which is strictly negative whenever $f < 0$ and $d_o > 0$, and the resulting magnification $m = -d_i/d_o$ is strictly between zero and one.

Lab Exercise. Using an optical bench, a diverging lens ($f \approx -20\text{ cm}$), and a luminous object, measure image positions for $d_o = 20, 30, 40\text{ cm}$. Because the image is virtual, place a converging lens downstream to project it onto a screen. Compare measured $d_i$ values against theoretical predictions and compute the percent error for each trial.
