🌱 Thin Lens Equation

🌱 Thin Lens Equation

Definition. A thin lens is an optical element whose thickness is negligible compared to its focal length, allowing refraction to be treated as occurring at a single plane. For a lens with focal length $f$, object distance $d_o$ (measured from the lens to the object), and image distance $d_i$ (measured from the lens to the image), the thin lens equation states:

$$ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} $$

The focal length $f$ is positive for converging (convex) lenses and negative for diverging (concave) lenses. By the sign convention, $d_o > 0$ when the object is on the incoming-light side, and $d_i > 0$ when the image forms on the opposite side (a real image). A negative $d_i$ indicates a virtual image on the same side as the object. The lateral magnification $m = -d_i/d_o$ is the one additional quantity this section introduces: $|m| > 1$ indicates enlargement, and a negative sign indicates an inverted image.

Worked Example. A converging lens has focal length $f = 20\ \text{cm}$. An object is placed $d_o = 30\ \text{cm}$ in front of the lens. Find the image distance and magnification.

Applying the thin lens equation:

$$ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{20} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3-2}{60} = \frac{1}{60} $$

Thus $d_i = 60\ \text{cm}$. The image is real and located $60\ \text{cm}$ beyond the lens. The magnification is:

$$ m = -\frac{60}{30} = -2 $$

The image is inverted and twice the size of the object.

Key Theorem (Symmetry of Conjugate Points). For a thin lens with $f > 0$, if an object at $d_o$ produces an image at $d_i$, then placing an object at $d_i$ produces an image at $d_o$. This reversibility follows directly from the symmetry of the thin lens equation in $d_o$ and $d_i$. A special case: when $d_o = 2f$, substitution gives $d_i = 2f$ and $m = -1$, so the image is real, inverted, and the same size as the object — confirming that $d_o = 2f$ is the unique symmetric conjugate pair for a converging lens.

Lab Exercise. Place a converging lens of unknown focal length on an optical bench. Position a luminous object at three different distances ($d_o = 40,\ 60,\ 80\ \text{cm}$) and locate the corresponding real image for each. Use each measurement to compute $f$ via the thin lens equation, then average the results and compare to the manufacturer's specification.
