🌱 Resistance

🌱 Resistance

Definition. Electrical resistance is the opposition a material offers to the flow of electric current. For a conductor carrying current $I$ under an applied potential difference $V$, the resistance $R$ is defined by Ohm's Law:

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} $$

The SI unit of resistance is the ohm ($\Omega$), where $1\,\Omega = 1\,\text{V}/\text{A}$. Resistance arises microscopically from collisions between conduction electrons and the ionic lattice of the material; each collision transfers kinetic energy to the lattice as thermal energy, producing Joule heating. For a uniform conductor of length $L$, cross-sectional area $A$, and resistivity $\rho$, resistance is given by:

$$ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} $$

Resistivity $\rho$ is an intrinsic material property (units: $\Omega\cdot\text{m}$) that characterizes how strongly a material opposes current flow, depending on temperature, composition, and microstructure.

Worked Example. A copper wire ($\rho = 1.72\times10^{-8}\,\Omega\cdot\text{m}$) has length $L = 2.00\,\text{m}$ and circular cross-section of diameter $d = 1.00\,\text{mm}$. Find its resistance.

First, compute the cross-sectional area:

$$ A = \pi\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi\left(5.00\times10^{-4}\right)^2 = 7.854\times10^{-7}\,\text{m}^2 $$

Then apply the resistivity formula:

$$ R = \rho\frac{L}{A} = (1.72\times10^{-8})\frac{2.00}{7.854\times10^{-7}} = 4.38\times10^{-2}\,\Omega \approx 43.8\,\text{m}\Omega $$

This small value confirms that copper is an excellent conductor, making it suitable for household wiring.

Key Theorem. For a network of resistors in series, total resistance is additive: $R_{\text{series}} = \sum_i R_i$. For a parallel combination, the reciprocal resistances add: $R_{\text{parallel}}^{-1} = \sum_i R_i^{-1}$. These results follow directly from Kirchhoff's voltage and current laws and enable systematic analysis of arbitrarily complex resistive circuits.

Lab Exercise. Using a digital multimeter and a decade resistance box, measure $R$ across five resistance settings spanning $10\,\Omega$ to $10\,\text{k}\Omega$. For each setting, apply a known voltage $V$ and measure the resulting current $I$. Plot $V$ versus $I$; verify that the slope equals the set resistance and compute the percent deviation. Investigate how substituting a tungsten lamp for the resistor produces a nonlinear $V$–$I$ curve, demonstrating that resistivity—and hence resistance—is not constant for all materials under all conditions.
