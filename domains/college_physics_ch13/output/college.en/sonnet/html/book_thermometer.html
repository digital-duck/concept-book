🌱 Kinetic Energy

🌱 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is defined as

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

This scalar quantity represents the energy a body possesses by virtue of its motion. In SI units, mass is measured in kilograms and speed in meters per second, so kinetic energy carries units of joules ($1\,\text{J} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-2}$). Because $v^2$ appears in the expression, kinetic energy is always non-negative and depends only on the magnitude of the velocity, not its direction.

Worked Example. A 1200 kg automobile accelerates from rest to a highway speed of 27 m/s (approximately 97 km/h). Determine the kinetic energy acquired.

Applying the definition directly:

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(1200\,\text{kg})(27\,\text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1200)(729)\,\text{J} = 437{,}400\,\text{J} \approx 4.37 \times 10^5\,\text{J}. $$

This energy must be supplied by the engine and ultimately derives from the chemical energy of the fuel. The same quantity of energy must be dissipated—primarily as heat through the brakes—when the car returns to rest, illustrating the intimate connection between kinetic energy and the work required to change a body's state of motion.

Key Theorem: Work–Energy Theorem. For a particle subject to a net force $\vec{F}_{\text{net}}$, the net work $W_{\text{net}}$ done on the particle as it moves from position $A$ to position $B$ equals the change in its kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K = K_B - K_A = \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_A^2. $$

The proof follows directly from Newton's second law: $F_{\text{net}} = ma$, combined with the kinematic relation $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x$ for constant acceleration, or more generally via the line integral $W = \int_A^B \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}$. This theorem reduces dynamical problems to energy bookkeeping, avoiding detailed force analysis at every instant.

Lab Exercise. Using a dynamics track, a cart of measured mass, and a photogate timer, record the cart's speed at two distinct points along the track while a hanging mass exerts a constant net force. Compute $W_{\text{net}}$ from $F_{\text{net}}\cdot\Delta x$ and compare it to $\Delta K$ calculated from the photogate data. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify sources of systematic error, such as friction and rotational inertia of the wheels.

🍃 Temperature

🍃 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar intensive property that characterizes the thermal state of a system and determines the direction of spontaneous heat flow between bodies in thermal contact. Formally, two systems are in thermal equilibrium if and only if they share the same temperature — a statement codified by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system A is in thermal equilibrium with system C, and system B is in thermal equilibrium with system C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This transitive property makes temperature a well-defined, measurable quantity.

On the microscopic level, temperature is proportional to the mean translational kinetic energy of molecules in an ideal gas:

$$ \bar{K} = \frac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2} = \frac{3}{2}k_B T $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\ \mathrm{J\,K^{-1}}$ is the Boltzmann constant and $T$ is the absolute temperature in kelvins (K). The SI unit is the kelvin; the Celsius scale connects to it by $T(\mathrm{K}) = T(°\mathrm{C}) + 273.15$.

Worked Example. A sample of nitrogen gas ($m = 4.65 \times 10^{-26}\ \mathrm{kg}$ per molecule) is held at $T = 300\ \mathrm{K}$. Find the root-mean-square speed $v_{\mathrm{rms}}$.

Rearranging the kinetic-energy relation:

$$ v_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\overline{v^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_B T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3(1.381\times10^{-23})(300)}{4.65\times10^{-26}}} $$ $$ v_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{1.243\times10^{-20}}{4.65\times10^{-26}}} \approx \sqrt{2.674\times10^{5}} \approx 517\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}} $$

This result illustrates that temperature alone sets the characteristic molecular speed for a given species; heavier molecules at the same $T$ move more slowly.

Key Theorem. For an ideal gas, absolute temperature is directly proportional to mean molecular kinetic energy: $T = \frac{2\bar{K}}{3k_B}$. Because $\bar{K} \geq 0$, absolute zero ($T = 0\ \mathrm{K}$) represents the lower bound of all temperatures, a limit approached but never reached by any finite system.

Lab Exercise. Using a constant-volume gas thermometer containing a fixed mass of helium, record the pressure $P$ at three known temperatures (ice water, boiling water, and a third reference). Plot $P$ vs. $T$ (in kelvins) and verify linearity. Extrapolate to $P = 0$ and confirm the intercept approaches $T = 0\ \mathrm{K}$, empirically validating absolute zero and the ideal-gas temperature scale.

🌱 Volume

🌱 Volume

Definition. Volume is the measure of the three-dimensional extent of a region in space. For a continuous matter distribution, the volume $V$ of a body is obtained by integrating over all infinitesimal volume elements $dV$:

$$ V = \int_{\mathcal{R}} dV $$

In Cartesian coordinates, $dV = dx\, dy\, dz$; in cylindrical coordinates, $dV = r\, dr\, d\phi\, dz$; in spherical coordinates, $dV = r^2 \sin\theta\, dr\, d\theta\, d\phi$. The SI unit of volume is the cubic meter ($\text{m}^3$), though liters ($1\,\text{L} = 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3$) are commonly used in thermodynamic contexts.

Worked Example. A solid cylinder of radius $R = 0.050\,\text{m}$ and height $h = 0.20\,\text{m}$ is filled with an ideal gas. Compute the volume of the container.

Using cylindrical coordinates and integrating:

$$ V = \int_0^{2\pi} d\phi \int_0^R r\, dr \int_0^h dz = 2\pi \cdot \frac{R^2}{2} \cdot h = \pi R^2 h $$

Substituting:

$$ V = \pi (0.050)^2 (0.20) = \pi (2.5 \times 10^{-3})(0.20) \approx 1.57 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3 $$

This volume directly enters thermodynamic equations of state such as the ideal gas law $PV = nRT$.

Key Theorem — Cavalieri's Principle. If two solids have equal cross-sectional areas at every height $z \in [0, h]$, then they have equal volumes. Formally, if $A_1(z) = A_2(z)$ for all $z$, then:

$$ V_1 = \int_0^h A_1(z)\, dz = \int_0^h A_2(z)\, dz = V_2 $$

This principle justifies the formula $V = Ah$ for any prism or cylinder of uniform cross-section $A$, regardless of the solid's lateral profile, and underpins the derivation of volumes for oblique cylinders and Cavalieri-equivalent solids encountered throughout physics and engineering.

Lab Exercise. Fill a graduated cylinder with water to a known volume $V_0$. Submerge a small irregularly shaped object (e.g., a metal bolt) and record the new reading $V_1$. By Archimedes' displacement principle, the object's volume equals $V_1 - V_0$. Compare your measured value to a geometric estimate obtained by approximating the bolt as a cylinder. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify sources of systematic error, including surface tension effects at the meniscus and partial submersion.

🍃 Thermal Expansion

🍃 Thermal Expansion

Definition. When the temperature of a substance changes, the average kinetic energy of its constituent particles changes, altering the mean separation between particles. For most solids and liquids, an increase in temperature produces an increase in volume — a phenomenon called thermal expansion. For a solid rod of initial length $L_0$ subjected to a temperature change $\Delta T$, the change in length is

$$ \Delta L = \alpha L_0 \,\Delta T, $$

where $\alpha$ is the coefficient of linear thermal expansion (units: $\mathrm{K^{-1}}$ or $\mathrm{°C^{-1}}$), a material-dependent constant. Extending this to three dimensions, the volumetric (or cubical) expansion of a solid is

$$ \Delta V = \beta V_0 \,\Delta T, \qquad \beta \approx 3\alpha, $$

because volume scales as the cube of a linear dimension and cross terms are negligible for small $\Delta T$.

Worked Example. A steel railroad rail has length $L_0 = 12.0\ \mathrm{m}$ at $T_i = 0\ \mathrm{°C}$. On a summer day the temperature rises to $T_f = 35.0\ \mathrm{°C}$. Given $\alpha_\text{steel} = 1.20 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{°C^{-1}}$, find the change in length.

$$ \Delta T = 35.0 - 0 = 35.0\ \mathrm{°C} $$ $$ \Delta L = (1.20 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{°C^{-1}})(12.0\ \mathrm{m})(35.0\ \mathrm{°C}) = 5.04 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 5.0\ \mathrm{mm}. $$

This small but non-negligible gap motivates the expansion joints placed between rail sections to prevent buckling.

Key Theorem. For isotropic materials, linear and volumetric expansion coefficients satisfy $\beta = 3\alpha$ exactly in the limit $\Delta T \to 0$. This follows from differentiating $V = L^3$ with respect to $T$ and applying the chain rule: $\frac{dV}{dT} = 3L^2 \frac{dL}{dT} = 3L^2(\alpha L) = 3\alpha V$.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a metal rod of measured length $L_0$ at room temperature $T_0$. Submerge it in a water bath and raise the temperature to $T_f$ in $10\ \mathrm{°C}$ increments. Record $\Delta L$ at each step using a micrometer. Plot $\Delta L$ versus $\Delta T$ and extract $\alpha$ from the slope, then compare to the tabulated value for the material. Estimate the uncertainty in $\alpha$ arising from measurement precision and discuss sources of systematic error.

🌸 Thermometer

🌸 Thermometer

Definition. A thermometer is an instrument that measures temperature by exploiting the temperature-dependent behavior of a physical property of a thermometric substance. The operating principle rests on thermal equilibrium: when a thermometer is placed in contact with an object and allowed to reach equilibrium, both share the same temperature. Any property that varies monotonically and reproducibly with temperature — volume, electrical resistance, pressure, or emitted radiation — can serve as the thermometric property. The temperature scale is defined by assigning numerical values at reproducible fixed points (e.g., the ice point at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ and the steam point at $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ at standard pressure) and interpolating between them.

For a liquid-in-glass thermometer, the thermometric property is the volume $V$ of the liquid. Over a limited range, the volume varies linearly with temperature:

$$ V(T) = V_0\left(1 + \beta\, T\right) $$

where $V_0$ is the volume at $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ and $\beta$ is the coefficient of volume expansion of the liquid.

Worked Example. A mercury thermometer has a bulb volume $V_0 = 0.400\,\text{cm}^3$ and a capillary tube of cross-sectional area $A = 1.20 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{cm}^2$. The coefficient of volume expansion for mercury (accounting for glass expansion) is $\beta_{\text{eff}} = 1.82 \times 10^{-4}\,^\circ\text{C}^{-1}$. Find the length the mercury column rises when the temperature increases by $\Delta T = 50.0\,^\circ\text{C}$.

$$ \Delta V = V_0\,\beta_{\text{eff}}\,\Delta T = (0.400)(1.82\times10^{-4})(50.0) = 3.64\times10^{-3}\,\text{cm}^3 $$ $$ \Delta L = \frac{\Delta V}{A} = \frac{3.64\times10^{-3}}{1.20\times10^{-3}} \approx 3.03\,\text{cm} $$

The column rises approximately $3.03\,\text{cm}$ for a $50\,^\circ\text{C}$ increase.

Key Theorem (Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics). If object A is in thermal equilibrium with object C, and object B is also in thermal equilibrium with object C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This law legitimizes the thermometer: the instrument (C) can reliably compare the temperatures of any two bodies without requiring direct contact between them.

Lab Exercise. Submerge a calibrated thermometer in an ice-water bath and record the equilibrium reading; repeat with boiling water at measured atmospheric pressure. Use these two fixed points to determine your thermometer's calibration offset and scale factor. Predict the column length at $37.0\,^\circ\text{C}$ and verify experimentally.

Payoff 🎯

Thermometer is the instrument through which the abstract concept of temperature acquires physical meaning. A thermometer does not merely report a number—it operationalizes the zeroth law of thermodynamics, which asserts that if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This logical structure permits temperature to function as a transitive, measurable quantity. The thermometer is therefore not an incidental tool but the conceptual keystone of the entire chapter: without it, assertions about heat flow, internal energy, and thermal expansion remain qualitative. With it, the entire quantitative machinery of thermodynamics becomes anchored to observation.

Consider a mercury-in-glass thermometer placed in contact with a gas sample. As heat flows from the sample into the mercury column, the liquid expands according to

$$ \Delta L = L_0 \, \alpha \, \Delta T, $$

where $\alpha$ is the coefficient of linear thermal expansion and $L_0$ is the reference length. Calibrating two fixed points—the ice point ($0\,^\circ\text{C}$) and the steam point ($100\,^\circ\text{C}$) at standard pressure—divides the expansion into 100 equal intervals and defines the Celsius scale. A worked reading: if the column rises $4.2\,\text{mm}$ out of a full-scale $21.0\,\text{mm}$ range, the temperature reads

$$ T = 0 + \frac{4.2}{21.0} \times 100 = 20\,^\circ\text{C}. $$

The central theorem governing thermometric consistency is the Zeroth Law: thermal equilibrium is an equivalence relation on the set of physical systems. Any instrument satisfying this relation—bimetallic strip, thermocouple, resistance thermometer, or pyrometer—yields the same temperature assignment for the same state, provided both are properly calibrated. This universality is what elevates temperature from a local sensation to a state function.

A laboratory exercise makes this precise: suspend two identical resistance thermistors in a water bath and a third in thermal contact only with the bath. Measure resistance $R(T) = R_0 \, e^{B(1/T - 1/T_0)}$, verify that all three devices converge to the same equilibrium reading, and confirm the zeroth law directly.

Mastery of the thermometer unlocks every subsequent domain in thermodynamics—ideal gas behavior, calorimetry, heat engines, and entropy—because each depends on reliable temperature measurement.
