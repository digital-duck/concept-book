Concept Book: Coordinate System

Coordinate System

Every map you have ever used works because someone agreed on a reference frame — a starting point and a pair of directions. Without that agreement, "three blocks east" means nothing. A coordinate system does the same thing for mathematics: it gives us a precise language to describe where things are.

Definition. A *coordinate system* in the plane is a pair of perpendicular number lines, called axes, that intersect at a common zero point called the origin $O$. The horizontal axis is typically labeled $x$ and the vertical axis $y$. Every point $P$ in the plane is uniquely identified by an ordered pair $(a, b)$, where $a$ is the signed distance from the $y$-axis and $b$ is the signed distance from the $x$-axis. This framework is called the Cartesian coordinate system, after René Descartes.

The axes divide the plane into four quadrants, numbered counterclockwise:

QuadrantSign of $x$Sign of $y$
I$+$$+$
II$-$$+$
III$-$$-$
IV$+$$-$

Worked example. Plot the point $P = (-3, 4)$ and determine its quadrant. Starting at the origin, move $3$ units left along the $x$-axis (since $-3 < 0$) and then $4$ units up parallel to the $y$-axis (since $4 > 0$). Because $x < 0$ and $y > 0$, the point lies in Quadrant II.

Why it works. The key property is uniqueness: every point corresponds to exactly one ordered pair, and every ordered pair corresponds to exactly one point. This one-to-one correspondence is what lets us translate geometric problems — distances, intersections, slopes — into algebraic equations, and vice versa. It is the bridge between geometry and algebra.

Practice problem. A triangle has vertices $A = (1, 2)$, $B = (5, 2)$, and $C = (1, 6)$. In which quadrant does each vertex lie? What is the length of side $AB$?
