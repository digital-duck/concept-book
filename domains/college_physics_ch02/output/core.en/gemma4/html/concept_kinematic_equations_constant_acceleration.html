🍃 Kinematic Equations Constant Acceleration

🍃 Kinematic Equations Constant Acceleration

Imagine driving a car that is braking smoothly, bringing it to a perfect stop over a measured distance. To understand how far you travel and how quickly your speed changes during this process, we rely on kinematic equations. These tools are fundamental for analyzing motion when an object experiences constant acceleration—meaning the rate of change of its velocity remains steady.

Definition: Kinematic equations relate displacement ($\Delta x$), initial velocity ($u$), final velocity ($v$), time ($t$), and constant acceleration ($a$). They assume continuous, uniform changes in speed. The primary equations are:

$$ v = u + at $$ $$ \Delta x = \frac{1}{2}(u + v)t $$ $$ \Delta x = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 $$

Worked Example: A runner starts from rest ($u=0$) and accelerates uniformly at $3.0 \text{ m/s}^2$. Using the first equation, what is their velocity after $4.0$ seconds? Solution: We use $v = u + at$. Plugging in the values: $v = 0 + (3.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(4.0 \text{ s})$. The final velocity is $12.0 \text{ m/s}$.
