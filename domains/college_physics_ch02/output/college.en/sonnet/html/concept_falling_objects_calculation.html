🌸 Falling Objects Calculation

🌸 Falling Objects Calculation

Definition. A falling object is one subject exclusively to gravitational acceleration, with all other forces (air resistance, buoyancy) neglected. Under this idealization, every object near Earth's surface experiences a constant downward acceleration

$$ g = 9.80 \, \text{m/s}^2 $$

directed toward Earth's center. Taking downward as the positive direction (a common convention in free-fall problems), the kinematic equations for constant acceleration reduce to:

$$ v = v_0 + g t $$ $$ y = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2} g t^2 $$ $$ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2g \Delta y $$

where $v_0$ is the initial velocity, $v$ is the velocity at time $t$, and $\Delta y$ is the displacement measured downward from the release point.

Worked Example. A stone is dropped from rest from the edge of a cliff. Determine its velocity and displacement after $t = 3.00 \, \text{s}$.

Given: $v_0 = 0$, $g = 9.80 \, \text{m/s}^2$, $t = 3.00 \, \text{s}$.

Velocity:

$$ v = v_0 + g t = 0 + (9.80)(3.00) = 29.4 \, \text{m/s \, (downward)} $$

Displacement:

$$ y = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2} g t^2 = 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(9.80)(3.00)^2 = 44.1 \, \text{m} $$

The stone has fallen $44.1 \, \text{m}$ and is moving at $29.4 \, \text{m/s}$ downward.

Key Theorem — Symmetry of Free Fall. For an object launched vertically upward with initial speed $v_0$, the time to reach maximum height equals the time to return to the launch point, and the speed upon return equals $v_0$. This follows directly from the time-reversal symmetry of $v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g\Delta y$: at $\Delta y = 0$, one recovers $|v| = v_0$. Consequently, the total flight time is

$$ T = \frac{2v_0}{g} $$

Lab Exercise. Drop a dense rubber ball from a measured height $H$ onto a motion sensor pad and record the time of flight $t_f$. Using $H = \tfrac{1}{2} g t_f^2$, calculate $g$ experimentally. Repeat for three different heights and average your results. Compare to the standard value $9.80 \, \text{m/s}^2$ and estimate the percentage discrepancy introduced by neglecting air resistance.
