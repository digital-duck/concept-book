🍃 Perfect Fourth

When you hear the interval from C up to F, that sound often feels incredibly stable and consonant—it’s one of the foundational sounds of Western harmony. This specific harmonic relationship is called the perfect fourth ($\text{P}4$). It represents a fundamental building block in music theory, linking the simplest whole-number ratios found in natural resonance.

Definition: The perfect fourth is an interval characterized by its specific mathematical ratio and fixed size regardless of the starting pitch. Theoretically, it spans exactly five semitones (or $9/12$ of an octave). Its precise frequency ratio relative to the root note is $\frac{4}{3}$. Intervals defined by simple integer ratios are generally considered "perfect" because they minimize beating frequencies when played simultaneously.

Worked Example: Consider the key of G major. If we take the interval starting on G and ending on C, we have a perfect fourth (G to C). Using semitones, if we assign G a value of 0, we count up through G#, A, A#, B, C. This confirms the interval size is exactly $5$ semitones. We can verify this using the ratio: $\frac{4}{3}$ represents the exact frequency relationship between the two notes.
