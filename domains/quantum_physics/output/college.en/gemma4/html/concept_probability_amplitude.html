🌱 Probability Amplitude

Probability amplitude, often denoted by $\psi$, is a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics that describes the state of a physical system. Unlike classical probability $P(x)$, which must be real and non-negative, the probability amplitude $\psi(x)$ is generally a complex-valued function. This complexity allows $\psi$ to encode not only the likelihood of finding a particle at a certain position but also crucial phase information, which governs interference effects—phenomena impossible to describe using classical probability alone. The relationship between measurable classical probabilities and the underlying quantum amplitudes is established by the Born rule: the probability density $P(x)$ is given by the squared magnitude of the amplitude vector $\psi$: $P(x) = |\psi(x)|^2$.

Consider an electron passing through a double-slit apparatus. Classically, if we could know which slit it passed through, the total probability would simply be the sum of probabilities from each path: $P_{classical} = P_1 + P_2$. Quantum mechanically, however, the state is described by a superposition of amplitudes: $\Psi = \psi_1 + \psi_2$.
