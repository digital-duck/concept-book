🌱 Acceleration Due To Gravity

🌱 Acceleration Due To Gravity

Definition. Near the surface of the Earth, all objects experience a constant downward gravitational acceleration denoted $g$. Its standard value is

$$ g = 9.80 \, \mathrm{m/s^2} $$

directed toward the center of the Earth (taken as the negative direction when the upward direction is positive). This value arises from Newton's law of universal gravitation: for an object of mass $m$ at distance $r$ from Earth's center (mass $M_E$), the gravitational force is $F = GM_E m / r^2$, giving acceleration $a = GM_E / r^2$. At the surface, $r \approx R_E = 6.371 \times 10^6 \, \mathrm{m}$, yielding $g \approx 9.80 \, \mathrm{m/s^2}$. The value varies slightly with latitude and altitude but is treated as constant for problems confined to small vertical scales.

Worked Example. A ball is dropped from rest at a height of $h = 45.0 \, \mathrm{m}$. Find the time to reach the ground and its speed at impact.

Taking downward as positive with $y_0 = 0$, $v_0 = 0$, and $a = g = 9.80 \, \mathrm{m/s^2}$:

$$ y = \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(45.0)}{9.80}} = \sqrt{9.184} \approx 3.03 \, \mathrm{s} $$

The impact speed is:

$$ v = g t = (9.80)(3.03) \approx 29.7 \, \mathrm{m/s} $$

Alternatively, using $v^2 = 2gh$:

$$ v = \sqrt{2(9.80)(45.0)} = \sqrt{882} \approx 29.7 \, \mathrm{m/s} $$

Key Theorem (Equivalence of Gravitational and Inertial Mass). Galileo's insight — confirmed to extraordinary precision by the Eötvös experiment — is that all objects, regardless of composition or mass, fall with the same acceleration $g$ in vacuum. This equivalence of gravitational mass (which determines gravitational force) and inertial mass (which resists acceleration) is exact to one part in $10^{12}$, and forms a foundational postulate of Einstein's general relativity.

Lab Exercise. Using a motion sensor and a freely falling picket fence, record position versus time data. Fit a quadratic $y(t) = \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 + v_0 t + y_0$ to extract the experimental value of $g$. Compare your result to $9.80 \, \mathrm{m/s^2}$ and compute the percent error. Identify two systematic sources of uncertainty in the measurement.
