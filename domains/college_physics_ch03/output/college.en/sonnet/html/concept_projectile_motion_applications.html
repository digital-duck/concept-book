🌸 Projectile Motion Applications

🌸 Projectile Motion Applications

Definition

Projectile motion describes the two-dimensional kinematics of an object launched with an initial velocity and subject only to gravitational acceleration. The horizontal and vertical components of motion are independent: horizontal velocity remains constant (neglecting air resistance), while vertical motion follows constant downward acceleration $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$. For a projectile launched from the origin with speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, the governing equations are:

$$ x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t $$ $$ y(t) = v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}g t^2 $$ $$ v_x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta, \qquad v_y(t) = v_0 \sin\theta - g t $$

The trajectory, obtained by eliminating $t$, is the parabola:

$$ y = x\tan\theta - \frac{g x^2}{2v_0^2 \cos^2\theta} $$

Worked Example

A ball is kicked from ground level at $v_0 = 20.0\ \text{m/s}$ and $\theta = 37.0°$. Find (a) the time of flight, (b) the horizontal range, and (c) the maximum height.

Initial components:

$$ v_{0x} = 20.0\cos 37.0° = 15.97\ \text{m/s}, \quad v_{0y} = 20.0\sin 37.0° = 12.04\ \text{m/s} $$

(a) Time of flight: Setting $y = 0$ gives $t(v_{0y} - \tfrac{1}{2}gt) = 0$, so:

$$ T = \frac{2v_{0y}}{g} = \frac{2(12.04)}{9.80} \approx 2.46\ \text{s} $$

(b) Horizontal range:

$$ R = v_{0x} \cdot T = 15.97 \times 2.46 \approx 39.3\ \text{m} $$

(c) Maximum height: At the apex, $v_y = 0$, so $t_{\text{apex}} = v_{0y}/g = 1.23\ \text{s}$:

$$ H = v_{0y} t_{\text{apex}} - \frac{1}{2}g t_{\text{apex}}^2 = 12.04(1.23) - 4.90(1.23)^2 \approx 7.40\ \text{m} $$

Key Theorem

The range formula for symmetric projectile motion (launch and landing at equal height) is:

$$ R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g} $$

Maximum range occurs at $\theta = 45°$, yielding $R_{\max} = v_0^2/g$. Complementary angles (e.g., $30°$ and $60°$) produce identical ranges because $\sin 2\theta = \sin(180° - 2\theta)$.

Lab Exercise

Using a projectile launcher, fire a steel ball at three angles—$30°$, $45°$, and $60°$—keeping $v_0$ fixed. Measure the horizontal range with a meter tape and compare against the theoretical predictions. Quantify the fractional deviation attributable to air drag and rolling friction at the launch barrel, and determine whether the measured optimal angle shifts above or below $45°$ when these dissipative effects are present.
