🍃 Trajectory

🍃 Trajectory

Definition. The trajectory of a particle is the geometric path traced by its position vector $\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + y(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}}$ in space as time $t$ varies over some interval. The trajectory is a curve in space, entirely independent of the time parametrization; it represents where the particle has been, not how fast it moved there. In two-dimensional kinematics, the trajectory is obtained by eliminating the parameter $t$ from the component equations $x(t)$ and $y(t)$, yielding a single relation $y = f(x)$ that depends only on spatial coordinates.

Worked Example. Consider a projectile launched from the origin with initial speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, in the absence of air resistance. The kinematic equations are:

$$ x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t, \qquad y(t) = v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2. $$

Solving the first equation for $t$ gives $t = x/(v_0\cos\theta)$. Substituting into the second:

$$ y = v_0\sin\theta \cdot \frac{x}{v_0\cos\theta} - \frac{1}{2}g\left(\frac{x}{v_0\cos\theta}\right)^2 = x\tan\theta - \frac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}\,x^2. $$

This has the form $y = Ax - Bx^2$ — a downward-opening parabola. The trajectory of any projectile under uniform gravitational acceleration is therefore parabolic, regardless of the launch speed or angle.

Key Theorem. For any particle moving under constant acceleration $\mathbf{a}$, the trajectory lies entirely within the plane spanned by the initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$ and $\mathbf{a}$. Moreover, if $\mathbf{v}_0$ and $\mathbf{a}$ are non-parallel, the trajectory is parabolic; the quadratic dependence of $y$ on $x$ is a direct consequence of the constant, uniform nature of the acceleration, and holds for any launch speed or angle within that plane.

Lab Exercise. A ball is launched horizontally from a table of height $H = 1.20\,\text{m}$ with initial speed $v_0 = 3.50\,\text{m/s}$. (a) Derive the equation of the trajectory $y(x)$, taking the launch point as the origin with $y$ positive upward. (b) Determine the horizontal distance $x$ at which the ball strikes the floor ($y = -H$). (c) Sketch the trajectory, labeling the launch point and landing point. Use $g = 9.80\,\text{m/s}^2$.
