🌱 Vector

🌱 Vector

Definition. A vector is a mathematical object characterized by both a magnitude and a direction. In two-dimensional Cartesian coordinates, a vector $\vec{A}$ is written in component form as

$$ \vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j}, $$

where $A_x$ and $A_y$ are the scalar components along the $x$- and $y$-axes, respectively, and $\hat{i}$, $\hat{j}$ are the unit vectors along those axes. The magnitude of $\vec{A}$ is

$$ |\vec{A}| = A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}, $$

and the direction angle $\theta$ measured counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis satisfies $\tan\theta = A_y/A_x$. In three dimensions the representation extends to $\vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j} + A_z\,\hat{k}$.

Worked Example. A hiker walks $3.0\,\text{km}$ due east, then $4.0\,\text{km}$ due north. Find the resultant displacement vector.

Let $\vec{A} = 3.0\,\hat{i}\,\text{km}$ and $\vec{B} = 4.0\,\hat{j}\,\text{km}$. Vector addition is performed component-wise:

$$ \vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B} = (3.0\,\hat{i} + 4.0\,\hat{j})\,\text{km}. $$

The magnitude is

$$ R = \sqrt{(3.0)^2 + (4.0)^2} = \sqrt{9.0 + 16.0} = 5.0\,\text{km}, $$

and the direction is

$$ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{4.0}{3.0}\right) \approx 53.1^\circ \text{ north of east.} $$

The result is consistent with the 3-4-5 Pythagorean triple, serving as a useful check.

Key Theorem (Parallelogram Law of Addition). For any two vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$, their sum $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$ is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by placing $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ tail-to-tail. Equivalently, in the triangle (tip-to-tail) method, $\vec{B}$ is placed at the tip of $\vec{A}$, and $\vec{R}$ extends from the tail of $\vec{A}$ to the tip of $\vec{B}$. Both constructions yield identical results, confirming that vector addition is commutative: $\vec{A}+\vec{B} = \vec{B}+\vec{A}$.

🌱 Displacement

🌱 Displacement

Definition. Displacement is the vector quantity that describes the change in position of an object. If a particle occupies position $\vec{r}_1$ at time $t_1$ and position $\vec{r}_2$ at time $t_2$, its displacement is defined as

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_2 - \vec{r}_1. $$

In one dimension, with positions given by coordinates $x_1$ and $x_2$ along a chosen axis, displacement reduces to the scalar

$$ \Delta x = x_2 - x_1. $$

Displacement differs fundamentally from distance traveled: displacement depends only on the initial and final positions, whereas distance accumulates the total path length traversed. A runner who completes one full lap of a 400 m track has traveled 400 m but has zero displacement.

Worked Example. A car starts at the origin, drives due east 12 km, then turns and drives 5 km due north. Using a coordinate system with east as the positive $x$-axis and north as the positive $y$-axis, the initial position is $\vec{r}_1 = (0, 0)\ \text{km}$ and the final position is $\vec{r}_2 = (12, 5)\ \text{km}$. The displacement vector is

$$ \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_2 - \vec{r}_1 = (12\ \hat{x} + 5\ \hat{y})\ \text{km}, $$

with magnitude

$$ |\Delta \vec{r}| = \sqrt{(12)^2 + (5)^2} = \sqrt{169} = 13\ \text{km}. $$

The total distance driven is $12 + 5 = 17\ \text{km}$, confirming that distance and displacement are not equivalent for a non-straight path.

Key Theorem. For motion in any number of dimensions, displacement is path-independent: it is determined entirely by the endpoints $\vec{r}_1$ and $\vec{r}_2$. This follows directly from the definition $\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_2 - \vec{r}_1$, which contains no information about intermediate positions. As a consequence, if an object returns to its starting point, $\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{0}$ regardless of the path taken. This property makes displacement the natural variable for kinematic equations involving velocity: the average velocity $\bar{\vec{v}} = \Delta \vec{r}/\Delta t$ is well-defined and physically meaningful precisely because it is based on displacement rather than distance.

🍃 Two Dimensional Kinematics

🍃 Two Dimensional Kinematics

Definition. Two-dimensional kinematics applies the kinematic equations already established for one-dimensional motion to planar trajectories by exploiting a single key principle: the independence of perpendicular components. Motion along $x$ does not influence motion along $y$; each axis evolves according to its own one-dimensional equation simultaneously, with the same time parameter $t$ shared between them. Given an initial position $\vec{r}_0 = x_0\,\hat{i} + y_0\,\hat{j}$ and initial velocity $\vec{v}_0 = v_{x0}\,\hat{i} + v_{y0}\,\hat{j}$, the component equations under constant acceleration $\vec{a} = a_x\,\hat{i} + a_y\,\hat{j}$ are simply the familiar 1D equations applied axis by axis:

$$ x = x_0 + v_{x0}\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}a_x t^2, \qquad y = y_0 + v_{y0}\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}a_y t^2 $$ $$ v_x = v_{x0} + a_x t, \qquad v_y = v_{y0} + a_y t $$

The magnitude and direction of any vector quantity are recovered via $|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$ and $\theta = \arctan(v_y/v_x)$.

Worked Example. A ball is launched from ground level with speed $v_0 = 20\,\text{m/s}$ at $\theta_0 = 30°$ above horizontal. Find the range $R$ and maximum height $H$.

Decompose: $v_{x0} = 20\cos30° = 10\sqrt{3}\,\text{m/s}$, $v_{y0} = 20\sin30° = 10\,\text{m/s}$. Apply $a_x = 0$, $a_y = -g = -9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$ independently.

Time of flight: setting $y = 0$ gives $t_f = \dfrac{2v_{y0}}{g} = \dfrac{20}{9.8} \approx 2.04\,\text{s}$.

Range: $R = v_{x0}\,t_f = 10\sqrt{3}\times 2.04 \approx 35.3\,\text{m}$.

Maximum height: $H = \dfrac{v_{y0}^2}{2g} = \dfrac{100}{19.6} \approx 5.10\,\text{m}$.

Key Theorem (Parabolic Trajectory). When $a_x = 0$ and $a_y = -g$, eliminating $t$ between the two position equations yields $y = x\tan\theta_0 - \dfrac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\!\theta_0}\,x^2$, which is a downward-opening parabola. The independence of components is what makes this elimination straightforward: the $x$-equation gives $t = x / v_{x0}$, which substitutes directly into the $y$-equation without coupling.

🍃 Vector Components

🍃 Vector Components

Definition. A vector $\vec{A}$ in two dimensions can be uniquely decomposed into mutually perpendicular parts along chosen coordinate axes. If $\theta$ denotes the angle $\vec{A}$ makes with the positive $x$-axis (measured counterclockwise), then the scalar components of $\vec{A}$ are

$$ A_x = A\cos\theta, \qquad A_y = A\sin\theta, $$

where $A = |\vec{A}|$ is the magnitude. The vector is then written

$$ \vec{A} = A_x\,\hat{i} + A_y\,\hat{j}, $$

with $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$ the unit vectors along $x$ and $y$, respectively. Conversely, given components, the magnitude and direction are recovered by

$$ A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}, \qquad \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{A_y}{A_x}\right), $$

where the quadrant of $\theta$ must be resolved using the signs of both components.

Worked Example. A displacement vector has magnitude $|\vec{d}| = 35.0\text{ m}$ directed at $\theta = 127°$ from the positive $x$-axis. Find its components.

$$ d_x = (35.0\text{ m})\cos 127° = (35.0)(-0.602) = -21.1\text{ m}, $$ $$ d_y = (35.0\text{ m})\sin 127° = (35.0)(0.799) = +28.0\text{ m}. $$

The negative $x$-component confirms the vector points into the second quadrant, consistent with $90° < 127° < 180°$. To verify: $\sqrt{(-21.1)^2+(28.0)^2} = \sqrt{445+784} = \sqrt{1229} \approx 35.1\text{ m}$, matching the original magnitude within rounding.

Key Theorem (Component Addition). Vector addition reduces to independent scalar addition along each axis. For $\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}$,

$$ R_x = A_x + B_x, \qquad R_y = A_y + B_y. $$

This follows directly from the distributive property of scalar multiplication over vector addition and the orthogonality of $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$. The theorem extends straightforwardly to three dimensions by appending $R_z = A_z + B_z$.

🌱 Acceleration Due To Gravity

🌱 Acceleration Due To Gravity

Definition. Near the surface of the Earth, all objects experience a constant downward gravitational acceleration denoted $g$. Its standard value is

$$ g = 9.80 \, \mathrm{m/s^2} $$

directed toward the center of the Earth (taken as the negative direction when the upward direction is positive). This value arises from Newton's law of universal gravitation: for an object of mass $m$ at distance $r$ from Earth's center (mass $M_E$), the gravitational force is $F = GM_E m / r^2$, giving acceleration $a = GM_E / r^2$. At the surface, $r \approx R_E = 6.371 \times 10^6 \, \mathrm{m}$, yielding $g \approx 9.80 \, \mathrm{m/s^2}$. The value varies slightly with latitude and altitude but is treated as constant for problems confined to small vertical scales.

Worked Example. A ball is dropped from rest at a height of $h = 45.0 \, \mathrm{m}$. Find the time to reach the ground and its speed at impact.

Taking downward as positive with $y_0 = 0$, $v_0 = 0$, and $a = g = 9.80 \, \mathrm{m/s^2}$:

$$ y = \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(45.0)}{9.80}} = \sqrt{9.184} \approx 3.03 \, \mathrm{s} $$

The impact speed is:

$$ v = g t = (9.80)(3.03) \approx 29.7 \, \mathrm{m/s} $$

Alternatively, using $v^2 = 2gh$:

$$ v = \sqrt{2(9.80)(45.0)} = \sqrt{882} \approx 29.7 \, \mathrm{m/s} $$

Key Theorem (Equivalence of Gravitational and Inertial Mass). Galileo's insight — confirmed to extraordinary precision by the Eötvös experiment — is that all objects, regardless of composition or mass, fall with the same acceleration $g$ in vacuum. This equivalence of gravitational mass (which determines gravitational force) and inertial mass (which resists acceleration) is exact to one part in $10^{12}$, and forms a foundational postulate of Einstein's general relativity.

🍃 Independence Of Perpendicular Motions

🍃 Independence Of Perpendicular Motions

Definition. When an object undergoes two-dimensional motion, its displacement, velocity, and acceleration can be resolved into components along any two mutually perpendicular axes. The principle of independence of perpendicular motions states that the motion along one axis proceeds entirely independently of the motion along the perpendicular axis. Mathematically, if $\hat{x}$ and $\hat{y}$ denote orthogonal unit vectors, then the equations of motion decouple:

$$ x(t) = x_0 + v_{x0}\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}a_x t^2, \qquad y(t) = y_0 + v_{y0}\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}a_y t^2. $$

Each equation is solved independently; knowledge of $x(t)$ conveys no information about $y(t)$ beyond the shared time parameter $t$.

Worked Example. A ball is launched horizontally from a cliff of height $H = 45\ \text{m}$ with initial speed $v_{x0} = 20\ \text{m/s}$. Taking downward as positive $y$ with $a_y = g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$ and $a_x = 0$:

  • Vertical: $H = \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2 \Rightarrow t = \sqrt{2H/g} = \sqrt{90/9.8} \approx 3.03\ \text{s}$.
  • Horizontal: $x = v_{x0}\,t = 20 \times 3.03 \approx 60.6\ \text{m}$.

The horizontal component does not affect the time of flight; only gravity (acting vertically) determines when the ball lands. This clean separation is a direct consequence of the independence principle.

Key Theorem. Newton's second law in vector form, $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$, projects onto orthogonal axes without cross-coupling when the coordinate axes are chosen to align with the principal force directions. Consequently, if the net force has no $x$-component ($F_x = 0$), then $a_x = 0$ and $v_x$ remains constant for all time, regardless of how $v_y$ evolves. This result holds rigorously for any inertial reference frame and any choice of orthogonal axes.

🌱 One Dimensional Kinematics

🌱 One Dimensional Kinematics

Definition. One-dimensional kinematics is the systematic description of motion along a single straight-line axis without reference to the forces that produce that motion. The fundamental quantities are position $x(t)$, displacement $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$, velocity $v = dx/dt$, and acceleration $a = dv/dt$. For the special case of constant acceleration, these relationships yield the four kinematic equations:

$$ v = v_0 + at $$ $$ x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 $$ $$ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x $$ $$ \bar{v} = \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v) $$

The section focuses on displacement as the single new primitive: the signed change in position $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$, which distinguishes how far an object has moved from where it started without reference to the path taken.

Worked Example. A car starts from rest at a traffic light and accelerates uniformly at $a = 3.0\ \text{m/s}^2$ for $t = 4.0\ \text{s}$. Find its final speed and the displacement.

Step 1 — Final speed:

$$ v = v_0 + at = 0 + (3.0)(4.0) = 12\ \text{m/s} $$

Step 2 — Displacement:

$$ \Delta x = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 = 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(3.0)(4.0)^2 = 24\ \text{m} $$

Verification using the third equation: $v^2 = 0 + 2(3.0)(24) = 144$, so $v = 12\ \text{m/s}$. Consistent.

Key Theorem. Under constant acceleration, the displacement equals the area under the $v$–$t$ graph (a trapezoid with parallel sides $v_0$ and $v$):

$$ \Delta x = \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)\,\Delta t $$

This result follows directly from integrating $v(t) = v_0 + at$ over the interval $[0, t]$, and it remains valid even when $v_0 = 0$ or the object decelerates ($a < 0$).

🍃 Projectile Motion

🍃 Projectile Motion

Definition. Projectile motion describes the two-dimensional kinematic behavior of an object launched into the air and subject only to gravitational acceleration. The motion decomposes into two independent components: uniform motion along the horizontal axis and uniformly accelerated motion along the vertical axis. Formally, if the launch point is the origin with initial speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, the equations of motion are

$$ x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t, \qquad y(t) = v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2, $$

where $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$ is the magnitude of gravitational acceleration near Earth's surface. The velocity components evolve as $v_x = v_0\cos\theta$ (constant) and $v_y = v_0\sin\theta - gt$.

Worked Example. A soccer ball is kicked from ground level with $v_0 = 20.0\ \text{m/s}$ at $\theta = 30.0°$. Find the maximum height, the time of flight, and the horizontal range.

Initial components: $v_{0x} = 20.0\cos30° = 17.3\ \text{m/s}$; $v_{0y} = 20.0\sin30° = 10.0\ \text{m/s}$.

Maximum height occurs when $v_y = 0$:

$$ t_{\text{top}} = \frac{v_{0y}}{g} = \frac{10.0}{9.80} = 1.02\ \text{s}, \qquad H = \frac{v_{0y}^2}{2g} = \frac{(10.0)^2}{2(9.80)} = 5.10\ \text{m}. $$

Time of flight (symmetric trajectory, lands at $y = 0$):

$$ T = \frac{2v_{0y}}{g} = \frac{2(10.0)}{9.80} = 2.04\ \text{s}. $$

Horizontal range:

$$ R = v_{0x}\,T = 17.3 \times 2.04 = 35.3\ \text{m}. $$

Key Theorem (Range Formula). For a projectile launched and landing at the same height, the horizontal range is

$$ R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}. $$

This result follows directly from substituting $T = 2v_0\sin\theta/g$ into $R = v_{0x}T$. A critical consequence is that complementary launch angles ($\theta$ and $90° - \theta$) yield identical ranges, and maximum range is achieved at $\theta = 45°$.

🌱 Trigonometric Functions

🌱 Trigonometric Functions

Definition. The trigonometric functions relate angles to ratios of side lengths in a right triangle. For a right triangle with an interior angle $\theta$, the three primary functions are defined by the ratios of its sides:

$$ \sin\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \quad \cos\theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} $$

These definitions extend to all real angles via the unit circle: placing angle $\theta$ at the origin measured from the positive $x$-axis, the terminal ray intersects the unit circle at the point $(\cos\theta,\, \sin\theta)$. The three reciprocal functions follow directly: $\csc\theta = 1/\sin\theta$, $\sec\theta = 1/\cos\theta$, and $\cot\theta = \cos\theta/\sin\theta$. Both $\sin$ and $\cos$ are periodic with period $2\pi$, while $\tan$ has period $\pi$. In physics, angles are almost universally expressed in radians, where a full rotation equals $2\pi$ rad.

Worked Example. A projectile is launched at speed $v_0 = 25\ \text{m/s}$ at an angle $\theta = 37°$ above the horizontal. Resolve the initial velocity into components:

$$ v_{0x} = v_0 \cos\theta = 25 \cos 37° \approx 25 \times 0.7986 \approx 19.97\ \text{m/s} $$ $$ v_{0y} = v_0 \sin\theta = 25 \sin 37° \approx 25 \times 0.6018 \approx 15.05\ \text{m/s} $$

These components propagate independently under kinematic equations, illustrating how trigonometric decomposition reduces a two-dimensional problem to two one-dimensional ones.

Key Theorem (Pythagorean Identity). For all $\theta$:

$$ \sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1 $$

Proof sketch: By the unit circle definition, the point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ lies on the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$. Substituting gives the identity immediately. Dividing through by $\cos^2\theta$ yields the companion identity $1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$, which proves useful when analyzing forces on inclined surfaces.

🍃 Projectile Maximum Height

🍃 Projectile Maximum Height

Definition. In projectile motion, the maximum height $H$ is the greatest vertical displacement reached by a projectile above its launch point. At this instant, the vertical component of velocity equals zero while the horizontal component remains unchanged. For a projectile launched from the origin with initial speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, the initial velocity components are

$$ v_{0x} = v_0 \cos\theta, \qquad v_{0y} = v_0 \sin\theta. $$

Using the kinematic relation $v_y^2 = v_{0y}^2 - 2g\,\Delta y$ and setting $v_y = 0$ at maximum height yields

$$ H = \frac{v_{0y}^2}{2g} = \frac{(v_0 \sin\theta)^2}{2g} = \frac{v_0^2 \sin^2\theta}{2g}. $$

Worked Example. A soccer ball is kicked from ground level with an initial speed of $v_0 = 20.0\ \text{m/s}$ at $\theta = 35.0°$. Find the maximum height.

First, compute the vertical component of the initial velocity:

$$ v_{0y} = (20.0\ \text{m/s})\sin 35.0° = (20.0)(0.5736) = 11.47\ \text{m/s}. $$

Apply the formula with $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$:

$$ H = \frac{(11.47)^2}{2(9.80)} = \frac{131.6}{19.60} \approx 6.71\ \text{m}. $$

The time to reach maximum height follows from $v_y = v_{0y} - g\,t = 0$, giving $t = v_{0y}/g = 11.47/9.80 \approx 1.17\ \text{s}$, consistent with the energy-based result.

Key Theorem. Maximum height is optimized at $\theta = 90°$ for a fixed launch speed, yielding $H_{\max} = v_0^2 / (2g)$. However, this corresponds to zero horizontal range. For a given $v_0$, the launch angle that simultaneously maximizes range ($\theta = 45°$) achieves $H = v_0^2/(4g)$, exactly half of the absolute maximum height. These two objectives are therefore in direct competition: higher trajectories sacrifice range.

🍃 Projectile Range

🍃 Projectile Range

Definition. The range $R$ of a projectile is the horizontal distance traveled from the launch point to the point at which the projectile returns to its original vertical height. For a projectile launched from level ground with initial speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, the horizontal and vertical motions are independent. Taking the launch point as the origin with $+x$ rightward and $+y$ upward, the equations of motion are

$$ x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t, \qquad y(t) = v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2, $$

where $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$. Setting $y(t) = 0$ and discarding the trivial solution $t = 0$ yields the total flight time

$$ T = \frac{2v_0 \sin\theta}{g}. $$

Substituting into $x(T)$ and applying the double-angle identity $2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin 2\theta$ gives the range formula:

$$ \boxed{R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}.} $$

Worked Example. A soccer ball is kicked at $v_0 = 20.0\ \text{m/s}$ and $\theta = 35.0°$. Find $R$.

$$ R = \frac{(20.0)^2 \sin(70.0°)}{9.80} = \frac{400 \times 0.9397}{9.80} \approx \frac{375.9}{9.80} \approx 38.4\ \text{m}. $$

The ball lands approximately $38.4\ \text{m}$ from the kick point.

Key Theorem — Maximum Range. Because $\sin 2\theta$ is maximized when $2\theta = 90°$, the range $R$ is greatest at $\theta = 45°$ for a fixed launch speed. Moreover, the range function is symmetric about $45°$: launch angles $\theta$ and $(90° - \theta)$ produce equal ranges. For example, kicks at $35°$ and $55°$ both yield $R \approx 38.4\ \text{m}$, yet have different maximum heights and flight times. This symmetry follows directly from $\sin 2\theta = \sin(180° - 2\theta)$.

🍃 Trajectory

Definition. The trajectory of a particle is the geometric path traced by its position vector $\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + y(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}}$ in space as time $t$ varies over some interval. The trajectory is a curve in space, entirely independent of the time parametrization; it represents where the particle has been, not how fast it moved there. In two-dimensional kinematics, the trajectory is obtained by eliminating the parameter $t$ from the component equations $x(t)$ and $y(t)$, yielding a single relation $y = f(x)$ that depends only on spatial coordinates.

Worked Example. Consider a projectile launched from the origin with initial speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, in the absence of air resistance. The kinematic equations are:

$$ x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t, \qquad y(t) = v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2. $$

Solving the first equation for $t$ gives $t = x/(v_0\cos\theta)$. Substituting into the second:

$$ y = v_0\sin\theta \cdot \frac{x}{v_0\cos\theta} - \frac{1}{2}g\left(\frac{x}{v_0\cos\theta}\right)^2 = x\tan\theta - \frac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}\,x^2. $$

This has the form $y = Ax - Bx^2$ — a downward-opening parabola. The trajectory of any projectile under uniform gravitational acceleration is therefore parabolic, regardless of the launch speed or angle.

Key Theorem. For any particle moving under constant acceleration $\mathbf{a}$, the trajectory lies entirely within the plane spanned by the initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$ and $\mathbf{a}$. Moreover, if $\mathbf{v}_0$ and $\mathbf{a}$ are non-parallel, the trajectory is parabolic; the quadratic dependence of $y$ on $x$ is a direct consequence of the constant, uniform nature of the acceleration, and holds for any launch speed or angle within that plane.

(b) Determine the horizontal distance $x$ at which the ball strikes the floor ($y = -H$). (c) Sketch the trajectory, labeling the launch point and landing point. Use $g = 9.80\,\text{m/s}^2$.

🌸 Projectile Motion Applications

🌸 Projectile Motion Applications

Definition

Projectile motion describes the two-dimensional kinematics of an object launched with an initial velocity and subject only to gravitational acceleration. The horizontal and vertical components of motion are independent: horizontal velocity remains constant (neglecting air resistance), while vertical motion follows constant downward acceleration $g = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$. For a projectile launched from the origin with speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, the governing equations are:

$$ x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t $$ $$ y(t) = v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}g t^2 $$ $$ v_x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta, \qquad v_y(t) = v_0 \sin\theta - g t $$

The trajectory, obtained by eliminating $t$, is the parabola:

$$ y = x\tan\theta - \frac{g x^2}{2v_0^2 \cos^2\theta} $$

Worked Example

A ball is kicked from ground level at $v_0 = 20.0\ \text{m/s}$ and $\theta = 37.0°$. Find (a) the time of flight, (b) the horizontal range, and (c) the maximum height.

Initial components:

$$ v_{0x} = 20.0\cos 37.0° = 15.97\ \text{m/s}, \quad v_{0y} = 20.0\sin 37.0° = 12.04\ \text{m/s} $$

(a) Time of flight: Setting $y = 0$ gives $t(v_{0y} - \tfrac{1}{2}gt) = 0$, so:

$$ T = \frac{2v_{0y}}{g} = \frac{2(12.04)}{9.80} \approx 2.46\ \text{s} $$

(b) Horizontal range:

$$ R = v_{0x} \cdot T = 15.97 \times 2.46 \approx 39.3\ \text{m} $$

(c) Maximum height: At the apex, $v_y = 0$, so $t_{\text{apex}} = v_{0y}/g = 1.23\ \text{s}$:

$$ H = v_{0y} t_{\text{apex}} - \frac{1}{2}g t_{\text{apex}}^2 = 12.04(1.23) - 4.90(1.23)^2 \approx 7.40\ \text{m} $$

Key Theorem

The range formula for symmetric projectile motion (launch and landing at equal height) is:

$$ R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g} $$

Maximum range occurs at $\theta = 45°$, yielding $R_{\max} = v_0^2/g$. Complementary angles (e.g., $30°$ and $60°$) produce identical ranges because $\sin 2\theta = \sin(180° - 2\theta)$.

Lab Exercise

Using a projectile launcher, fire a steel ball at three angles—$30°$, $45°$, and $60°$—keeping $v_0$ fixed. Measure the horizontal range with a meter tape and compare against the theoretical predictions. Quantify the fractional deviation attributable to air drag and rolling friction at the launch barrel, and determine whether the measured optimal angle shifts above or below $45°$ when these dissipative effects are present.

Payoff 🎯

Projectile motion applications represent the natural culmination of Chapter 3 because they synthesize every kinematic tool developed across the chapter into a single, physically rich framework. The decomposition of motion into independent horizontal and vertical components—each governed by its own kinematic equations—transforms an apparently complex trajectory problem into two tractable one-dimensional problems solved in parallel. This is not merely a computational convenience; it reflects a deep physical truth: a constant gravitational field $\mathbf{g} = -g\hat{j}$ acts exclusively along one axis, leaving the perpendicular direction entirely free of net force.

Definition and governing equations. For a projectile launched from position $(x_0, y_0)$ with initial speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal, the equations of motion are:

$$ x(t) = x_0 + v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t, \qquad y(t) = y_0 + v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2. $$

The trajectory shape follows by eliminating $t$:

$$ y = y_0 + x\tan\theta - \frac{g}{2v_0^2\cos^2\theta}\,x^2, $$

a parabola whose coefficients encode launch conditions directly.

Worked example. A ball is kicked from ground level at $v_0 = 20\ \text{m/s}$ and $\theta = 30°$. The time of flight is found by setting $y = 0$: $t = 2v_0\sin\theta/g = 2(20)(0.5)/9.8 \approx 2.04\ \text{s}$. The horizontal range is $R = v_0\cos\theta \cdot t = (20)(0.866)(2.04) \approx 35.3\ \text{m}$. The maximum height is $H = v_0^2\sin^2\theta/(2g) = (400)(0.25)/19.6 \approx 5.1\ \text{m}$.

Key theorem. For fixed launch speed on level ground, the range $R = v_0^2\sin 2\theta / g$ is maximized at $\theta = 45°$, and launch angles symmetric about $45°$ yield identical ranges—a result derivable by maximizing $\sin 2\theta$.

Lab exercise. Using a spring-loaded launcher set to a fixed $v_0$, fire a ball at three angles ($30°$, $45°$, $60°$) and record impact positions. Compare measured ranges to theoretical predictions; account for air resistance qualitatively if discrepancies exceed $5\%$.
