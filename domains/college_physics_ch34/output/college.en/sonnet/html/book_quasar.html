🌱 Special Relativity

🌱 Special Relativity

Definition. Special relativity, formulated by Albert Einstein in 1905, rests on two postulates: (1) the laws of physics are identical in all inertial reference frames, and (2) the speed of light in a vacuum, $c \approx 2.998 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$, is the same for all observers regardless of the motion of the source or observer. These postulates force a revision of Newtonian mechanics, replacing absolute time and space with a unified spacetime in which simultaneity is relative. The Lorentz factor $\gamma$ governs the magnitude of relativistic effects:

$$ \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}, \qquad v < c. $$

Two cardinal consequences follow: time dilation, $\Delta t = \gamma\, \Delta t_0$, where $\Delta t_0$ is the proper time measured in the rest frame of the event; and length contraction, $L = L_0/\gamma$, where $L_0$ is the proper length along the direction of motion.

Worked Example. A muon produced in the upper atmosphere travels toward Earth at $v = 0.98c$. Its rest-frame lifetime is $\tau_0 = 2.2\ \mu\text{s}$. In the Earth frame, the observed lifetime is

$$ \tau = \gamma\,\tau_0 = \frac{2.2\ \mu\text{s}}{\sqrt{1 - (0.98)^2}} = \frac{2.2\ \mu\text{s}}{0.199} \approx 11.1\ \mu\text{s}. $$

Without time dilation, a muon traveling at $0.98c$ could cover only $0.98c \times 2.2\ \mu\text{s} \approx 645\ \text{m}$, far less than the $\sim 15\ \text{km}$ atmospheric depth. With dilation, the covered distance becomes $0.98c \times 11.1\ \mu\text{s} \approx 3.26\ \text{km}$—consistent with observed muon fluxes at sea level.

Key Theorem — Relativistic Energy-Momentum Relation. The total relativistic energy and momentum of a particle of rest mass $m$ satisfy

$$ E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2. $$

For a particle at rest ($p = 0$), this reduces to Einstein's celebrated $E = mc^2$. The quantity $mc^2$ is called the rest energy; kinetic energy is $K = (\gamma - 1)mc^2$.

🍃 Equivalence Principle

🍃 Equivalence Principle

Definition

The equivalence principle, central to Einstein's general theory of relativity, asserts that the effects of gravitational acceleration are locally indistinguishable from the effects of inertial acceleration. More precisely, in any sufficiently small region of spacetime, a uniformly accelerating reference frame is physically equivalent to a frame at rest in a uniform gravitational field. This leads to two related formulations: the weak equivalence principle states that inertial mass $m_i$ and gravitational mass $m_g$ are identical for all bodies, so that $m_i = m_g$; the strong equivalence principle extends this to include all laws of physics, asserting that no local experiment can distinguish between gravitational and inertial effects.

Because $m_i = m_g$, Newton's second law $F = m_i a$ combined with the gravitational force $F = m_g g$ immediately yields $a = g$ for all objects regardless of composition — the origin of Galileo's observation that bodies fall at equal rates in vacuum.

Worked Example

Consider an astronaut inside a sealed, windowless spacecraft accelerating at $a = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$ in deep space, far from any gravitational source. If the astronaut releases a ball from rest (relative to the spacecraft), the ball appears to fall toward the floor with acceleration $g_{\text{eff}} = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$. By the equivalence principle, every mechanical, optical, and electromagnetic measurement the astronaut performs yields results identical to those obtained in a stationary laboratory on Earth's surface. No internal experiment can reveal whether the acceleration arises from thrust or gravity.

Key Theorem

A direct consequence is the gravitational redshift. A photon emitted with frequency $f_0$ at the floor of an accelerating elevator and received at the ceiling, a height $h$ above, is observed at frequency

$$ f = f_0\!\left(1 - \frac{gh}{c^2}\right), $$

where $c$ is the speed of light. Since the situation is locally equivalent to a gravitational field of strength $g$, an identical redshift must occur for photons climbing out of a gravitational potential well — confirmed experimentally by Pound and Rebka in 1959 using the Mössbauer effect over a height of $22.5\ \text{m}$.

🌱 Escape Velocity

🌱 Escape Velocity

Definition. The escape velocity of a body is the minimum speed required for a projectile to escape the gravitational influence of a massive object—reaching infinity with zero remaining kinetic energy. Derived from energy conservation, it represents the threshold at which the total mechanical energy of the projectile equals zero. For a planet of mass $M$ and radius $R$, the escape velocity is:

$$ v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}} $$

where $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/kg}^2$ is the universal gravitational constant. This result is independent of the mass of the escaping object and independent of the direction of launch (in the absence of atmosphere).

Derivation sketch. Setting the sum of kinetic and gravitational potential energy to zero:

$$ \frac{1}{2}mv_e^2 - \frac{GMm}{R} = 0 $$

Solving for $v_e$ yields the expression above. The choice of zero total energy corresponds to the object arriving at $r \to \infty$ with $v \to 0$—the boundary condition for marginal escape.

Worked Example. Calculate the escape velocity from Earth's surface, given $M_\oplus = 5.972 \times 10^{24}\ \text{kg}$ and $R_\oplus = 6.371 \times 10^6\ \text{m}$.

$$ v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.972 \times 10^{24})}{6.371 \times 10^6}} $$ $$ v_e = \sqrt{\frac{7.972 \times 10^{14}}{6.371 \times 10^6}} = \sqrt{1.251 \times 10^8} \approx 1.118 \times 10^4\ \text{m/s} $$

Thus $v_e \approx 11.2\ \text{km/s}$, a well-established benchmark in astrodynamics.

Key Theorem (Schwarzschild Limit). When $v_e = c$ (the speed of light), the corresponding radius $R_s = 2GM/c^2$ defines the Schwarzschild radius. Any object compressed within $R_s$ becomes a black hole—light itself cannot escape. This connects Newtonian escape velocity to general relativistic event horizons, illustrating the concept's reach across classical and modern physics.

🍃 General Relativity

🍃 General Relativity

Definition. General relativity (GR), formulated by Albert Einstein in 1915, is a geometric theory of gravitation that supersedes Newtonian gravity. Its central premise is the equivalence principle: the effects of gravitational acceleration are locally indistinguishable from those of inertial acceleration. GR encodes gravity not as a force but as the curvature of four-dimensional spacetime caused by the presence of mass and energy. The governing equations are the Einstein field equations:

$$ G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu} $$

where $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}Rg_{\mu\nu}$ is the Einstein tensor (encoding spacetime curvature via the Ricci tensor $R_{\mu\nu}$ and Ricci scalar $R$), $\Lambda$ is the cosmological constant, $g_{\mu\nu}$ is the metric tensor, $T_{\mu\nu}$ is the stress-energy tensor representing matter and energy content, and $G$ is Newton's gravitational constant.

Worked Example: Gravitational Time Dilation. A clock on Earth's surface (gravitational potential $\phi_1 = -GM_\oplus/R_\oplus$) and a GPS satellite at altitude $h$ (potential $\phi_2 = -GM_\oplus/(R_\oplus + h)$) run at different rates. The Schwarzschild metric predicts the fractional time difference:

$$ \frac{\Delta t_{\text{surface}}}{\Delta t_{\text{satellite}}} \approx 1 - \frac{\Delta\phi}{c^2} $$

For $h = 20{,}200\ \text{km}$, $\Delta\phi/c^2 \approx 5.3 \times 10^{-10}$, giving a rate discrepancy of roughly $45\ \mu\text{s/day}$. Without GR corrections, GPS positional errors would accumulate at approximately $11\ \text{km/day}$.

Key Theorem: Geodesic Equation. In curved spacetime, freely falling particles (no non-gravitational forces) follow geodesics — the generalization of straight lines. The geodesic equation is:

$$ \frac{d^2 x^\mu}{d\tau^2} + \Gamma^\mu_{\alpha\beta}\frac{dx^\alpha}{d\tau}\frac{dx^\beta}{d\tau} = 0 $$

where $\Gamma^\mu_{\alpha\beta}$ are the Christoffel symbols derived from the metric, and $\tau$ is proper time. In the weak-field, slow-motion limit, this reduces exactly to Newton's second law under gravitational acceleration.

Lab Exercise. Using the Schwarzschild radius formula $r_s = 2GM/c^2$, calculate the Schwarzschild radius of (a) Earth ($M_\oplus = 5.97 \times 10^{24}\ \text{kg}$) and (b) the Sun ($M_\odot = 1.99 \times 10^{30}\ \text{kg}$). Compare each result to the object's actual radius and discuss what it would mean physically if a body were compressed to within $r_s$.

🍃 Black Hole

🍃 Black Hole

Definition. A black hole is a region of spacetime in which the gravitational field is so intense that no matter or electromagnetic radiation—including light—can escape to spatial infinity. The boundary of this region is called the event horizon, located at the Schwarzschild radius $r_s$ given by

$$ r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2} $$

where $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\ \text{N·m}^2\text{/kg}^2$ is Newton's gravitational constant, $M$ is the mass of the collapsed object, and $c$ is the speed of light. Any object or signal originating at $r \leq r_s$ cannot reach $r > r_s$; the escape velocity equals $c$ precisely at $r = r_s$.

Worked Example. Compute the Schwarzschild radius of a black hole whose mass equals ten solar masses ($M = 10\, M_\odot$, where $M_\odot = 1.989 \times 10^{30}\ \text{kg}$).

$$ r_s = \frac{2(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(10 \times 1.989 \times 10^{30})}{(3.00 \times 10^8)^2} $$ $$ r_s = \frac{2(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(1.989 \times 10^{31})}{9.00 \times 10^{16}} $$ $$ r_s = \frac{2.655 \times 10^{21}}{9.00 \times 10^{16}} \approx 2.95 \times 10^4\ \text{m} \approx 29.5\ \text{km} $$

A stellar-mass black hole of $10\, M_\odot$ would thus have an event horizon roughly 30 km in radius—smaller than a typical city.

Key Theorem (Birkhoff's Theorem). The exterior gravitational field of any spherically symmetric mass distribution is identical to that of a point mass $M$ located at the origin. As a corollary, the Schwarzschild metric

$$ ds^2 = -\!\left(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\right)c^2\,dt^2 + \left(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\right)^{-1}dr^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2 $$

uniquely describes the vacuum spacetime outside any non-rotating, uncharged black hole. At $r \to \infty$ this reduces to flat Minkowski spacetime, while at $r = r_s$ the metric component $g_{tt}$ vanishes, signaling the event horizon.

🌱 Galaxy

🌱 Galaxy

Definition. A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system of stars, stellar remnants, interstellar gas and dust, and dark matter, ranging in mass from approximately $10^7 M_\odot$ (dwarf galaxies) to $10^{13} M_\odot$ (giant ellipticals), where $M_\odot = 1.989 \times 10^{30}$ kg denotes one solar mass. Galaxies are classified morphologically by the Hubble sequence into ellipticals (E0–E7), lenticulars (S0), spirals (Sa–Sd), and irregulars (Irr). The observable universe contains an estimated $2 \times 10^{12}$ galaxies, clustered hierarchically into groups, clusters, and superclusters separated by vast cosmic voids.

The recession velocity of a distant galaxy is governed by Hubble's Law:

$$ v = H_0 \, d $$

where $H_0 \approx 70 \; \text{km s}^{-1}\text{Mpc}^{-1}$ is the Hubble constant and $d$ is the proper distance in megaparsecs ($1 \; \text{Mpc} = 3.086 \times 10^{22}$ m).

Worked Example. The galaxy NGC 4889 has a measured recession velocity of $v = 6,500 \; \text{km s}^{-1}$. Estimate its distance.

$$ d = \frac{v}{H_0} = \frac{6{,}500 \; \text{km s}^{-1}}{70 \; \text{km s}^{-1}\text{Mpc}^{-1}} \approx 92.9 \; \text{Mpc} $$

Converting: $d \approx 92.9 \times 3.086 \times 10^{22} \; \text{m} \approx 2.87 \times 10^{24}$ m, or roughly 303 million light-years.

Key Theorem — Virial Theorem for Self-Gravitating Systems. For a gravitationally bound system in statistical equilibrium, the time-averaged kinetic energy $\langle K \rangle$ and potential energy $\langle U \rangle$ satisfy:

$$ 2\langle K \rangle + \langle U \rangle = 0 $$

This result underpins galaxy mass estimates from stellar velocity dispersions. If stars in a galaxy have a characteristic velocity dispersion $\sigma$ and the system has characteristic radius $R$, then the total mass is approximated by:

$$ M \approx \frac{5 \sigma^2 R}{G} $$

Discrepancies between this dynamical mass and the observed luminous mass constitute strong observational evidence for dark matter in galactic halos.

Lab Exercise. Using a published table of galaxy recession velocities and angular diameters, apply Hubble's Law to compute distances for five galaxies. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including peculiar velocities (typically $\sim 300 \; \text{km s}^{-1}$) and the limitations of assuming a linear velocity–distance relation at large redshifts where general-relativistic cosmology becomes necessary.

🌸 Quasar

🌸 Quasar

Definition. A quasar (quasi-stellar object, QSO) is an extremely luminous active galactic nucleus (AGN) powered by accretion of matter onto a supermassive black hole with mass $M \sim 10^6$–$10^{10}\,M_\odot$. The released gravitational potential energy drives luminosities reaching $L \sim 10^{40}$–$10^{41}$ W, outshining entire host galaxies by factors of $10^2$–$10^3$. Radiation is emitted across the full electromagnetic spectrum, from radio to X-ray, with the characteristic optical spectrum displaying broad emission lines (widths $\Delta v \sim 10^3$–$10^4$ km s$^{-1}$) arising from high-velocity gas in the broad-line region (BLR) surrounding the central engine. The observed redshifts of quasars, many with $z > 2$, place them at cosmological distances exceeding several gigaparsecs, confirming they represent the early universe.

Worked Example. Quasar 3C 273 has a measured redshift $z = 0.158$. Using Hubble's law in its relativistic form, estimate its recession velocity and luminosity distance. The recession velocity is approximated by:

$$ v = cz = (3.00 \times 10^8\,\text{m s}^{-1})(0.158) \approx 4.74 \times 10^7\,\text{m s}^{-1} \approx 0.158\,c. $$

With Hubble constant $H_0 = 70\,\text{km s}^{-1}\text{Mpc}^{-1}$, the luminosity distance is:

$$ d_L \approx \frac{cz}{H_0} = \frac{(3 \times 10^5\,\text{km s}^{-1})(0.158)}{70\,\text{km s}^{-1}\text{Mpc}^{-1}} \approx 677\,\text{Mpc} \approx 2.2 \times 10^9\,\text{ly}. $$

Its observed flux $F \approx 1.4 \times 10^{-11}$ W m$^{-2}$ then yields luminosity $L = 4\pi d_L^2 F \approx 4 \times 10^{39}$ W, confirming extreme AGN activity.

Key Theorem (Eddington Luminosity). Accretion onto a black hole is self-limiting. The Eddington luminosity,

$$ L_\text{Edd} = \frac{4\pi G M m_p c}{\sigma_T} \approx 1.26 \times 10^{31}\!\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)\text{W}, $$

defines the threshold at which radiation pressure on ionized hydrogen balances gravitational infall. Quasars accreting near $L_\text{Edd}$ imply black hole masses consistent with $M \sim 10^8$–$10^9\,M_\odot$.

Payoff 🎯

What Quasars Achieve and Why They Are the Natural Endpoint

Quasars — quasi-stellar objects — represent the most luminous, energetically extreme phenomena in the observable universe, and they stand as the fitting culmination of College Physics 2E Chapter 34 because they synthesize virtually every principle encountered across the course into a single coherent astrophysical object. A quasar is defined formally as an active galactic nucleus (AGN) powered by accretion onto a supermassive black hole of mass $M \sim 10^6$–$10^{10}\,M_\odot$, radiating across the entire electromagnetic spectrum with luminosities reaching $L \sim 10^{47}\ \text{erg s}^{-1}$, outshining their host galaxies by factors of $10^2$–$10^3$.

Worked Example

Consider quasar 3C 273 at redshift $z = 0.158$. Its observed flux at optical wavelengths is $F \approx 3 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{W m}^{-2}$. Using the luminosity–distance relation $d_L = (c/H_0)\,z\,(1 + z/2)$ for small $z$, one obtains $d_L \approx 749\ \text{Mpc}$. The isotropic luminosity then follows:

$$ L = 4\pi d_L^2\, F \approx 2 \times 10^{39}\ \text{W}, $$

roughly $5 \times 10^{12}\,L_\odot$ — an output that no stellar process can sustain, confirming gravitational energy release via accretion as the only viable mechanism.

Key Theorem: The Eddington Luminosity

For a radiatively driven accretion flow, the maximum sustainable luminosity is the Eddington limit,

$$ L_{\rm Edd} = \frac{4\pi G M m_p c}{\sigma_T} \approx 1.3 \times 10^{31}\!\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)\ \text{W}, $$

where $m_p$ is the proton mass and $\sigma_T$ is the Thomson cross-section. Quasars accreting near this limit convert mass to radiation with efficiency $\eta \approx 0.1$, far exceeding nuclear fusion ($\eta \approx 0.007$). This result unites Newtonian gravity, electromagnetism, and special relativity in one expression.
