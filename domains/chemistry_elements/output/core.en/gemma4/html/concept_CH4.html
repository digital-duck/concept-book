Methane ($\text{CH}_4$) is arguably one of the most studied industrial gases, serving as the primary component of natural gas. Because of its ubiquity—found in everything from decomposing organic matter in landfills to biogas produced by livestock—understanding its chemical behavior and stoichiometry is critical for energy science and environmental modeling. Methane possesses a highly stable tetrahedral structure ($\text{sp}^3$ hybridization), giving it specific reactivity patterns that govern global cycles, such as the carbon cycle.

Definition: Methane is a simple alkane characterized by one carbon atom bonded to four hydrogen atoms. Its molecular formula is $\text{CH}_4$, and its inherent stability makes it relatively unreactive compared to unsaturated hydrocarbons like ethene ($\text{C}_2\text{H}_4$). Chemically, studying methane often involves analyzing its combustion efficiency or its role as a limiting reactant in oxidation reactions.

Worked Example: Consider the complete combustion of natural gas (methane) reacting with oxygen gas:

$$ \text{CH}_4(g) + 2\text{O}_2(g) \longrightarrow \text{CO}_2(g) + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) $$
