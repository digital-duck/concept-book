🍃 O2

🍃 O$_2$

The air we breathe is fundamental to life, but understanding the role of its components requires focusing on specific molecules. Oxygen gas ($\text{O}_2$) is arguably the most critical molecule in biological systems, driving respiration and supporting nearly all known aerobic life forms. From the deepest ocean trenches to the highest altitudes, the availability and concentration of $\text{O}_2$ dictate environmental feasibility.

Definition: Oxygen gas ($\text{O}_2$) is a diatomic element that exists naturally as a stable molecule composed of two covalently bonded oxygen atoms. Its chemical formula is $\text{O}_2$, and it is highly reactive, making it essential for combustion and cellular respiration (the process where organic molecules are oxidized to release energy).

Worked Example: Consider the reaction used in standard candle combustion: the burning of methane ($\text{CH}_4$). This reaction requires oxygen as a reactant. We can write the balanced chemical equation:

$$ \text{CH}_4(g) + 2\text{O}_2(g) \longrightarrow \text{CO}_2(g) + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(g) $$

If we burn $50.0$ mL of methane gas at Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP), how much volume of $\text{O}_2$ is required? According to the stoichiometry, a mole of $\text{CH}_4$ requires two moles of $\text{O}_2$. Using the molar volume at STP ($22.4 \text{ L/mol}$), we first convert $50.0 \text{ mL}$ to liters:

$$ \text{Volume } \text{O}_2 = 2 \times (50.0 \text{ mL} / 22.4 \text{ L}) = 2 \times (0.00223 \text{ mol}) \approx 0.00446 \text{ L} $$

Thus, $4.46 \text{ mL}$ of $\text{O}_2$ is consumed for every $50.0 \text{ mL}$ of methane burned.

Why it's true: The stoichiometry coefficient '2' in the balanced equation $\text{CH}_4 + 2\text{O}_2 \longrightarrow \dots$ dictates that two moles (or, at constant temperature and pressure, two volumes) of oxygen are consumed for every one mole of methane. This adherence to the Law of Conservation of Mass ensures that matter is neither created nor destroyed during the chemical process.
