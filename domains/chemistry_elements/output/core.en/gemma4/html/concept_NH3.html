🍃 $\text{NH}_3$

Ammonia ($\text{NH}_3$) is far more ubiquitous than its simple formula suggests. It forms the basis of countless products, from fertilizers that feed global populations to household cleaning agents and chemical synthesis materials. Understanding how ammonia behaves in solution is critical because it rarely exists as a pure base; instead, its properties are dictated by equilibrium with water. This inherent tendency to react when dissolved allows us to predict its effective basicity—a cornerstone of aqueous chemistry.

Definition: Ammonia gas is a weak base that dissolves in water according to the following equilibrium reaction:

$$ \text{NH}_3(g) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4^+(aq) + \text{OH}^-(aq) $$

Because it establishes this reversible equilibrium, its strength is quantified by the base dissociation constant, $K_b$.

Worked Example: Consider a solution of ammonia with an initial concentration of $[\text{NH}_3] = 0.10\ M$. We can calculate the concentration of hydroxide ions ($\text{OH}^-$) present. Using the equilibrium expression and the known $K_b$ for $\text{NH}_3$, we solve for $[\text{OH}^-]$.

Why it's true: The initial mixing of $\text{NH}_3$ and water drives the reaction to the right, producing ammonium ions ($\text{NH}_4^+$) and hydroxide ions ($\text{OH}^-$). While this suggests that all the original ammonia reacts completely, the equilibrium constant $K_b$ dictates that a portion of $\text{NH}_3$ remains unreacted. This requirement for an equilibrium calculation is fundamental: the system reaches a balance where the rate of protonation equals the rate of deprotonation.
