🍃 Conservation Of Atoms

Have you ever burned a piece of wood? The solid biomass turns into smoke, ash, and gases—it seems like the material has vanished. However, chemistry tells us that nothing is truly lost; it only changes form. This fundamental principle governs all chemical reactions: atoms are neither created nor destroyed during these transformations. This concept is often called the Law of Conservation of Mass, but at its core, it speaks to the conservation of the constituent atoms themselves.

Definition: The law of conservation of atoms states that in a closed system, the total number and types of atoms (and thus the total mass) before a chemical reaction must equal the total number and types of atoms after the reaction. Chemical reactions merely rearrange the bonds between existing atoms; they do not add or remove any fundamental particles.

Worked Example: Consider the formation of water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$) from its elements, hydrogen gas ($\text{H}_2$) and oxygen gas ($\text{O}_2$). If we start with 2 moles of $\text{H}_2$ and 1 mole of $\text{O}_2$, the balanced equation is:

$$ 2 \text{H}_2 + 1 \text{O}_2 \longrightarrow 2 \text{H}_2\text{O} $$

Before the reaction, we have $(4 \times \text{H} \text{ atoms}) + (2 \times \text{O} \text{ atoms})$. After the reaction, we still have $4$ hydrogen atoms and $2$ oxygen atoms incorporated into two molecules of water. The count remains perfectly balanced.

Why It's True: Chemical reactions involve breaking existing chemical bonds and forming new ones. When a bond breaks (e.g., stretching $\text{H}-\text{H}$), the electrons and nuclei comprising those atoms are still present; they simply become free to interact with other atoms. Because matter is fundamentally composed of discrete, indestructible particles (atoms), the initial inventory must match the final inventory for the system to remain closed and consistent.

Definition: Ionic bonding is the type of chemical bond formed by the electrostatic attraction between a metal (which readily loses valence electrons) and a nonmetal (which readily gains them). This process involves the complete transfer of one or more valence electrons, resulting in the formation of positively charged cations ($\text{M}^+$) and negatively charged anions ($\text{X}^-$), which then attract each other in an orderly crystal lattice structure.

Worked Example: Consider magnesium reacting with oxygen, forming magnesium oxide ($\text{MgO}$). Magnesium has two outer electrons ($[\text{Ne}]3s^2$), while oxygen needs two electrons to complete its octet. The reaction proceeds by $\text{Mg}$ losing both electrons and becoming the cation $\text{Mg}^{2+}$, and $\text{O}$ gaining those electrons to become the anion $\text{O}^{2-}$. The resultant bond is held together by the strong attraction between $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ and $\text{O}^{2-}$ ions.

Why it's true: Ionic bonding persists because the resulting combination of charges minimizes the overall potential energy of the system. This driving force is quantified by a large difference in electronegativity ($\Delta\text{EN}$) between the participating atoms, typically exceeding $1.7$. The stability of these compounds arises from maximizing the attractive Coulombic forces ($F \propto q_1 q_2 / r^2$) while forming an extended, repeating crystal lattice that accommodates all ions equally.

🌸 Reading Chemical Formulas

🌸 Reading Chemical Formulas

Have you ever wondered what ingredients are truly present when reading a label? Chemists use formulas that act as universal recipe cards, telling us exactly which elements and how many of them combine to form a compound. Learning to read these symbols is the foundational skill for understanding matter itself—it’s like learning the language of chemistry.

A chemical formula is a shorthand notation that represents the constituent elements of a substance. Each element symbol (like $\text{H}$ or $\text{O}$) identifies an element from the periodic table, while the subscripts are crucial counters. A subscript number placed immediately after an element symbol indicates how many atoms of that specific element are present in one molecule of the compound. If no subscript is visible (like in $\text{Fe}$), it implies a count of one atom.

Consider carbon dioxide, $\text{CO}_2$. This formula tells us that the compound contains one atom of Carbon ($\text{C}$) bonded to two atoms of Oxygen ($\text{O}$). We read this as "one carbon and two oxygens." The subscript '2' on the $\text{O}$ is not merely descriptive; it dictates the stoichiometry—the precise ratio of elements in the molecule.

This structure works because chemistry follows strict rules of bonding. When we write $\text{CO}_2$, the formula implies a stable unit where one carbon atom shares its bonds with two separate oxygen atoms. The subscripts are thus essential counters, not merely labels. They provide the initial physical proof that these elements combine in fixed ratios to create a specific compound.
