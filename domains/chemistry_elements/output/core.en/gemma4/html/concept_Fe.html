Imagine a team proposes building a fully autonomous drone delivery network across a metropolitan area. Before writing a single line of code or purchasing an aircraft, they must ask: Is this actually possible? This critical question leads us to the concept of Feasibility ($\text{Fe}$). In structured reasoning and engineering analysis, $\text{Fe}$ is not merely asking "can we do it?" but rather systematically determining if a proposed solution or plan is viable—i.e., technically achievable, economically sustainable, and operationally practical with available resources and technology.

Definition: Feasibility ($\text{Fe}$) is the structured assessment that determines the practicality of a project by analyzing constraints across multiple dimensions: technical $\text{Fe}$, economic $\text{Fe}$ (cost-benefit), legal $\text{Fe}$, and operational $\text{Fe}$. Mathematically, we can view feasibility as verifying if the proposed solution set $S$ intersects with the constraint space $C$:

$$ \text{Fe}(S) = 1 \iff S \cap C \neq \emptyset $$

Here, $S$ is the goal/solution, and $C$ represents all identified constraints (budget, time, physics, law).

Worked Example: Consider developing a decentralized blockchain system using existing public infrastructure.

  • Goal ($S$): Create a secure ledger accessible globally in real-time.
  • Constraints ($C$): Must operate within current internet bandwidth limitations and comply with GDPR data residency laws. A feasibility analysis would confirm that while the concept is sound, the constraint $C_{\text{bandwidth}}$ might force a modification of $S$, perhaps requiring regionalization rather than true global real-time access.

Why it's True: A project fails not because the initial idea was bad, but often because one critical dimension (e.g., cost or technology) was overlooked during planning. By forcing the systematic evaluation against known constraints—the $\text{Fe}$ process—we transform ambitious ideas into structured, actionable plans that respect physical and economic reality.
