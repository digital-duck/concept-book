🌱 Normal Force

Definition. The normal force $\vec{N}$ is the contact force exerted by a surface on an object perpendicular to the surface at the point of contact. It is a reactive force arising from the electromagnetic interactions between the atoms of the object and those of the surface. Crucially, the normal force is not universally equal to the object's weight; its magnitude is determined by Newton's second law applied to the direction perpendicular to the surface.

For an object of mass $m$ resting on a horizontal surface in equilibrium, the net force in the vertical direction is zero:

$$ \sum F_y = N - mg = 0 \implies N = mg $$

When the surface is inclined at angle $\theta$ from the horizontal, resolving forces along the axis perpendicular to the surface gives:

$$ N = mg\cos\theta $$

More generally, whenever an external force $F_{\text{ext}}$ has a component pressing the object into or away from the surface, the normal force adjusts accordingly.

Worked Example. A 10.0 kg box rests on a frictionless incline of $30^\circ$. Find the normal force.

Taking axes parallel and perpendicular to the incline, the weight component perpendicular to the surface is $mg\cos 30^\circ$. Since there is no acceleration perpendicular to the surface:

$$ N = mg\cos 30^\circ = (10.0\,\text{kg})(9.80\,\text{m/s}^2)\cos 30^\circ = (98.0\,\text{N})(0.866) \approx 84.9\,\text{N} $$

Note that $N < mg = 98.0\,\text{N}$, consistent with the incline supporting only a fraction of the full weight.

Key Theorem (Newton's Third Law Pair). The normal force $\vec{N}$ exerted by the surface on the object and the reaction force $\vec{N}'$ exerted by the object on the surface form a Newton's third law action–reaction pair: $\vec{N}' = -\vec{N}$. These forces act on different bodies and therefore do not cancel in a free-body diagram.

Lab Exercise. Place a bathroom scale on a flat surface and stand on it while holding a 5.0 kg mass. Record the reading $W_1$. Now push down on the mass with a measured force $F = 20\,\text{N}$ and record $W_2$. Predict both readings using $N = mg + F_{\text{applied}}$ and compare your predictions with the measured values, commenting on any discrepancy.
