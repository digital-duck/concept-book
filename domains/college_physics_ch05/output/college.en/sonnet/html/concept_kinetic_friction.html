🍃 Kinetic Friction

Definition. Kinetic friction is the contact force that opposes the relative motion of two surfaces sliding against each other. When an object moves across a surface, the frictional force acts parallel to the interface and antiparallel to the velocity of the moving object. Quantitatively, the magnitude of the kinetic friction force is given by

$$ f_k = \mu_k N $$

where $\mu_k$ is the coefficient of kinetic friction (a dimensionless, empirically determined constant dependent on the materials in contact) and $N$ is the magnitude of the normal force pressing the surfaces together. Unlike static friction, kinetic friction does not vary with the applied force — once sliding occurs, $f_k$ remains essentially constant regardless of speed (for most everyday conditions).

Worked Example. A 10 kg wooden crate is pushed across a concrete floor with a horizontal applied force of 50 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction between wood and concrete is $\mu_k = 0.30$. Determine the crate's acceleration.

First, identify the normal force. Since the floor is horizontal and there is no vertical acceleration:

$$ N = mg = (10\,\text{kg})(9.8\,\text{m/s}^2) = 98\,\text{N} $$

The kinetic friction force is therefore:

$$ f_k = \mu_k N = (0.30)(98\,\text{N}) = 29.4\,\text{N} $$

Applying Newton's second law in the horizontal direction:

$$ \sum F_x = F_{\text{applied}} - f_k = ma $$ $$ 50\,\text{N} - 29.4\,\text{N} = (10\,\text{kg})\,a $$ $$ a = \frac{20.6\,\text{N}}{10\,\text{kg}} = 2.06\,\text{m/s}^2 $$

Key Theorem. For a body undergoing kinetic friction, the work done by friction over a displacement $d$ is

$$ W_f = -f_k\, d = -\mu_k N\, d $$

This quantity is always negative, confirming that kinetic friction removes mechanical energy from the system and converts it to thermal energy. The total thermal energy generated is $\Delta E_{\text{th}} = \mu_k N\, d$.

Lab Exercise. Place a block of known mass on an inclined board and increase the tilt angle $\theta$ until the block slides at constant velocity (net force zero, so $f_k = mg\sin\theta$ and $N = mg\cos\theta$). Compute $\mu_k = \tan\theta$. Repeat with different masses and verify that $\mu_k$ is independent of the normal force magnitude.
