🍃 Friction

🍃 Friction

Definition. Friction is a contact force that opposes relative motion (or the tendency of motion) between two surfaces in contact. It arises from microscopic interactions—adhesion and asperity interlocking—at the interface of the surfaces. Two distinct regimes are recognized. Static friction $f_s$ prevents the onset of motion and satisfies

$$ f_s \leq \mu_s N, $$

where $\mu_s$ is the coefficient of static friction and $N$ is the normal force. Kinetic friction $f_k$ acts on surfaces already sliding relative to each other and is modeled as

$$ f_k = \mu_k N, $$

with $\mu_k < \mu_s$ in virtually all material pairs. Both coefficients are dimensionless empirical constants that depend on the materials and surface conditions, not on the contact area or sliding speed (within the classical regime).

Worked Example. A 12.0 kg crate rests on a horizontal floor with $\mu_s = 0.55$ and $\mu_k = 0.40$. A horizontal applied force $F$ is gradually increased from zero.

  1. Maximum static friction: $N = mg = (12.0)(9.80) = 117.6\ \text{N}$, so $f_{s,\max} = \mu_s N = (0.55)(117.6) = 64.7\ \text{N}$. The crate does not move for $F \leq 64.7\ \text{N}$.
  2. Kinetic friction once sliding: $f_k = \mu_k N = (0.40)(117.6) = 47.0\ \text{N}$. If $F = 70.0\ \text{N}$ is maintained after the crate begins to slide, the net force is $70.0 - 47.0 = 23.0\ \text{N}$, giving acceleration $a = 23.0/12.0 \approx 1.92\ \text{m/s}^2$.

Key Theorem (Amontons–Coulomb Laws). The classical friction model rests on two empirical laws: (i) the friction force is proportional to the normal force, and (ii) the friction force is independent of the apparent contact area. A consequence is that on an incline of angle $\theta$, an object is on the verge of sliding when $\tan\theta = \mu_s$, providing a clean experimental method to measure $\mu_s$ directly.

Lab Exercise. Place a wooden block of known mass on an adjustable inclined plane. Slowly increase the incline angle $\theta$ until the block just begins to slide. Record $\theta_c$ and compute $\mu_s = \tan\theta_c$. Repeat with a sheet of sandpaper between the surfaces and compare the two coefficients. Use a motion sensor to measure the block's acceleration as it slides, and verify that $a = g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)$ is consistent with your measured $\mu_k$.
