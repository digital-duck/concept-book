🌱 Normal Force

🌱 Normal Force

Imagine you're sitting in a chair right now. Gravity is pulling you downward, yet you're not falling through the seat. Something must be pushing back up on you — and that something is the normal force.

The normal force is the contact force a surface exerts on an object pressing against it. It always points perpendicular to (that is, directly away from) the surface. "Normal" here is a geometry word meaning "at a right angle," not a synonym for ordinary.

Picture a book resting on a flat table. Gravity pulls the book down with force $\vec{F}_g = mg$, where $m$ is the book's mass and $g \approx 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2$. Because the book isn't accelerating, Newton's second law tells us the net force must be zero:

$$ \sum F_y = N - mg = 0 \implies N = mg $$

The table pushes up with a normal force $N$ exactly equal to the book's weight. If you stack another book on top, $mg$ increases, and the table automatically pushes harder — the surface adjusts.

Simple rule: On a flat, horizontal surface with no other vertical forces, the normal force equals the object's weight. On a tilted surface or when extra forces act vertically, you must redo the force balance to find $N$.

Try it yourself: Hold a paperback book on your palm. Press down gently with your other hand. What do you feel pushing back against your bottom palm? That upward push is the normal force — now you can feel it directly.
