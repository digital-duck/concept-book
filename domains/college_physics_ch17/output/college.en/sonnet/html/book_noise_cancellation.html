🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the magnitude of the normal force $F_\perp$ exerted on a surface divided by the area $A$ over which that force is distributed:

$$ P = \frac{F_\perp}{A} $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; at any point within a static fluid, it acts equally in all directions (Pascal's principle). In a fluid column of uniform density $\rho$ under gravitational acceleration $g$, the gauge pressure at depth $h$ below the free surface is:

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface. This linear dependence on depth arises because each additional fluid layer contributes its weight per unit area to the layers beneath it.

Worked Example. A rectangular tank is filled with water ($\rho = 1000\,\text{kg/m}^3$) to a depth of $h = 3.0\,\text{m}$. The atmospheric pressure at the surface is $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Find the absolute pressure at the bottom of the tank.

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1000)(9.81)(3.0) $$ $$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 2.943 \times 10^4 = 1.307 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa} $$

The gauge pressure (excess above atmospheric) is simply $\rho g h = 2.94 \times 10^4\,\text{Pa} \approx 0.29\,\text{atm}$.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. Any change in pressure applied to an enclosed incompressible fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if the applied surface pressure changes by $\Delta P$, then at every interior point the pressure also changes by $\Delta P$. This principle underlies hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small piston area $A_1$ produces an equal pressure increase throughout the fluid, generating a large force $F_2 = P \cdot A_2$ over a larger piston area $A_2$.

Laboratory Exercise. Using a U-tube manometer partially filled with water, apply a known gauge pressure to one arm by connecting it to a sealed reservoir at a measured height. Record the height difference $\Delta h$ between the two arms and verify that $\Delta P = \rho g \Delta h$. Repeat with a denser fluid (e.g., glycerin, $\rho \approx 1260\,\text{kg/m}^3$) and compare the height differences for the same applied pressure, confirming the inverse dependence of $\Delta h$ on $\rho$.

🌱 Simple Harmonic Motion

🌱 Simple Harmonic Motion

Definition. Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a special class of periodic motion in which the restoring force on an object is directly proportional to its displacement from equilibrium and directed opposite to that displacement. Mathematically, this condition is expressed as

$$ F = -kx, $$

where $k > 0$ is the spring constant (or, more generally, the stiffness coefficient) and $x$ is the displacement from the equilibrium position. Applying Newton's second law, $F = ma$, yields the equation of motion

$$ m\ddot{x} + kx = 0, $$

whose general solution is

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), $$

with angular frequency $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$, amplitude $A$, and phase constant $\phi$ determined by initial conditions. The period and frequency follow immediately:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T}. $$

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 0.50\ \text{kg}$ is attached to a horizontal spring with $k = 200\ \text{N/m}$. At $t = 0$ the block is displaced $x_0 = 0.10\ \text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest.

Find the angular frequency, period, and position at $t = 0.30\ \text{s}$.

Step 1: $\omega = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{200/0.50} = 20\ \text{rad/s}$.

Step 2: $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/20 \approx 0.314\ \text{s}$.

Step 3: Since $v(0) = 0$ and $x(0) = x_0$, we identify $A = 0.10\ \text{m}$ and $\phi = 0$. Therefore

$$ x(0.30) = 0.10\cos(20 \times 0.30) = 0.10\cos(6.0) \approx 0.10 \times 0.960 = 0.096\ \text{m}. $$

Key Theorem. Because the spring force is conservative, the mechanical energy conservation principle established in classical mechanics applies directly to SHM:

$$ E = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2 = \text{const.} $$

This result, rather than introducing new physics, is the familiar kinetic-plus-potential energy balance evaluated for a linear restoring force. It immediately yields the maximum speed $v_{\max} = A\omega$ at $x = 0$, and confirms that the object momentarily stops ($v = 0$) at the turning points $x = \pm A$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a mass on a vertical spring and measure the period for three different masses. Plot $T^2$ versus $m$ and extract $k$ from the slope. Compare with the value obtained from a static force–extension measurement. Identify two potential sources of systematic error in each method.

🌱 Wave

🌱 Wave

Definition. A wave is a disturbance that propagates through a medium (or through free space, in the case of electromagnetic waves), transferring energy and momentum without the net transport of matter. Mathematically, any function of the form

$$ y(x, t) = A\sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) $$

describes a sinusoidal traveling wave moving in the positive $x$-direction, where $A$ is the amplitude, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, and $\phi$ is the initial phase. The wave speed is related to these quantities by

$$ v = \frac{\omega}{k} = \lambda f. $$

Waves are classified as transverse (displacement perpendicular to propagation, e.g., light on a string) or longitudinal (displacement parallel to propagation, e.g., sound in air).

Worked Example. A sinusoidal wave on a stretched string is described by $y(x,t) = 0.020\sin(6.28x - 31.4t)$ meters, where $x$ is in meters and $t$ in seconds. Identify the amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and wave speed.

  • Amplitude: $A = 0.020\ \text{m}$
  • Wave number: $k = 6.28\ \text{rad/m}$, so $\lambda = 2\pi/k = 1.00\ \text{m}$
  • Angular frequency: $\omega = 31.4\ \text{rad/s}$, so $f = \omega/2\pi = 5.00\ \text{Hz}$
  • Wave speed: $v = \omega/k = 31.4/6.28 = 5.00\ \text{m/s}$

Key Theorem — Wave Equation. Any wave satisfying the linear wave equation

$$ \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} $$

obeys the superposition principle: if $y_1$ and $y_2$ are both solutions, then $y_1 + y_2$ is also a solution. This underpins phenomena such as interference and standing waves. For a transverse wave on a string under tension $T$ with linear mass density $\mu$, the wave speed is $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$, confirming that $v$ depends only on the medium's physical properties.

Using a function generator and a stretched string of known linear mass density $\mu = 5.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{kg/m}$, set the tension to $T = 12\ \text{N}$ and drive the string at $f = 15\ \text{Hz}$. Predict the wavelength using $v = \sqrt{T/\mu}$ and $\lambda = v/f$, then measure the node spacing in the resulting standing wave pattern to verify your prediction experimentally. Quantify any discrepancy and identify its likely sources.

🌱 Amplitude

🌱 Amplitude

Definition. The amplitude of a periodic oscillation is the maximum magnitude of displacement from the equilibrium position. For a particle undergoing simple harmonic motion (SHM), the displacement as a function of time is

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi_0), $$

where $A > 0$ is the amplitude, $\omega$ is the angular frequency, and $\phi_0$ is the initial phase. The amplitude is not the range of the oscillation but rather the half-range: the motion spans the interval $[-A, A]$, giving a total excursion of $2A$. Dimensionally, amplitude carries the same units as the displacement it measures — meters for translational oscillations, radians for angular ones.

The mechanical energy of an ideal SHM system (spring constant $k$, mass $m$) is entirely determined by $A$:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2. $$

This relation makes amplitude the single most important parameter characterizing the intensity of an oscillation.

Worked Example. A $0.50\,\text{kg}$ block attached to a spring with $k = 200\,\text{N/m}$ is displaced $8.0\,\text{cm}$ from equilibrium and released from rest. Find the amplitude and the total mechanical energy.

Because the block is released from rest at maximum displacement, the release point coincides with the turning point, so

$$ A = 8.0\,\text{cm} = 0.080\,\text{m}. $$

The total energy is

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.080)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.0064) = 0.64\,\text{J}. $$

At equilibrium ($x = 0$) this energy is entirely kinetic. The maximum speed follows from energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}mv_{\max}^2 = E$, giving $v_{\max} = \sqrt{2E/m} = \sqrt{2(0.64)/0.50} = 1.6\,\text{m/s}$.

Key Theorem. For undamped SHM, the amplitude is a conserved quantity: it remains constant for all time because no energy leaves the system. Formally, since $E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \text{const}$, any combination of potential and kinetic energy at any phase yields the same $A$. This contrasts sharply with damped oscillations, where $A(t) = A_0 e^{-\gamma t}$ decays exponentially.

Lab Exercise. Mount a spring vertically, hang a known mass, and displace it by measured amounts $A = 2, 4, 6, 8\,\text{cm}$. Record the period $T$ and the maximum speed using a motion sensor. Verify that $T$ is independent of $A$ and that $v_{\max}$ scales linearly with $A$, confirming $E \propto A^2$.

🍃 Sound

🍃 Sound

Definition. Sound is a mechanical, longitudinal wave that propagates through a compressible medium by alternating compressions and rarefactions of the medium's constituent particles. Unlike electromagnetic waves, sound requires a material medium and cannot travel through a vacuum. A sound wave is fully characterized by a single primitive quantity—frequency $f$ (Hz), the number of complete oscillation cycles per second—together with derived quantities: wavelength $\lambda$ (m), amplitude $A$ (Pa for pressure waves), and propagation speed $v$ (m/s). These are related by the fundamental wave equation:

$$ v = f\lambda $$

The speed of sound in an ideal gas is given by:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P_0}{\rho}} $$

where $\gamma$ is the adiabatic index (approximately 1.40 for diatomic gases such as air), $P_0$ is the equilibrium pressure, and $\rho$ is the mass density. For dry air at $T = 20\,^\circ\text{C}$, this yields $v \approx 343\,\text{m/s}$.

Worked Example. A tuning fork vibrating at $f = 440\,\text{Hz}$ (concert A) is struck in air at $20\,^\circ\text{C}$. Determine the wavelength of the sound wave produced.

Using $v = f\lambda$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{343\,\text{m/s}}{440\,\text{Hz}} \approx 0.780\,\text{m} $$

This wavelength falls within the range of typical room dimensions, which is why standing-wave resonances are audible in enclosed spaces.

Key Theorem: Newton–Laplace Correction. Newton originally computed the speed of sound assuming an isothermal process, obtaining $v = \sqrt{P_0/\rho}$, which underestimates the measured value by roughly 15%. Laplace corrected this by recognizing that compressions and rarefactions in a sound wave occur too rapidly for heat exchange with the surroundings, making the process adiabatic rather than isothermal. Replacing $P_0$ with $\gamma P_0$ yields the accurate expression above. This correction reflects a fundamental principle: the thermodynamic process governing wave propagation determines the appropriate elastic modulus.

Lab Exercise. Using a resonance tube apparatus open at one end and closed at the other, strike a tuning fork of known frequency $f$ and slide the tube until the first resonance is detected (tube length $L = \lambda/4$). Measure $L$ with a ruler and compute $v = 4Lf$. Compare your result to the theoretical value $v = 331\sqrt{1 + T/273}\,\text{m/s}$ (with $T$ in degrees Celsius) and calculate the percent error. Repeat at three different temperatures to verify the temperature dependence.

🍃 Interference

🍃 Interference

Definition. Interference is the phenomenon arising when two or more coherent waves superpose in the same region of space, producing a resultant amplitude determined by the principle of superposition. For two sinusoidal waves of equal amplitude $A$ and angular frequency $\omega$, differing only by a phase difference $\delta$, the superposition yields:

$$ y = 2A\cos\!\left(\frac{\delta}{2}\right)\sin\!\left(\omega t - kx + \frac{\delta}{2}\right) $$

The resultant amplitude is $2A\cos(\delta/2)$. When $\delta = 2m\pi$ for integer $m$, the amplitude reaches its maximum $2A$ — constructive interference. When $\delta = (2m+1)\pi$, the amplitude vanishes — destructive interference. The path length difference $\Delta L$ relates to phase by $\delta = 2\pi\Delta L/\lambda$, so constructive interference requires $\Delta L = m\lambda$ and destructive interference requires $\Delta L = (m + \tfrac{1}{2})\lambda$.

Worked Example. Two coherent speakers separated by $d = 4.00\ \text{m}$ emit sound at $f = 686\ \text{Hz}$ in air ($v = 343\ \text{m/s}$, so $\lambda = 0.500\ \text{m}$). A listener stands on the perpendicular bisector at distance $L = 3.00\ \text{m}$ from the midpoint, then moves laterally to position $y$ along a line parallel to the speaker axis. In the small-angle approximation ($y \operatorname{ll} L$), the path difference is:

$$ \Delta L \approx \frac{yd}{L} $$

For the first destructive minimum, set $\Delta L = \lambda/2 = 0.250\ \text{m}$:

$$ y = \frac{\Delta L \cdot L}{d} = \frac{0.250 \times 3.00}{4.00} = 0.188\ \text{m} $$

The listener first hears silence at approximately $18.8\ \text{cm}$ off-center.

Key Theorem (Young's Double-Slit). For a double-slit geometry with slit separation $d$ and screen distance $L \operatorname{gg} d$, bright fringes (constructive maxima) occur at lateral positions:

$$ y_m = \frac{m\lambda L}{d}, \quad m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots $$

Dark fringes satisfy $y_m = \left(m + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\lambda L/d$. The fringe spacing $\Delta y = \lambda L/d$ provides a direct experimental measure of wavelength. This result follows directly from applying the constructive-interference condition $\Delta L = m\lambda$ to the small-angle approximation introduced above.

Lab Exercise. Set up a laser ($\lambda \approx 633\ \text{nm}$) incident on a double slit with $d = 0.250\ \text{mm}$ and project the pattern on a screen $1.20\ \text{m}$ away. Measure five consecutive bright-fringe positions, compute the mean fringe spacing $\Delta y$, and solve for $\lambda$. Compare your result with the labeled wavelength and compute the percent error. Identify one systematic error source and propose a correction.

🌸 Noise Cancellation

🌸 Noise Cancellation

Definition. Noise cancellation is the deliberate superposition of a secondary sound wave upon an unwanted primary (noise) wave such that destructive interference reduces the resultant amplitude toward zero. For two sinusoidal waves of identical frequency and amplitude, total destructive interference occurs when their phase difference satisfies $\Delta\phi = (2n+1)\pi$ for integer $n$. In the time domain, if the noise signal is $p_1(t) = P_0\sin(\omega t)$, the canceling signal must be $p_2(t) = P_0\sin(\omega t + \pi) = -P_0\sin(\omega t)$, yielding superposition $p_1 + p_2 = 0$. Active noise-cancellation (ANC) systems implement this principle electronically: a microphone samples the incoming noise, a processor computes the inverted waveform, and a speaker emits that anti-noise signal in real time. The residual pressure amplitude at the listener's ear is

$$ P_\text{res} = \left|P_1 e^{i\phi_1} + P_2 e^{i\phi_2}\right|, $$

which approaches zero when $P_1 = P_2$ and $|\phi_1 - \phi_2| = \pi$.

Worked Example. A noise source produces a pure tone at $f = 250\,\text{Hz}$ with pressure amplitude $P_0 = 0.80\,\text{Pa}$. An ANC speaker, co-located with the listener, emits a tone of equal frequency and amplitude but with a phase lag of $185°$. Find the residual amplitude.

The deviation from perfect anti-phase is $\delta = 185° - 180° = 5° = 0.0873\,\text{rad}$. Using the interference formula,

$$ P_\text{res} = 2P_0\left|\sin\!\left(\frac{\delta}{2}\right)\right| = 2(0.80)\sin(0.0436) \approx 0.070\,\text{Pa}. $$

The sound pressure level reduction is $\Delta L = 20\log_{10}(0.80/0.070) \approx 21\,\text{dB}$, illustrating how even a small phase error significantly limits attenuation.

Key Theorem. For broadband noise, complete cancellation at a single spatial point requires the anti-noise signal to match the noise in amplitude, frequency content, and phase simultaneously at that location. Effective ANC is therefore confined to small, well-controlled regions—such as the cavity near a headphone driver—where path lengths are short and the processor can maintain accurate phase alignment across the relevant frequency band.

Lab Exercise. Using a function generator, produce a $400\,\text{Hz}$ sine wave through one speaker. Connect a second speaker to an inverting amplifier driven by the same signal. Place a microphone equidistant from both speakers and measure the sound pressure level with and without the second speaker active. Record the achieved attenuation in dB, then vary the separation distance in $5\,\text{cm}$ increments and plot attenuation versus separation. Identify the path-length difference at which cancellation degrades by $10\,\text{dB}$, and compare this to the quarter-wavelength condition $\Delta d = \lambda/4$.

Payoff 🎯

Noise cancellation represents the culminating synthesis of wave physics: the deliberate engineering of destructive interference to eliminate unwanted sound. Where earlier chapters established the mathematical machinery of wave superposition, phase relationships, and standing waves, noise cancellation demonstrates that these principles are not merely descriptive but prescriptive — they can be harnessed to shape the acoustic environment with precision. At its core, the technique exploits the condition for complete destructive interference: two waves of equal amplitude and frequency, offset in phase by exactly $\pi$ radians, produce a net displacement of zero at every point in space and time.

Formally, if a noise signal is represented as $p_{\text{noise}}(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$, the canceling wave must satisfy $p_{\text{cancel}}(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi + \pi) = -A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$, so that their superposition yields $p_{\text{total}}(t) = p_{\text{noise}} + p_{\text{cancel}} = 0$. In practice, a microphone samples the incoming noise, a digital signal processor inverts the waveform in real time, and a speaker emits the anti-phase signal. The fidelity of cancellation depends critically on matching amplitude and maintaining phase accuracy across all relevant frequencies.

Worked example. A constant engine hum at $f = 120\ \text{Hz}$ enters a cabin with pressure amplitude $A = 0.4\ \text{Pa}$. The active system generates $p_{\text{cancel}}(t) = -0.4\cos(2\pi \cdot 120\, t)\ \text{Pa}$. At the listener's ear, the resultant amplitude is $|A_{\text{net}}| = |0.4 - 0.4| = 0\ \text{Pa}$, achieving theoretical silence. Real systems attain $20$–$30\ \text{dB}$ of reduction across a band, limited by latency in the feedback loop.

Key theorem. The principle of linear superposition guarantees that for any finite sum of sinusoidal components, each frequency component can be independently canceled, provided the system bandwidth and processing latency satisfy $\tau_{\text{latency}} \operatorname{ll} 1/f_{\text{max}}$.

This principle underlies aviation headsets that protect pilots from rotor noise, architectural acoustic panels that quiet open-plan offices, and industrial hearing protection that preserves speech intelligibility while attenuating machinery.
