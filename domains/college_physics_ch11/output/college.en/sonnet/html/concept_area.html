🌱 Area

Definition. In physics and mathematics, area is a scalar quantity that measures the two-dimensional extent of a surface or planar region. For a rectangle with side lengths $a$ and $b$, the area is $A = ab$. For an arbitrary planar region $\mathcal{R}$, area is defined via the double integral

$$ A = \iint_{\mathcal{R}} dA, $$

where $dA$ is the area element. In Cartesian coordinates, $dA = dx\, dy$; in polar coordinates, $dA = r\, dr\, d\theta$. Area carries SI units of square meters ($\text{m}^2$).

In College Physics 2E, area arises centrally in the study of fluids, thermodynamics, and electromagnetism—wherever a physical law involves a flux or a distributed force acting over a surface.

Worked Example. A circular piston of diameter $d = 0.10\ \text{m}$ is subjected to a gauge pressure of $P = 2.0 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$. Find the net force on the piston face.

First, compute the cross-sectional area:

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi \left(\frac{0.10}{2}\right)^2 = \pi (0.05)^2 \approx 7.85 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2. $$

Applying the relation $F = PA$:

$$ F = (2.0 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa})(7.85 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2) \approx 1.57 \times 10^3\ \text{N}. $$

The net force on the piston is approximately $1.57\ \text{kN}$, directed perpendicular to the piston face.

Key Theorem (Pressure–Force Relation). For a uniform pressure $P$ acting over a flat surface of area $A$, the magnitude of the resulting force is

$$ F = PA. $$

This follows directly from the definition of pressure as force per unit area, $P = F/A$. When pressure varies over the surface, the total force is obtained by integration: $F = \int P\, dA$. This generalization is essential when analyzing non-uniform pressure distributions in fluid statics and aerodynamics.

Lab Exercise. Using a vernier caliper, measure the inner diameter of a cylindrical syringe barrel at three positions along its length. Compute the mean cross-sectional area $\bar{A} = \pi \bar{r}^2$ and its uncertainty $\delta A = 2\pi \bar{r}\, \delta r$. With the syringe sealed, apply a known force $F$ via a spring scale and determine the resulting gauge pressure $P = F/\bar{A}$. Compare your measured pressure with the reading from an attached pressure gauge. Discuss sources of systematic error arising from friction between the plunger and barrel wall.
