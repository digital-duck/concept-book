🌱 Mass

🌱 Mass

Definition. Mass is a fundamental scalar quantity that measures the amount of matter contained in an object and quantifies its resistance to changes in translational motion. In classical mechanics, two distinct but empirically equivalent concepts of mass arise. Inertial mass $m_i$ is defined operationally through Newton's second law,

$$ \mathbf{F} = m_i \mathbf{a}, $$

where $\mathbf{F}$ is the net applied force and $\mathbf{a}$ is the resulting acceleration. Gravitational mass $m_g$ characterizes the strength of an object's coupling to a gravitational field, appearing in Newton's law of universal gravitation:

$$ F_g = G\frac{m_g M_g}{r^2}. $$

The equivalence principle, confirmed experimentally to one part in $10^{12}$ by Eötvös-type experiments, asserts $m_i = m_g$, a cornerstone underlying general relativity. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg), defined since 2019 by fixing the Planck constant $h = 6.62607015 \times 10^{-34}\ \text{J·s}$.

Worked Example. A block of unknown mass rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A constant horizontal force $F = 12.0\ \text{N}$ is applied, and the block accelerates at $a = 3.00\ \text{m/s}^2$. Applying Newton's second law:

$$ m = \frac{F}{a} = \frac{12.0\ \text{N}}{3.00\ \text{m/s}^2} = 4.00\ \text{kg}. $$

The weight of this block near Earth's surface ($g = 9.81\ \text{m/s}^2$) is then $W = mg = (4.00)(9.81) = 39.2\ \text{N}$, illustrating the distinction between mass (intrinsic, invariant) and weight (context-dependent force).

Key Theorem — Conservation of Mass (Non-relativistic). In any closed system undergoing collisions or interactions at speeds $v \operatorname{ll} c$, the total mass is conserved:

$$ \sum_i m_i = \text{constant}. $$

This follows from Newton's third law and the additivity of inertial mass. At relativistic speeds, mass conservation is subsumed by conservation of the total relativistic energy–momentum four-vector.

🌱 Angular Velocity

🌱 Angular Velocity

Definition. Angular velocity describes the rate at which a rigid body rotates about a fixed axis. For a particle moving along a circular path, let $\theta$ denote the angular position measured in radians from a reference direction. The instantaneous angular velocity $\omega$ is defined as

$$ \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt}, $$

with SI units of radians per second ($\text{rad/s}$). Angular velocity is a vector quantity; its direction is given by the right-hand rule—curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of rotation, and the extended thumb points along $\boldsymbol{\omega}$. For rotation in the $xy$-plane, counterclockwise motion yields $\boldsymbol{\omega} = \omega\,\hat{k}$ (positive), while clockwise motion yields a negative component along $\hat{k}$.

When $\omega$ is constant, the motion is called uniform circular motion, and the angular displacement over a time interval $\Delta t$ is simply $\Delta\theta = \omega\,\Delta t$.

Worked Example. A grinding wheel starts from rest and reaches an angular speed of $120\,\text{rpm}$ in $4.0\,\text{s}$ under constant angular acceleration. Convert the final angular velocity to SI units and find the angular displacement.

Converting: $\omega_f = 120 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 4\pi \approx 12.6\,\text{rad/s}$.

Since $\omega_0 = 0$ and acceleration is constant, the average angular velocity is $\bar{\omega} = \frac{\omega_0 + \omega_f}{2} = 2\pi\,\text{rad/s}$. The total angular displacement is

$$ \Delta\theta = \bar{\omega}\,\Delta t = 2\pi \times 4.0 = 8\pi \approx 25.1\,\text{rad}, $$

corresponding to exactly four complete revolutions.

Key Theorem. The relationship between angular velocity and the linear (tangential) speed $v$ of a point at radius $r$ from the rotation axis is

$$ v = r\omega. $$

This follows directly from the arc-length definition $s = r\theta$, differentiated with respect to time. Points farther from the axis move faster, even though all points on a rigid body share the same $\omega$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a bicycle wheel on a frictionless axle and mark a point on the rim. Using a stroboscope or high-speed camera, measure the time $T$ for ten complete revolutions. Compute $\omega = 2\pi/T$ and compare it to the linear speed of the rim point measured by timing the passage of the mark across a fixed reference. Verify that $v = r\omega$ holds to within experimental uncertainty, and discuss sources of error including axle friction and parallax in angle measurement.

🍃 Moment Of Inertia

🍃 Moment Of Inertia

Definition. The moment of inertia $I$ of a rigid body about a specified axis is the rotational analog of mass in translational mechanics. For a discrete system of $n$ point masses, it is defined as

$$ I = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i r_i^2, $$

where $m_i$ is the mass of the $i$-th particle and $r_i$ is its perpendicular distance from the rotation axis. For a continuous body with mass density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$, this sum becomes an integral:

$$ I = \int r^2 \, dm = \int r^2 \rho(\mathbf{r}) \, dV. $$

The SI unit of moment of inertia is $\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$. Unlike mass, $I$ depends critically on the choice of axis; the same object can have very different moments of inertia about different axes.

Worked Example. Consider a uniform thin rod of mass $M$ and length $L$ rotating about an axis perpendicular to the rod through one end. Let $x$ measure distance from the pivot, so a differential element $dx$ carries mass $dm = (M/L)\,dx$. Then

$$ I_{\text{end}} = \int_0^L x^2 \frac{M}{L}\,dx = \frac{M}{L}\cdot\frac{L^3}{3} = \frac{1}{3}ML^2. $$

Had the axis passed through the center of mass instead, the integration limits shift to $-L/2$ to $L/2$, yielding $I_{\text{cm}} = \frac{1}{12}ML^2$, a smaller value—confirming that rotating about a more central axis requires less torque for the same angular acceleration.

Key Theorem (Parallel-Axis Theorem). If $I_{\text{cm}}$ is the moment of inertia about an axis through the center of mass, then the moment of inertia about any parallel axis displaced by distance $d$ is

$$ I = I_{\text{cm}} + Md^2. $$

This follows directly from expanding $r^2 = |\mathbf{r}_{\text{cm}} + \mathbf{d}|^2$ in the integral and noting that the cross term vanishes by definition of the center of mass. The theorem eliminates the need to re-derive $I$ from scratch for every possible axis.

Lab Exercise. Set a solid disk (mass $M$, radius $R$) on a low-friction axle through its center. Hang a known mass $m$ from a string wrapped around the axle (radius $r_0$). Measure the linear acceleration $a$ of the hanging mass using a motion sensor. Apply Newton's second law to the hanging mass ($mg - T = ma$) and the rotational equation ($Tr_0 = I\alpha$, with $\alpha = a/r_0$) to solve for $I_{\text{exp}}$. Compare to the theoretical value $I_{\text{theory}} = \frac{1}{2}MR^2$ and compute the percent discrepancy, accounting for axle friction if necessary.

🌱 Linear Velocity

🌱 Linear Velocity

Definition. For a particle undergoing circular motion, the linear velocity (also called tangential velocity) is the instantaneous rate of change of arc length with respect to time. If $s$ denotes the arc length traveled along a circular path of radius $r$, then

$$ v = \frac{ds}{dt}. $$

Since arc length and angular displacement $\theta$ are related by $s = r\theta$, differentiating with respect to time yields the fundamental kinematic relation

$$ v = r\omega, $$

where $\omega = d\theta/dt$ is the angular velocity in radians per second. The direction of $\vec{v}$ is always tangent to the circular path, perpendicular to the radius vector at that instant, and its magnitude changes only if $\omega$ changes.

Worked Example. A bicycle wheel of radius $r = 0.35\ \text{m}$ rotates at a constant rate of $n = 120\ \text{rpm}$. Find the linear speed of a point on the rim.

Step 1. Convert to SI: $\omega = 120\ \text{rev/min} \times \frac{2\pi\ \text{rad}}{1\ \text{rev}} \times \frac{1\ \text{min}}{60\ \text{s}} = 4\pi\ \text{rad/s} \approx 12.57\ \text{rad/s}$.

Step 2. Apply $v = r\omega$:

$$ v = (0.35\ \text{m})(4\pi\ \text{rad/s}) = 1.4\pi\ \text{m/s} \approx 4.40\ \text{m/s}. $$

A point at half the radius ($r' = 0.175\ \text{m}$) would have $v' = 2.20\ \text{m/s}$, illustrating that linear speed scales linearly with radial distance while $\omega$ remains uniform throughout the rigid body.

Key Theorem. For uniform circular motion ($\omega = \text{const}$), the linear speed $v$ is constant in magnitude, yet the velocity vector continuously changes direction. The centripetal acceleration $a_c$ required to sustain this motion is

$$ a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2, $$

directed radially inward. This result follows directly from differentiating the velocity vector expressed in polar coordinates; it is the single new primitive introduced in this section.

Using a stroboscope, measure the rotational frequency $f$ at a fixed motor setting. Compute $\omega = 2\pi f$ and predict $v_i = r_i \omega$ for each tape position. Compare predictions with linear speeds determined from high-speed video frame analysis. Quantify discrepancies and identify dominant sources of systematic error.

🍃 Rotational Kinetic Energy

🍃 Rotational Kinetic Energy

Definition. Given a rigid body with moment of inertia $I$ rotating about a fixed axis with angular velocity $\omega$, the rotational kinetic energy is defined as

$$ K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 $$

This single new quantity follows directly from summing the ordinary kinetic energies of all mass elements: each element $dm$ at perpendicular distance $r$ from the axis moves at speed $v = r\omega$, so $\frac{1}{2}v^2\,dm = \frac{1}{2}r^2\omega^2\,dm$, and integrating over the entire body yields $\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$. The form is structurally identical to translational kinetic energy $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$, with $I$ replacing mass and $\omega$ replacing linear speed.

Worked Example. A solid uniform cylinder of mass $M = 4.0\,\text{kg}$ and radius $R = 0.20\,\text{m}$ spins freely about its symmetry axis at $\omega = 30\,\text{rad/s}$. Its moment of inertia about that axis is $I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2$. Substituting:

$$ I = \frac{1}{2}(4.0)(0.20)^2 = 0.080\,\text{kg·m}^2 $$ $$ K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}(0.080)(30)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.080)(900) = 36\,\text{J} $$

This energy was stored in the cylinder by the external torque that spun it up and is entirely recoverable — for example, by coupling the cylinder to a generator.

Key Theorem — Work–Energy Theorem for Rotation. The net work done by all torques acting on a rigid body equals the change in its rotational kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}I\omega_i^2 $$

This follows from integrating $dW = \tau\,d\theta$ and applying Newton's second law for rotation $\tau_{\text{net}} = I\alpha$. When both translational and rotational motion are present, the total mechanical kinetic energy is $K = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I_{\text{cm}}\omega^2$.

Lab Exercise. Release a solid sphere, a hollow spherical shell, and a cylindrical hoop — all of identical mass $m$ and radius $R$ — from rest at the top of an inclined plane of height $h = 0.50\,\text{m}$. Applying energy conservation with the rolling constraint $v_{\text{cm}} = R\omega$:

$$ mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I_{\text{cm}}\omega^2 $$

Predict the finishing speed of each object, verify predictions by timing the descent experimentally, and rank the objects by arrival order. Explain why objects with smaller $I_{\text{cm}}$ relative to $mR^2$ reach the bottom first.

🌱 Translational Kinetic Energy

🌱 Translational Kinetic Energy

Definition. The translational kinetic energy of a rigid body is the energy associated with the motion of its center of mass through space, without regard to any rotational motion about that center. For a body of mass $m$ moving with center-of-mass speed $v_{\text{cm}}$, the translational kinetic energy is defined as

$$ K_{\text{trans}} = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2. $$

This quantity is a scalar, measured in joules (J) in SI units, and is always non-negative. It represents the capacity of the body to do work by virtue of its translational motion alone. When a body simultaneously translates and rotates—as in rolling without slipping—the total mechanical kinetic energy is the sum of translational and rotational contributions: $K_{\text{total}} = K_{\text{trans}} + K_{\text{rot}}$. The rotational term $K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$ is treated fully in the section on rotational kinetic energy; here we focus exclusively on $K_{\text{trans}}$.

Worked Example. A solid bowling ball of mass $m = 5.0\ \text{kg}$ rolls without slipping along a level lane at a center-of-mass speed $v_{\text{cm}} = 4.0\ \text{m/s}$. Compute its translational kinetic energy.

$$ K_{\text{trans}} = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2 = \frac{1}{2}(5.0\ \text{kg})(4.0\ \text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(5.0)(16.0)\ \text{J} = 40\ \text{J}. $$

This result depends only on the mass and the speed of the center of mass; the ball's size, shape, and rotational state do not enter the calculation of $K_{\text{trans}}$.

Key Theorem (Work–Energy Theorem for Translation). The net work done on a body by all external forces equals the change in its translational kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K_{\text{trans}} = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2. $$

This theorem follows directly from Newton's second law applied to the center-of-mass motion and is independent of any internal forces or rotational effects.

🌸 Rolling Without Slipping

🌸 Rolling Without Slipping

Definition. A rigid body rolls without slipping on a surface when every point of contact between the body and the surface has zero instantaneous velocity relative to that surface. For a uniform cylinder or sphere of radius $R$ rolling along a flat surface, this constraint links the translational velocity of the center of mass $v_{\text{cm}}$ to the angular velocity $\omega$ about the center of mass by the relation

$$ v_{\text{cm}} = R\omega. $$

Differentiating with respect to time yields the analogous acceleration constraint $a_{\text{cm}} = R\alpha$, where $\alpha$ is the angular acceleration. The static friction force at the contact point enforces this constraint but does no work, since the contact point is instantaneously at rest.

Worked Example. A solid sphere of mass $m = 2.0\ \text{kg}$ and radius $R = 0.10\ \text{m}$ starts from rest and rolls without slipping down an incline of angle $\theta = 30°$ and length $L = 2.0\ \text{m}$. Find the speed of the center of mass at the bottom.

Apply conservation of energy. The moment of inertia of a solid sphere is $I = \frac{2}{5}mR^2$. Setting potential energy equal to the sum of translational and rotational kinetic energy:

$$ mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2}{5}mR^2\cdot\frac{v_{\text{cm}}^2}{R^2} = \frac{7}{10}mv_{\text{cm}}^2. $$

With $h = L\sin\theta = (2.0)(0.5) = 1.0\ \text{m}$:

$$ v_{\text{cm}} = \sqrt{\frac{10gh}{7}} = \sqrt{\frac{10(9.8)(1.0)}{7}} \approx 3.74\ \text{m/s}. $$

Key Theorem. For any rotationally symmetric object of moment of inertia $I = \beta mR^2$ rolling without slipping down an incline of height $h$, the no-slip constraint reduces the speed at the bottom relative to a sliding object. The center-of-mass speed satisfies

$$ v_{\text{cm}} = \sqrt{\frac{2gh}{1+\beta}}. $$

A larger $\beta$ means a greater fraction of energy goes into rotation, so the object translates more slowly. For a solid sphere $\beta = 2/5$; a solid cylinder $\beta = 1/2$ arrives at the bottom more slowly, and a thin-walled hollow cylinder $\beta = 1$ more slowly still.

Payoff 🎯

Rolling without slipping is the culminating principle of rotational dynamics because it forges an exact, constraint-based link between translational and rotational motion. Throughout Chapter 10, the student has built up angular kinematics, torque, moment of inertia, and rotational kinetic energy as parallel but separate formalisms. Rolling without slipping is the condition that collapses that separation: for a rigid body of radius $R$ rolling on a surface without sliding, the instantaneous contact point is at rest, imposing the kinematic constraint

$$ v_{\text{cm}} = R\omega, $$

where $v_{\text{cm}}$ is the speed of the center of mass and $\omega$ is the angular velocity. This single equation transforms two independent degrees of freedom into one, making problems that would otherwise require separate translational and rotational equations tractable through a unified energy method.

Worked Example. A solid sphere of mass $M$ and radius $R$ is released from rest at the top of an incline of height $h$. Applying conservation of mechanical energy with the rolling constraint:

$$ Mgh = \tfrac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}I\omega^2. $$

For a solid sphere, $I = \tfrac{2}{5}MR^2$. Substituting $\omega = v_{\text{cm}}/R$:

$$ Mgh = \tfrac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\tfrac{2}{5}MR^2\right)\frac{v_{\text{cm}}^2}{R^2} = \tfrac{7}{10}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2. $$

Therefore $v_{\text{cm}} = \sqrt{\tfrac{10gh}{7}}$, a result independent of both $M$ and $R$. This universality is not an accident — it reflects that the fraction of kinetic energy stored in rotation depends only on the geometry encoded in the moment of inertia.

Key Theorem. For any axially symmetric rigid body rolling without slipping on a fixed surface, the total mechanical energy decomposes as $K = \tfrac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2(1 + I/MR^2)$. The dimensionless factor $I/MR^2$ — equal to $\tfrac{1}{2}$ for a disk, $\tfrac{2}{5}$ for a sphere, and $1$ for a thin ring — uniquely determines the ratio of rotational to translational kinetic energy and governs every aspect of the body's dynamics on an incline.

Lab Exercise. Release a solid cylinder, a hollow cylinder, and a sphere simultaneously down the same inclined plane. Measure their finishing times and compare with the theoretical predictions from the rolling-without-slipping constraint. Identify which geometric factor — not mass, not radius — is the sole determinant of the race outcome.

The physics unlocked here extends far beyond ramps. The rolling constraint is the conceptual seed of gear-train analysis in mechanical engineering, wheel-ground interaction in vehicle dynamics, and the no-slip assumption underlying much of contact mechanics. To see how profoundly this single constraint shapes the modern world, follow the thread into the mechanics of automotive drivetrains — where rolling without slipping is not merely an idealization, but an engineering requirement enforced by friction, traction control, and differential design.
