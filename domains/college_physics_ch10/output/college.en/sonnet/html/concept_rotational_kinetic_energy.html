🍃 Rotational Kinetic Energy

🍃 Rotational Kinetic Energy

Definition. Given a rigid body with moment of inertia $I$ rotating about a fixed axis with angular velocity $\omega$, the rotational kinetic energy is defined as

$$ K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 $$

This single new quantity follows directly from summing the ordinary kinetic energies of all mass elements: each element $dm$ at perpendicular distance $r$ from the axis moves at speed $v = r\omega$, so $\frac{1}{2}v^2\,dm = \frac{1}{2}r^2\omega^2\,dm$, and integrating over the entire body yields $\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$. The form is structurally identical to translational kinetic energy $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$, with $I$ replacing mass and $\omega$ replacing linear speed.

Worked Example. A solid uniform cylinder of mass $M = 4.0\,\text{kg}$ and radius $R = 0.20\,\text{m}$ spins freely about its symmetry axis at $\omega = 30\,\text{rad/s}$. Its moment of inertia about that axis is $I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2$. Substituting:

$$ I = \frac{1}{2}(4.0)(0.20)^2 = 0.080\,\text{kg·m}^2 $$ $$ K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}(0.080)(30)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.080)(900) = 36\,\text{J} $$

This energy was stored in the cylinder by the external torque that spun it up and is entirely recoverable — for example, by coupling the cylinder to a generator.

Key Theorem — Work–Energy Theorem for Rotation. The net work done by all torques acting on a rigid body equals the change in its rotational kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}I\omega_i^2 $$

This follows from integrating $dW = \tau\,d\theta$ and applying Newton's second law for rotation $\tau_{\text{net}} = I\alpha$. When both translational and rotational motion are present, the total mechanical kinetic energy is $K = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I_{\text{cm}}\omega^2$.

Lab Exercise. Release a solid sphere, a hollow spherical shell, and a cylindrical hoop — all of identical mass $m$ and radius $R$ — from rest at the top of an inclined plane of height $h = 0.50\,\text{m}$. Applying energy conservation with the rolling constraint $v_{\text{cm}} = R\omega$:

$$ mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I_{\text{cm}}\omega^2 $$

Predict the finishing speed of each object, verify predictions by timing the descent experimentally, and rank the objects by arrival order. Explain why objects with smaller $I_{\text{cm}}$ relative to $mR^2$ reach the bottom first.
