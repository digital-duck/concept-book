🌱 Linear Velocity

🌱 Linear Velocity

Definition. For a particle undergoing circular motion, the linear velocity (also called tangential velocity) is the instantaneous rate of change of arc length with respect to time. If $s$ denotes the arc length traveled along a circular path of radius $r$, then

$$ v = \frac{ds}{dt}. $$

Since arc length and angular displacement $\theta$ are related by $s = r\theta$, differentiating with respect to time yields the fundamental kinematic relation

$$ v = r\omega, $$

where $\omega = d\theta/dt$ is the angular velocity in radians per second. The direction of $\vec{v}$ is always tangent to the circular path, perpendicular to the radius vector at that instant, and its magnitude changes only if $\omega$ changes.

Worked Example. A bicycle wheel of radius $r = 0.35\ \text{m}$ rotates at a constant rate of $n = 120\ \text{rpm}$. Find the linear speed of a point on the rim.

Step 1. Convert to SI: $\omega = 120\ \text{rev/min} \times \frac{2\pi\ \text{rad}}{1\ \text{rev}} \times \frac{1\ \text{min}}{60\ \text{s}} = 4\pi\ \text{rad/s} \approx 12.57\ \text{rad/s}$.

Step 2. Apply $v = r\omega$:

$$ v = (0.35\ \text{m})(4\pi\ \text{rad/s}) = 1.4\pi\ \text{m/s} \approx 4.40\ \text{m/s}. $$

A point at half the radius ($r' = 0.175\ \text{m}$) would have $v' = 2.20\ \text{m/s}$, illustrating that linear speed scales linearly with radial distance while $\omega$ remains uniform throughout the rigid body.

Key Theorem. For uniform circular motion ($\omega = \text{const}$), the linear speed $v$ is constant in magnitude, yet the velocity vector continuously changes direction. The centripetal acceleration $a_c$ required to sustain this motion is

$$ a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2, $$

directed radially inward. This result follows directly from differentiating the velocity vector expressed in polar coordinates; it is the single new primitive introduced in this section.

Lab Exercise. Mount a disk on a variable-speed motor. Attach reflective tape at radii $r_1 = 5\ \text{cm}$, $r_2 = 10\ \text{cm}$, and $r_3 = 15\ \text{cm}$. Using a stroboscope, measure the rotational frequency $f$ at a fixed motor setting. Compute $\omega = 2\pi f$ and predict $v_i = r_i \omega$ for each tape position. Compare predictions with linear speeds determined from high-speed video frame analysis. Quantify discrepancies and identify dominant sources of systematic error.
