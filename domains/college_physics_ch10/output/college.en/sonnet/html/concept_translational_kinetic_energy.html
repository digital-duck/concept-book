🌱 Translational Kinetic Energy

Definition. The translational kinetic energy of a rigid body is the energy associated with the motion of its center of mass through space, without regard to any rotational motion about that center. For a body of mass $m$ moving with center-of-mass speed $v_{\text{cm}}$, the translational kinetic energy is defined as

$$ K_{\text{trans}} = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2. $$

This quantity is a scalar, measured in joules (J) in SI units, and is always non-negative. It represents the capacity of the body to do work by virtue of its translational motion alone. When a body simultaneously translates and rotates—as in rolling without slipping—the total mechanical kinetic energy is the sum of translational and rotational contributions: $K_{\text{total}} = K_{\text{trans}} + K_{\text{rot}}$. The rotational term $K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$ is treated fully in the section on rotational kinetic energy; here we focus exclusively on $K_{\text{trans}}$.

Worked Example. A solid bowling ball of mass $m = 5.0\ \text{kg}$ rolls without slipping along a level lane at a center-of-mass speed $v_{\text{cm}} = 4.0\ \text{m/s}$. Compute its translational kinetic energy.

$$ K_{\text{trans}} = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2 = \frac{1}{2}(5.0\ \text{kg})(4.0\ \text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(5.0)(16.0)\ \text{J} = 40\ \text{J}. $$

This result depends only on the mass and the speed of the center of mass; the ball's size, shape, and rotational state do not enter the calculation of $K_{\text{trans}}$.

Key Theorem (Work–Energy Theorem for Translation). The net work done on a body by all external forces equals the change in its translational kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K_{\text{trans}} = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2. $$

This theorem follows directly from Newton's second law applied to the center-of-mass motion and is independent of any internal forces or rotational effects.

Lab Exercise. Using a dynamics cart on a low-friction track equipped with a photogate timer, launch the cart with a known applied force over a measured distance. Record the initial and final speeds $v_i$ and $v_f$. Compute $\Delta K_{\text{trans}}$ and compare it against $W_{\text{net}} = F \cdot d$. Quantify the discrepancy as a percentage and propose two sources of systematic error.
