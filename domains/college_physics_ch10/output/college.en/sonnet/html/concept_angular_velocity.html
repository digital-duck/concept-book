🌱 Angular Velocity

🌱 Angular Velocity

Definition. Angular velocity describes the rate at which a rigid body rotates about a fixed axis. For a particle moving along a circular path, let $\theta$ denote the angular position measured in radians from a reference direction. The instantaneous angular velocity $\omega$ is defined as

$$ \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt}, $$

with SI units of radians per second ($\text{rad/s}$). Angular velocity is a vector quantity; its direction is given by the right-hand rule—curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of rotation, and the extended thumb points along $\boldsymbol{\omega}$. For rotation in the $xy$-plane, counterclockwise motion yields $\boldsymbol{\omega} = \omega\,\hat{k}$ (positive), while clockwise motion yields a negative component along $\hat{k}$.

When $\omega$ is constant, the motion is called uniform circular motion, and the angular displacement over a time interval $\Delta t$ is simply $\Delta\theta = \omega\,\Delta t$.

Worked Example. A grinding wheel starts from rest and reaches an angular speed of $120\,\text{rpm}$ in $4.0\,\text{s}$ under constant angular acceleration. Convert the final angular velocity to SI units and find the angular displacement.

Converting: $\omega_f = 120 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 4\pi \approx 12.6\,\text{rad/s}$.

Since $\omega_0 = 0$ and acceleration is constant, the average angular velocity is $\bar{\omega} = \frac{\omega_0 + \omega_f}{2} = 2\pi\,\text{rad/s}$. The total angular displacement is

$$ \Delta\theta = \bar{\omega}\,\Delta t = 2\pi \times 4.0 = 8\pi \approx 25.1\,\text{rad}, $$

corresponding to exactly four complete revolutions.

Key Theorem. The relationship between angular velocity and the linear (tangential) speed $v$ of a point at radius $r$ from the rotation axis is

$$ v = r\omega. $$

This follows directly from the arc-length definition $s = r\theta$, differentiated with respect to time. Points farther from the axis move faster, even though all points on a rigid body share the same $\omega$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a bicycle wheel on a frictionless axle and mark a point on the rim. Using a stroboscope or high-speed camera, measure the time $T$ for ten complete revolutions. Compute $\omega = 2\pi/T$ and compare it to the linear speed of the rim point measured by timing the passage of the mark across a fixed reference. Verify that $v = r\omega$ holds to within experimental uncertainty, and discuss sources of error including axle friction and parallax in angle measurement.
