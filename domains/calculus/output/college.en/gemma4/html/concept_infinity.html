🌱 Infinity

The concept of infinity ($\infty$) in mathematics cannot be treated merely as a boundless quantity, but rather must be defined rigorously within set theory and measure theory. Formally, an infinite cardinality describes the size of an endless set. The smallest such cardinality is denoted $\aleph_0$ (Aleph-null), which corresponds to the countability of the natural numbers $\mathbb{N} = \{1, 2, 3, \dots\}$. When we speak of transfinite numbers, we are discussing cardinalities—measures of potential size—rather than points on a number line. Understanding infinity requires shifting from arithmetic intuition to the formal properties of bijections between sets.

Worked Example: Equinumerosity of $\mathbb{N}$ and $\mathbb{Z}$ A classic demonstration involves comparing the natural numbers ($\mathbb{N}$) with the integers ($\mathbb{Z}$). Intuitively, $\mathbb{Z} = \{\dots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \dots\}$ appears twice as large as $\mathbb{N}$. However, in set theory, two sets are considered to have the same size (are equinumerous) if a one-to-one correspondence (a bijection) exists between their elements.
