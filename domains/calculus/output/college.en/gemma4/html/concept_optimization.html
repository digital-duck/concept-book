🍃 Optimization

Optimization is the mathematical process of finding the best possible element from some set of available alternatives. Formally, it involves maximizing or minimizing a scalar-valued function, known as the objective function $f(\mathbf{x})$, subject to one or more constraints that restrict the domain of the variables $\mathbf{x}$. If we seek to maximize $f(\mathbf{x})$ subject to an equality constraint $g(\mathbf{x}) = c$, we are looking for the point $\mathbf{x}^*$ where the objective function attains its global extremum within the feasible region defined by the constraints. Optimization theory is foundational across engineering, economics, and scientific computing, enabling solutions to resource allocation problems and system design challenges.

Consider a concrete example: maximizing the volume of an open-top rectangular box that must be constructed from $V_0$ square meters of material. Let the dimensions of the box be length $x$, width $y$, and height $z$. The objective function is the Volume, $f(x, y, z) = xyz$. The constraint is the surface area, which must equal $V_0$: $g(x, y, z) = xy + 2xz + 2yz = V_0$. To solve this, we need a method that handles the interdependence of variables imposed by the constraint.

The primary tool for solving equality-constrained optimization is the Method of Lagrange Multipliers. This technique establishes that at an extremum point $\mathbf{x}^*$, the gradient of the objective function must be proportional to the gradient of the constraint function.
