Fundamental Theorem

🌱 Function

🌱 Function

Definition. A function $f$ from a set $A$ to a set $B$, written $f: A \to B$, is a rule that assigns to each element $x \in A$ exactly one element $f(x) \in B$. The set $A$ is called the domain of $f$, and the set $B$ is called the codomain. The range (or image) of $f$ is the subset $\{f(x) \mid x \in A\} \subseteq B$. The defining criterion is well-definedness: for every $x \in A$, the value $f(x)$ is unique. A relation that assigns more than one output to a single input is not a function.

Worked Example. Consider $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f(x) = x^2 - 3x + 2$. To evaluate $f$ at $x = 4$:

$$ f(4) = (4)^2 - 3(4) + 2 = 16 - 12 + 2 = 6. $$

To find where $f$ equals zero, solve $x^2 - 3x + 2 = 0$. Factoring gives $(x-1)(x-2) = 0$, so $x = 1$ or $x = 2$. These are the zeros of $f$, corresponding to the $x$-intercepts of its graph. Note that the domain is all of $\mathbb{R}$ and the range is $\left[-\tfrac{1}{4}, \infty\right)$, obtained by completing the square: $f(x) = \left(x - \tfrac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \tfrac{1}{4}$.

Key Theorem (Vertical Line Test). A curve in the $xy$-plane is the graph of a function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ if and only if every vertical line $x = c$ intersects the curve in at most one point.

Proof sketch. If $x = c$ intersects the curve at two distinct points $(c, y_1)$ and $(c, y_2)$ with $y_1 \neq y_2$, then $f(c) = y_1$ and $f(c) = y_2$ simultaneously, violating uniqueness of assignment. Conversely, if each vertical line meets the curve at most once, the assignment $x \mapsto f(x)$ is unambiguous wherever defined. $\square$

🌱 Limit

🌱 Limit

Definition. Let $f$ be a function defined on an open interval containing $c$, except possibly at $c$ itself. We say the limit of $f$ as $x$ approaches $c$ equals $L$, written

$$ \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L, $$

if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that

$$ 0 < |x - c| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon. $$

This $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ formulation captures the intuition that $f(x)$ can be made arbitrarily close to $L$ by restricting $x$ sufficiently close to $c$, independent of any value $f$ may or may not take at $c$ itself.

Worked Example. Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$.

The expression is indeterminate at $x = 3$ because both numerator and denominator vanish there. Factor the numerator:

$$ \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x + 3, \quad x \neq 3. $$

Since the limit concerns values of $x$ near 3 but not equal to 3, cancellation is valid. Therefore

$$ \lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = \lim_{x \to 3}(x + 3) = 6. $$

To verify rigorously: given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $\delta = \varepsilon$. Then $0 < |x - 3| < \delta$ implies $|(x+3) - 6| = |x - 3| < \varepsilon$. $\square$

Key Theorem (Limit Laws). If $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L$ and $\lim_{x \to c} g(x) = M$, then

$$ \lim_{x \to c}[f(x) + g(x)] = L + M, \qquad \lim_{x \to c}[f(x)\,g(x)] = LM, $$

and, provided $M \neq 0$,

$$ \lim_{x \to c}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M}. $$

These laws follow directly from the $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ definition via standard triangle-inequality arguments and justify the algebraic manipulation of limits in computation.

🌱 Infinity

🌱 Infinity

Definition. In calculus, infinity is not a real number but a limiting concept describing unbounded growth. We say $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty$ and call $x = a$ a vertical asymptote of $f$ if: for every $M > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that whenever $0 < |x - a| < \delta$, we have $f(x) > M$. This epsilon-delta formulation replaces the informal phrase "grows without bound" with a precise, falsifiable condition: no matter how large the threshold $M$, one can always find a punctured neighbourhood of $a$ on which $f$ exceeds it.

Worked Example. Consider $f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x^2}$. We claim $\lim_{x \to 0} f(x) = \infty$. Given $M > 0$, choose $\delta = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$. Then $0 < |x| < \delta$ implies $x^2 < \dfrac{1}{M}$, hence $\dfrac{1}{x^2} > M$. Because $M$ was arbitrary, the definition is satisfied. Geometrically, the graph of $f$ rises steeply on both sides of the $y$-axis, approaching the vertical asymptote $x = 0$ without ever reaching it.

Key Theorem (Reciprocal Law for Infinite Limits). Suppose $f(x) > 0$ for all $x$ in a punctured neighbourhood of $a$. Then

$$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty \quad\text{if and only if}\quad \lim_{x \to a} \frac{1}{f(x)} = 0. $$

Proof sketch. $(\Rightarrow)$ Given $\varepsilon > 0$, set $M = 1/\varepsilon$. By hypothesis there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $0 < |x - a| < \delta$ implies $f(x) > 1/\varepsilon$. Since $f(x) > 0$, this gives $0 < 1/f(x) < \varepsilon$, i.e., $|1/f(x) - 0| < \varepsilon$. The converse $(\Leftarrow)$ follows by exchanging the roles of $\varepsilon$ and $M$. $\square$

The theorem is useful in practice: once an infinite limit is established by the definition, the reciprocal limit follows without another epsilon-delta argument.

🌱 Real Number

🌱 Real Number

Definition. The real numbers, denoted $\mathbb{R}$, form a complete ordered field. Formally, $\mathbb{R}$ is a set equipped with two binary operations, addition $+$ and multiplication $\cdot$, satisfying the field axioms (commutativity, associativity, distributivity, identity elements $0$ and $1$, and inverses), together with a total order $\leq$ compatible with both operations, and the completeness axiom: every non-empty subset of $\mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above possesses a least upper bound (supremum) in $\mathbb{R}$. It is this completeness property that distinguishes $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$ and makes calculus possible.

Worked Example. Consider the set $S = \{x \in \mathbb{Q} : x^2 < 2\}$. Within $\mathbb{Q}$, $S$ is bounded above (e.g., by $2$) but has no least upper bound in $\mathbb{Q}$, because $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$. In $\mathbb{R}$, the completeness axiom guarantees $\sup S = \sqrt{2} \in \mathbb{R}$. To verify: for any $\varepsilon > 0$, the element $\sqrt{2} - \varepsilon$ is not an upper bound of $S$ (one can find a rational $q$ with $\sqrt{2} - \varepsilon < q < \sqrt{2}$, so $q^2 < 2$), confirming $\sqrt{2}$ is the least such bound.

Key Theorem (Archimedean Property). For every $x \in \mathbb{R}$ there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $n > x$.

Proof sketch. Suppose for contradiction that $\mathbb{N}$ is bounded above in $\mathbb{R}$. By completeness, $M = \sup \mathbb{N}$ exists. Then $M - 1$ is not an upper bound, so there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n > M - 1$, giving $n + 1 > M$. But $n + 1 \in \mathbb{N}$, contradicting the assumption that $M$ is an upper bound. $\square$

The Archimedean property implies that $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$: between any two real numbers lies a rational number.

🍃 Derivative

🍃 Derivative

Definition. Let $f : (a, b) \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function and let $x_0 \in (a, b)$. The derivative of $f$ at $x_0$, denoted $f'(x_0)$, is defined as the limit

$$ f'(x_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h}, $$

provided this limit exists and is finite. When $f'(x_0)$ exists, $f$ is said to be differentiable at $x_0$. Geometrically, $f'(x_0)$ represents the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at the point $(x_0, f(x_0))$.

Worked Example. Compute $f'(x)$ for $f(x) = x^2$.

Applying the definition directly:

$$ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h}. $$

Factoring $h$ from the numerator and cancelling (valid for $h \neq 0$):

$$ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h) = 2x. $$

Hence $f'(x) = 2x$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$.

Key Theorem (Differentiability Implies Continuity). If $f$ is differentiable at $x_0$, then $f$ is continuous at $x_0$.

Proof sketch. Write

$$ f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0) = \frac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h} \cdot h. $$

As $h \to 0$, the first factor converges to $f'(x_0)$ and the second factor converges to $0$. By the product rule for limits, the left-hand side converges to $f'(x_0) \cdot 0 = 0$, which gives $\lim_{h \to 0} f(x_0 + h) = f(x_0)$, establishing continuity. $\square$

Note that the converse fails: the function $f(x) = |x|$ is continuous at $x = 0$ but not differentiable there, since the one-sided limits $\lim_{h \to 0^+} h/h = 1$ and $\lim_{h \to 0^-} (-h)/h = -1$ disagree.

🍃 Riemann Sum

🍃 Riemann Sum

Definition. Let $f$ be a bounded function on a closed interval $[a, b]$. A partition of $[a, b]$ is a finite set $P = \{x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ satisfying $a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = b$. For each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, let $\Delta x_i = x_i - x_{i-1}$ denote its length and $c_i \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$ a chosen sample point. The Riemann sum of $f$ with respect to $P$ and sample points $\{c_i\}$ is

$$ S(f, P, \{c_i\}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(c_i)\,\Delta x_i. $$

The quantity $\|P\| = \max_{1 \le i \le n} \Delta x_i$ is called the mesh (or norm) of the partition. When all subintervals share equal length, $\Delta x_i = (b-a)/n$ for every $i$, the partition is called uniform.

Worked Example. Approximate $\int_0^1 x^2\,dx$ using a uniform partition with $n = 4$ and right endpoints as sample points. Here $\Delta x = 1/4$ and $c_i = i/4$ for $i = 1, 2, 3, 4$.

$$ S = \sum_{i=1}^{4} \left(\frac{i}{4}\right)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{4}\left(\frac{1}{16} + \frac{4}{16} + \frac{9}{16} + \frac{16}{16}\right) = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{30}{16} = \frac{30}{64} = \frac{15}{32} \approx 0.469. $$

The exact value is $\int_0^1 x^2\,dx = 1/3 \approx 0.333$; the error reflects the coarseness of the partition.

Key Theorem (Riemann Integrability). If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for any partition $P$ with $\|P\| < \delta$ and any choice of sample points,

$$ \left|S(f, P, \{c_i\}) - \int_a^b f(x)\,dx\right| < \varepsilon. $$

In particular, $\lim_{\|P\| \to 0} S(f, P, \{c_i\}) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$, independent of how sample points are chosen. Continuity guarantees that the oscillation of $f$ on each subinterval can be made uniformly small.

🍃 Antiderivative

🍃 Antiderivative

Definition. Let $f$ be a function defined on an interval $I \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. A function $F : I \to \mathbb{R}$ is called an antiderivative of $f$ on $I$ if $F$ is differentiable on $I$ and

$$ F'(x) = f(x) \quad \text{for all } x \in I. $$

Because the derivative of any constant is zero, if $F$ is one antiderivative of $f$, then $F(x) + C$ is also an antiderivative for every $C \in \mathbb{R}$. Conversely, if $F$ and $G$ are both antiderivatives of $f$ on $I$, then $(F - G)' = 0$ on $I$, so the Mean Value Theorem forces $F(x) - G(x) = C$ for some fixed constant. Hence the general antiderivative of $f$ is the family $F(x) + C$, written using indefinite integral notation as

$$ \int f(x)\, dx = F(x) + C. $$

Worked Example. Find the general antiderivative of $f(x) = 3x^2 - 4x + 5$.

Apply the Power Rule for antidifferentiation, $\int x^n\, dx = \dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$ for $n \neq -1$, term by term:

$$ \int (3x^2 - 4x + 5)\, dx = 3 \cdot \frac{x^3}{3} - 4 \cdot \frac{x^2}{2} + 5x + C = x^3 - 2x^2 + 5x + C. $$

Verification: Differentiating yields $(x^3 - 2x^2 + 5x + C)' = 3x^2 - 4x + 5 = f(x)$. $\checkmark$

Key Theorem (Existence of Antiderivatives). If $f$ is continuous on a closed interval $[a, b]$, then $f$ possesses an antiderivative on $[a, b]$. Specifically, the function

$$ F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt $$

is differentiable on $(a, b)$ and satisfies $F'(x) = f(x)$.

This is the first part of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. The proof proceeds by forming the difference quotient $\frac{F(x+h)-F(x)}{h} = \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\, dt$ and applying the Mean Value Theorem for Integrals to show the limit equals $f(x)$ as $h \to 0$.

🍃 Definite Integral

🍃 Definite Integral

Definition. Let $f$ be a bounded function on a closed interval $[a, b]$. Partition $[a, b]$ into $n$ subintervals by choosing points $a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = b$, and let $\Delta x_i = x_i - x_{i-1}$. For each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, choose a sample point $c_i \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$. The corresponding Riemann sum is

$$ S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(c_i)\,\Delta x_i. $$

If $S_n$ converges to a unique finite limit as $\|\mathcal{P}\| = \max_i \Delta x_i \to 0$, independently of the choice of partition and sample points, that limit is called the definite integral of $f$ over $[a, b]$:

$$ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{\|\mathcal{P}\|\to 0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(c_i)\,\Delta x_i. $$

Every continuous function on a closed bounded interval is integrable in this sense.

Worked Example. Compute $\displaystyle\int_0^2 x^2\,dx$ using a uniform partition with right endpoints. With $n$ equal subintervals, $\Delta x = 2/n$ and $c_i = 2i/n$, so

$$ S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(\frac{2i}{n}\right)^2 \cdot \frac{2}{n} = \frac{8}{n^3}\sum_{i=1}^{n} i^2 = \frac{8}{n^3}\cdot\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}. $$

Taking $n \to \infty$:

$$ \int_0^2 x^2\,dx = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{8}{6}\cdot\frac{(n+1)(2n+1)}{n^2} = \frac{8}{6}\cdot 2 = \frac{8}{3}. $$

Key Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 2). If $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$—that is, $F'(x) = f(x)$—then

$$ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a). $$

This theorem bridges the antidifferentiation problem with the limit of Riemann sums, reducing numerical accumulation to algebraic evaluation. For the example above, $F(x) = x^3/3$ gives $F(2) - F(0) = 8/3$, confirming the direct computation.

🍃 Fundamental Theorem

🍃 Fundamental Theorem

Definition. Let $f$ be a continuous function on the closed interval $[a, b]$. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus comprises two complementary parts that together establish the inverse relationship between differentiation and integration.

Part I (Differentiation of an Integral). Define the accumulation function $F : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ by

$$ F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt. $$

Then $F$ is differentiable on $(a, b)$, and $F'(x) = f(x)$ for every $x \in (a, b)$.

Part II (Evaluation Theorem). If $G$ is any antiderivative of $f$ on $[a, b]$—that is, $G' = f$—then

$$ \int_a^b f(x)\, dx = G(b) - G(a). $$

Worked Example. Compute $\displaystyle\int_1^4 \sqrt{x}\, dx$.

We seek an antiderivative of $f(x) = x^{1/2}$. Using the power rule,

$$ G(x) = \frac{x^{3/2}}{3/2} = \frac{2}{3} x^{3/2}. $$

By Part II,

$$ \int_1^4 \sqrt{x}\, dx = G(4) - G(1) = \frac{2}{3}(4)^{3/2} - \frac{2}{3}(1)^{3/2} = \frac{2}{3}(8) - \frac{2}{3}(1) = \frac{16}{3} - \frac{2}{3} = \frac{14}{3}. $$

Key Theorem (Proof Sketch). For Part I, fix $x \in (a, b)$ and form the difference quotient

$$ \frac{F(x+h) - F(x)}{h} = \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\, dt. $$

By continuity of $f$, the Mean Value Theorem for integrals guarantees a point $c_h$ between $x$ and $x+h$ such that the right side equals $f(c_h)$. As $h \to 0$, we have $c_h \to x$, so $f(c_h) \to f(x)$, yielding $F'(x) = f(x)$. Part II follows by noting that $F$ and $G$ are both antiderivatives of $f$, so $F - G$ is constant on $[a, b]$; evaluating at the endpoints gives $G(b) - G(a) = F(b) - F(a) = \int_a^b f(t)\, dt$.

  2. Evaluate $F(\pi/2)$ explicitly using Part II.

Payoff 🎯

Definition. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus consists of two complementary parts. Let $f$ be continuous on $[a, b]$.

  • Part I. Define $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$. Then $F$ is differentiable on $(a,b)$ and $F'(x) = f(x)$.
  • Part II. If $G$ is any antiderivative of $f$, then $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = G(b) - G(a)$.

Together these two parts establish that differentiation and integration are inverse operations — the central unifying insight of single-variable calculus.

Worked Example. Compute $\int_1^4 \sqrt{x}\,dx$.

We seek an antiderivative of $f(x) = x^{1/2}$. By the power rule, $G(x) = \tfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}$. Part II then gives

$$ \int_1^4 \sqrt{x}\,dx = G(4) - G(1) = \frac{2}{3}(4)^{3/2} - \frac{2}{3}(1)^{3/2} = \frac{2}{3}(8) - \frac{2}{3}(1) = \frac{14}{3}. $$

No Riemann sums, no limit of partitions — a closed-form evaluation in three lines.

Key Theorem (Proof Sketch). Part I follows from the mean value theorem for integrals: for small $h > 0$,

$$ \frac{F(x+h) - F(x)}{h} = \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt. $$

Because $f$ is continuous, it is approximately constant on $[x, x+h]$, so the right side approaches $f(x)$ as $h \to 0^+$. Part II is derived by applying Part I to recognize that any two antiderivatives of $f$ differ by a constant, then evaluating the boundary difference.

This theorem is the natural endpoint of the course because it resolves the apparent paradox introduced in Chapter 1: the derivative measures instantaneous rate of change, while the integral accumulates totals — two ideas defined through entirely different limiting processes. The Fundamental Theorem reveals them to be two faces of a single structure.
