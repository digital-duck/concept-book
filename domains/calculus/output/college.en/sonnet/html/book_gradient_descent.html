🌱 Function

🌱 Function

Definition. A function $f$ from a set $A$ to a set $B$, written $f: A \to B$, is a rule that assigns to each element $x \in A$ exactly one element $f(x) \in B$. The set $A$ is called the domain of $f$, and the set $B$ is called the codomain. The range (or image) of $f$ is the subset $\{f(x) \mid x \in A\} \subseteq B$. The defining criterion is well-definedness: for every $x \in A$, the value $f(x)$ is unique. A relation that assigns more than one output to a single input is not a function.

Worked Example. Consider $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f(x) = x^2 - 3x + 2$. To evaluate $f$ at $x = 4$:

$$ f(4) = (4)^2 - 3(4) + 2 = 16 - 12 + 2 = 6. $$

To find where $f$ equals zero, solve $x^2 - 3x + 2 = 0$. Factoring gives $(x-1)(x-2) = 0$, so $x = 1$ or $x = 2$. These are the zeros of $f$, corresponding to the $x$-intercepts of its graph. Note that the domain is all of $\mathbb{R}$ and the range is $\left[-\tfrac{1}{4}, \infty\right)$, obtained by completing the square: $f(x) = \left(x - \tfrac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \tfrac{1}{4}$.

Key Theorem (Vertical Line Test). A curve in the $xy$-plane is the graph of a function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ if and only if every vertical line $x = c$ intersects the curve in at most one point.

Proof sketch. If $x = c$ intersects the curve at two distinct points $(c, y_1)$ and $(c, y_2)$ with $y_1 \neq y_2$, then $f(c) = y_1$ and $f(c) = y_2$ simultaneously, violating uniqueness of assignment. Conversely, if each vertical line meets the curve at most once, the assignment $x \mapsto f(x)$ is unambiguous wherever defined. $\square$

🌱 Limit

🌱 Limit

Definition. Let $f$ be a function defined on an open interval containing $c$, except possibly at $c$ itself. We say the limit of $f$ as $x$ approaches $c$ equals $L$, written

$$ \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L, $$

if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that

$$ 0 < |x - c| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon. $$

This $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ formulation captures the intuition that $f(x)$ can be made arbitrarily close to $L$ by restricting $x$ sufficiently close to $c$, independent of any value $f$ may or may not take at $c$ itself.

Worked Example. Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$.

The expression is indeterminate at $x = 3$ because both numerator and denominator vanish there. Factor the numerator:

$$ \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x + 3, \quad x \neq 3. $$

Since the limit concerns values of $x$ near 3 but not equal to 3, cancellation is valid. Therefore

$$ \lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = \lim_{x \to 3}(x + 3) = 6. $$

To verify rigorously: given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $\delta = \varepsilon$. Then $0 < |x - 3| < \delta$ implies $|(x+3) - 6| = |x - 3| < \varepsilon$. $\square$

Key Theorem (Limit Laws). If $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L$ and $\lim_{x \to c} g(x) = M$, then

$$ \lim_{x \to c}[f(x) + g(x)] = L + M, \qquad \lim_{x \to c}[f(x)\,g(x)] = LM, $$

and, provided $M \neq 0$,

$$ \lim_{x \to c}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M}. $$

These laws follow directly from the $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ definition via standard triangle-inequality arguments and justify the algebraic manipulation of limits in computation.

🍃 Derivative

🍃 Derivative

Definition. Let $f : (a, b) \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function and let $x_0 \in (a, b)$. The derivative of $f$ at $x_0$, denoted $f'(x_0)$, is defined as the limit

$$ f'(x_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h}, $$

provided this limit exists and is finite. When $f'(x_0)$ exists, $f$ is said to be differentiable at $x_0$. Geometrically, $f'(x_0)$ represents the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at the point $(x_0, f(x_0))$.

Worked Example. Compute $f'(x)$ for $f(x) = x^2$.

Applying the definition directly:

$$ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h}. $$

Factoring $h$ from the numerator and cancelling (valid for $h \neq 0$):

$$ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h) = 2x. $$

Hence $f'(x) = 2x$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$.

Key Theorem (Differentiability Implies Continuity). If $f$ is differentiable at $x_0$, then $f$ is continuous at $x_0$.

Proof sketch. Write

$$ f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0) = \frac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h} \cdot h. $$

As $h \to 0$, the first factor converges to $f'(x_0)$ and the second factor converges to $0$. By the product rule for limits, the left-hand side converges to $f'(x_0) \cdot 0 = 0$, which gives $\lim_{h \to 0} f(x_0 + h) = f(x_0)$, establishing continuity. $\square$

Note that the converse fails: the function $f(x) = |x|$ is continuous at $x = 0$ but not differentiable there, since the one-sided limits $\lim_{h \to 0^+} h/h = 1$ and $\lim_{h \to 0^-} (-h)/h = -1$ disagree.

🍃 Continuity

🍃 Continuity

Definition. Let $f : D \subseteq \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ and let $c \in D$. The function $f$ is continuous at $c$ if

$$ \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c). $$

Unpacking this single equation requires three simultaneous conditions: (i) $f(c)$ is defined, (ii) $\lim_{x \to c} f(x)$ exists, and (iii) the limit equals the function value. In the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ language, $f$ is continuous at $c$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|x - c| < \delta$ implies $|f(x) - f(c)| < \varepsilon$. A function is continuous on an interval $I$ if it is continuous at every point of $I$.

Worked Example. Determine whether

$$ f(x) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} & x \neq 2 \\ 5 & x = 2 \end{cases} $$

is continuous at $c = 2$. First, $f(2) = 5$. Second, for $x \neq 2$ we factor: $\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x + 2$, so $\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 4$. Since $\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 4 \neq 5 = f(2)$, condition (iii) fails. Therefore $f$ has a removable discontinuity at $x = 2$; redefining $f(2) = 4$ restores continuity.

Key Theorem (Intermediate Value Theorem). If $f$ is continuous on the closed interval $[a, b]$ and $N$ is any value strictly between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ such that $f(c) = N$.

Proof sketch. Define $S = \{x \in [a,b] : f(x) \leq N\}$. By assumption $S$ is nonempty and bounded above by $b$, so $c = \sup S$ exists. The continuity of $f$ forces $f(c) = N$; any value above or below $N$ leads to a neighborhood contradicting the supremum definition. $\square$

The IVT guarantees existence but not uniqueness, and it underpins root-finding algorithms such as the bisection method.

🍃 Partial Derivative

🍃 Partial Derivative

Definition. Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a function of $n$ variables $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$. The partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x_i$ at the point $\mathbf{a} = (a_1, \ldots, a_n)$ is defined as the limit

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a_1, \ldots, a_i + h, \ldots, a_n) - f(a_1, \ldots, a_n)}{h}, $$

provided the limit exists. Geometrically, $\partial f / \partial x_i$ measures the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ when $x_i$ alone varies, while all other variables are held fixed.

Worked Example. Let $f(x, y) = x^3 y + \sin(xy)$. To compute $\partial f / \partial x$, treat $y$ as a constant and differentiate with respect to $x$:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 3x^2 y + y\cos(xy). $$

Similarly, treating $x$ as a constant and differentiating with respect to $y$:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^3 + x\cos(xy). $$

Evaluating both at the point $(1, \pi)$ gives

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(1, \pi) = 3\pi + \pi\cos(\pi) = 3\pi - \pi = 2\pi, $$ $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(1, \pi) = 1 + \cos(\pi) = 0. $$

Theorem (Clairaut–Schwarz Symmetry). If $f : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ has continuous second-order partial derivatives on an open set $U$, then the mixed partials are equal:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \,\partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \,\partial x}. $$

Proof sketch. Both mixed partials can be expressed as a limit of a symmetric difference quotient over a rectangle; continuity of the second partials guarantees the two limiting processes commute. $\square$

This symmetry, which fails without the continuity hypothesis, underpins the equality of mixed partials in nearly every function encountered in applied mathematics.

🍃 Gradient

🍃 Gradient

Definition. Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a differentiable scalar field. The gradient of $f$ at a point $\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the vector of all first-order partial derivatives:

$$ \nabla f(\mathbf{p}) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(\mathbf{p}),\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(\mathbf{p}),\; \ldots,\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}(\mathbf{p})\right). $$

The gradient encodes both the magnitude and direction of the steepest rate of increase of $f$ at $\mathbf{p}$. It resides in the same space as the domain, $\nabla f(\mathbf{p}) \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and is itself a vector field when evaluated across all points.

Worked Example. Consider $f(x, y) = x^2 + 3xy - y^2$. Computing each partial derivative:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x + 3y, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 3x - 2y. $$

Thus the gradient field is $\nabla f(x,y) = (2x + 3y,\; 3x - 2y)$. At the specific point $\mathbf{p} = (1, -1)$:

$$ \nabla f(1,-1) = \bigl(2(1)+3(-1),\; 3(1)-2(-1)\bigr) = (-1,\; 5). $$

This vector indicates that moving from $(1,-1)$ in the direction $(-1, 5)/\sqrt{26}$ produces the largest instantaneous rate of increase of $f$, approximately $\|\nabla f(1,-1)\| = \sqrt{26} \approx 5.10$ units of $f$ per unit of distance.

Key Theorem (Gradient and Directional Derivative). For any unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{u}} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the directional derivative of $f$ at $\mathbf{p}$ satisfies:

$$ D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f(\mathbf{p}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{p}) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}. $$

Proof sketch. By the chain rule applied to $g(t) = f(\mathbf{p} + t\hat{\mathbf{u}})$, we obtain $g'(0) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{p})\,\hat{u}_i$, which is precisely the dot product $\nabla f(\mathbf{p}) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$. Maximizing over all unit vectors via Cauchy–Schwarz confirms the maximum is $\|\nabla f(\mathbf{p})\|$, attained when $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \nabla f(\mathbf{p})/\|\nabla f(\mathbf{p})\|$. $\square$

🍃 Optimization

🍃 Optimization

Definition. Optimization is the process of finding the value(s) of an independent variable at which a given function attains its maximum or minimum value, either over an entire domain (global extremum) or within an open neighborhood (local extremum). Formally, a function $f : I \to \mathbb{R}$ defined on an interval $I$ has a local maximum at $c \in I$ if there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $f(c) \geq f(x)$ for all $x \in (c - \delta, c + \delta) \cap I$; a global maximum requires $f(c) \geq f(x)$ for every $x \in I$. Local minima are defined analogously with the inequality reversed. Points at which $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ does not exist are called critical points and are the primary candidates for extrema on open intervals.

Worked Example. Maximize the area of a rectangle inscribed in the ellipse $\dfrac{x^2}{9} + \dfrac{y^2}{4} = 1$, with sides parallel to the coordinate axes.

A vertex in the first quadrant has coordinates $(x, y)$ with $y = \frac{2}{3}\sqrt{9 - x^2}$, so the area is

$$ A(x) = 4xy = \frac{8x}{3}\sqrt{9 - x^2}, \quad x \in (0, 3). $$

Setting $A'(x) = 0$:

$$ A'(x) = \frac{8}{3} \cdot \frac{9 - 2x^2}{\sqrt{9 - x^2}} = 0 \implies x = \frac{3}{\sqrt{2}}. $$

The corresponding $y = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} = \sqrt{2}$, giving maximum area $A = 4 \cdot \frac{3}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \sqrt{2} = 12$.

Key Theorem (Extreme Value Theorem). If $f$ is continuous on a closed bounded interval $[a, b]$, then $f$ attains both a global maximum and a global minimum on $[a, b]$. Consequently, these extrema occur either at interior critical points or at the endpoints $a$ and $b$. To locate global extrema, one evaluates $f$ at all critical points in $(a, b)$ and compares with $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, selecting the largest and smallest values.

🌸 Gradient Descent

🌸 Gradient Descent

Definition. Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a differentiable function. The gradient of $f$ at a point $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the vector of partial derivatives

$$ \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right)^\top. $$

The direction $-\nabla f(\mathbf{x})$ is the direction of steepest descent of $f$ at $\mathbf{x}$. Gradient descent is the iterative scheme

$$ \mathbf{x}^{(k+1)} = \mathbf{x}^{(k)} - \alpha \, \nabla f\!\left(\mathbf{x}^{(k)}\right), \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, $$

where $\alpha > 0$ is the learning rate (step size) and $\mathbf{x}^{(0)}$ is an initial guess. The sequence $\{\mathbf{x}^{(k)}\}$ is intended to converge to a local minimum of $f$.

Worked Example. Minimize $f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 7$ using gradient descent with $\alpha = 0.3$ and $x^{(0)} = 0$.

Compute $f'(x) = 2x - 4$. The update rule becomes $x^{(k+1)} = x^{(k)} - 0.3\,(2x^{(k)} - 4)$.

$k$ $x^{(k)}$ $f'(x^{(k)})$ $x^{(k+1)}$
0 0.000 $-4.000$ 1.200
1 1.200 $-1.600$ 1.680
2 1.680 $-0.640$ 1.872
3 1.872 $-0.256$ 1.949

The sequence converges to $x^* = 2$, the exact minimizer of $f$, since $f'(2) = 0$ and $f''(2) = 2 > 0$.

Key Theorem (Convergence for $L$-Smooth Functions). Suppose $f$ is convex and its gradient is $L$-Lipschitz continuous, meaning $\|\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) - \nabla f(\mathbf{y})\| \leq L\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\|$ for all $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$. If $0 < \alpha \leq \tfrac{1}{L}$, then gradient descent satisfies

$$ f\!\left(\mathbf{x}^{(k)}\right) - f(\mathbf{x}^*) \leq \frac{\|\mathbf{x}^{(0)} - \mathbf{x}^*\|^2}{2\alpha k}, $$

guaranteeing convergence at rate $O(1/k)$ to the global minimum $f(\mathbf{x}^*)$.

Payoff 🎯

What Gradient Descent Achieves

Every major thread woven through this course — limits, derivatives, the chain rule, multivariable partial derivatives — converges at a single algorithmic idea: gradient descent. The method answers a deceptively simple question: given a differentiable loss function $\mathcal{L} : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, how does one find a parameter vector $\mathbf{w}^*$ that minimizes it?

Definition. Let $\mathcal{L}$ be continuously differentiable. The gradient descent iteration is

$$ \mathbf{w}_{k+1} = \mathbf{w}_k - \eta \, \nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}_k), \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots $$

where $\eta > 0$ is the learning rate and $\nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \mathcal{L} = \bigl(\tfrac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_1}, \ldots, \tfrac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_n}\bigr)^\top$ is the gradient vector. The negative gradient points in the direction of steepest local decrease, so each step moves $\mathbf{w}$ downhill on the loss surface.

Worked Example. Consider $\mathcal{L}(w) = (w - 3)^2$. Then $\nabla \mathcal{L} = 2(w - 3)$. Starting at $w_0 = 0$ with $\eta = 0.1$:

$$ w_1 = 0 - 0.1 \cdot 2(0-3) = 0.6, \quad w_2 = 0.6 - 0.1 \cdot 2(0.6 - 3) = 1.08, \quad \ldots $$

The sequence converges geometrically to $w^* = 3$, the true minimizer.

Key Theorem (Convergence for $L$-smooth functions). If $\mathcal{L}$ is convex and $\nabla \mathcal{L}$ is $L$-Lipschitz continuous, then gradient descent with $\eta = 1/L$ satisfies

$$ \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}_k) - \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}^*) \leq \frac{\|\mathbf{w}_0 - \mathbf{w}^*\|^2}{2\eta k}. $$

This $\mathcal{O}(1/k)$ rate guarantees that the method is not merely heuristic — it is a provably efficient optimizer for a wide and practically important class of objectives.

Why This Is the Natural Endpoint

The calculus you have built is precisely the machinery gradient descent consumes. Limits define the derivative; the chain rule propagates gradients through compositions of functions; partial derivatives extend the idea to high-dimensional parameter spaces. Gradient descent is the application for which that entire apparatus was designed.
