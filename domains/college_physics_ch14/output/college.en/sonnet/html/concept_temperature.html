🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar intensive property that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. Two systems are said to be in thermal equilibrium when they share the same temperature and no net energy transfer occurs between them upon contact. This observation is formalized by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, and system $B$ is also in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then $A$ and $B$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. The Zeroth Law establishes temperature as a well-defined, transitive property and underpins the logical foundation of thermometry.

Temperature is measured on several scales. The Celsius scale sets $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the ice point and $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the steam point of water at standard pressure. The Kelvin (absolute) scale shifts the origin to absolute zero, the theoretical minimum temperature at which molecular motion ceases:

$$ T(\text{K}) = T(^\circ\text{C}) + 273.15. $$

The Fahrenheit scale, common in everyday U.S. usage, relates to Celsius via $T_F = \frac{9}{5}T_C + 32$.

Worked Example. A mercury thermometer reads $98.6\,^\circ\text{F}$, the normal human body temperature. Convert to Celsius and Kelvin.

Step 1 — Fahrenheit to Celsius:

$$ T_C = \frac{5}{9}(T_F - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(98.6 - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(66.6) = 37.0\,^\circ\text{C}. $$

Step 2 — Celsius to Kelvin:

$$ T_K = 37.0 + 273.15 = 310.15\,\text{K} \approx 310\,\text{K}. $$

Key Theorem. From kinetic theory, the average translational kinetic energy of a monatomic ideal-gas molecule is directly proportional to the absolute temperature:

$$ \langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2}k_B T, $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\,\text{J/K}$ is the Boltzmann constant. This result bridges the macroscopic observable $T$ and the microscopic world, confirming that absolute zero ($T = 0\,\text{K}$) corresponds to complete cessation of thermal motion.

Lab Exercise. Immerse three identical thermometers in (a) an ice–water mixture, (b) boiling water at sea level, and (c) a room-temperature water bath. Record the readings, convert each to Kelvin, and verify that the ice-point and steam-point values match $273.15\,\text{K}$ and $373.15\,\text{K}$, respectively. Discuss sources of systematic error such as thermometer lag and impurity effects on the boiling point.
