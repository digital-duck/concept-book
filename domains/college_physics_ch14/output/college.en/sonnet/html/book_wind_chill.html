🌱 Temperature

🌱 Temperature

Definition. Temperature is a scalar intensive property that quantifies the average translational kinetic energy of the microscopic constituents of a system. Two systems are said to be in thermal equilibrium when they share the same temperature and no net energy transfer occurs between them upon contact. This observation is formalized by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, and system $B$ is also in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then $A$ and $B$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other. The Zeroth Law establishes temperature as a well-defined, transitive property and underpins the logical foundation of thermometry.

Temperature is measured on several scales. The Celsius scale sets $0\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the ice point and $100\,^\circ\text{C}$ at the steam point of water at standard pressure. The Kelvin (absolute) scale shifts the origin to absolute zero, the theoretical minimum temperature at which molecular motion ceases:

$$ T(\text{K}) = T(^\circ\text{C}) + 273.15. $$

The Fahrenheit scale, common in everyday U.S. usage, relates to Celsius via $T_F = \frac{9}{5}T_C + 32$.

Worked Example. A mercury thermometer reads $98.6\,^\circ\text{F}$, the normal human body temperature. Convert to Celsius and Kelvin.

Step 1 — Fahrenheit to Celsius:

$$ T_C = \frac{5}{9}(T_F - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(98.6 - 32) = \frac{5}{9}(66.6) = 37.0\,^\circ\text{C}. $$

Step 2 — Celsius to Kelvin:

$$ T_K = 37.0 + 273.15 = 310.15\,\text{K} \approx 310\,\text{K}. $$

Key Theorem. From kinetic theory, the average translational kinetic energy of a monatomic ideal-gas molecule is directly proportional to the absolute temperature:

$$ \langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2}k_B T, $$

where $k_B = 1.381 \times 10^{-23}\,\text{J/K}$ is the Boltzmann constant. This result bridges the macroscopic observable $T$ and the microscopic world, confirming that absolute zero ($T = 0\,\text{K}$) corresponds to complete cessation of thermal motion.

🍃 Heat

🍃 Heat

Definition. Heat is the energy transferred between a system and its surroundings due solely to a temperature difference. It is not a property possessed by a body but rather a process quantity—energy in transit. Formally, if a system undergoes a quasi-static process, the infinitesimal heat transfer is denoted $\delta Q$, with the sign convention that $Q > 0$ when energy flows into the system and $Q < 0$ when energy flows out. Heat is measured in joules (J) in SI, though the calorie (1 cal = 4.186 J) remains common in thermochemical contexts. Unlike internal energy $U$, heat has no meaning independent of a process; one cannot speak of "the heat contained in a body." The rate at which heat is transferred depends on the temperature difference, the material properties, and the geometry of the system—but the quantity $Q$ itself refers only to the total energy exchanged during a process.

Worked Example. A copper block of mass $m = 0.500\ \text{kg}$ is heated from $T_i = 20.0^\circ\text{C}$ to $T_f = 80.0^\circ\text{C}$. The specific heat capacity of copper is $c = 385\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$. The heat absorbed is

$$ Q = mc\Delta T = (0.500\ \text{kg})(385\ \text{J/(kg·K)})(80.0 - 20.0\ \text{K}) = 1.16 \times 10^4\ \text{J}. $$

Since $Q > 0$, energy flows into the copper, consistent with the temperature rise. Note that $\Delta T$ in kelvins equals $\Delta T$ in degrees Celsius for a difference, so no unit conversion is required beyond the subtraction.

Key Theorem — First Law Connection. The First Law of Thermodynamics relates heat to work and internal energy:

$$ \Delta U = Q - W, $$

where $W$ is the work done by the system on its surroundings. This equation codifies energy conservation: the change in internal energy equals the heat added minus the work extracted. For a constant-volume process ($W = 0$), all heat transferred goes directly into raising the internal energy, giving $\Delta U = Q_V = nC_V\Delta T$ for an ideal gas, where $C_V$ is the molar heat capacity at constant volume.

Lab Exercise. Fill a calorimeter with $200\ \text{g}$ of water at $25^\circ\text{C}$ and submerge a pre-heated $50\ \text{g}$ aluminum slug ($c_{Al} = 900\ \text{J/(kg·K)}$) initially at $95^\circ\text{C}$. Record the equilibrium temperature $T_{eq}$, then calculate $Q_{Al} = -Q_{water}$ to verify energy conservation and determine whether any heat was lost to the calorimeter walls.

🌱 Kinetic Energy

🌱 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is defined as

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

This scalar quantity measures the energy an object possesses by virtue of its motion. In SI units, kinetic energy is expressed in joules (J), where $1\,\text{J} = 1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2$. For a system of particles, the total kinetic energy is the sum of individual contributions: $K_{\text{total}} = \sum_i \frac{1}{2}m_i v_i^2$. Kinetic energy is always non-negative, vanishing only when the object is at rest.

Worked Example. A hockey puck of mass $m = 0.170\,\text{kg}$ is struck and accelerates from rest to a speed of $v = 45.0\,\text{m/s}$. Determine its kinetic energy at that instant.

Applying the definition directly:

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.170\,\text{kg})(45.0\,\text{m/s})^2. $$

Computing step by step: $(45.0)^2 = 2025\,\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2$, so

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(0.170)(2025) = 0.0850 \times 2025 = 172\,\text{J}. $$

The puck carries $172\,\text{J}$ of kinetic energy after the impact.

Key Theorem — Work-Energy Theorem. The net work $W_{\text{net}}$ done on a particle equals the change in its kinetic energy:

$$ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2. $$

Proof sketch. Starting from Newton's second law, $F_{\text{net}} = ma$, and using the kinematic identity $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x$, one obtains $F_{\text{net}}\,\Delta x = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_0^2$. The left side is precisely the net work done over displacement $\Delta x$, yielding the result. The theorem generalizes to variable forces via integration: $W = \int_{x_i}^{x_f} F(x)\,dx$.

Lab Exercise. Place a dynamics cart of known mass on a frictionless air track. Use a photogate to measure the cart's speed before and after a compressed-spring plunger releases. Calculate the kinetic energy gained and compare it to the elastic potential energy stored in the spring (measured separately by the compression distance and spring constant). Quantify any discrepancy and identify its likely physical source.

🍃 Conduction

🍃 Conduction

Definition. Thermal conduction is the transfer of internal energy through a material by direct molecular interaction, without any bulk motion of the material itself. In a solid, more energetic (higher-temperature) atoms vibrate with greater amplitude and collide with adjacent atoms, transferring kinetic energy along the temperature gradient. The macroscopic rate of heat transfer by conduction is governed by Fourier's Law:

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = -kA\frac{dT}{dx} $$

where $\frac{dQ}{dt}$ is the rate of heat flow (in watts), $k$ is the thermal conductivity of the material (W/m·K), $A$ is the cross-sectional area perpendicular to heat flow (m²), and $\frac{dT}{dx}$ is the temperature gradient along the direction of flow. The negative sign indicates that heat flows from higher to lower temperature. Thermal conductivity $k$ is the single new material property introduced here; it characterizes how readily a substance transmits heat and varies widely across materials, from roughly $0.025\ \text{W/m·K}$ for air to over $400\ \text{W/m·K}$ for copper.

Worked Example. A steel rod ($k = 50\ \text{W/m·K}$) has a cross-sectional area of $A = 4.0 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{m}^2$ and a length of $L = 0.50\ \text{m}$. One end is maintained at $T_H = 200°\text{C}$ and the other at $T_C = 20°\text{C}$. Find the steady-state rate of heat conduction through the rod.

Assuming a uniform temperature gradient:

$$ \frac{dT}{dx} \approx \frac{T_C - T_H}{L} = \frac{20 - 200}{0.50} = -360\ \text{K/m} $$

Applying Fourier's Law:

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = -kA\frac{dT}{dx} = -(50)(4.0 \times 10^{-4})(-360) = 7.2\ \text{W} $$

The rod conducts heat at a rate of $7.2\ \text{W}$ from the hot end toward the cold end.

Key Theorem. In steady-state conduction through a composite slab (layers in series), the heat flux is identical through every layer, and the total thermal resistance is additive:

$$ R_{\text{total}} = \sum_i \frac{L_i}{k_i A} \implies \frac{dQ}{dt} = \frac{\Delta T}{R_{\text{total}}} $$

This result follows directly from requiring energy conservation at each interface under steady-state conditions. It is the thermal analogue of Ohm's law, with heat current corresponding to electric current and temperature difference to voltage.

Quantify the percentage error and identify possible sources of systematic uncertainty such as lateral heat loss.

🍃 Convection

🍃 Convection

Definition. Convection is a mode of heat transfer in which thermal energy is carried by the bulk motion of a fluid (liquid or gas). Unlike conduction, which transfers heat through molecular collisions within a stationary medium, convection requires macroscopic fluid movement. Two regimes are distinguished: natural (free) convection, driven by buoyancy forces that arise from density differences due to temperature gradients, and forced convection, in which an external agent (pump, fan, or wind) imposes the fluid flow. The convective heat flux is governed by Newton's law of cooling:

$$ q = h(T_s - T_f) $$

where $q$ is the heat flux (W m$^{-2}$), $h$ is the convective heat transfer coefficient (W m$^{-2}$ K$^{-1}$), $T_s$ is the surface temperature, and $T_f$ is the bulk fluid temperature. The rate of heat transfer from a surface of area $A$ is therefore:

$$ \dot{Q} = hA(T_s - T_f) $$

Worked Example. A flat metal plate of area $A = 0.50\ \text{m}^2$ is maintained at $T_s = 80^\circ\text{C}$. Air at $T_f = 20^\circ\text{C}$ flows over the plate with a convective coefficient $h = 25\ \text{W m}^{-2}\text{K}^{-1}$. The rate of heat loss is:

$$ \dot{Q} = (25)(0.50)(80 - 20) = (25)(0.50)(60) = 750\ \text{W} $$

This result illustrates that doubling either the area or the temperature difference doubles the heat transfer rate, while $h$ itself encapsulates the complex fluid dynamics of the boundary layer.

Key Theorem (Dimensional Analysis — Nusselt Number). The convective coefficient $h$ is characterized by the dimensionless Nusselt number $Nu = hL/k$, where $L$ is a characteristic length and $k$ is the fluid's thermal conductivity. For forced convection over a flat plate, empirical correlations take the form $Nu = C\, Re^m Pr^n$, where $Re = \rho v L/\mu$ is the Reynolds number and $Pr = c_p \mu / k$ is the Prandtl number. These correlations encode the dependence of $h$ on fluid velocity, viscosity, and thermal properties.

Lab Exercise. Place a beaker of water on a hot plate and add a few drops of food coloring near the bottom. Observe the circulation patterns as heating proceeds. Record the time for convection cells to develop, then estimate $h$ by measuring surface temperature and rate of temperature rise in the bulk fluid using a thermocouple. Compare your result with tabulated values for free convection in water ($h \approx 200$–$1000\ \text{W m}^{-2}\text{K}^{-1}$).

🌸 Wind Chill

🌸 Wind Chill

Definition. Wind chill is the perceived decrease in air temperature felt by the body due to the flow of air across exposed skin. Physiologically, the body generates heat and maintains a boundary layer of warm air near the skin. Wind disrupts this insulating layer, accelerating convective heat transfer and increasing the rate at which the body loses thermal energy. The wind chill temperature $T_{wc}$ is not a measure of the actual air temperature but rather an equivalent still-air temperature that would produce the same rate of heat loss from exposed flesh.

The National Weather Service formula (valid for wind speeds $v \geq 3$ mph and air temperatures $T \leq 50^\circ\text{F}$) is:

$$ T_{wc} = 35.74 + 0.6215\,T - 35.75\,v^{0.16} + 0.4275\,T\,v^{0.16} $$

where $T$ is the air temperature in °F and $v$ is the wind speed in mph. In SI-compatible form using °C and km/h:

$$ T_{wc} = 13.12 + 0.6215\,T_c - 11.37\,v_{km}^{0.16} + 0.3965\,T_c\,v_{km}^{0.16} $$

Worked Example. Suppose the air temperature is $T = -10^\circ\text{C}$ and the wind speed is $v_{km} = 30\ \text{km/h}$. Compute $v_{km}^{0.16}$:

$$ 30^{0.16} = e^{0.16\ln 30} = e^{0.16 \times 3.401} \approx e^{0.544} \approx 1.723 $$

Substituting:

$$ T_{wc} = 13.12 + 0.6215(-10) - 11.37(1.723) + 0.3965(-10)(1.723) $$ $$ T_{wc} = 13.12 - 6.215 - 19.58 - 6.827 \approx -19.5^\circ\text{C} $$

The wind makes $-10^\circ\text{C}$ feel nearly $-20^\circ\text{C}$—a dangerous difference for frostbite risk.

Key Theorem (Newton's Law of Cooling). The underlying physics is governed by Newton's Law of Cooling: the rate of heat loss from a surface is proportional to the temperature difference between the surface and the ambient fluid and to the convective heat transfer coefficient $h$:

$$ \frac{dQ}{dt} = h A (T_{skin} - T_{air}) $$

Wind increases $h$ substantially; the wind chill formula empirically encodes how $h$ grows with wind speed.

Lab Exercise. Using a thermometer enclosed in a damp cloth to simulate skin, measure the rate of temperature drop at three wind speeds (still air, fan on low, fan on high). Plot $dT/dt$ versus $v^{0.16}$ and determine whether the relationship is linear, confirming the functional form embedded in the wind chill formula.

Payoff 🎯

Wind chill represents the terminal synthesis of Chapter 14's thermodynamic and fluid-mechanical principles. Throughout this chapter, students have built a rigorous framework encompassing conductive, convective, and radiative heat transfer, Newton's Law of Cooling, and the coupling between fluid velocity and thermal boundary layers. Wind chill is not merely an application of these ideas — it is the point at which they converge into a single, measurable quantity that governs real heat loss from a body embedded in a moving fluid environment.

What Wind Chill Achieves. The wind chill temperature $T_{wc}$ is defined as the still-air temperature that would produce the same rate of convective heat loss from an exposed surface as the actual combination of ambient temperature $T_a$ and wind speed $v$. The National Weather Service formula,

$$ T_{wc} = 35.74 + 0.6215\,T_a - 35.75\,v^{0.16} + 0.4275\,T_a\,v^{0.16}, $$

encodes the dependence of the convective heat transfer coefficient $h$ on flow velocity. As $v$ increases, the thermal boundary layer thins, $h$ rises proportionally to $v^{0.16}$, and the effective temperature experienced by the surface drops well below $T_a$. This is Newton's Law of Cooling, $\dot{Q} = hA(T_s - T_a)$, made quantitative under realistic meteorological conditions.

Worked Example. At $T_a = -10\,°\text{C}$ and $v = 30\,\text{km/h}$, substituting into the formula gives $T_{wc} \approx -20\,°\text{C}$. A runner whose skin surface is at $33\,°\text{C}$ loses heat at a rate equivalent to standing motionless in $-20\,°\text{C}$ air, doubling the physiological stress compared to the still-air case.

Key Theorem. The Nusselt–Reynolds scaling, $Nu \propto Re^{1/2}$ for laminar boundary-layer flow, provides the theoretical underpinning. Since $Re = \rho v L / \mu$ and $h = Nu\,k/L$, the convective coefficient scales as $h \propto v^{1/2}$ in idealized laminar flow — a result the empirical exponent $0.16$ approximates under turbulent meteorological conditions.

Lab Exercise. Using a thermocouple mounted on a cylindrical rod in a wind tunnel, measure surface cooling rates at five wind speeds between $0$ and $40\,\text{km/h}$ with $T_a$ held fixed. Fit $h$ versus $v$ to a power law and compare your extracted exponent to both $0.5$ (laminar theory) and $0.16$ (NWS model). Discuss sources of discrepancy, including turbulence intensity and surface geometry.

The physics of wind chill extends directly into aerospace thermal management, where boundary-layer thinning governs heat shield design, and into building-envelope engineering, where wind-driven convection sets heating loads on facades. To pursue the latter, examine how the same convective principles scale from a human face to an entire curtain-wall system — a natural next step that places this chapter's formalism at the center of modern energy-efficient architecture.
