🍃 Isolated System

🍃 Isolated System

Definition. A mechanical system is called isolated if it experiences no net external force—that is, every force acting on any constituent particle originates from within the system itself. Under this condition, Newton's second law applied to the entire system gives

$$ \sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}} = \frac{d\vec{p}_{\text{sys}}}{dt} = \vec{0}, $$

which immediately implies that the total linear momentum of the system is constant:

$$ \vec{p}_{\text{sys}} = \sum_{i} m_i \vec{v}_i = \text{const.} $$

This result is the law of conservation of momentum: whenever the net external force on a system is zero, the total linear momentum cannot change, regardless of how large the internal forces between parts may be.

Worked Example. Two ice skaters, A (mass $m_A = 60\,\text{kg}$) and B (mass $m_B = 80\,\text{kg}$), stand at rest on a frictionless rink and push off each other. After the push, skater A moves at $\vec{v}_A = +3.0\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}$. Find skater B's velocity.

The rink is horizontal and frictionless; gravity and the normal force cancel vertically; no horizontal external forces act. The system is therefore isolated in the horizontal direction.

Initial momentum: $\vec{p}_i = \vec{0}$.

Conservation gives

$$ m_A \vec{v}_A + m_B \vec{v}_B = \vec{0} $$ $$ \vec{v}_B = -\frac{m_A}{m_B}\vec{v}_A = -\frac{60}{80}(3.0\,\hat{x}) = -2.25\,\hat{x}\ \text{m/s}. $$

Skater B moves in the $-x$ direction at $2.25\,\text{m/s}$, consistent with Newton's third law.

Key Theorem. If $\sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}} = \vec{0}$ over an interval $[t_1, t_2]$, then

$$ \vec{p}_{\text{sys}}(t_2) - \vec{p}_{\text{sys}}(t_1) = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sum \vec{F}_{\text{ext}}\, dt = \vec{0}. $$

Conservation of momentum holds component-by-component: a system may be isolated in one direction (e.g., horizontal) while non-isolated in another (e.g., vertical), permitting partial application of the law.

Lab Exercise. Using two low-friction carts on a dynamics track with a spring bumper, measure each cart's mass and velocity before and after a collision with a motion sensor. Compute $\vec{p}_{\text{before}}$ and $\vec{p}_{\text{after}}$, and calculate the percent discrepancy. Identify all external forces present and assess whether the track-cart system qualifies as isolated. Repeat with one cart carrying additional mass and compare the resulting velocity ratios to the prediction from conservation of momentum.
