🌱 兔

Imagine you are walking through a field and spot a small white bunny (that's our rabbit, or tù, 兔). Rabbits are part of nature's predictable cycles, often appearing in groups when food is abundant. When we talk about the rabbit in this context, we aren't just talking about an animal; we're talking about patterns—the successful pattern that draws attention and expectation.

Picture this: You keep finding rabbits near a specific patch of clover every single day. Because they are so dependable, you start to assume all good things will happen right there, right now. This feeling is called over-reliance; it means depending on one single source or pattern too much. The rabbit represents that initial, easily visible success—the successful outcome that makes us feel safe but also blind.

The simple rule here is: Nature's patterns are wonderful guides, but they are not guarantees. If we only wait for the predictable perfect event (like waiting for the bunny to reappear at the stump), we forget that other opportunities exist elsewhere. We must learn to look up and around, rather than just staring at the expected spot.

Try it yourself: Think of an area in your life where you are waiting for a single result—maybe waiting for one specific grade, or one particular person's approval. Instead of just waiting there, what is one small action you could take today to explore a different path?
