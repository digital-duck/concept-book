🌱 Binding Energy Per Nucleon

🌱 Binding Energy Per Nucleon

Definition. The binding energy per nucleon, denoted $\varepsilon$, is defined as the total nuclear binding energy of a nuclide divided by its mass number $A$:

$$ \varepsilon = \frac{BE}{A} $$

where the total binding energy is

$$ BE = \left(Z m_p + N m_n - m_{\text{nucleus}}\right)c^2 $$

with $Z$ protons of mass $m_p$, $N$ neutrons of mass $m_n$, and nuclear mass $m_{\text{nucleus}}$. In practice, atomic masses $m_{\text{atom}}$ are tabulated, so one uses

$$ BE = \left(Z m_H + N m_n - m_{\text{atom}}\right)c^2 $$

where $m_H$ is the atomic mass of hydrogen (electron masses cancel). The quantity $\varepsilon$ measures how tightly, on average, each nucleon is bound within the nucleus; a larger $\varepsilon$ indicates a more stable nuclide.

Worked Example. Consider $^{56}\text{Fe}$, which has $Z = 26$, $N = 30$, and atomic mass $m = 55.934939\ \text{u}$.

$$ BE = \bigl(26(1.007825\ \text{u}) + 30(1.008665\ \text{u}) - 55.934939\ \text{u}\bigr) \times 931.5\ \text{MeV/u} $$

Computing the mass defect:

$$ \Delta m = 26.20345 + 30.25995 - 55.934939 = 0.528461\ \text{u} $$ $$ BE = 0.528461 \times 931.5\ \text{MeV} \approx 492.3\ \text{MeV} $$ $$ \varepsilon = \frac{492.3\ \text{MeV}}{56} \approx 8.79\ \text{MeV/nucleon} $$

This places iron near the maximum of the binding energy per nucleon curve, explaining its role as the endpoint of stellar nucleosynthesis fusion chains.

Key Theorem — Peak Stability. The binding energy per nucleon curve exhibits a broad maximum near $A \approx 56$–62 (the iron-nickel peak), with $\varepsilon \approx 8.7$–$8.8\ \text{MeV/nucleon}$. Nuclides lighter than this peak release energy via fusion; nuclides heavier than the peak release energy via fission. Both processes drive the system toward the energy minimum at the peak.

🌱 Coulomb Repulsion

🌱 Coulomb Repulsion

Definition. Coulomb repulsion is the electrostatic force of repulsion between two charged particles of like sign. According to Coulomb's Law, the magnitude of the force between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by a distance $r$ in vacuum is

$$ F = k_e \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}, $$

where $k_e = 8.988 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N \cdot m^2 \cdot C^{-2}}$ is the Coulomb constant, related to the permittivity of free space by $k_e = 1/(4\pi\epsilon_0)$. When $q_1$ and $q_2$ carry the same sign, the force is directed along the line joining the charges and points away from the source charge — that is, the interaction is repulsive. The force obeys an inverse-square law, meaning it decays rapidly with increasing separation but never vanishes entirely.

Worked Example. Two protons in a helium nucleus are separated by approximately $r = 1.0 \times 10^{-15} \, \mathrm{m}$ (roughly one femtometer). Each proton carries charge $q = +1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \mathrm{C}$. The Coulomb repulsion between them is

$$ F = (8.988 \times 10^9) \frac{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})^2}{(1.0 \times 10^{-15})^2} \approx 230 \, \mathrm{N}. $$

This enormous force — comparable to the weight of a 23 kg mass — must be overcome by the strong nuclear force for the nucleus to remain bound. The calculation illustrates why nuclear physics cannot neglect electrostatic effects even at sub-femtometer scales.

Key Theorem (Superposition Principle). For a system of $N$ point charges, the total Coulomb force on a test charge $q_0$ is the vector sum of the individual pairwise forces:

$$ \vec{F}_{\mathrm{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} k_e \frac{q_0 \, q_i}{r_{0i}^2} \hat{r}_{0i}. $$

This linearity is exact in classical electrostatics and permits the analysis of arbitrarily complex charge distributions by decomposition into point-charge contributions.

🌱 Nuclear Force

🌱 Nuclear Force

Definition. The nuclear force (also called the strong nuclear force or residual strong force) is the short-range interaction that binds protons and neutrons—collectively termed nucleons—within an atomic nucleus. Unlike the electromagnetic force, which follows an inverse-square law over unlimited range, the nuclear force is characterized by three essential properties: (1) it is strongly attractive at internucleon separations of approximately $1\text{–}3\ \text{fm}$ ($1\ \text{fm} = 10^{-15}\ \text{m}$), (2) it becomes repulsive at separations below roughly $0.5\ \text{fm}$, preventing nuclear collapse, and (3) it is charge-independent, acting equally between proton-proton, proton-neutron, and neutron-neutron pairs. The potential energy associated with this force is often modeled by the Yukawa potential:

$$ U(r) = -\frac{g^2}{r}e^{-r/r_0} $$

where $g$ is a coupling constant and $r_0 \approx 1.5\ \text{fm}$ is the characteristic range, determined by the mass of the mediating pion via $r_0 = \hbar / (m_\pi c)$.

Worked Example. Consider two protons separated by $r = 2.0\ \text{fm}$. The repulsive Coulomb potential energy between them is:

$$ U_C = \frac{ke^2}{r} = \frac{(8.99\times10^9)(1.60\times10^{-19})^2}{2.0\times10^{-15}} \approx +0.72\ \text{MeV} $$

At this same separation, the nuclear attractive potential energy is on the order of $-20\ \text{MeV}$, roughly 28 times larger in magnitude. This demonstrates why the nuclear force overcomes electromagnetic repulsion to produce stable nuclei.

Key Theorem — Saturation Property. The binding energy per nucleon $B/A$ reaches a plateau of approximately $8.5\ \text{MeV}$ for nuclei with mass number $A \gtrsim 30$. This saturation arises because each nucleon interacts only with its immediate neighbors—a direct consequence of the nuclear force's short range. Were the force long-range, $B/A$ would grow without bound with $A$, which is not observed.

🍃 Nuclear Fission

🍃 Nuclear Fission

Definition. Nuclear fission is a nuclear reaction in which a heavy nucleus splits into two or more lighter nuclei (called fission fragments), accompanied by the release of several neutrons and a substantial amount of energy. For a nucleus to undergo fission, it must first absorb a neutron (or receive sufficient excitation energy), forming a compound nucleus in a highly excited state. The binding energy per nucleon curve peaks near iron ($A \approx 56$), so splitting a very heavy nucleus (e.g., $^{235}_{92}\text{U}$) into medium-mass fragments releases energy equal to the difference in total binding energies:

$$ Q = \left(M_{\text{initial}} - M_{\text{final}}\right)c^2 $$

where masses are evaluated in atomic mass units and converted via $1\,\text{u} \cdot c^2 = 931.5\,\text{MeV}$.

Worked Example. Consider thermal-neutron-induced fission of uranium-235:

$$ n + {}^{235}_{92}\text{U} \;\longrightarrow\; {}^{141}_{56}\text{Ba} + {}^{92}_{36}\text{Kr} + 3n $$

The $Q$-value is computed from atomic masses: $M(^{235}\text{U}) = 235.0439\,\text{u}$, $M(^{141}\text{Ba}) = 140.9144\,\text{u}$, $M(^{92}\text{Kr}) = 91.9262\,\text{u}$, $M(n) = 1.008665\,\text{u}$.

$$ \Delta M = (235.0439 + 1.008665) - (140.9144 + 91.9262 + 3 \times 1.008665) \approx 0.1860\,\text{u} $$ $$ Q \approx 0.1860 \times 931.5\,\text{MeV} \approx 173\,\text{MeV} $$

This energy — roughly $10^8$ times that of a chemical reaction — is distributed primarily as kinetic energy of the fragments.

Key Theorem (Critical Mass and Chain Reaction). Each fission event releases on average $\bar{\nu} \approx 2.5$ neutrons. If the multiplication factor $k = \bar{\nu} \cdot p_{\text{fission}}$ (where $p_{\text{fission}}$ is the probability a released neutron induces another fission) satisfies $k \geq 1$, a self-sustaining chain reaction occurs. The minimum mass of fissile material required to achieve $k = 1$ under given geometry and enrichment is called the critical mass; for weapons-grade $^{235}\text{U}$, this is approximately $52\,\text{kg}$.

🍃 Chain Reaction

🍃 Chain Reaction

Definition. A chain reaction is a self-sustaining sequence of nuclear fission events in which each fission produces, on average, at least one neutron that induces a subsequent fission. The critical parameter governing this behavior is the multiplication factor $k$, defined as

$$ k = \frac{\text{number of neutrons in one generation}}{\text{number of neutrons in preceding generation}}. $$

When $k < 1$ the reaction is subcritical and dies out; when $k = 1$ it is critical and self-sustaining at constant power; when $k > 1$ it is supercritical and the neutron population grows exponentially. In uranium-235, a single thermal-neutron-induced fission releases an average of $\bar{\nu} \approx 2.43$ neutrons and approximately $200\,\text{MeV}$ of energy per event.

Worked Example. Suppose a fissile assembly has a reproduction factor $\eta = 2.43$ (neutrons produced per neutron absorbed in fuel), a thermal utilization $f = 0.90$, a resonance escape probability $p = 0.85$, and a fast fission factor $\varepsilon = 1.03$. The four-factor formula for an infinite medium gives

$$ k_\infty = \eta \, f \, p \, \varepsilon = (2.43)(0.90)(0.85)(1.03) \approx 1.91. $$

A finite reactor additionally multiplies by leakage terms $P_{NL}$ (non-leakage probability), yielding $k_{\text{eff}} = k_\infty P_{NL}$. If $P_{NL} = 0.524$, then $k_{\text{eff}} \approx 1.00$, placing the reactor at criticality.

Key Theorem (Criticality Condition). For a bare spherical reactor of radius $R$ and material buckling $B^2 = k_\infty/M^2 - 1/M^2$, criticality requires

$$ B^2 R^2 = \pi^2, $$

where $M^2$ is the migration area. This geometric buckling condition defines the critical radius, below which leakage prevents a sustained chain reaction.

🍃 Critical Mass

🍃 Critical Mass

Definition. The critical mass of a fissile material is the minimum mass required to sustain a self-propagating nuclear chain reaction. For a bare sphere of fissile material, criticality is achieved when the neutron production rate equals the neutron loss rate (via leakage and non-fission absorption). Formally, the system reaches criticality when the effective neutron multiplication factor satisfies

$$ k_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\text{neutrons produced per generation}}{\text{neutrons lost per generation}} = 1. $$

For a homogeneous sphere of radius $R$, the one-group diffusion equation yields the criticality condition:

$$ B^2 = \left(\frac{\pi}{R}\right)^2 = \frac{\nu \Sigma_f - \Sigma_a}{D}, $$

where $\nu$ is the average number of neutrons emitted per fission, $\Sigma_f$ and $\Sigma_a$ are the macroscopic fission and absorption cross-sections, and $D$ is the neutron diffusion coefficient. The critical radius is therefore

$$ R_c = \pi \sqrt{\frac{D}{\nu \Sigma_f - \Sigma_a}}, $$

giving a critical mass $M_c = \frac{4}{3}\pi R_c^3 \rho$, where $\rho$ is the material density.

Worked Example. Estimate the critical radius for a bare sphere of $^{235}$U given $D = 1.10\ \text{cm}$, $\Sigma_a = 0.118\ \text{cm}^{-1}$, $\Sigma_f = 0.107\ \text{cm}^{-1}$, and $\nu = 2.44$.

Compute the denominator:

$$ \nu \Sigma_f - \Sigma_a = (2.44)(0.107) - 0.118 = 0.261 - 0.118 = 0.143\ \text{cm}^{-1}. $$

Then:

$$ R_c = \pi\sqrt{\frac{1.10}{0.143}} = \pi\sqrt{7.69} \approx \pi(2.77) \approx 8.7\ \text{cm}. $$

With $\rho \approx 18.7\ \text{g/cm}^3$, this gives $M_c \approx \frac{4}{3}\pi(8.7)^3(18.7) \approx 52\ \text{kg}$, consistent with uncompressed critical mass estimates for $^{235}$U.

Key Theorem. A neutron reflector surrounding the fissile core scatters escaping neutrons back inward, effectively reducing $R_c$ and thus substantially lowering the critical mass — sometimes by a factor of two or more.

🌸 Fission Bomb

🌸 Fission Bomb

Definition. A fission bomb (also called an atomic bomb) is a weapon that releases an enormous quantity of energy through a rapid, uncontrolled chain reaction of nuclear fission. When a fissile nucleus—typically uranium-235 ($^{235}_{92}\text{U}$) or plutonium-239 ($^{239}_{94}\text{Pu}$)—absorbs a slow (thermal) or fast neutron, it splits into two lighter daughter nuclei (fission fragments), releases 2–3 prompt neutrons, and liberates approximately $200\ \text{MeV}$ of energy per event. If each liberated neutron triggers at least one additional fission, the reaction becomes self-sustaining; the condition for explosive amplification is that the effective neutron multiplication factor satisfies $k > 1$, achieved by assembling a supercritical mass of fissile material.

Worked Example. Estimate the energy released if $1\ \text{kg}$ of $^{235}\text{U}$ undergoes complete fission.

The molar mass of $^{235}\text{U}$ is $M = 0.235\ \text{kg mol}^{-1}$, so the number of nuclei is

$$ N = \frac{m}{M} N_A = \frac{1}{0.235} \times 6.022\times10^{23} \approx 2.56\times10^{24}. $$

With $Q \approx 200\ \text{MeV} = 200\times10^6 \times 1.602\times10^{-19}\ \text{J} \approx 3.20\times10^{-11}\ \text{J}$ per fission,

$$ E = NQ \approx 2.56\times10^{24} \times 3.20\times10^{-11}\ \text{J} \approx 8.2\times10^{13}\ \text{J} \approx 82\ \text{TJ}, $$

equivalent to roughly $20\ \text{kilotons}$ of TNT — consistent with the yield of early fission devices.

Key Theorem (Critical Mass Condition). For a bare sphere of fissile material with density $\rho$, the critical mass $m_c$ satisfies

$$ m_c = \frac{4}{3}\pi \rho \left(\frac{\pi}{\sqrt{3\Sigma_f \Sigma_{tr}}}\right)^3, $$

where $\Sigma_f$ is the macroscopic fission cross-section and $\Sigma_{tr}$ is the transport cross-section. Surrounding the fissile core with a dense neutron reflector (e.g., natural uranium or beryllium) scatters neutrons back into the core, reducing $m_c$ substantially and enabling practical weapon configurations.

Payoff 🎯

A fission bomb represents the most consequential application of nuclear physics developed in the twentieth century, transforming the abstract binding-energy curve into a macroscopic release of energy on a scale previously unimaginable. It stands as the natural endpoint of Chapter 32 because it unifies every preceding concept — radioactive decay, nuclear stability, cross-section theory, and chain reactions — into a single, coherent physical system operating at the edge of controllability.

The core mechanism rests on the neutron-induced fission of a fissile nucleus, typically $^{235}\text{U}$ or $^{239}\text{Pu}$. When a slow neutron is absorbed, the compound nucleus $^{236}\text{U}^*$ oscillates between electrostatic repulsion and the short-range nuclear force until it splits:

$$ ^{235}_{92}\text{U} + n \;\longrightarrow\; ^{141}_{56}\text{Ba} + ^{92}_{36}\text{Kr} + 3n + Q $$

where $Q \approx 200\,\text{MeV}$ per event, released primarily as kinetic energy of the fragments. The emitted neutrons trigger additional fissions, and if the assembly exceeds the critical mass — the minimum mass for which the neutron multiplication factor $k \geq 1$ — the population grows geometrically. For a bare sphere of $^{235}\text{U}$, the critical mass is approximately $52\,\text{kg}$; a beryllium reflector reduces this to roughly $15\,\text{kg}$ by scattering escaping neutrons back into the core.

Worked Example. Suppose a chain reaction undergoes 80 generations in $1\,\mu\text{s}$, with $k = 1.5$. The number of fissions after $n$ generations is $N = N_0 k^n$. Starting with a single neutron, $N = (1.5)^{80} \approx 2.5 \times 10^{14}$ fissions, releasing energy $E = 2.5 \times 10^{14} \times 200\,\text{MeV} \approx 8\,\text{kJ}$ — only a fraction of weapon-scale yield, illustrating that actual devices exploit far larger neutron populations and tighter timescales.

The fission bomb is the direct physical precursor to the thermonuclear bomb. An uncontrolled fission detonation generates the extreme temperatures ($T \gtrsim 10^7\,\text{K}$) and pressures necessary to overcome the Coulomb barrier between light nuclei such as deuterium and tritium, initiating fusion reactions with energy yields orders of magnitude larger. Without a reliable fission primary, thermonuclear ignition is not achievable with present technology.
