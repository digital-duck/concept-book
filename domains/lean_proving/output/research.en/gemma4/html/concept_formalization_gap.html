🍃 Formalization Gap

Definition. Let $\mathcal{A}$ be an informal domain of human knowledge or practice (e.g., ethical reasoning, common sense, intuition). A formalization gap exists when $\mathcal{A}$ cannot be fully mapped to a sufficiently expressive and tractable Formal System $\mathcal{F}$, such that the truth value $\text{Truth}_{\mathcal{A}}(P)$ for an assertion $P \in \mathcal{A}$ does not uniquely correspond to the provability or semantic truth value $\text{Truth}_{\mathcal{F}}(P)$ within $\mathcal{F}$. This gap manifests as a failure of fidelity, meaning there exists at least one instance where $\mathcal{A} \not\equiv \mathcal{F}$, regardless of the chosen axioms for $\mathcal{F}$ (see related work on incompleteness in semantics).

Theorem. If an informal domain $\mathcal{A}$ exhibits non-monotonic reasoning or context-dependent truth assignment, then any attempt to model $\mathcal{A}$ using a standard first-order logic ($\mathcal{L}_1$) will necessarily encounter a formalization gap that cannot be bridged solely by expanding the axiomatization of $\mathcal{L}_1$. Specifically, $\text{Gap}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{L}_1) \neq \emptyset$ if $\mathcal{A}$ requires mechanisms beyond standard satisfiability semantics.

Proof Sketch. The failure stems from the inherent limitations of classical logic in capturing defeasible knowledge and implicit context. Consider a canonical example: common sense entailment, where $\text{P}(x)$ implies $\text{Q}(x)$, but this implication $P \implies Q$ only holds under specific environmental constraints $E$. Because first-order languages require implications to be globally defined (i.e., $P \models Q$), they cannot naturally encode the scope restriction $\{P \Rightarrow_{E} Q\}$. To formalize such a constraint requires moving beyond $\mathcal{L}_1$, typically necessitating modal logics, non-monotonic logics (such as default logic), or circumscription, which fundamentally alters the semantics and computational complexity of the system. Therefore, the gap is structurally necessary unless the initial domain $\mathcal{A}$ is voluntarily restricted to be monotonic and context-independent.

Remark. The identification, rather than elimination, of this formalization gap is often viewed as a critical step in AI theory (e.g., McCarthy’s critique of computational realism). While some research attempts to close the gap through highly expressive logics or probabilistic frameworks (e.g., Bayesian Networks), these methods often trade completeness for tractability. The persistence of the gap suggests that capturing human reasoning may require conceptual shifts rather than merely syntactic extensions, pointing toward a potential need for higher-order systems or meta-level representations beyond current standard formalisms.
