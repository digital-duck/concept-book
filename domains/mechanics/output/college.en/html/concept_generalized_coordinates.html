🍃 Generalized Coordinates

🍃 Generalized Coordinates

Generalized coordinates ($\mathbf{q}$) constitute the minimum set of independent parameters necessary to uniquely define the position and configuration of a mechanical system subject to constraints. While systems in free space are often naturally described using $N$ Cartesian coordinates, $(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_N)$, physical reality frequently restricts movement. When these constraints confine the system's trajectory to an embedded manifold within the full $N$-dimensional Euclidean space, the number of required independent variables decreases significantly. By utilizing only a minimal set of parameters $\mathbf{q} = \{q_1, q_2, \dots, q_k\}$, where $k < N$, we achieve a fundamental simplification in physical analysis. This choice is essential because it allows us to formulate the system's dynamics using powerful methods like Lagrangian mechanics, effectively incorporating the effects of any constraint forces ($\mathbf{F}_c$) without requiring their explicit calculation or resolution.

Worked Example: Consider a simple pendulum of mass $m$ and length $L$. In a standard 2D Cartesian frame $(x, y)$, the particle's position must satisfy the geometric constraint $x^2 + y^2 = L^2$, which reduces the degrees of freedom from two to one. We eliminate redundant variables by choosing the single generalized coordinate, $\theta$, defined as the angle between the rod and the vertical axis. The entire configuration is thus specified solely by $\mathbf{q} = \{\theta\}$. This reduction profoundly simplifies the Lagrangian formulation:

$$L = T - V = \frac{1}{2} m L^2 \dot{\theta}^2 - m g L \cos(\theta)$$

The fact that the resulting Lagrangian depends only on $\theta$ and its time derivative confirms that $\theta$ is indeed a generalized coordinate, validating the principle of minimal representation.
