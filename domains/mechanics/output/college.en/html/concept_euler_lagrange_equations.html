🍃 Euler–Lagrange Equations

The Euler–Lagrange equations constitute a foundational principle in theoretical physics and advanced classical mechanics. They provide the necessary condition for determining the path—or trajectory—that an isolated physical system takes between two fixed points in time, a path defined by minimizing or maximizing a quantity known as the action functional ($S$). For a dynamical system whose evolution is described by generalized coordinates $q_i(t)$, the principle asserts that the true motion must satisfy $\delta S = 0$. The action itself is defined as the time integral of the Lagrangian ($L$), where this Lagrangian is generally formulated as the difference between the kinetic energy ($T$) and the potential energy ($V$): $L = T - V$.

The formal statement of the principle dictates that for any continuous variation in the path $\delta q_i(t)$ such that $\delta q_i(t_1) = \delta q_i(t_2) = 0$, the following condition must hold:

$$S[q] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q, \dot{q}, t)\, dt$$
