🌱 Position

🌱 Position

Definition. In classical mechanics, the position of a particle is a vector quantity that specifies the location of that particle relative to a chosen reference point called the origin. Given an inertial reference frame with a fixed origin $O$ and a right-handed Cartesian coordinate system, the position of a particle $P$ at time $t$ is the vector

$$ \mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + y(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} + z(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}, $$

where $x(t)$, $y(t)$, and $z(t)$ are the signed scalar projections of $\mathbf{r}$ onto the coordinate axes, and $\hat{\mathbf{i}}$, $\hat{\mathbf{j}}$, $\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ are the standard orthonormal basis vectors. The magnitude $|\mathbf{r}(t)|$ gives the straight-line distance from the origin to the particle. Position is a state variable: its specification at a given instant, together with velocity, completely determines the future evolution of an isolated Newtonian particle.

Worked Example. A ball is launched from the point $(2,\,0,\,0)\,\text{m}$ with initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0 = 3\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + 4\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}\;\text{m\,s}^{-1}$ under constant gravitational acceleration $\mathbf{g} = -9.8\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}\;\text{m\,s}^{-2}$. Integrating $\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{g}$ twice with the given initial conditions yields

$$ \mathbf{r}(t) = (2 + 3t)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + \left(4t - \tfrac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2\right)\hat{\mathbf{k}}\;\text{m}. $$

At $t = 0.5\,\text{s}$ the position is $\mathbf{r}(0.5) = 3.5\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + 0.775\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}\;\text{m}$, with magnitude $|\mathbf{r}(0.5)| = \sqrt{3.5^2 + 0.775^2} \approx 3.585\,\text{m}$.

Key Theorem (Frame Transformation). If two inertial frames $S$ and $S'$ share the same orientation and $S'$ moves at constant velocity $\mathbf{V}$ relative to $S$, with origins coinciding at $t = 0$, then the position of a particle as measured in each frame satisfies the Galilean transformation

$$ \mathbf{r}'(t) = \mathbf{r}(t) - \mathbf{V}t. $$

This relation preserves Newton's second law because the acceleration $\ddot{\mathbf{r}}' = \ddot{\mathbf{r}}$ is frame-independent under Galilean relativity, confirming that position is frame-dependent while the equations of motion retain the same form across all inertial frames.

Lab Exercise. Set up a two-dimensional coordinate system on a large sheet of graph paper. Place a marble at a measured initial position $\mathbf{r}_0$ and roll it across a level surface. Record its position $\mathbf{r}(t)$ at equal time intervals using stroboscopic photography or video frame analysis. Plot $x(t)$ and $y(t)$ separately and verify that each component varies linearly with time, consistent with constant-velocity motion. Compute $|\mathbf{r}(t) - \mathbf{r}_0|$ and compare with the measured path length.
