🍃 Constraints

🍃 Constraints

Definition. A holonomic constraint is a geometric restriction on the configuration of a mechanical system, expressible as an algebraic relation among positions and time:

$$ f(q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_{3N}, t) = 0. $$

A system of $N$ particles in three dimensions possesses $3N$ coordinates $\{q_i\}$; each independent holonomic constraint reduces the degrees of freedom by one, leaving $n = 3N - k$ generalized coordinates for $k$ constraints. Constraints involving velocities in a form that cannot be integrated to a position relation—such as $g(q_i, \dot{q}_i, t) = 0$—are called non-holonomic and are not treated here.

Worked Example. Consider a bead of mass $m$ constrained to move along a rigid wire bent into a circle of radius $R$ in the $xy$-plane. The two holonomic constraints are

$$ x^2 + y^2 = R^2, \qquad z = 0. $$

These reduce the system from three degrees of freedom to one. Introducing the single generalized coordinate $\theta$ (the polar angle), the position is $x = R\cos\theta$, $y = R\sin\theta$, $z = 0$. The kinetic energy becomes $T = \tfrac{1}{2}mR^2\dot{\theta}^2$, and the constraint forces (the normal reaction of the wire) do no virtual work, so they vanish from the equations of motion.

Key Theorem (D'Alembert's Principle). For a system subject to ideal constraints—those whose constraint forces are perpendicular to all virtual displacements $\delta\mathbf{r}_i$—the equations of motion satisfy

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{applied}} - m_i\ddot{\mathbf{r}}_i\right)\cdot\delta\mathbf{r}_i = 0. $$

This principle eliminates constraint forces from the dynamical equations entirely, providing the foundation for Lagrangian mechanics. It holds rigorously for holonomic constraints.

Lab Exercise. A particle of mass $m$ slides without friction on the inner surface of a hemispherical bowl of radius $R$. Write the two holonomic constraints in Cartesian coordinates, identify the remaining degrees of freedom, introduce appropriate generalized coordinates, and derive the Lagrangian. Determine the normal force as a function of $\theta$ using the constraint equation combined with Newton's second law in the radial direction.
