🍃 Newtons Second Law

🍃 Newton's Second Law

Definition. Newton's Second Law of Motion states that the net force acting on a particle of constant mass $m$ is equal to the time rate of change of its linear momentum $\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}$:

$$ \mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt} = m\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = m\mathbf{a}, $$

where $\mathbf{v}$ is the velocity vector and $\mathbf{a} = \ddot{\mathbf{r}}$ is the acceleration. The law is a vector equation: force, velocity, and acceleration all carry both magnitude and direction. In component form under Cartesian coordinates,

$$ F_x = ma_x, \quad F_y = ma_y, \quad F_z = ma_z. $$

This constitutes a second-order ordinary differential equation in the position $\mathbf{r}(t)$, which, together with initial conditions $\mathbf{r}(t_0)$ and $\mathbf{v}(t_0)$, determines the subsequent motion uniquely.

Worked Example. A block of mass $m = 2\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A constant horizontal force $F = 6\ \text{N}$ is applied. Find the position $x(t)$ given $x(0) = 0$ and $v(0) = 0$.

Applying Newton's Second Law along the $x$-axis:

$$ F = ma \implies a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{6}{2} = 3\ \text{m/s}^2. $$

Integrating once gives the velocity $v(t) = at = 3t\ \text{m/s}$, and integrating again gives

$$ x(t) = \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 = \tfrac{3}{2}t^2\ \text{m}. $$

At $t = 2\ \text{s}$, the block has traveled $x = 6\ \text{m}$ and is moving at $v = 6\ \text{m/s}$.

Key Theorem (Existence and Uniqueness of Motion). When $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}, \dot{\mathbf{r}}, t)$ is Lipschitz continuous in $\mathbf{r}$ and $\dot{\mathbf{r}}$, the Picard–Lindelöf theorem guarantees that the initial-value problem $m\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{F}$, with prescribed $\mathbf{r}(t_0)$ and $\dot{\mathbf{r}}(t_0)$, has a unique solution on some open interval containing $t_0$. Physically, this means the present position and velocity of a particle, together with the force law, completely determine all future motion. Most forces encountered in classical mechanics—gravity, spring forces, viscous drag—satisfy the Lipschitz condition, so the theorem applies broadly.

Lab Exercise. A particle of mass $m = 0.5\ \text{kg}$ moves along the $x$-axis under a time-varying force $F(t) = 4t\ \text{N}$. Given $x(0) = 1\ \text{m}$ and $v(0) = 2\ \text{m/s}$, determine $x(t)$ and $v(t)$ for $t \geq 0$ by integrating Newton's Second Law directly. Then confirm that $\Delta\mathbf{p}$ over $[0, 3]\ \text{s}$ equals $\int_0^3 F(t)\, dt$, using only the momentum $\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}$ defined above.
