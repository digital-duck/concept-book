🍃 Configuration Space

🍃 Configuration Space

Definition. The configuration space of a mechanical system is the smooth manifold $\mathcal{Q}$ whose points represent every geometrically admissible arrangement of the system's components, without reference to velocities or momenta. If the system has $n$ degrees of freedom, then $\mathcal{Q}$ is locally homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$, and a point $q = (q^1, q^2, \ldots, q^n) \in \mathcal{Q}$ is called a generalized coordinate tuple. The dimension of $\mathcal{Q}$ equals the number of independent coordinates needed to fully specify the system's geometry after all holonomic constraints have been applied.

Worked Example. Consider a planar double pendulum: two rigid rods of lengths $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$, each pinned at one end, with the second rod attached to the free end of the first. The first rod makes angle $\theta_1$ with the vertical; the second makes angle $\theta_2$ with the vertical. Both angles range freely over $[0, 2\pi)$, so each coordinate lives on the circle $S^1$. The configuration space is therefore the two-torus,

$$ \mathcal{Q} = S^1 \times S^1 = \mathbb{T}^2, $$

parameterized by $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$. A single point $({\pi}/{4},\, {3\pi}/{2}) \in \mathbb{T}^2$ uniquely determines the position of every mass particle in the system. Note that $\mathcal{Q}$ is compact and curved — it cannot be globally identified with $\mathbb{R}^2$.

Key Theorem (Dimension Reduction by Holonomic Constraints). Let a system of $N$ particles in $\mathbb{R}^3$ be subject to $k$ independent holonomic constraints $f_j(q) = 0$, $j = 1, \ldots, k$. Then the configuration space is a smooth submanifold of $\mathbb{R}^{3N}$ of dimension

$$ n = 3N - k. $$

Consequently, the system's motion is governed by exactly $n$ independent second-order ODEs in the generalized coordinates $q^1, \ldots, q^n$.

Lab Exercise. A bead of mass $m$ is constrained to slide without friction on a torus of major radius $R$ and minor radius $r < R$, embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$. (i) Identify $\mathcal{Q}$ and its dimension. (ii) Write the parametric embedding $\mathbf{x}(\phi, \psi)$ of the torus and compute the induced metric tensor $g_{ij}$. (iii) Derive the kinetic energy $T = \frac{1}{2}g_{ij}\dot{q}^i\dot{q}^j$ explicitly in terms of $(\phi, \dot\phi, \psi, \dot\psi)$.
