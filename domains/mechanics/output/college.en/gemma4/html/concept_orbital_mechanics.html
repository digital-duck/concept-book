🌸 Orbital Mechanics

Orbital mechanics is the branch of astrodynamics concerned with predicting the motion of celestial bodies and spacecraft under gravitational influence. Fundamentally, it models the interaction between masses governed by Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation and the principles of classical mechanics. For most initial analyses, the system is simplified to a two-body problem: an orbiting object and a central primary body (e.g., a satellite around Earth). The resulting trajectories are not arbitrary; they are always conic sections—ellipses, parabolas, or hyperbolas—determined by the total energy and angular momentum of the system. Key variables include the gravitational parameter $\mu = G(M+m)$, where $G$ is the gravitational constant, and $(M+m)$ is the combined mass of the primary and secondary bodies.

Worked Example: Calculating Circular Orbital Velocity Consider a satellite placed in a perfectly circular orbit around Earth at an altitude $h$. We can determine the necessary orbital velocity ($v$) using the principle of uniform centripetal acceleration balanced by gravitational force. At radius $r = R_{\oplus} + h$, the gravitational force provides the required centripetal force:

$$ F_g = F_c $$ $$ \frac{G M m}{r^2} = \frac{m v^2}{r} $$

Solving for $v$ yields the orbital velocity formula:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{G M}{r}} $$

If we assume a specific radius $r$, this equation gives the minimum constant speed required to maintain that orbit, assuming no atmospheric drag.
