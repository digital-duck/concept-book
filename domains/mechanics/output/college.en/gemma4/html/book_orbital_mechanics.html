🌱 Position

Position is the vector quantity that specifies the location of a point particle within a defined coordinate system relative to a fixed origin $\mathcal{O}$. Since position describes location in space, it must be represented by a vector $\mathbf{r}$, typically written as a function of time $t$, denoted $\mathbf{r}(t)$. $$ \mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\hat{\mathbf{i}} + y(t)\hat{\mathbf{j}} + z(t)\hat{\mathbf{k}} $$

The components $(x(t), y(t), z(t))$ are scalar functions that dictate how the particle's location changes over time. Unlike displacement, which is a change in position ($\Delta \mathbf{r}$), position itself requires specifying where the object is relative to $\mathcal{O}$.

🌱 Time

Time ($t$) is a fundamental dimension used to quantify change and order events within the physical universe. Conceptually, it represents the parameterization of evolving states of a system. In classical Newtonian mechanics, time was treated as an absolute, universal quantity—a backdrop against which all physical processes unfolded. However, modern physics, particularly Special and General Relativity, reveals that time is not independent but is intrinsically interwoven with space into a single manifold known as spacetime ($\mathcal{M}$). Mathematically, the passage of time is often represented by a coordinate $x^0$, where coordinates are typically written as $(ct, x, y, z)$. The core definition relates to the infinitesimal temporal separation between two events, $\Delta t$. This interval must be analyzed alongside spatial separations to understand how various physical measurements change relative to observers in different inertial frames.

Consider calculating the time elapsed for a particle moving at a constant velocity $v$ between two points separated by a distance $L$. The basic relationship is $\Delta t = L/v$. A more sophisticated approach involves examining the proper time ($\tau$), which is the time interval measured by a clock that moves along the path of the object. If an observer measures a time interval $\Delta t$, but the moving particle experiences only $\Delta \tau$, they are related by the Lorentz transformation: $\Delta \tau = \Delta t / \gamma$, where $\gamma$ is the Lorentz factor, defined as $\gamma = (1 - v^2/c^2)^{-1/2}$. This relationship demonstrates that time measurement itself depends on the relative velocity between the observer and the event being timed.

A crucial concept governing time's geometry is the spacetime interval ($ds^2$), which defines the invariant distance between two events, regardless of the reference frame:

$$ ds^2 = c^2 d\tau^2 = \eta_{\mu\nu} dx^\mu dx^\nu $$

where $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ is the metric tensor (e.g., diag$(1, -1, -1, -1)$ in Minkowski space). The magnitude of this interval is invariant under coordinate transformations.

Key Theorem: The Principle of Relativity states that the laws of physics are the same for all observers in uniform motion (inertial frames). This principle necessitates the time dilation and length contraction effects described by the Lorentz transformation, fundamentally linking temporal measurement to relative velocity.

🍃 Velocity

Velocity ($\vec{v}$) is a fundamental vector quantity in kinematics that describes the rate at which an object changes its position over time. Unlike speed, which is a scalar representing only the magnitude of motion, velocity incorporates both magnitude and direction. Mathematically, if $\vec{x}(t)$ represents the position vector of an object as a function of time $t$, the average velocity ($\vec{v}_{avg}$) over a time interval $\Delta t = \Delta t$ is defined by the change in displacement ($\Delta \vec{x} / \Delta t$). Specifically, $\vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{\vec{x}_f - \vec{x}_i}{t_f - t_i}$. It is crucial to distinguish that velocity depends only on initial and final positions, whereas speed depends on the total path length traveled.

Worked Example: A particle moves along the $x$-axis, starting at position $\vec{x}_i = 2.0\text{ m}$ at time $t_i = 1.0 \text{ s}$. It then reaches a final position $\vec{x}_f = 7.0\text{ m}$ at time $t_f = 4.0 \text{ s}$. Calculate the average velocity of the particle during this interval.

$$ \vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{\Delta \vec{x}}{\Delta t} = \frac{(7.0 \text{ m}) - (2.0 \text{ m})}{(4.0 \text{ s}) - (1.0 \text{ s})} = \frac{5.0 \text{ m}}{3.0 \text{ s}} \approx 1.67 \text{ m/s} $$

The average velocity is $1.67\text{ m/s}$ in the positive $\hat{\mathbf{i}}$ direction.

🌱 Force

A force is fundamentally an interaction that causes a change in an object's motion, or tends to cause such a change. In classical mechanics, it is defined as a vector quantity $\mathbf{F}$ measured in Newtons ($\text{N}$), which are equivalent to kilograms-meters per second squared ($\text{kg} \cdot \text{m/s}^2$). Mathematically, force acts upon an object of mass $m$ and has components along specific coordinate axes (e.g., $\mathbf{F} = F_x \hat{\mathbf{i}} + F_y \hat{\mathbf{j}} + F_z \hat{\mathbf{k}}$). Forces can arise from various sources, including contact forces (like normal force or friction) and field forces (like gravity or electromagnetism). The net force acting on an object is the vector sum of all individual forces contributing to its motion.

Worked Example: Calculating Net Force on a Block Consider a rectangular block resting on a horizontal surface. Two forces act upon this block: a gravitational force $\mathbf{F}_g$ pulling it downward, and a normal force $\mathbf{N}$ exerted by the surface upward. If the block has mass $m$, the gravitational force is $\mathbf{F}_g = -mg \hat{\mathbf{j}}$ (assuming the negative $\hat{\mathbf{j}}$ direction points down). Since the block remains at rest, the net force $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}$ must be zero. We apply Newton's First Law:

$$ \mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{N} + \mathbf{F}_g $$

Setting $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = 0$:

$$ 0 = N\hat{\mathbf{j}} - mg\hat{\mathbf{j}} \implies N = mg $$

The net force is zero, confirming the block's equilibrium.

Key Theorem: Newton's Second Law of Motion Newton’s Second Law provides the quantitative relationship between force and acceleration. It states that the net force $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}$ acting on an object is directly proportional to its mass $m$ and the resulting acceleration $\mathbf{a}$, with the constant of proportionality being one (in SI units). This law is expressed as:

$$ \mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = m\mathbf{a} $$

This theorem forms the cornerstone of dynamics, allowing us to predict motion given known forces. If a force $\mathbf{F}$ causes acceleration $\mathbf{a}$, then $\mathbf{F}$ must be calculated as $m\mathbf{a}$.

🍃 Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy ($K$, often denoted $E_k$) is defined as the energy an object possesses due to its motion. It quantifies the rate at which work is done by a force acting on an object, and it is fundamentally dependent upon both the object's mass and its velocity. For classical mechanics, the kinetic energy of a particle with rest mass $m$ moving with a constant velocity $\vec{v}$ is given by the scalar quantity:

$$ K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 $$

This definition assumes that the speed $v$ is much less than the speed of light ($c$), which allows us to use non-relativistic mechanics. A formal derivation, often involving the work-energy theorem, shows that the change in kinetic energy ($\Delta K$) equals the net work done ($W_{net}$) on an object: $\Delta K = W_{net}$. If we consider a constant force $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$, then $W_{net} = \int \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r}$. Using the chain rule and the definition of acceleration ($\vec{a} = d\vec{v}/dt$), this integral simplifies directly to the established form, proving that $\frac{1}{2} m v^2$ is the correct energy measure for macroscopic systems.

Worked Example: A cart with a mass $m = 5.0 \text{ kg}$ accelerates uniformly from rest to a final speed of $4.0 \text{ m/s}$. Calculate the kinetic energy gained by the cart. Given: $m = 5.0 \text{ kg}$, initial velocity $v_i = 0$, final velocity $v_f = 4.0 \text{ m/s}$. The change in kinetic energy is $\Delta K = K_f - K_i$. Since the cart starts from rest, $K_i = 0$.

$$ K_f = \frac{1}{2} m v_f^2 = \frac{1}{2} (5.0 \text{ kg}) (4.0 \text{ m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2} (5.0)(16) \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2 $$ $$ K_f = 40 \text{ J} $$

The kinetic energy gained is $40 \text{ Joules}$.

Key Theorem: The Work-Energy Theorem states that the net work done on an object by all applied forces ($\vec{F}_{net}$) is equal to the change in its kinetic energy:

$$ W_{net} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i = \frac{1}{2} m v_f^2 - \frac{1}{2} m v_i^2 $$

This theorem provides a powerful alternative way to calculate final velocities or required forces without needing to know the detailed path of motion.

🍃 Momentum

Momentum ($\vec{p}$) is a fundamental physical vector quantity that measures the "quantity of motion" an object possesses. Mathematically, it is defined as the product of an object's mass ($m$) and its velocity ($\vec{v}$):

$$ \vec{p} = m\vec{v} $$

Because both mass and velocity are scalars multiplied by a direction, momentum is inherently a vector quantity. It describes not only how much matter is moving (the magnitude) but also the specific direction of that motion. The units of momentum in the SI system are kilograms-meters per second ($\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}/\text{s}$). Understanding momentum is crucial because it allows us to analyze systems where forces and collisions occur, particularly those confined by conservation laws.

🍃 Energy Conservation

The principle of energy conservation is one of the most fundamental tenets of physics, asserting that in a closed or isolated system—defined as one where no net work is done by external forces, and there are no exchanges of matter or energy with the surroundings—the total energy remains invariant. This law dictates that energy cannot be spontaneously created or destroyed; rather, it merely transforms from one measurable form to another (e.g., mechanical potential into thermal heat, or electrical into kinetic). Mathematically, if $E_{\text{total}}$ represents the summation of all forms of energy within the system, then for an isolated system:

$$ E_{\text{initial}} = E_{\text{final}} $$

When considering a particle moving exclusively under the influence of conservative forces (such as gravity or ideal spring forces), this general principle simplifies to the conservation of mechanical energy, $E_M$. This quantity is defined as the sum of the kinetic energy ($K$) and the potential energy ($U$):

$$ E_M = K + U = \frac{1}{2}m v^2 + U(y) $$

This powerful formulation allows for sophisticated analysis of system dynamics without requiring the explicit calculation or tracking of every individual force component, provided that all operative forces are conservative.

Worked Example: Consider a mass $m$ attached to an ideal spring with constant $k$, oscillating horizontally from its maximum displacement $A$. 🌸 Orbital Mechanics

Orbital mechanics is the branch of astrodynamics concerned with predicting the motion of celestial bodies and spacecraft under gravitational influence. Fundamentally, it models the interaction between masses governed by Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation and the principles of classical mechanics. For most initial analyses, the system is simplified to a two-body problem: an orbiting object and a central primary body (e.g., a satellite around Earth). The resulting trajectories are not arbitrary; they are always conic sections—ellipses, parabolas, or hyperbolas—determined by the total energy and angular momentum of the system. Key variables include the gravitational parameter $\mu = G(M+m)$, where $G$ is the gravitational constant, and $(M+m)$ is the combined mass of the primary and secondary bodies.

Worked Example: Calculating Circular Orbital Velocity Consider a satellite placed in a perfectly circular orbit around Earth at an altitude $h$. We can determine the necessary orbital velocity ($v$) using the principle of uniform centripetal acceleration balanced by gravitational force. At radius $r = R_{\oplus} + h$, the gravitational force provides the required centripetal force:

$$ F_g = F_c $$ $$ \frac{G M m}{r^2} = \frac{m v^2}{r} $$

Solving for $v$ yields the orbital velocity formula:

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{G M}{r}} $$

If we assume a specific radius $r$, this equation gives the minimum constant speed required to maintain that orbit, assuming no atmospheric drag.
