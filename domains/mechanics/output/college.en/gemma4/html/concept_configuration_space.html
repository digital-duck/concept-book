🍃 Configuration Space

🍃 Configuration Space

The configuration space $\mathcal{C}$ of a mechanical system provides a comprehensive mathematical description of all geometrically possible arrangements—or configurations—of its constituent bodies. Formally, consider a system comprising $N$ rigid bodies (or point masses) moving within an ambient Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^D$. The position of the $i$-th body is defined by a set of coordinates $q_i \in Q_i$, where $Q_i$ represents the unconstrained space of allowed positions for that single body. Assuming these bodies are independent, the full configuration space is initially conceived as the product manifold:

$$ \mathcal{C}_{full} = Q_1 \times Q_2 \times \dots \times Q_N $$

However, real-world systems operate under mutual physical restrictions (e.g., joints limits, non-overlap constraints). These necessary interactions restrict $\mathcal{C}$ to an admissible subset of the full product space. Thus, the true configuration space $\mathcal{C}$ is generally defined as a submanifold of the Cartesian product that satisfies all kinematic and collision avoidance criteria. The dimension and topology of this resulting manifold are fundamental properties that dictate the nature and feasibility of motion for the physical system.

Worked Example: Consider a planar two-link robotic arm, a classic example of a 2-Degrees-of-Freedom (DOF) manipulator. Each link is treated as a rigid body whose position relative to the previous one is governed by an angle. If $\theta_1$ is the absolute angle of the first link and $\theta_2$ is the relative angle between the two links, the configuration space is parameterized by $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$. Since both angles can traverse a full circle, the admissible configurations define a torus:

$$ \mathcal{C} = S^1 \times S^1 \cong \mathbb{T}^2 $$

Where $S^1$ represents the compact manifold of allowed angular ranges $[0, 2\pi)$. This structure accurately describes all unique relative orientations and positions achievable by the two links.
