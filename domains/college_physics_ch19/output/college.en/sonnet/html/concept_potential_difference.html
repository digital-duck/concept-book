🍃 Potential Difference

🍃 Potential Difference

Definition. The electric potential difference between two points A and B in an electric field is defined as the work done per unit positive charge by an external agent in moving a test charge quasi-statically from A to B:

$$ \Delta V = V_B - V_A = -\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{\ell} $$

The SI unit is the volt (V), where $1\ \text{V} = 1\ \text{J/C}$. Equivalently, the work done on a charge $q$ moved through a potential difference $\Delta V$ is:

$$ W = q\,\Delta V $$

Note the sign convention: a positive charge moving from high to low potential loses potential energy and gains kinetic energy; work done by the field is $W_{\text{field}} = -q\,\Delta V$.

Worked Example. A uniform electric field $E = 500\ \text{V/m}$ points in the $+x$ direction. Find the potential difference $V_B - V_A$ between point A at $x_A = 0$ and point B at $x_B = 0.30\ \text{m}$.

$$ V_B - V_A = -\int_0^{0.30} E\,dx = -E\,(x_B - x_A) = -(500)(0.30) = -150\ \text{V} $$

Point B is at lower potential than A. If a proton ($q = 1.6\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}$) moves from A to B, the work done by the field is:

$$ W = q(V_A - V_B) = (1.6\times10^{-19})(150) = 2.4\times10^{-17}\ \text{J} $$

This work appears as kinetic energy gained by the proton, consistent with energy conservation.

Key Theorem (Equipotential Surfaces). For a static electric field, the work done in moving a charge along any path lying entirely on an equipotential surface — a surface on which $V$ is constant — is zero, because $\Delta V = 0$ implies $W = q\,\Delta V = 0$. It follows directly that $\vec{E}$ is always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces, since no work is done by a force acting perpendicular to displacement.

Lab Exercise. Using a dc power supply set to $12\ \text{V}$, two parallel conducting plates separated by $d = 4.0\ \text{cm}$, and a voltmeter, measure the potential at five equally spaced positions between the plates. Plot $V$ versus position, extract the slope, and compare your experimental field magnitude $E = -dV/dx$ with the theoretical value $E = \Delta V/d$. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify two sources of systematic error.
