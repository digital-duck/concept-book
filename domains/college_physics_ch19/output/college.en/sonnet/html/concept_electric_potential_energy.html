🍃 Electric Potential Energy

🍃 Electric Potential Energy

Definition. Electric potential energy is the work done by an external agent against the electric force to assemble a configuration of charges, stored as potential energy in the system. For a pair of point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$, the electric potential energy is

$$ U = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r} $$

where $k_e = 8.99 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N \cdot m^2 / C^2}$ is Coulomb's constant. By convention, $U = 0$ when the charges are infinitely separated. For like-sign charges, $U > 0$ (energy must be supplied to bring them together); for opposite-sign charges, $U < 0$ (the system releases energy as the charges approach). More generally, for a charge $q_0$ moved through a region of electric field $\vec{E}$, the change in potential energy is related to the work done by the field:

$$ \Delta U = -W_{\text{field}} = -\int_A^B q_0 \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} $$

Worked Example. Two protons ($q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \, \mathrm{C}$) are initially separated by $r_i = 2.00 \, \mathrm{nm}$ and are released from rest. Find their common speed when their separation doubles to $r_f = 4.00 \, \mathrm{nm}$.

Using energy conservation ($\Delta K + \Delta U = 0$) and noting each proton gains identical kinetic energy:

$$ \Delta U = U_f - U_i = k_e \frac{q^2}{r_f} - k_e \frac{q^2}{r_i} = k_e q^2 \left(\frac{1}{r_f} - \frac{1}{r_i}\right) $$ $$ \Delta U = (8.99 \times 10^9)(1.60 \times 10^{-19})^2\left(\frac{1}{4.00 \times 10^{-9}} - \frac{1}{2.00 \times 10^{-9}}\right) = -5.75 \times 10^{-20} \, \mathrm{J} $$

Each proton gains $K = 2.88 \times 10^{-20} \, \mathrm{J}$, giving $v = \sqrt{2K/m_p} \approx 5.87 \times 10^3 \, \mathrm{m/s}$.

Key Theorem. The electric force is conservative: the work done moving a charge between two points is path-independent and depends only on the endpoints. Consequently, $\oint q_0 \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} = 0$ for any closed path. This path-independence is precisely what makes electric potential energy a well-defined state function of the charge configuration, analogous to gravitational potential energy in a uniform field.

Lab Exercise. Using a parallel-plate capacitor with known plate separation $d$ and measured voltage $V$, calculate the electric field $E = V/d$. Then compute the potential energy gained by a test charge $q_0$ displaced from one plate to the other and compare to $q_0 V$. Discuss sources of discrepancy arising from fringe fields near the plate edges.
