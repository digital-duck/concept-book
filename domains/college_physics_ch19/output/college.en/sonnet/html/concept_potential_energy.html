🌱 Potential Energy

🌱 Potential Energy

Definition. In classical mechanics, potential energy is the energy stored in a system by virtue of the configuration or position of its constituent parts relative to a force field. For a conservative force $\vec{F}$, the potential energy $U$ is defined through the work–energy relation:

$$ \Delta U = -W = -\int_{\vec{r}_i}^{\vec{r}_f} \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} $$

This definition ensures that when the system moves from configuration $A$ to configuration $B$, the work done by the conservative force equals the decrease in potential energy: $W_{A \to B} = U_A - U_B$. The primary form introduced here is gravitational potential energy $U_g = mgh$, valid near Earth's surface, where $h$ is measured upward from an arbitrary reference level and $g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$.

Worked Example. A 2.0 kg block is released from rest at the top of a frictionless ramp 5.0 m above the ground. Determine the block's speed at the bottom.

Taking the ground as the reference level ($U = 0$ there), the initial state has $K_i = 0$ and $U_i = mgh = (2.0)(9.8)(5.0) = 98\ \text{J}$. At the bottom, $U_f = 0$, so conservation of mechanical energy gives:

$$ K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f \implies 0 + 98 = \tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)v^2 + 0 $$ $$ v = \sqrt{\frac{2(98)}{2.0}} = \sqrt{98} \approx 9.9\ \text{m/s} $$

Key Theorem. For any conservative force, mechanical energy $E_{\text{mech}} = K + U$ is conserved in the absence of non-conservative forces. More precisely, $\frac{dE_{\text{mech}}}{dt} = 0$, which follows directly from the work–energy theorem and the path-independence of work done by conservative forces. This theorem fails when friction or other dissipative forces are present; in such cases the work–energy theorem must account for the additional energy transfer.

Lab Exercise. Set up a low-friction inclined track with a photogate at the bottom to measure speed. Release a block of known mass $m$ from a series of measured heights $h$ and record the speed $v$ at the base of the ramp. Verify the relation $mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ by plotting $v^2$ against $h$ and checking that the slope equals $2g$. Discuss sources of discrepancy, including residual friction and measurement uncertainty in $h$.
