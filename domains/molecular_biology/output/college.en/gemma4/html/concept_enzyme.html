🌱 Enzyme

An enzyme is defined as a biological macromolecule, typically a protein, that acts as a highly specific catalyst within living organisms. Its fundamental function is to accelerate the rate of biochemical reactions by significantly lowering the activation energy ($\Delta G^\ddagger$) required for the reaction to proceed, without being consumed in the process. Enzymes achieve this through binding a specific reactant molecule, known as the substrate (S), at a specialized region called the active site. The interaction between the enzyme (E) and substrate forms an enzyme-substrate complex ($\text{ES}$), which stabilizes the transition state and facilitates bond rearrangement. The specificity of enzymes is dictated by the geometric complementarity and charge interactions between the active site residues and the substrate molecule, allowing them to execute reactions far faster than would occur spontaneously under physiological conditions.

Worked Example: Catalase Action. Consider the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide ($\text{H}_2\text{O}_2$), a reaction that is thermodynamically favorable but kinetically slow at moderate temperatures. The enzyme catalase catalyzes this breakdown into water and oxygen gas:

$$ 2 \text{H}_2\text{O}_2(aq) \xrightarrow{\text{Catalase}} 2 \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) + \text{O}_2(g) $$

The rate of reaction catalyzed by catalase is vastly higher than the uncatalyzed rate. By forming an intermediate complex, the enzyme stabilizes the transition state structure ($\text{TS}^\ddagger$), effectively bypassing a high-energy barrier and allowing the reaction to proceed rapidly at $37^\circ\text{C}$.

Key Theorem: Michaelis–Menten Kinetics. The steady-state assumption is foundational for understanding most enzyme kinetics. The relationship is given by:
