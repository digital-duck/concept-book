🌸 Rainbow

🌸 Rainbow

Definition. A rainbow is an optical phenomenon produced by the dispersion, refraction, and total internal reflection of sunlight within spherical water droplets suspended in the atmosphere. Each droplet acts as a miniature prism: an incident ray refracts upon entering the droplet, undergoes one or more internal reflections, then refracts again upon exiting. Because the index of refraction $n$ of water depends on wavelength (dispersion), different colors exit at slightly different angles. For a primary rainbow (one internal reflection), the angle $\theta$ between the incident ray and the emergent ray satisfies:

$$ \theta = 4r - 2i $$

where $i$ is the angle of incidence at the droplet surface and $r$ is the angle of refraction, related by Snell's law: $n_1 \sin i = n_2 \sin r$. The emergent angle is extremized (the rainbow angle or Descartes angle) when $d\theta/dr = 0$, which determines the precise angle at which light of each wavelength is concentrated most intensely.

Worked Example. For red light in water, $n \approx 1.331$. Applying Snell's law at the air-water interface, $\sin i = 1.331 \sin r$. Setting $d\theta/dr = 4 - 2(dn/dr)^{-1} \cdot \cos i / \cos r = 0$ and solving numerically yields $r \approx 40.4°$ and a rainbow angle of $\theta_{\text{red}} \approx 42.5°$. For violet light ($n \approx 1.343$), the same procedure gives $\theta_{\text{violet}} \approx 40.4°$. Consequently, red appears on the outer arc and violet on the inner arc of the primary rainbow, spanning roughly $2°$ of angular width.

Key Theorem (Descartes' Rainbow Condition). The rainbow angle $\theta_{\min}$ for a primary bow is obtained by solving:

$$ \cos i = \sqrt{\frac{n^2 - 1}{3}} $$

which follows from $d\theta/di = 0$ combined with Snell's law. Substituting back yields $\theta_{\min}$; the rainbow appears as a bright arc at this characteristic angle relative to the antisolar point.

Lab Exercise. Obtain a spherical water-filled flask ($\approx 10\text{ cm}$ diameter) and illuminate it with a narrow white-light beam. Observe the emergent light on a white screen placed at varying angles. Measure the angular positions of red and violet emergent beams, and compare your measured values to the theoretical Descartes angles for $n_{\text{red}} = 1.331$ and $n_{\text{violet}} = 1.343$. Compute the percentage error and discuss how droplet size and shape affect the sharpness of the observed color bands.
