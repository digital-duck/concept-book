🌱 Speed Of Light Vacuum

🌱 Speed Of Light Vacuum

Definition. The speed of light in vacuum, denoted $c$, is the universal physical constant representing the maximum speed at which electromagnetic radiation propagates through free space. Its exact defined value is

$$ c = 299{,}792{,}458 \text{ m/s} \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}. $$

This value is not merely an experimental measurement but a definition: since 1983, the metre itself is defined such that $c$ is exactly $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s. In Maxwell's electromagnetic theory, $c$ emerges from the permittivity $\varepsilon_0$ and permeability $\mu_0$ of free space:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_0 \mu_0}}. $$

Substituting $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12}$ F/m and $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ T·m/A confirms the numerical value. This relation demonstrates that light is an electromagnetic phenomenon and that $c$ is intrinsic to the structure of spacetime, not to any particular medium.

Worked Example. A laser pulse is fired from Earth to the Moon and back. The mean Earth–Moon distance is $d = 3.84 \times 10^8$ m. Find the round-trip travel time.

The total path length is $2d = 7.68 \times 10^8$ m. Using $t = \frac{2d}{c}$:

$$ t = \frac{7.68 \times 10^8 \text{ m}}{3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}} = 2.56 \text{ s}. $$

This result is confirmed experimentally by lunar laser ranging, validating both the constancy and the measured value of $c$.

Key Theorem (Invariance of $c$). Einstein's special relativity postulates that $c$ is the same in all inertial reference frames, regardless of the motion of the source or observer. This invariance, combined with the principle of relativity, leads to time dilation, length contraction, and the mass–energy equivalence $E = mc^2$. The Lorentz transformation replaces the Galilean transformation precisely to preserve this constancy.

Lab Exercise. Using a rotating-mirror apparatus (Foucault method), a light beam is directed at a mirror $L = 8.63$ km away. The mirror must rotate by $\theta = 0.0135°$ during the round-trip travel time to deflect the returning beam by the observed amount. Compute $c$ from these values and estimate the percentage discrepancy from the accepted value, identifying the dominant source of experimental uncertainty.
