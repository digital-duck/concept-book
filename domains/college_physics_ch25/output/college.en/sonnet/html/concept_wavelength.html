🌱 Wavelength

🌱 Wavelength

Definition. The wavelength of a periodic wave is the spatial distance over which the wave's shape repeats exactly once. For a sinusoidal wave traveling through a medium, it is formally defined as the distance between two successive points that are in phase—that is, points sharing identical displacement and velocity at any instant. If the wave displacement is written as

$$ y(x, t) = A \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}x - \omega t + \phi_0\right), $$

then $\lambda$ (lambda) is the wavelength, $A$ is the amplitude, $\omega$ is the angular frequency, and $\phi_0$ is the initial phase. The argument $\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}x$ advances by exactly $2\pi$ when $x$ increases by $\lambda$, confirming the periodicity in space.

Worked Example. A sinusoidal light wave in vacuum has a frequency $f = 6.00 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}$. Find its wavelength.

Using the fundamental wave relation

$$ v = f\lambda, $$

and substituting $v = c = 3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$:

$$ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}}{6.00 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Hz}} = 5.00 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m} = 500\ \text{nm}. $$

This places the wave in the green portion of the visible spectrum, consistent with human color perception near $\sim 500\ \text{nm}$.

Key Theorem. For any traveling wave, wavelength, wave speed, and frequency are related by

$$ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = vT, $$

where $T = 1/f$ is the period. In dispersive media, $v$ depends on frequency, so $\lambda$ changes as the wave enters a new medium even though $f$ remains fixed. This principle underlies refraction: when light passes from air into glass ($v$ decreases), the wavelength shortens while the frequency is preserved by the boundary condition.

Lab Exercise. Set up a ripple tank and drive a straight-wave generator at $f = 10.0\ \text{Hz}$. Measure the distance spanning five complete wave crests and divide by five to obtain $\lambda$. Compare the experimental value with the theoretical prediction $\lambda = v/f$, using a separately measured wave speed. Quantify the percent discrepancy and propose sources of systematic error, such as surface tension effects or edge reflections.
