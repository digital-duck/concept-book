🌱 Conservation Of Energy

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

Definition. The law of conservation of energy states that the total mechanical energy of an isolated system remains constant in time. For a system subject only to conservative forces, the sum of kinetic energy $K$ and potential energy $U$ is invariant:

$$ E_{\text{total}} = K + U = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + U = \text{constant}. $$

When applied to an ideal, incompressible fluid in steady flow, energy conservation per unit volume along a streamline yields:

$$ P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant}, $$

where $P$ is the local pressure, $\rho$ the fluid density, $v$ the flow speed, and $h$ the elevation. This expression follows directly from the work-energy theorem applied to a fluid parcel: the net work done by pressure forces and gravity equals the change in kinetic energy. In the context of static equilibrium and fluid statics (Ch. 12), the kinetic term vanishes and the relation reduces to the familiar hydrostatic pressure law, confirming that conservation of energy subsumes the conditions of fluid rest as a limiting case.

Worked Example. A cylindrical tank of cross-sectional area $A_1 = 0.10\ \text{m}^2$ has a small hole of area $A_2 = 1.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2$ at its base. The water surface is maintained at height $h = 2.0\ \text{m}$ above the hole. Find the exit speed of water at the hole.

Both surfaces are open to atmosphere, so $P_1 = P_2 = P_{\text{atm}}$, which cancel. Since $A_1 \operatorname{gg} A_2$, continuity requires $v_1 \approx 0$. Setting $h_1 - h_2 = h = 2.0\ \text{m}$ and applying energy conservation:

$$ v_2 = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8\ \text{m/s}^2)(2.0\ \text{m})} \approx 6.3\ \text{m/s}. $$

Key Theorem. For any conservative force $\mathbf{F}$, the work-energy theorem gives $\Delta K = -\Delta U$, so $\Delta(K + U) = 0$. This holds for every conservative interaction: gravitational, elastic, and electrostatic. Mechanical energy is not conserved only when non-conservative forces (friction, viscosity) perform net work, converting ordered mechanical energy into disordered thermal energy — but the total energy of the closed system remains unchanged.

Lab Exercise. Fill a large cylindrical container to a measured height $h$, then open a small valve at the base. Using a stopwatch and a graduated cylinder, measure the volumetric flow rate and compute the exit speed experimentally. Compare your result to $v = \sqrt{2gh}$ and calculate the percent discrepancy. Repeat for three different water levels and plot $v^2$ versus $h$; the slope should equal $2g$. Discuss sources of deviation, such as viscosity and the finite ratio $A_2/A_1$.
