🌱 Kinetic Energy

🌱 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is defined as

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

This scalar quantity measures the energy associated with translational motion. In SI units, $m$ is in kilograms and $v$ in meters per second, giving $K$ in joules ($1\,\text{J} = 1\,\text{kg·m}^2\text{/s}^2$). Kinetic energy is always non-negative and is zero only when the object is at rest relative to the chosen inertial frame.

Worked Example. A hockey puck of mass $m = 0.170\,\text{kg}$ is struck and leaves the stick with speed $v = 45.0\,\text{m/s}$. Find its kinetic energy.

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(0.170\,\text{kg})(45.0\,\text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.170)(2025) = 172\,\text{J}. $$

Note that doubling the speed would quadruple the kinetic energy, reflecting the $v^2$ dependence—a fact with important consequences for vehicle stopping distances and collision dynamics.

Key Theorem: Work–Energy Theorem. For a particle acted upon by a net force $\vec{F}_\text{net}$, the net work done on the particle equals the change in its kinetic energy:

$$ W_\text{net} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2. $$

Proof sketch. Applying Newton's second law along the direction of displacement $ds$:

$$ W_\text{net} = \int_{x_i}^{x_f} F_\text{net}\,dx = \int_{x_i}^{x_f} ma\,dx. $$

Using $a\,dx = v\,dv$ (from the chain rule $a = dv/dt$ and $dx = v\,dt$), this becomes

$$ W_\text{net} = \int_{v_i}^{v_f} mv\,dv = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 = \Delta K. \qquad \square $$

This theorem is particularly powerful because it relates scalar quantities, bypassing the vector nature of force and acceleration.

Lab Exercise. Place a dynamics cart of measured mass $m$ on a low-friction track. Use a photogate to record its speed $v_i$ before and $v_f$ after a known hanging mass does work over a measured displacement. Compute $W = mgh$ for the hanging mass and compare it to $\Delta K = \frac{1}{2}m_\text{cart}(v_f^2 - v_i^2)$. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify sources of systematic error, including friction and rotational kinetic energy of the pulley.
