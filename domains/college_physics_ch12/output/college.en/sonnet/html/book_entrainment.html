🌱 Fluid Density

🌱 Fluid Density

Definition. The density of a fluid is defined as the mass per unit volume of the substance:

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} $$

where $\rho$ (Greek rho) has SI units of kilograms per cubic meter ($\text{kg/m}^3$). Density is an intrinsic property of a material under specified thermodynamic conditions; for liquids it is nearly independent of pressure but varies with temperature, while for gases both pressure and temperature govern $\rho$ through the ideal gas law. Water at $4^\circ\text{C}$ has the reference density $\rho_w = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3$; the specific gravity of any substance is the dimensionless ratio $\text{SG} = \rho/\rho_w$.

Worked Example. A rigid container holds $2.50 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3$ (2.50 L) of ethanol. A laboratory balance measures the mass of ethanol as $1.975\ \text{kg}$. Determine the density and specific gravity of the sample.

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{1.975\ \text{kg}}{2.50 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3} = 790\ \text{kg/m}^3 $$ $$ \text{SG} = \frac{790}{1000} = 0.790 $$

Because $\text{SG} < 1$, ethanol is less dense than water and will float on its surface—consistent with everyday observation.

Key Theorem (Continuity of Mass). For an incompressible fluid ($\rho = \text{const}$) flowing through a pipe of cross-sectional area $A$ and flow speed $v$, conservation of mass requires:

$$ \rho A_1 v_1 = \rho A_2 v_2 \implies A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2 $$

This is the equation of continuity. Its proof follows directly from the divergence theorem applied to a fixed control volume: the net mass flux out of any closed surface equals the rate of decrease of mass inside. Density enters as the proportionality constant linking volumetric flow rate $Q = Av$ to mass flow rate $\dot{m} = \rho Q$.

Lab Exercise. Using a graduated cylinder and an analytical balance, measure the mass and volume of three different liquids (water, vegetable oil, and a saturated salt solution). Compute $\rho$ for each, rank them by density, and predict the stable layering order when all three are carefully poured into a single tall beaker. Observe whether the experimental layering matches your prediction and discuss sources of systematic error in your volume measurement.

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

🌱 Conservation Of Energy

Definition. The law of conservation of energy states that the total mechanical energy of an isolated system remains constant in time. For a system subject only to conservative forces, the sum of kinetic energy $K$ and potential energy $U$ is invariant:

$$ E_{\text{total}} = K + U = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + U = \text{constant}. $$

When applied to an ideal, incompressible fluid in steady flow, energy conservation per unit volume along a streamline yields:

$$ P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant}, $$

where $P$ is the local pressure, $\rho$ the fluid density, $v$ the flow speed, and $h$ the elevation. This expression follows directly from the work-energy theorem applied to a fluid parcel: the net work done by pressure forces and gravity equals the change in kinetic energy. In the context of static equilibrium and fluid statics (Ch. 12), the kinetic term vanishes and the relation reduces to the familiar hydrostatic pressure law, confirming that conservation of energy subsumes the conditions of fluid rest as a limiting case.

Worked Example. A cylindrical tank of cross-sectional area $A_1 = 0.10\ \text{m}^2$ has a small hole of area $A_2 = 1.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2$ at its base. The water surface is maintained at height $h = 2.0\ \text{m}$ above the hole. Find the exit speed of water at the hole.

Both surfaces are open to atmosphere, so $P_1 = P_2 = P_{\text{atm}}$, which cancel. Since $A_1 \operatorname{gg} A_2$, continuity requires $v_1 \approx 0$. Setting $h_1 - h_2 = h = 2.0\ \text{m}$ and applying energy conservation:

$$ v_2 = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8\ \text{m/s}^2)(2.0\ \text{m})} \approx 6.3\ \text{m/s}. $$

Key Theorem. For any conservative force $\mathbf{F}$, the work-energy theorem gives $\Delta K = -\Delta U$, so $\Delta(K + U) = 0$. This holds for every conservative interaction: gravitational, elastic, and electrostatic. Mechanical energy is not conserved only when non-conservative forces (friction, viscosity) perform net work, converting ordered mechanical energy into disordered thermal energy — but the total energy of the closed system remains unchanged.

Lab Exercise. Fill a large cylindrical container to a measured height $h$, then open a small valve at the base. Using a stopwatch and a graduated cylinder, measure the volumetric flow rate and compute the exit speed experimentally. Compare your result to $v = \sqrt{2gh}$ and calculate the percent discrepancy. Repeat for three different water levels and plot $v^2$ versus $h$; the slope should equal $2g$. Discuss sources of deviation, such as viscosity and the finite ratio $A_2/A_1$.

🌱 Gravitational Potential Energy

🌱 Gravitational Potential Energy

Definition. Gravitational potential energy is the energy stored in a system due to the relative position of objects within a gravitational field. For an object of mass $m$ located at height $h$ above a chosen reference level near Earth's surface, where the gravitational field strength $g \approx 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$ is approximately uniform, the gravitational potential energy is defined as

$$ U_g = mgh, $$

where $h$ is measured from the reference point (typically the ground or lowest point of motion). This quantity is a scalar and may be positive, negative, or zero depending on the chosen reference. Because only differences in potential energy are physically meaningful, the choice of reference level is arbitrary but must be held fixed throughout a problem.

Worked Example. A 2.0 kg textbook is lifted from a desk 0.80 m above the floor to a shelf 2.3 m above the floor. Taking the floor as the reference level, the initial potential energy is $U_i = (2.0)(9.8)(0.80) = 15.7\ \text{J}$ and the final potential energy is $U_f = (2.0)(9.8)(2.3) = 45.1\ \text{J}$. The change in gravitational potential energy is

$$ \Delta U_g = U_f - U_i = 45.1 - 15.7 = 29.4\ \text{J}. $$

This equals the work done by an external agent against gravity during the lift, consistent with $W_{\text{ext}} = \Delta U_g$.

Key Theorem — Conservation of Mechanical Energy. When only conservative forces (including gravity) act on a system, the total mechanical energy $E = K + U_g$ is conserved:

$$ K_i + U_{g,i} = K_f + U_{g,f} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \tfrac{1}{2}mv_i^2 + mgh_i = \tfrac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + mgh_f. $$

This result is a direct consequence of the work–energy theorem combined with the definition of a conservative force, for which the work done is path-independent and equals the negative change in potential energy: $W_g = -\Delta U_g$.

Lab Exercise. Release a 0.50 kg ball from rest at a measured height $h = 1.2\ \text{m}$ above a motion sensor. Record the speed $v_f$ at the lowest point and verify that $v_f \approx \sqrt{2gh}$. Repeat for three different release heights and plot $v_f^2$ versus $h$; the slope should equal $2g$. Discuss sources of discrepancy such as air resistance and measurement uncertainty.

🍃 Incompressible Fluid

🍃 Incompressible Fluid

Definition. A fluid is said to be incompressible if its density $\rho$ remains constant throughout the flow, regardless of pressure variations. For a steady, incompressible flow, the continuity equation reduces to the divergence-free condition

$$ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0, $$

where $\mathbf{v}$ is the local velocity field. In one-dimensional pipe flow, this becomes the familiar equation of continuity:

$$ A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2, $$

where $A$ is the cross-sectional area and $v$ is the flow speed at any two points along a streamtube. This states that the volumetric flow rate $Q = Av$ is conserved: because the fluid cannot compress, whatever volume per unit time enters a section must exit it.

Worked Example. Water flows through a horizontal pipe that narrows from a diameter of $d_1 = 8\,\text{cm}$ to $d_2 = 4\,\text{cm}$. The inlet speed is $v_1 = 2\,\text{m/s}$. Find the outlet speed $v_2$.

The cross-sectional areas are $A_1 = \pi(0.04)^2 = 5.03 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2$ and $A_2 = \pi(0.02)^2 = 1.26 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2$. Applying the continuity equation:

$$ v_2 = \frac{A_1}{A_2}\,v_1 = \frac{5.03 \times 10^{-3}}{1.26 \times 10^{-3}} \times 2\,\text{m/s} = 8\,\text{m/s}. $$

Halving the diameter quadruples the speed, consistent with $v \propto 1/A \propto 1/d^2$.

Key Theorem. For steady, incompressible flow along a streamline, Bernoulli's equation holds:

$$ p + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant}, $$

where $p$ is the static pressure, $\rho$ the (constant) fluid density, $v$ the local speed, and $h$ the elevation. This is a direct consequence of energy conservation combined with the incompressibility constraint. Where the pipe narrows and $v$ increases, $p$ must decrease — a result that is non-intuitive but follows directly from the equation. Bernoulli's equation fails when viscosity or turbulence become significant.

Lab Exercise. Set up a Venturi meter connected to a water supply. Measure the static pressures $p_1$ and $p_2$ at the wide and narrow sections using manometer tubes. Record the diameters $d_1$ and $d_2$. Use the continuity equation to predict $v_2/v_1$, then apply Bernoulli's equation to compute the theoretical pressure difference $\Delta p = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(v_2^2 - v_1^2)$. Compare your measured $\Delta p$ with the prediction and quantify the percentage discrepancy, attributing deviations to viscous losses or measurement uncertainty.

🌱 Kinetic Energy

🌱 Kinetic Energy

Definition. The kinetic energy of a particle of mass $m$ moving with speed $v$ is defined as

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2. $$

This scalar quantity measures the energy associated with translational motion. In SI units, $m$ is in kilograms and $v$ in meters per second, giving $K$ in joules ($1\,\text{J} = 1\,\text{kg·m}^2\text{/s}^2$). Kinetic energy is always non-negative and is zero only when the object is at rest relative to the chosen inertial frame.

Worked Example. A hockey puck of mass $m = 0.170\,\text{kg}$ is struck and leaves the stick with speed $v = 45.0\,\text{m/s}$. Find its kinetic energy.

$$ K = \frac{1}{2}(0.170\,\text{kg})(45.0\,\text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.170)(2025) = 172\,\text{J}. $$

Note that doubling the speed would quadruple the kinetic energy, reflecting the $v^2$ dependence—a fact with important consequences for vehicle stopping distances and collision dynamics.

Key Theorem: Work–Energy Theorem. For a particle acted upon by a net force $\vec{F}_\text{net}$, the net work done on the particle equals the change in its kinetic energy:

$$ W_\text{net} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2. $$

Proof sketch. Applying Newton's second law along the direction of displacement $ds$:

$$ W_\text{net} = \int_{x_i}^{x_f} F_\text{net}\,dx = \int_{x_i}^{x_f} ma\,dx. $$

Using $a\,dx = v\,dv$ (from the chain rule $a = dv/dt$ and $dx = v\,dt$), this becomes

$$ W_\text{net} = \int_{v_i}^{v_f} mv\,dv = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 = \Delta K. \qquad \square $$

This theorem is particularly powerful because it relates scalar quantities, bypassing the vector nature of force and acceleration.

Lab Exercise. Place a dynamics cart of measured mass $m$ on a low-friction track. Use a photogate to record its speed $v_i$ before and $v_f$ after a known hanging mass does work over a measured displacement. Compute $W = mgh$ for the hanging mass and compare it to $\Delta K = \frac{1}{2}m_\text{cart}(v_f^2 - v_i^2)$. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify sources of systematic error, including friction and rotational kinetic energy of the pulley.

🌱 Pressure

🌱 Pressure

Definition. Pressure is defined as the normal force exerted per unit area on a surface. For a uniform force $F$ applied perpendicularly over an area $A$, the pressure is

$$ P = \frac{F}{A}. $$

The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), where $1\,\text{Pa} = 1\,\text{N/m}^2$. Pressure is a scalar quantity; it acts equally in all directions at a point within a static fluid. For a fluid at rest, the pressure at depth $h$ below the surface is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h, $$

where $P_0$ is the pressure at the surface, $\rho$ is the fluid density, and $g$ is the gravitational acceleration. This relationship shows that pressure increases linearly with depth and depends on the fluid's density but not on the total volume of fluid.

Worked Example. A diver descends to a depth of $h = 30\,\text{m}$ in seawater ($\rho = 1025\,\text{kg/m}^3$). The surface pressure is atmospheric, $P_0 = 1.013 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. The absolute pressure at that depth is

$$ P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.013 \times 10^5 + (1025)(9.81)(30). $$

Computing the second term: $(1025)(9.81)(30) = 3.015 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$. Therefore

$$ P = 1.013 \times 10^5 + 3.015 \times 10^5 = 4.028 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa} \approx 4.0\,\text{atm}. $$

The gauge pressure, defined as the pressure in excess of atmospheric, is $P_\text{gauge} = \rho g h \approx 3.0 \times 10^5\,\text{Pa}$.

Key Theorem — Pascal's Principle. A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. Formally, if the applied pressure changes by $\Delta P$, then every location in the fluid experiences the same increment $\Delta P$. This underpins hydraulic systems: a small force applied over a small piston area produces a large force over a large piston area, since $F_1/A_1 = F_2/A_2$.

Lab Exercise. Fill a tall graduated cylinder with water and attach pressure sensors at depths of $10\,\text{cm}$, $20\,\text{cm}$, and $30\,\text{cm}$. Record the gauge pressure at each depth, plot $P_\text{gauge}$ versus $h$, and determine the slope. Compare the experimental slope to the theoretical value $\rho g$ using the known density of water. Quantify any discrepancy and identify likely sources of systematic error.

🍃 Bernoullis Equation

🍃 Bernoulli's Equation

Definition. Bernoulli's equation is a statement of energy conservation applied to steady, incompressible, non-viscous flow along a streamline. For a fluid of density $\rho$ moving with speed $v$ at height $y$ and pressure $P$, the equation states:

$$ P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g y = \text{constant} $$

Equivalently, between any two points along a streamline:

$$ P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g y_1 = P_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g y_2 $$

The three terms represent, respectively, the static pressure energy per unit volume, the kinetic energy per unit volume, and the gravitational potential energy per unit volume. The equation is derived by applying the work-energy theorem to a fluid parcel: the net work done by pressure forces equals the change in kinetic and potential energy of that parcel.

Proof sketch. Consider a fluid element of volume $\Delta V$ moving from point 1 to point 2. The pressure force at the inlet does positive work $P_1 \Delta V$, while the pressure at the outlet does negative work $-P_2 \Delta V$. By the work-energy theorem, the net work equals the change in mechanical energy:

$$ P_1 \Delta V - P_2 \Delta V = \Delta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\rho \Delta V\, v^2\right) + \Delta(\rho \Delta V\, g y) $$

Dividing through by $\Delta V$ and rearranging yields Bernoulli's equation.

Worked example. Water flows through a horizontal pipe that narrows from a cross-sectional area $A_1 = 0.04\ \text{m}^2$ to $A_2 = 0.01\ \text{m}^2$. The inlet speed is $v_1 = 2\ \text{m/s}$ and the inlet gauge pressure is $P_1 = 3.0 \times 10^4\ \text{Pa}$. Find $P_2$.

By continuity, $v_2 = v_1 A_1/A_2 = 2 \times 4 = 8\ \text{m/s}$. Since $y_1 = y_2$:

$$ P_2 = P_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(v_1^2 - v_2^2) = 3.0\times10^4 + \tfrac{1}{2}(1000)(4 - 64) = 3.0\times10^4 - 3.0\times10^4 = 0\ \text{Pa} $$

The pressure drops to zero gauge pressure at the constriction, illustrating the inverse relationship between speed and pressure.

Key theorem. Bernoulli's equation subsumes free-fall kinematics as a special case. For a tank open to the atmosphere with a small orifice at depth $h$ below the surface, setting $P_1 = P_2 = P_\text{atm}$ and $v_1 \approx 0$ (large reservoir) reduces the equation to $\rho g h = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2$, giving the efflux speed $v = \sqrt{2gh}$—identical to the speed acquired by a body falling freely through height $h$.

Lab exercise. Connect two manometer taps to a converging–diverging nozzle carrying water at a measured flow rate. Record $P$, $v$, and $y$ at three cross-sections and verify that $P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g y$ remains constant to within experimental uncertainty.

🍃 Bernoullis Principle

🍃 Bernoulli's Principle

Definition. Bernoulli's Principle is a statement of energy conservation for steady, incompressible, inviscid fluid flow along a streamline — an imaginary curve everywhere tangent to the local velocity field, tracing the path a fluid element follows. For any two points on the same streamline, the sum of pressure energy per unit volume, kinetic energy per unit volume, and gravitational potential energy per unit volume is conserved:

$$ P_1 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g h_1 = P_2 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g h_2 $$

where $P$ is static pressure, $\rho$ is fluid density, $v$ is flow speed, $g$ is gravitational acceleration, and $h$ is elevation above a chosen reference. The principle encodes a fundamental trade-off: where fluid speed increases along a streamline, pressure must decrease, and vice versa.

Worked Example. Water flows horizontally through a pipe that narrows from $A_1 = 0.050\ \text{m}^2$ to $A_2 = 0.010\ \text{m}^2$. The inlet speed is $v_1 = 2.0\ \text{m/s}$ and inlet pressure is $P_1 = 1.5 \times 10^5\ \text{Pa}$. Find $P_2$ at the narrow section.

By the continuity equation ($A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2$):

$$ v_2 = \frac{A_1}{A_2}v_1 = \frac{0.050}{0.010}(2.0) = 10.0\ \text{m/s} $$

Since the pipe is horizontal ($h_1 = h_2$), Bernoulli's equation reduces to:

$$ P_2 = P_1 + \frac{1}{2}\rho(v_1^2 - v_2^2) = 1.5\times10^5 + \frac{1}{2}(1000)(4.0 - 100.0) $$ $$ P_2 = 1.5\times10^5 - 4.8\times10^4 \approx 1.02\times10^5\ \text{Pa} $$

The pressure drops substantially as speed increases — a direct manifestation of Bernoulli's Principle.

Key Theorem. Bernoulli's equation is derived by applying the work-energy theorem to a fluid parcel of mass $dm = \rho\,dV$ moving along a streamline. The net work done by pressure forces and gravity equals the change in kinetic energy, yielding the Bernoulli relation as an exact first integral of Euler's equation under the assumptions of steady, inviscid, incompressible flow.

Lab Exercise. Connect two vertical manometer tubes to the wide and narrow sections of a Venturi meter carrying water at a measured volumetric flow rate $Q$. Record manometer heights $h_1$ and $h_2$, compute the theoretical pressure difference $\Delta P = \frac{1}{2}\rho(v_2^2 - v_1^2)$ using the device's cross-sectional areas, and compare with the measured $\Delta P = \rho g \Delta h$. Quantify the percent discrepancy and identify sources of deviation from ideal behavior, such as viscous losses or flow separation from the streamline model.

🌸 Entrainment

🌸 Entrainment

Definition. Entrainment is the phenomenon in which a driven oscillating system is forced to synchronize its frequency with that of an external periodic driving force, even when the driving frequency differs from the system's natural resonant frequency. When a damped oscillator is subjected to a periodic external force of angular frequency $\omega_d$, the steady-state response oscillates at $\omega_d$ rather than at the system's natural frequency $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$. This frequency capture occurs because damping continuously dissipates energy stored in free oscillations, leaving only the driven response at $\omega_d$ to persist in steady state.

The amplitude of the entrained oscillation is given by:

$$ A = \frac{F_0/m}{\sqrt{(\omega_0^2 - \omega_d^2)^2 + (b\omega_d/m)^2}} $$

where $F_0$ is the magnitude of the driving force, $b$ is the damping coefficient, and $m$ is the mass. As $\omega_d \to \omega_0$, the denominator reaches its minimum, and $A$ reaches its maximum — the condition of resonance. Entrainment persists for all driving frequencies, but maximum energy transfer occurs at resonance.

Worked Example. A mass-spring system has $m = 0.50\,\text{kg}$, $k = 50\,\text{N/m}$, and $b = 1.0\,\text{kg/s}$. It is driven by $F(t) = 4.0\cos(8.0t)\,\text{N}$, so $\omega_d = 8.0\,\text{rad/s}$. The natural frequency is $\omega_0 = \sqrt{50/0.50} = 10.0\,\text{rad/s}$. Substituting:

$$ A = \frac{4.0/0.50}{\sqrt{(100 - 64)^2 + (1.0 \cdot 8.0/0.50)^2}} = \frac{8.0}{\sqrt{1296 + 256}} = \frac{8.0}{\sqrt{1552}} \approx 0.203\,\text{m} $$

The system oscillates at exactly $8.0\,\text{rad/s}$, entrained to the driver, not at its natural $10.0\,\text{rad/s}$.

Key Theorem. In any linearly damped driven oscillator, the transient (homogeneous) solution decays exponentially as $e^{-bt/2m}$, leaving only the particular solution oscillating at $\omega_d$. Entrainment is therefore mathematically guaranteed for all $t \operatorname{gg} 2m/b$.

Lab Exercise. Mount a spring-mass system on a motorized shaker with adjustable frequency. Starting well below $\omega_0$, slowly increase $\omega_d$ while recording steady-state amplitude and phase lag. Plot $A$ versus $\omega_d$ and identify the resonance peak. Measure the phase difference between driving force and displacement; confirm it approaches $\pi/2$ exactly at resonance and $\pi$ for $\omega_d \operatorname{gg} \omega_0$.

Payoff 🎯

What Entrainment Achieves

Entrainment is the process by which a moving fluid draws surrounding fluid—or suspended matter—into its flow, accelerating it from rest to a common velocity through momentum transfer rather than direct pressure forcing. Having studied hydrostatics, continuity, Bernoulli's equation, viscosity, and turbulence throughout Chapter 12, the student now arrives at entrainment as the phenomenon that unifies them: it is the observable signature of every principle encountered so far, operating simultaneously in a single physical event.

Formally, entrainment occurs when a high-velocity jet or stream creates a low-pressure region (via Bernoulli's principle, $P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 = \text{const}$) that draws ambient fluid inward, whereupon viscous and turbulent shear stresses transfer momentum outward from the jet core. The entrained mass flux $\dot{m}_e$ grows with downstream distance $x$ roughly as $\dot{m}_e \propto x$, a result derivable from self-similar solutions to the turbulent jet equations.

Worked Example

Consider a circular air jet of diameter $d = 2\,\text{cm}$ issuing at $v_0 = 30\,\text{m/s}$. At a downstream distance $x = 10d = 0.2\,\text{m}$, the centerline velocity decays as $v_c \approx 6.3\, v_0 (d/x)$, giving $v_c \approx 9.5\,\text{m/s}$. The jet half-width grows as $r_{1/2} \approx 0.1\, x = 0.02\,\text{m}$. The volumetric flow rate at this station, $Q(x) = \int_0^\infty v(r)\,2\pi r\,dr$, exceeds the exit flow $Q_0 = \pi(d/2)^2 v_0 \approx 9.4\times10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3/\text{s}$ by a factor of approximately $0.32\,(x/d)$, confirming that roughly three times more air is moving at $x = 10d$ than was originally supplied—pure entrainment.

Key Theorem

For a turbulent free jet in an unbounded quiescent medium, conservation of streamwise momentum $J = \rho \int v^2 \, dA = \text{const}$ combined with self-similarity yields

$$ Q(x) = Q_0\left(1 + \alpha\frac{x}{d}\right), $$

where $\alpha \approx 0.32$ is the entrainment coefficient, an empirical constant encoding all turbulent mixing physics.

Lab Exercise

Fill a large aquarium with water seeded with neutrally buoyant tracer particles and inject a thin dye-colored jet horizontally at one wall. Photograph the flow at $x = 5d$, $10d$, and $20d$, measure the dyed region width, and plot $Q(x)/Q_0$ versus $x/d$. Compare your slope to the theoretical $\alpha$. Consider next how this same mechanism governs the aspiration action in a medical nebulizer—an application worth pursuing in depth, where the quantitative entrainment model you have just verified determines drug-particle delivery efficiency with direct clinical consequences.
