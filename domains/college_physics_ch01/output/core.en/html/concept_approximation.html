🌸 Approximation

🌸 Approximation

When a GPS app estimates your arrival time as "23 minutes," it isn't computing the exact path of every photon reflecting off road signs. It is deliberately trading precision for speed, accepting a small error in exchange for an answer you can act on. This trade-off is the essence of approximation — and it sits at the heart of nearly every computational and analytical technique in modern mathematics.

Definition. Given a quantity $x$ and an estimate $\hat{x}$, the *absolute error* is $|x - \hat{x}|$ and the *relative error* is $\dfrac{|x - \hat{x}|}{|x|}$ (assuming $x \neq 0$). We say $\hat{x}$ *approximates* $x$ to $k$ decimal places if $|x - \hat{x}| < 0.5 \times 10^{-k}$.

Worked example. Consider $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$. The true value begins $1.41421356\ldots$, so

$$|{\sqrt{2} - 1.414}| = |1.41421\ldots - 1.414| \approx 0.00021.$$

Since $0.00021 < 0.5 \times 10^{-2} = 0.005$, the estimate is accurate to 2 decimal places. The relative error is $\dfrac{0.00021}{1.41421} \approx 0.015\%$ — tiny enough for most engineering contexts.

Why this works. The decimal expansion of $\sqrt{2}$ is infinite and non-repeating, so we can never write it exactly with finitely many digits. Truncating or rounding the expansion at position $k$ introduces an error bounded by $5 \times 10^{-(k+1)}$, which shrinks geometrically with each additional digit. This geometric decay guarantees that more digits always yield a closer estimate — a fact formalized in the theory of *Cauchy sequences*.

Practice problem. The number $\pi \approx 3.14159265\ldots$. The approximation $\tfrac{22}{7} \approx 3.142857\ldots$ is classical.

  • Compute the absolute and relative errors of $\tfrac{22}{7}$ as an approximation of $\pi$.
  • How many decimal places of accuracy does $\tfrac{22}{7}$ achieve?
