🌱 Physical Quantity

🌱 Physical Quantity

When a nurse checks your temperature and says "38.5 degrees Celsius," she is not just saying a number — she is communicating a measurement with a unit. Strip away either piece and the statement becomes meaningless: "38.5" alone tells us nothing, and "Celsius" alone tells us nothing. This pairing of number and unit is the foundation of all quantitative science.

Definition. A physical quantity is an attribute of a physical phenomenon that can be expressed as the product of a numerical value and a unit of measurement:

$$Q = \{Q\} \cdot [Q]$$

where $\{Q\}$ denotes the numerical value and $[Q]$ denotes the unit. Two quantities are equal if and only if both their numerical values agree and their units are compatible.

Worked example. Suppose a car travels a distance $d = 90 \text{ km}$ in a time $t = 1.5 \text{ h}$. Its average speed is:

$$v = \frac{d}{t} = \frac{90 \text{ km}}{1.5 \text{ h}} = 60 \;\frac{\text{km}}{\text{h}}$$

Notice that the units divide just like numbers: $\text{km} / \text{h}$ is itself a valid unit. Carrying units through every step of a calculation is the primary tool for catching arithmetic errors in physics.

Why it works. Physical laws are relationships between quantities, not bare numbers. Because any valid law must hold regardless of which unit system we choose (SI, CGS, imperial), both sides of every equation must carry the same units. This requirement turns unit tracking from bookkeeping into a consistency check: if the units on the left and right don't match, the equation cannot be correct.

Practice problem. A cyclist rides $d = 15 \text{ km}$ at a constant speed of $v = 25 \;\text{km/h}$. Write the time $t$ as a physical quantity with appropriate units and verify that the units on both sides of $t = d/v$ are consistent.

🍃 Accuracy

🍃 Accuracy

When a doctor reads a diagnostic test, the first question is simple: how often does it get the right answer? This intuition — the fraction of correct predictions out of all predictions made — is exactly what accuracy captures, and it is the most natural starting point for evaluating any classifier.

Definition. Given a dataset of $n$ labeled examples $\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ and a model that produces predictions $\hat{y}_i$, accuracy is defined as

$$\text{Accuracy} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{1}[\hat{y}_i = y_i]$$

where $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator function, equal to $1$ when its argument is true and $0$ otherwise. Equivalently, accuracy is the ratio of correctly classified examples to total examples.

Worked example. Suppose a spam filter is tested on 100 emails: 60 are legitimate and 40 are spam. The filter correctly identifies 55 of the 60 legitimate emails and 36 of the 40 spam emails. The total number of correct predictions is $55 + 36 = 91$, so

$$\text{Accuracy} = \frac{91}{100} = 0.91 = 91\%.$$

Why it works — and when it doesn't. Accuracy is an unweighted average over all classes, so it is sensitive to class imbalance. If 95 of those 100 emails were legitimate, a model that labels everything as legitimate would achieve $95\%$ accuracy while catching zero spam. Formally, when $P(y = 0) \operatorname{gg} P(y = 1)$, the majority-class baseline already pushes accuracy close to $1$, masking failures on the minority class. This is why accuracy is most trustworthy when classes are roughly balanced.

Practice problem. A classifier is tested on 200 samples: 120 positives and 80 negatives. It predicts 110 positives correctly and 60 negatives correctly. Compute the accuracy. Then ask: would a model that always predicts "positive" do better or worse?

Payoff 🎯

Every measurement you have ever taken carries a silent question: how close is this to the truth? A thermometer reads 98.9 °F, a model predicts "spam," a numerical solver returns 3.14159 — in each case, the real question is not what the answer is, but how much you can trust it. That question is answered by accuracy.

Definition. Given a true value $y^*$ and an approximation $\hat{y}$, accuracy quantifies how well $\hat{y}$ represents $y^*$. In classification it is the fraction of correct predictions:

$$\text{accuracy} = \frac{\text{number of correct predictions}}{\text{total predictions}}.$$

In numerical analysis it appears as absolute or relative error: $|y^* - \hat{y}|$ or $\dfrac{|y^* - \hat{y}|}{|y^*|}$. Both forms measure the same underlying idea — deviation from ground truth.

Worked example. A classifier labels 90 of 100 emails correctly. Its accuracy is $90/100 = 0.90$. If instead you approximate $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$, the relative error is $\dfrac{|1.41421\ldots - 1.414|}{1.41421\ldots} \approx 0.015\%$. Two domains, one concept.

Why it matters. Accuracy is the natural endpoint because every earlier concept in this book — sets, functions, limits, probability — was building toward the ability to make a justified claim about closeness. Without a precise error measure, "close enough" is opinion. With it, you can set thresholds, compare methods, and guarantee behavior. Accuracy transforms intuition into a contract.

Practice problem. A weather model predicts rain on 40 of 50 rainy days and incorrectly predicts rain on 10 of 50 clear days. Compute the overall accuracy over the 100-day window. Then ask: is accuracy alone sufficient to evaluate this model? What might it hide?
