🌱 Speed Of Light

🌱 Speed Of Light

Definition. The speed of light in a vacuum, denoted $c$, is a universal physical constant defined as exactly $c = 299{,}792{,}458 \ \text{m/s}$, commonly approximated as $c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \ \text{m/s}$. This value emerges from Maxwell's electromagnetic theory, which predicts that all electromagnetic waves propagate through vacuum at the speed

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}} $$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \ \text{T·m/A}$ is the permeability of free space and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \ \text{C}^2/\text{N·m}^2$ is the permittivity of free space. When light travels through a medium of refractive index $n > 1$, its phase velocity is reduced to $v = c/n$.

Worked Example. A pulse of light travels through a glass fiber with refractive index $n = 1.50$ over a distance of $d = 600 \ \text{km}$. Determine the travel time and compare it to propagation through vacuum.

In vacuum: $t_{\text{vac}} = d/c = (6.00 \times 10^5 \ \text{m}) / (3.00 \times 10^8 \ \text{m/s}) = 2.00 \times 10^{-3} \ \text{s} = 2.00 \ \text{ms}$.

In glass fiber: $v = c/n = (3.00 \times 10^8)/1.50 = 2.00 \times 10^8 \ \text{m/s}$, so $t_{\text{glass}} = d/v = (6.00 \times 10^5)/(2.00 \times 10^8) = 3.00 \ \text{ms}$.

The signal is delayed by $1.00 \ \text{ms}$ relative to free-space propagation — a critical consideration in fiber-optic communications.

Key Theorem (Constancy of $c$). Einstein's special theory of relativity postulates that $c$ is the same in all inertial reference frames, independent of the motion of the source or observer. This invariance, confirmed by the Michelson–Morley experiment's null result, implies that no material object or signal can exceed $c$, and leads directly to time dilation ($\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_0$) and length contraction ($L = L_0/\gamma$), where $\gamma = (1 - v^2/c^2)^{-1/2}$.

🍃 Second Postulate

🍃 Second Postulate

Definition. Einstein's second postulate of special relativity states that the speed of light in a vacuum, denoted $c$, is the same in all inertial reference frames, regardless of the motion of the light source or the observer. Formally, for any two inertial frames $S$ and $S'$ in relative motion, if a light signal is measured to travel at speed $c \approx 2.998 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}$ in $S$, then it is also measured to travel at the same speed $c$ in $S'$. This postulate is in direct conflict with the classical Galilean velocity addition formula $u' = u - v$, which would predict different measured speeds depending on the observer's motion.

Worked Example. Suppose a rocket moves at $v = 0.8c$ relative to Earth and fires a laser pulse forward. By classical mechanics, an Earth observer would expect the pulse to travel at $c + 0.8c = 1.8c$. However, the second postulate asserts this is incorrect. Applying the relativistic velocity addition formula,

$$ u' = \frac{u + v}{1 + \frac{uv}{c^2}}, $$

with $u = c$ (the laser speed in the rocket frame) and $v = 0.8c$:

$$ u' = \frac{c + 0.8c}{1 + \frac{c \cdot 0.8c}{c^2}} = \frac{1.8c}{1 + 0.8} = \frac{1.8c}{1.8} = c. $$

The Earth observer measures the pulse moving at exactly $c$, confirming the postulate.

Key Theorem. The invariance of $c$ demands that the Lorentz transformation replace the Galilean transformation when relating coordinates between inertial frames. For motion along the $x$-axis, the time transformation is

$$ t' = \gamma\!\left(t - \frac{vx}{c^2}\right), \quad \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}, $$

which encodes the phenomenon of time dilation and ensures that light speed is preserved across all inertial frames.

🌸 Michelson Morley Experiment

🌸 Michelson Morley Experiment

Definition. The Michelson–Morley experiment (1887) was a precise optical interferometry measurement designed to detect Earth's motion through the hypothetical luminiferous ether — the medium then believed to transmit electromagnetic waves. Albert Michelson and Edward Morley used a half-silvered beam splitter to divide a coherent light beam into two perpendicular arms of equal length $L$. After reflection from mirrors at each arm's end, the beams recombined and interfered. If the ether existed and Earth moved through it at velocity $v$, the two light paths would require different transit times, producing a measurable fringe shift.

Worked Example. Consider the expected time difference. A beam traveling parallel to Earth's motion (velocity $v$ relative to ether, speed of light $c$) takes time

$$ t_{\parallel} = \frac{L}{c - v} + \frac{L}{c + v} = \frac{2Lc}{c^2 - v^2} = \frac{2L/c}{1 - v^2/c^2}. $$

The transverse beam, traveling perpendicular to $v$, must angle slightly into the ether wind; its effective speed across the arm is $\sqrt{c^2 - v^2}$, giving

$$ t_{\perp} = \frac{2L}{\sqrt{c^2 - v^2}} = \frac{2L/c}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}. $$

The time difference is $\Delta t = t_{\parallel} - t_{\perp}$. Using Earth's orbital speed $v \approx 3 \times 10^4\ \text{m/s}$ and $c = 3 \times 10^8\ \text{m/s}$, so $\beta = v/c \approx 10^{-4}$. Expanding to second order in $\beta$:

$$ \Delta t \approx \frac{L}{c}\beta^2 \approx \frac{11\ \text{m}}{3\times10^8\ \text{m/s}} \times 10^{-8} \approx 3.7 \times 10^{-16}\ \text{s}. $$

The predicted fringe shift is $\Delta\phi = c\,\Delta t/\lambda \approx 0.4$ fringe for $\lambda = 590\ \text{nm}$. Michelson and Morley observed a shift of less than $0.01$ fringe — effectively null.

Key Theorem. The null result implies that the speed of light is independent of the observer's velocity relative to any inertial frame. This invariance, $c = \text{const}$, became the second postulate of Einstein's special theory of relativity (1905), making the ether concept superfluous.

Payoff 🎯

The Michelson-Morley experiment stands as one of the most consequential null results in the history of science. Conducted in 1887 by Albert Michelson and Edward Morley, its purpose was to detect the relative motion of Earth through the hypothetical luminiferous aether — the medium then believed necessary for the propagation of electromagnetic waves. Using a precision interferometer, the apparatus split a coherent light beam along two perpendicular paths of equal length $L$, then recombined them to observe interference fringes. If Earth moved through the aether at velocity $v$, the travel-time difference between the two arms would produce a fringe shift of magnitude

$$ \Delta = \frac{2L}{\lambda} \cdot \frac{v^2}{c^2}, $$

where $\lambda$ is the wavelength of light and $c$ is its speed. The apparatus was sensitive enough to detect fringe shifts as small as $0.01$ fringe. The measured shift was effectively zero — far below even the most conservative theoretical prediction.

Worked Example. With arm length $L = 11\,\text{m}$, $v = 3\times10^4\,\text{m/s}$ (Earth's orbital speed), $c = 3\times10^8\,\text{m/s}$, and $\lambda = 590\,\text{nm}$:

$$ \Delta = \frac{2(11)}{590\times10^{-9}} \cdot \frac{(3\times10^4)^2}{(3\times10^8)^2} \approx 0.37 \text{ fringes}. $$

The experiment predicted a clearly detectable shift — yet none appeared.

Key Theorem (Invariance of $c$). The null result forced acceptance of the postulate that the speed of light in vacuum is constant for all inertial observers, independent of the motion of the source or detector. This principle, $c = \text{const}$, is the foundation of Einstein's Special Theory of Relativity (1905), from which the Lorentz transformation, time dilation $\Delta t' = \gamma\,\Delta t$, and length contraction $L' = L/\gamma$ follow directly.

The experiment thus does not merely close a chapter — it opens an entirely new theoretical universe. It invalidates classical mechanics as a complete framework and establishes relativistic physics as the correct description of nature at high velocities. Every modern technology dependent on precise timing — GPS satellites, particle accelerators, and atomic clocks — implicitly relies on the relativistic corrections that the Michelson-Morley null result made necessary.
