🌱 Electron

🌱 Electron

Definition. The electron is a fundamental subatomic particle carrying a charge of $q_e = -e$, where $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$ is the elementary charge. Its rest mass is $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \text{kg}$, making it approximately 1836 times lighter than the proton. Electrons are classified as leptons — point-like particles with no known internal structure — and they occupy quantized energy states in atomic orbitals. In the context of electrostatics and circuit theory, the electron serves as the primary charge carrier in conducting materials, where conduction electrons move quasi-freely through a metallic lattice under an applied electric field.

Worked Example. Consider a copper wire carrying a steady current $I = 2.0\ \text{A}$. How many electrons pass through a cross-sectional plane of the wire each second?

Current is defined as charge per unit time:

$$ I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} \implies \Delta Q = I\,\Delta t. $$

In one second, $\Delta Q = 2.0\ \text{C}$. The number of electrons is:

$$ N = \frac{\Delta Q}{e} = \frac{2.0\ \text{C}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}} \approx 1.25 \times 10^{19}\ \text{electrons}. $$

This enormous number underscores the collective nature of macroscopic electric current; no single electron traverses the entire wire in one second, but the coordinated drift of the electron ensemble produces a measurable, continuous current.

Key Theorem (Conservation of Electron Charge). The charge of an isolated electron is invariant: $q_e = -e$ regardless of the electron's kinetic energy, reference frame, or chemical environment. This invariance, confirmed to at least one part in $10^{21}$ by precision spectroscopy, is the empirical foundation for treating $e$ as a universal constant. Consequently, the total charge in any closed system is conserved because each electron contributes exactly $-e$, with no partial values possible.

Lab Exercise. Using a cathode-ray tube apparatus, apply a known electric field $\vec{E}$ perpendicular to the electron beam and a magnetic field $\vec{B}$ parallel to it. Adjust $B$ until the beam travels undeflected, satisfying $eE = evB$, giving $v = E/B$. Then remove $\vec{B}$ and measure the deflection $y$ of the beam to determine $e/m_e$ via:

$$ \frac{e}{m_e} = \frac{2yE}{B^2 L^2}, $$

where $L$ is the length of the deflecting field region. Compare your result to the accepted value $e/m_e = 1.759 \times 10^{11}\ \text{C\,kg}^{-1}$.
