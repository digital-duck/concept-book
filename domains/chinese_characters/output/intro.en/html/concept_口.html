🌱 口

🌱 口

Everyday example. Think about the shape your lips make when you say "oh" — a small, rounded opening. Now imagine drawing that opening as a simple square. That's exactly what the Chinese character 口 captures: a mouth, an opening, a hole.

Picture this. Look at the character: 口. It's one of the simplest shapes you'll ever write — just a small box. Ancient people drew it as a literal picture of an open mouth seen from the front. Over thousands of years the round shape squared off, but the meaning stayed the same. Whenever you see 口 sitting inside a more complex character, it usually signals something related to speaking, eating, or openings.

Simple rule. 口 (pronounced kǒu) means mouth. As a building block — called a radical — it appears in dozens of characters connected to the mouth's two jobs: making sounds and taking things in. For example, 吃 (chī, to eat) and 叫 (jiào, to shout) both contain 口. Spotting the little square inside a new character is your first clue to its meaning family.

Try it yourself. Write 口 three times — it's just three strokes forming a box (left side down, bottom across, then the right side and top in one stroke). Now look at these characters and find the 口 hiding in each: 吗, 唱, 品. How many mouths does 品 contain?
