🌱 子

🌱 子

Everyday example. Think of a toddler reaching up with both arms, asking to be picked up. That image — a small person with arms stretched wide — is exactly what the ancient Chinese captured when they drew the character 子 (pronounced *zǐ*). It means child or son.

Picture this. The oldest versions of 子 show a baby with a large head, a body, and two little arms waving. Over centuries the strokes simplified, but the essence stayed: a small human figure. Today the character has just three strokes — a horizontal sweep on top, a curving vertical stroke down the middle, and a small horizontal at the bottom. You can still imagine a little person standing there if you squint.

Simple rule. 子 does double duty in Chinese. On its own it means *child*. But it also works as a suffix — a small piece tacked onto the end of another word to make a noun, much like English *-let* in "booklet" or *-ling* in "duckling." For example, 桌 (*zhuō*, table surface) becomes 桌子 (*zhuōzi*, a table — the everyday object). When you see 子 at the end of a word, it usually just signals "this is a thing."

Try it yourself. Write 子 three times slowly. Then spot it hiding inside these common characters: 好 (good), 学 (learn), 字 (written character). In each case, 子 contributes its meaning of *child* or *small person* to the larger character's story.

🌱 女

🌱 女

Everyday example. Think about a person sitting gracefully with their legs tucked to one side — maybe on a mat, maybe beside a low table. That calm, composed posture is exactly what the ancient Chinese captured when they drew the character 女 (*nǚ*), meaning woman or female.

Picture this. The oldest forms of 女, carved into oracle bones over three thousand years ago, show a kneeling figure with arms crossed in front of the body — a formal, respectful pose common in ancient China. Over centuries the picture simplified, but the sweeping strokes still echo that seated silhouette. Look at the modern character: the left-falling stroke and the turning stroke suggest legs folded beneath, while the horizontal stroke hints at arms resting across the lap.

Simple rule. Whenever you see 女 appear as part of a larger character — tucked into the left side or sitting at the bottom — it almost always signals that the meaning is connected to women, family, or relationships. Characters like 妈 (mother), 姐 (older sister), and 好 (good, literally "woman + child") all carry 女 as a building block. Recognizing this one radical unlocks dozens of related characters.

Try it yourself. Write 女 stroke by stroke: (1) a left-falling slash, (2) a turning stroke that curves right then down, (3) a horizontal line through the middle. Now spot 女 hiding inside these characters: 她, 安, 妹. What role might "woman" play in each meaning?

🌱 宀

🌱 宀

Everyday example. Think about what a roof does — it sits on top of everything inside a house, keeping the people and objects beneath it sheltered and safe. That simple idea is exactly what the character component 宀 captures.

Picture this. Look at 宀 carefully. You see a short horizontal stroke on top (the ridge of the roof), with two angled strokes falling away on either side like sloping eaves. It looks like a simple line drawing of a rooftop viewed from the front. This component is called *mián* (pronounced roughly "mee-EN"), and it means roof or shelter.

Simple rule. Whenever you spot 宀 sitting on top of a character, think "something to do with a house, a home, or being sheltered." You can see this pattern across many common characters:

  • 家 (jiā) means "family" or "home" — a roof over a household.
  • 安 (ān) means "peace" — someone safe under a roof.
  • 宝 (bǎo) means "treasure" — precious things kept safe indoors.

    • Notice: the roof always appears at the top, and the meaning always connects back to shelter, safety, or domestic life. You don't need to know the bottom parts yet — just recognise the roof and let it guide your guess.

    Try it yourself. Look at the character 字 (zì, "character/word"). It has 宀 on top. Knowing that the roof suggests nurturing and shelter, can you guess why the ancient Chinese linked that image to the idea of writing and learning?

    🍃 好

    🍃 好

    Everyday example. When a friend asks how you're doing, you might answer with a single word: *hǎo* — "good." This character, 好, is one of the most common words in Chinese. You'll see it in greetings (*nǐ hǎo* — hello), in approval (*hǎo de* — okay), and whenever something is pleasant or satisfactory.

    Picture this. Look closely at 好 and you'll notice it's built from two smaller pieces sitting side by side. On the left is 女 (*nǚ*), meaning "woman." On the right is 子 (*zǐ*), meaning "child." A woman together with her child — that image of a mother and child is the ancient picture of something *good*. This is called a compound ideograph: two meaningful parts combined to suggest a new meaning through their relationship, not their sounds.

    Simple rule. When you see a character split into a left half and a right half, ask yourself: *what story do these two pieces tell together?* In 好, the story is straightforward — a mother with her child represents goodness, harmony, and well-being. Recognizing these building blocks turns an unfamiliar character into a small picture you can read.

    Try it yourself. Write 好 stroke by stroke: first complete 女 (three strokes, left side), then 子 (three strokes, right side). Now think of three situations today where you might say *hǎo* — greeting someone, agreeing to a plan, describing a meal. Notice how one character stretches across all of them.

    🍃 字

    🍃 字

    Everyday example. Think about how families grow — a child is born inside a home, and that home now holds new meaning. The Chinese character 字 (zì) captures exactly this image: it means *written character* or *word*, and its shape tells a little story about creation.

    Picture this. Look at 字 and notice its two parts. On top sits 宀 (mián), a small roof — picture a cozy house. Underneath sits 子 (zǐ), which means *child*. A child sheltered under a roof. The ancient idea is beautiful: just as a family *gives birth* to a child inside a home, a writer *gives birth* to meaning by combining simple pieces into a character. The word 字 itself demonstrates what it describes.

    Simple rule. Many Chinese characters are built by stacking or combining smaller pieces, each carrying its own meaning. When you see an unfamiliar character, look for parts you already recognize — they are clues. Here, knowing 宀 (roof) and 子 (child) lets you read the character like a tiny picture: *something born under a roof* → a created symbol → a written character.

    Try it yourself. Cover the top of 字 with your finger — you see 子 (child). Now cover the bottom — you see 宀 (roof). Practice writing 字 stroke by stroke: first the roof (three strokes), then the child below (three strokes). You have just written the word for *word* itself!

    🌸 Vocabulary Compounds

    🌸 Vocabulary Compounds

    Think about LEGO bricks. Each brick is simple on its own, but snap two together and you build something entirely new. Chinese vocabulary works the same way — individual characters combine into compounds, words made of two or more characters whose meaning grows from its parts.

    Picture this. You already know 火 (fire) and 山 (mountain). Put them together — 火山 — and you get *volcano*. A fire-mountain! Once you see the pieces, the compound almost explains itself.

    A compound (复合词, *fùhécí*) is a word built from two or more characters, where each character contributes a strand of meaning to the whole. Think of it as a simple sum:

    $$\text{compound} = c_1 + c_2$$

    Not every combination is a perfect sum of its parts. Some compounds drift from the literal meaning of their characters — these surprises are normal. Treat each compound as a small puzzle where the pieces give you a head start, not always the full answer.

    Simple rule: Start with compounds whose meaning is transparent from the characters. Build confidence with the predictable ones before tackling the quirky ones.

    Try it yourself. You know 大 (big) and 人 (person). What might 大人 mean? Now try 火 and 人 together — 火人 isn't a real word, which tells you something important: not every pair of characters forms a valid compound. The language decides which combinations stick.

    Payoff 🎯

    Everyday example. You already know the words sun and flower. Put them together and you get sunflower — a brand-new idea built entirely from parts you already own. That small act of combination is exactly what vocabulary compounds are: two or more familiar words joined to create a meaning greater than either piece alone.

    Picture this. Imagine your vocabulary as a box of building blocks. Each block is a word you have learned. A compound is what happens when you snap two blocks together — book + shelf, rain + coat, fire + fly. The number of blocks grows slowly, but the number of possible combinations grows fast. With just 100 blocks you can build thousands of meaningful pairs.

    Simple rule. A vocabulary compound inherits meaning from its parts, so if you recognise the parts you can guess the whole. This is the natural endpoint of everything we have studied: once you own the individual pieces and understand how combination works, unfamiliar words stop being mysteries and start being puzzles you can solve.

    Try it yourself. Pick any compound word you have never seen before — in a newspaper headline, a menu, a product label. Break it into its parts, guess the meaning, then check. Each correct guess proves the same lesson: the building blocks you already carry are more powerful than you think.
