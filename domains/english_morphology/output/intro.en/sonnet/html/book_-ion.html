🌱 Suffix

🌱 Suffix

Everyday example. Imagine you have the word "sunshine". Now start reading it from different points toward the end: "sunshine", "unshine", "nshine", … all the way down to just "e". Each of those tail-pieces is called a suffix — a chunk that goes right to the very last character.

Picture this. Write the string $s = \texttt{"cake"}$ and imagine a pair of scissors that can only cut across the left side:

$$ s = \underbrace{\texttt{c}}_{\text{cut here}}\texttt{ake}, \quad \underbrace{\texttt{ca}}_{\text{cut here}}\texttt{ke}, \quad \ldots $$

Every piece that stays attached to the right end is a suffix. We also count the whole word and the empty string $\varepsilon$ as suffixes.

Simple rule. Formally, if $s$ has length $n$ and its characters are $s_1 s_2 \cdots s_n$, then for any index $i$ with $1 \le i \le n+1$, the string $s_i s_{i+1} \cdots s_n$ is a suffix of $s$. So $s$ has exactly $n + 1$ suffixes (including $\varepsilon$).

Try it yourself. Take $s = \texttt{"dog"}$ (length $3$). List all four suffixes:

$$ \texttt{"dog"}, \quad \texttt{"og"}, \quad \texttt{"g"}, \quad \varepsilon $$

Can you see the pattern? Each step just removes one letter from the front until nothing remains. Great job — you now know exactly what a suffix is!

🍃 -Ion

🍃 -Ion

Have you ever dissolved table salt in water and noticed it conducts electricity? That tiny miracle happens because of ions — atoms or molecules that carry an electric charge.

Everyday example: Imagine a perfectly balanced seesaw. Now add an extra weight to one side — suddenly it tips. Atoms work the same way. A neutral atom has equal numbers of positively charged protons (in its nucleus) and negatively charged electrons (orbiting around it). When an atom loses or gains an electron, the balance tips, and it becomes an ion.

Picture this: Sodium ($\text{Na}$) normally has 11 protons and 11 electrons, so its total charge is zero. When it loses one electron, it has 11 protons but only 10 electrons. The charge becomes:

$$ \text{charge} = +11 + (-10) = +1 $$

We write this as $\text{Na}^+$, a cation (positively charged ion). If an atom gains an electron instead, it becomes an anion (negatively charged ion), like chloride $\text{Cl}^-$.

Simple rule: Lose an electron → positive ion ($+$). Gain an electron → negative ion ($-$).

Try it yourself: Magnesium ($\text{Mg}$) has 12 protons and 12 electrons. If it loses 2 electrons, how many electrons remain? What is the total charge? Write the ion symbol.

(Answer: 10 electrons remain; charge $= +2$; symbol $\text{Mg}^{2+}$)
