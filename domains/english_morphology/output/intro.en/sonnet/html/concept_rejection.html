🍃 Rejection

🍃 Rejection

Everyday example: Imagine you hand your friend a sandwich and say, "Do you want this?" Your friend shakes their head and says, "No, I reject it." The word reject carries a clear action — pushing something away. But notice what happens when we add a small ending to that word: reject becomes rejection. Suddenly, instead of an action, we have a thing — the act of being turned down.

Picture this: Think of a word as a small building. The base of the building is called the root — the core meaning. In rejection, the root is reject (meaning "to push back"). The ending -ion is a suffix — a little piece added to the back of the root. The suffix -ion works like a magic switch: it turns verbs (action words) into nouns (name words).

$$ \text{verb} + \text{-ion} \rightarrow \text{noun} $$

So: $\text{reject} + \text{-ion} = \text{rejection}$

Notice the spelling: the final letter of reject stays, but -ion smoothly attaches to give us rejection, not rejecton.

Simple rule: When you see -ion at the end of an English word, it almost always signals a noun built from a verb.
