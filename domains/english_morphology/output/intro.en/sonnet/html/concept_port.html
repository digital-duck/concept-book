🍃 Port

🍃 Port

Everyday example: Think about the word transport. You use it when moving something — a bus transports passengers, a ship transports cargo. Now think about portable (easy to carry), import (carry goods into a country), and export (carry goods out). What do all these words share? The chunk port.

Picture this: Imagine a tiny building block — a LEGO piece — that means "carry" in Latin. Whenever you see this block snapped into a word, the word has something to do with moving or carrying things from one place to another.

Simple rule: Port is a root — the core meaning-piece of a word. A root is the smallest unit that carries real meaning. The root port always signals carrying or movement. Pieces added before it (called prefixes) tell you the direction:

$$ \underbrace{\text{im}}_{\text{prefix: in}} + \underbrace{\text{port}}_{\text{root: carry}} \;\longrightarrow\; \textit{import} $$ $$ \underbrace{\text{ex}}_{\text{prefix: out}} + \underbrace{\text{port}}_{\text{root: carry}} \;\longrightarrow\; \textit{export} $$

Pieces added after it (called suffixes) adjust the grammar: port + -able → portable (able to be carried).
