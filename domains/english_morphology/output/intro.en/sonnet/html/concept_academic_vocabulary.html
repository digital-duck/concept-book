🌸 Academic Vocabulary

When you walk into a bakery, you quickly learn words like crumb, proof, and glaze — terms that mean something very specific in that world. Academic writing works the same way: it has its own set of words that carry precise, agreed-upon meanings.

Picture this. Imagine two scientists both want to say "the result we found supports our guess." Instead, they write: "The data corroborate our hypothesis." Both sentences mean the same thing, but the second one is shorter, exact, and understood the same way by every reader in the field. That shared precision is the whole point of academic vocabulary.

Simple rule. Academic vocabulary falls into two buckets:

  1. General academic words — words that appear across many subjects, like analyze, evaluate, significant, or hypothesis.
  2. Domain-specific terms — words that belong to one field, like $\Delta$ (change) in physics, or metaphor in literature.

When you encounter an unfamiliar academic word, ask: Is this used everywhere, or only here? That one question tells you how broadly you can apply its meaning.

Try it yourself. Pick any paragraph from a textbook. Underline every word that feels "formal" or field-specific. For each underlined word, write a one-sentence definition in your own everyday language. If you cannot, that is your signal to look it up — you have just found a gap worth closing.
