Have you ever noticed how adding just a couple of letters to the end of a word can completely change it? Consider the word happy. A suffix is a meaningful group of letters added to the end of a larger word. The original word that remains—like happy in our example—is called the base or root.

For example, if we talk about the word act (the deed), adding the suffix -ion turns it into action, which means the whole process or energy of acting out that deed. The suffix $-ion$ basically signals that we are naming a specific event or a continuous flow.

Picture this: Think of an electrical circuit. The core concept is electricity, but if you want to name the flow of that electricity through a wire, you might use the word current. That "-ent" ending describes the constant movement—the process itself. Similarly, $-ion$ allows us to label any action as a complete process. It takes something basic (like an idea or a concept) and names the journey it takes to become real.

The simple rule is this: If you can describe the word's root as a physical or mental process—an activity that happens over time—it probably ends in $-ion$. To figure out if a word belongs here, try asking yourself: "What is the name of this process?" The answer likely has an "-ion" ending.
