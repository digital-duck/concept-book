🌱 Prefix

🌱 Prefix

Have you ever noticed how adding just a few letters to the beginning of a word can totally change its meaning? Consider the word happy. Now, if we put the prefix $\text{un-}$ in front of it, we get unhappy. The root idea—the core concept of happiness—remains, but the entire meaning shifts. We didn't add letters to the end (that would be a suffix), nor did we change anything inside the word. Instead, we added something entirely new right at the start.

Picture this: Think of a base word like a sturdy box. A prefix is like a small sign or sticker that you tape onto the very front edge of that box. This sticker changes what the box represents without altering the box itself. In morphology (the study of word structure), we call these tiny meaning units morphemes. The core, standalone morpheme is called the base (like happy). A prefix is simply a full morpheme added to the beginning of that base.

The simple rule for prefixes is: $\text{Prefix} + \text{Base} = \text{New Word}$. Remember that the prefix itself might not be a word on its own, but it carries meaning (like un- meaning 'not'). To solidify this concept, look at the word re-read. The base is read, and the prefix $\text{re-}$ tells us to do the action again. Try identifying the prefix in words like disagree or preheat.
