🍃 Immune Response

Definition. An immune response ($\mathcal{R}$) is a highly regulated, multi-stage biological process initiated by the recognition of conserved molecular patterns associated with pathogens (PAMPs) or altered self-components (DAMPs). Formally, $\mathcal{R}: \text{Stimulus} \to \{E_1, E_2, \dots\}$, where $E_i$ are effector mechanisms. The process relies critically on Pattern Recognition Receptors (PRRs), which mediate initial signal transduction leading to the activation of transcription factors such as NF-$\kappa\text{B}$ and IRFs ($\text{Signal}_{\text{initial}}$).

Theorem. The adaptive immune response exhibits quantitative scaling properties, specifically satisfying a threshold-dependent amplification criterion. Let $S(t)$ be the concentration of specific antigen detection at time $t$, and let $\theta$ be the activation threshold for clonal expansion. A robust secondary response requires that the initial signal strength $S_0 > \theta/k_{\text{affinity}}$, where $k_{\text{affinity}}$ is the average binding affinity of the receptor-antigen complex, ensuring $\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} \propto (C - S_{crit})^p$ with $p>1$.

Proof. The initial activation signal generates a cascade of cytokines ($c_i$), promoting T-cell proliferation and B-cell differentiation. This process is non-linear; the commitment to effector status ($\text{Effector}$) requires overcoming multiple checkpoints (e.g., $\text{MHC}$ presentation, costimulatory molecule binding). The amplification phase, driven by cytokine feedback loops (e.g., IL-2), ensures that the population growth rate $r$ rapidly exceeds the decay rate $d$, maintaining a positive net reproductive coefficient ($\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$) long enough to achieve pathogen clearance.

Remark. The generalized concept of $\mathcal{R}$ can be viewed through the lens of statistical physics, modeling immune memory as a persistent attractor in the phase space of immunological states. Furthermore, the specificity and breadth observed are generally modeled by computational frameworks that incorporate negative selection (tolerance) and positive selection (MHC restriction), linking traditional immunology to network theory and graph embeddings.
